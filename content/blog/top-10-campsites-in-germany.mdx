German camping is orderly, scenic, and superbly equipped. From Alpine lakes to
Baltic dunes, these ten areas show why it is one of Europe's great camping
countries.

## 1. Königssee, Bavaria

Camp beneath sheer cliffs by an emerald fjord-lake in Berchtesgaden — the
postcard of German camping.

## 2. Lake Constance (Bodensee)

Lakeshore sites with the Alps across the water and three countries within a
ferry ride.

## 3. Black Forest (Schwarzwald)

Deep-forest and valley camping, cuckoo-clock villages, and excellent cycling.

## 4. Müritz & the Mecklenburg Lakes

A thousand lakes and Germany's largest inland water park for canoe camping.

## 5. Bavarian Alps — Tegernsee & Walchensee

Turquoise mountain lakes with classic well-run lakeside sites.

## 6. Baltic coast — Rügen & Usedom

Dune and pine camping on long white beaches and chalk cliffs.

## 7. Moselle Valley

Riverside vineyard camping beneath castles and steep slopes.

## 8. Saxon Switzerland

Sandstone-tower national-park camping near Dresden, a hiker's paradise.

## 9. Berchtesgaden National Park

Alpine wilderness camping at the foot of the Watzmann.

## 10. Spreewald, Brandenburg

Camp among canals and punt your gear in — uniquely tranquil water camping.

## How German camping works

German sites are typically well-maintained, family-run or club-run, with
strong facilities and clear rules. Many require a *Campingcard* and have firm
quiet hours (Ruhezeit) — Germans take these seriously. Wild camping is largely
prohibited; *Stellplätze* (motorhome aires) are abundant and cheap for vans.

## When to go

May–September is the season; July–August is busiest, especially lakeside.
Late spring and early autumn bring space and mild weather. Alpine sites open
later as snow clears.

## Booking and etiquette

Lakeside Bavarian sites book out for summer — reserve ahead. Observe quiet
hours strictly, separate and dispose of waste correctly (recycling is taken
seriously), keep pitches tidy, and follow site rules on fires and generators.
