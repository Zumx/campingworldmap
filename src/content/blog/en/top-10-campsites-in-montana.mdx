Montana is one of the few places in the contiguous United States where you can genuinely camp in wilderness that feels complete — where grizzly bears, wolves, wolverines, and mountain goats still inhabit their historic ranges in functional numbers, where rivers run clear from mountain glaciers, and where a week of backpacking may not require crossing a paved road. The state covers nearly 150,000 square miles, but its population of just over one million people means that the human imprint outside the few small cities is light. Camping in Montana is often a matter of choosing between the merely spectacular and the genuinely profound.

The camping season varies by elevation. Valley campsites at lower elevations are accessible from May through October; high-country backcountry routes may not be snow-free until late June or early July and can see early snowfall again by September. Grizzly bear safety is not optional in Montana — hanging food, using bear canisters, and carrying bear spray are essential practice across most of the western half of the state. Once that becomes habitual, it adds rather than detracts from the experience: you are camping in a place where the full predator community still exists. ## 1. Glacier National Park, Flathead County

Glacier National Park is the crown jewel of Montana's camping landscape — a compact cluster of glaciated peaks, turquoise lakes, and high alpine meadows straddling the Continental Divide near the Canadian border. The park has thirteen front-country campgrounds and a backcountry permit system with more than 200 designated campsites accessible by trail. The Going-to-the-Sun Road, crossing Logan Pass at 2,026 metres, is one of the most scenic drives in North America and provides access to trailheads for backcountry routes including the High Route traverse above the Highline Trail. Many Glacier campground in the northeastern sector is one of the most consistently stunning campsite locations in the American West.

## 2. Bob Marshall Wilderness Complex, Lewis and Clark/Flathead/Powell Counties

The "Bob" — as it is universally known — is a contiguous wilderness complex of more than 1.5 million acres making it one of the largest wilderness areas in the lower 48 states. It encompasses the Bob Marshall, Scapegoat, and Great Bear wilderness areas, connected in a roadless block east of the Swan Range. The Chinese Wall, a 22-mile-long escarpment rising 1,000 feet above the surrounding terrain, is the iconic landmark of the interior. Access requires significant walking from peripheral trailheads — the shortest approaches are 10-plus miles. Horse-packing is traditional and widely used. Grizzly bear encounters are common, and this is genuine grizzly country that demands respect and preparedness.

## 3. Beartooth Plateau, Carbon County

The Beartooth Highway (US Route 212) between Red Lodge, Montana, and Cooke City crests a plateau of nearly 11,000 feet — the highest highway in the American West — and the terrain on either side of the road is open, rolling tundra, dotted with alpine lakes and accessible for dispersed camping under Forest Service regulations. The plateau is part of the Custer Gallatin National Forest, and free dispersed camping is permitted at least 100 feet from water and roads. The Beartooth Plateau camping experience — broad skies, visible weather systems, wildflower meadows of brief intensity — is one of the defining Montana experiences, and the proximity of Yellowstone's northeast entrance via Cooke City extends the itinerary possibilities.

## 4. Lee Metcalf Wilderness, Madison County

The Lee Metcalf Wilderness encompasses several wilderness units in the Madison and Gallatin ranges south of the Gallatin Gateway near Bozeman. The Taylor-Hilgard unit in the Madison Range is the most expansive, with high granite peaks above 11,000 feet, glacial lakes, and extensive backcountry trails. Camping within the wilderness is unregulated except for designated zones around popular lakes. The Hilgard Peak area and the Spanish Peaks zone are particularly rewarding, with high ridgelines giving views across the greater Yellowstone ecosystem. Wildlife includes grizzly bears, black bears, mountain goats, and a growing wolf presence.

## 5. Absaroka-Beartooth Wilderness, Park County

The A-B Wilderness east of Yellowstone in Park County includes the Absaroka Range — a complex of volcanic mountains visually very different from the granite terrain of the northern Rockies — and the Beartooth alpine plateau. The Slough Creek drainage accessible from the Yellowstone northeastern entrance is prime grizzly and wolf habitat, and the wilderness boundary just north allows backcountry camping adjacent to one of the most wildlife-rich valleys in the Yellowstone ecosystem. The Stillwater drainage and the Rosebud Lakes area on the eastern edge of the wilderness are less traveled and offer solitude that the more famous Yellowstone adjacent routes cannot.

## 6. Rattlesnake National Recreation Area and Wilderness, Missoula County

The Rattlesnake Wilderness is immediately north of Missoula, making it one of the most accessible backcountry camping areas relative to an urban center in the entire Rocky Mountain West. The wilderness trails begin at the trailhead just minutes from downtown Missoula and ascend quickly into the rugged terrain of the Rattlesnake Creek drainage. Day hikers give way to backcountry campers within a few miles of the trailhead. The area is modest in scale compared to the larger wilderness areas of western Montana but serves as an invaluable first-taste of genuine Montana backcountry for visitors based in Missoula.

## 7. Kootenai National Forest, Lincoln County

The Kootenai in the extreme northwest corner of Montana — bordered by Canada to the north and Idaho to the west — is the state's most heavily forested national forest, with enormous western red cedar and western hemlock trees in the wetter valleys near the Idaho border reaching dimensions more typical of the Pacific Northwest than the Rocky Mountain interior. Several developed campgrounds on the Yaak River and at Lake Koocanusa, a vast reservoir on the Kootenai River, provide car-accessible camping. The area holds grizzly bears, gray wolves, and westslope cutthroat trout. The Yaak Valley, in particular, is famous among conservation writers as one of the most isolated habitats in the lower 48.

## 8. Missouri River Breaks National Monument, Phillips/Blaine Counties

The Upper Missouri River Breaks National Monument in north-central Montana protects the river corridor as Lewis and Clark navigated it in 1805 — a landscape of white chalk cliffs, juniper draws, and prairie coulees that looks essentially as it did two centuries ago. The 150-mile canoe route from Fort Benton to the Fred Robinson Bridge is one of the finest river camping trips in North America, with sandbar camping permitted throughout. Bighorn sheep, mule deer, pronghorn, and prairie falcons inhabit the breaks. The solitude is extraordinary — this is one of the least-visited national monument corridors in the United States.

## 9. Makoshika State Park, Dawson County

Montana's largest state park sits in the badlands of eastern Montana near Glendive — a landscape of eroded dinosaur-fossil-bearing Cretaceous sediments forming buttes, hoodoos, and dry gulches very different from the mountain landscapes of western Montana. The campground at Makoshika is small and uncrowded, surrounded by the pastel-colored formation walls. Triceratops and Tyrannosaurus rex fossils have been found throughout the park's badland terrain, and an interpretive center at the entrance explains the paleontological context. The sunsets over the badlands — warm light raking across the eroded forms — are as spectacular as anything in the more famous Dakota badlands.

## 10. Bighorn Canyon National Recreation Area, Bighorn County

Bighorn Canyon straddles the Montana-Wyoming border, where the Bighorn River has cut through Pryor Mountain limestone in a deep canyon system backed by the Bighorn Lake reservoir. The Afterbay campground near Fort Smith is the primary base for fishing and kayaking the tailwater below the Yellowtail Dam — one of the most productive trout fisheries in the Rocky Mountain West. The Pryor Mountains east of the canyon are home to a herd of wild horses descended from Spanish colonial stock. The canyon cliffs support large raptor populations, and the area's combination of geological drama, fishing, and wild horse viewing makes it one of eastern Montana's most distinctive camping destinations.
