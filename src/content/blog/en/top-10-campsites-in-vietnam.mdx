Vietnam stretches more than 1,600 kilometres from the Chinese border in the north to the Ca Mau Peninsula in the deep south, and encompasses a striking range of environments: the karst limestone peaks and terraced rice valleys of the northern highlands, the tropical forests and cave systems of central Vietnam, and the coastal plains and delta landscape of the south. Camping culture has grown rapidly over the past decade, driven by a young Vietnamese domestic travel market alongside international backpackers and adventure tourists. Facilities range from organised campgrounds near tourist attractions to community-guided camping in ethnic minority villages in the Sapa and Ha Giang highlands, where overnight stays form part of community-based tourism programs.

## 1. Cat Ba Island Camping, Cat Ba National Park, Hai Phong

Cat Ba Island is the largest island in Halong Bay, and Cat Ba National Park covers approximately half of the island's area in tropical moist forest. The national park's core zone contains the golden-headed langur, one of the world's rarest primates with a population of around 60-70 individuals, all confined to the island. Camping in Cat Ba can be organised through local tour operators on the island, with options for overnight stays in the forest interior or on beaches on the island's south coast accessible by kayak. Cat Ba town has full tourist infrastructure and regular ferries connect to Hai Phong on the mainland. The park's Ngu Lam peak trail offers views over Halong Bay's karst islands.

## 2. Phong Nha-Ke Bang Camping, Quang Binh Province

Phong Nha-Ke Bang National Park in central Vietnam contains the world's largest cave system by several measures: Son Doong cave, discovered in 1990 and opened to expeditions in 2013, has a measured volume larger than any other known cave, with passages wide enough to contain a Boeing 747 and a jungle ecosystem inside growing under sky-holes in the ceiling. Overnight camping inside Son Doong requires a guided multi-day expedition costing several thousand US dollars — but the park offers other cave camping experiences at more accessible price points through Oxalis Adventure Tours, the licensed operator. Camping at the Tu Lan cave complex is the lower-cost alternative, with underground river passages and jungle camps.

## 3. Sapa Valley Trekking Camps, Lao Cai Province

Sapa is the highland town in the Hoang Lien Son range near the Chinese border where the Hmong, Dao, and other highland ethnic minorities have maintained distinct cultural traditions for centuries. Trekking itineraries from Sapa into the surrounding valleys, including the Muong Hoa valley with its rice terraces and the villages of Ta Van and Lao Chai, incorporate overnight camping or homestays with local families. Community-based tourism operators in Sapa organise village camping where tents are pitched in farmyards with meals provided by the host family. The landscape around Sapa includes Vietnam's highest peak, Fansipan at 3,147 metres, which can be climbed in two days with overnight camping on the ridge.

## 4. Con Dao Island Camping, Ba Ria-Vung Tau Province

The Con Dao archipelago, 180 kilometres south of Vung Tau, is one of Vietnam's most ecologically important marine areas — the nesting ground for the largest sea turtle population in the country, primarily green turtles that nest on Dam Trau and Bay Canh beaches from June through September. Con Dao National Park manages the islands and operates a turtle monitoring and protection program. Camping on Bay Canh island, the most important nesting beach, is organised through the park with guided night watches for turtle observation. The island is accessible by flight from Ho Chi Minh City or by ferry, and accommodation on the main Con Son island ranges from resort to basic guesthouse.

## 5. Da Lat City Forest Camping, Lam Dong Province

Da Lat is Vietnam's highland resort city, at 1,500 metres elevation in the Lang Biang Plateau, developed during the French colonial period as a retreat from the coastal heat. The surrounding pine forests and lakes have a temperate character unusual in Southeast Asia, and night temperatures can drop to single figures in December and January. Camping in the Da Lat area, including organised sites around Tuyen Lam Lake and the Elephant Waterfall area south of town, has become popular with Vietnamese domestic campers who appreciate the cool climate. The Lang Biang mountain above town, at 2,167 metres, is a straightforward day hike with summit views across the plateau.

## 6. Ba Be Lake Camping, Bac Kan Province

Ba Be National Park in the north-central highlands protects the largest natural freshwater lake in Vietnam — Ba Be (Three Seas) Lake, formed by three connected bodies of water totalling about 8 kilometres in length. The park's karst limestone forest is one of the most biodiverse in northern Vietnam, and the lake supports freshwater fish species including the Ba Be giant softshell turtle, listed as critically endangered. Homestay camping on islands in the lake and in the Tay ethnic minority villages around the shore is organised through local community operators. Night boat trips on the lake, listening to the forest sounds and observing bats emerging from cave roosts, are a distinctive feature of the Ba Be experience.

## 7. Bach Ma National Park Camping, Thua Thien Hue Province

Bach Ma National Park occupies a ridge of mountains rising steeply from the coastal plain near Hue, with a summit plateau at 1,444 metres that marks the boundary between Vietnam's northern and southern climate zones — a distinction with profound ecological significance. The French built a hill station on the summit in the 1930s; the ruins of the resort buildings and water tanks remain. The Ngu Ho (Five Lakes) trail descends from the summit through a series of connected waterfalls and pools. The park campsite at the summit area is basic but the birding is exceptional: Bach Ma has over 330 recorded species and is one of the better sites for the endemic crested argus pheasant.

## 8. Mui Ne Sand Dunes Camping, Binh Thuan Province

Mui Ne is famous for its red and white sand dunes on the coast of southern Vietnam's Binh Thuan Province, an area with the lowest rainfall in the country and a desert character unusual in a tropical country. The red dune area north of the resort town allows camping with organised operators offering sunrise and sunset dune experiences. The Fairy Stream, a shallow reddish-coloured stream cutting through chalk and red sandstone formations, runs parallel to the coast and can be waded for several kilometres. The fishing village at Mui Ne harbour operates one of the largest basket-boat fleets in Vietnam, visible in the early morning as boats return from the night's fishing.

## 9. Bidoup Nui Ba National Park, Lam Dong Province

Bidoup Nui Ba is adjacent to Da Lat and protects the highest biodiversity forest in the Lang Biang Plateau, including the habitat of the Germain's peacock-pheasant and the Vietnamese pheasant. The park's trekking and camping program is managed through the national park office in Da Lat and is oriented toward birders and naturalists. Multi-day treks with overnight camping in the forest access areas rarely visited by tourists. The park contains a significant orchid collection, with several hundred species identified in the plateau forest, many endemic to the Central Highlands. The Bidoup mountain at 2,287 metres is the second-highest peak in the south of the country.

## 10. Ly Son Island Camping, Quang Ngai Province

Ly Son Island, 15 nautical miles off the Quang Ngai coast, is a small volcanic island noted for its garlic cultivation — the island produces a distinctive small-bulb garlic known as Ly Son toi that has geographic indication protection. The volcanic landscape includes the Thoi Loi crater lake, which fills seasonally with rainwater, and coral reef surrounding the island that is accessible for snorkelling. Camping on the island is basic and organised through guesthouse operators who have added tent accommodation for the growing domestic camping tourism market. The island's remoteness — it is not on major tourist circuits — preserves a working fishing community character that the more visited destinations in Vietnam have largely lost.
