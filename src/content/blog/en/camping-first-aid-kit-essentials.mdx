A camping first aid kit is not just a box of supplies — it is a system for managing medical emergencies in environments where professional medical help may be hours or days away. The difference between a front-country first aid kit (for campsite camping with vehicle access within a short drive of a town) and a backcountry kit (for multi-day wilderness camping where evacuation may require helicopter or foot travel) is significant in both scope and weight. Understanding which situation you are preparing for is the starting point for building an appropriate kit.

Many campers carry the pre-assembled first aid kits sold at outdoor retailers and consider the medical preparedness problem solved. These kits provide a reasonable starting point but are typically designed as commercial compromise products — adequate coverage of minor wound care, insufficient for the scenarios most likely to create serious problems in the backcountry. A thoughtfully assembled custom kit, matched to your actual trip type and combined with basic wilderness first aid training, is substantially more effective than any off-the-shelf option. The following covers the core categories and specific items that should be in any serious camping first aid kit.

## Wound Care

The most common camping injuries are cuts, abrasions, blisters, and puncture wounds, and wound care is therefore the category that deserves the most attention. Carry a range of wound closure options: adhesive bandages in multiple sizes, butterfly closure strips for deeper cuts that gape open, and sterile gauze pads (4 x 4 inch and 2 x 2 inch) for larger wounds. Medical tape (1-inch and 2-inch rolls) is the universal fixative. Moleskin and pre-cut blister treatment pads (Compeed is the most widely recommended brand) belong in every kit regardless of trip type.

Irrigation is critical for wound infection prevention: a 20 mL or larger irrigation syringe with a splash guard delivers water at sufficient pressure to clean debris from wounds effectively. Povidone-iodine solution or tablets (used to make dilute irrigation solution, not poured directly into wounds in full concentration) provide antiseptic cleaning. A small bottle of benzalkonium chloride wound irrigation solution is a good alternative. SAM splints (two of them) handle improvised immobilisation for suspected fractures or sprains.

## Medications

A camping medication kit requires both over-the-counter and, for serious backcountry travel, prescription medications discussed in advance with a physician. Core over-the-counter medications include: ibuprofen (anti-inflammatory and analgesic for pain and fever), acetaminophen (paracetamol — alternative analgesic for those who cannot take ibuprofen), diphenhydramine (antihistamine for allergic reactions and sleep aid), loperamide (anti-diarrhoeal for gastrointestinal illness), and antacid tablets.

For backcountry trips in wilderness settings, discuss the following prescription medications with your doctor: an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if there is any known or suspected anaphylaxis risk from insect stings or food allergies; a broad-spectrum antibiotic (most commonly azithromycin or doxycycline, suitable for wound infections, respiratory infections, and gastrointestinal illness depending on the prescription); and a prescription altitude sickness medication (acetazolamide, also called Diamox) if your route includes significant elevation gain above 3,000 metres.

## Blister Prevention and Treatment

Blisters are among the most trip-limiting injuries in camping and hiking and deserve a dedicated section of any kit. Prevention materials include Leukotape P or Fixomull stretch tape applied to hotspot areas before hiking begins — this prophylactic taping approach dramatically reduces blister incidence on long hiking days. For blisters that have already formed, the Compeed hydrocolloid pad approach (leave in place until it falls off naturally) is more effective than the traditional approach of draining and protecting with gauze.

A needle sterilised with a flame or alcohol swab is needed to drain large, painful blisters before applying protective coverage. Drain blisters from the side, keeping the blister roof intact as a natural wound covering. Drain, treat with antiseptic, and cover with a hydrocolloid dressing.

## Splints, Wraps, and Immobilisation

Musculoskeletal injuries — sprains, fractures, and dislocations — are the second most common serious camping injury category after wounds. Carry two SAM splints (the flexible aluminium foam-covered strips used to immobilise arms and legs), an ACE elastic bandage (for compression wrapping sprains), and additional medical tape. A triangular bandage (cravat) functions as a sling, as a wound dressing, and as a general-purpose immobilisation tool. Trekking poles, if carried, can be repurposed as improvised splint supports in an emergency.

## Tools and Assessment Equipment

A small headlamp (always in the kit rather than assuming battery availability in a primary headlamp), bandage scissors with blunt tips for safe removal of clothing from wounds, medical exam gloves (nitrile, multiple pairs), a hypothermia thermometer rated for low temperatures, and tweezers designed for tick removal (Tick Twister or similar) complete the tools section. A SAM splint and a space blanket (emergency foil blanket) should be present in every kit regardless of trip type.

## Signalling and Communication

While not strictly first aid equipment, a personal locator beacon (PLB) or satellite communicator (Garmin inReach, SPOT) belongs in the same category as the first aid kit — it is the tool you use when the first aid situation exceeds your kit's capacity. Without a communication device, a serious injury in a remote location becomes an evacuation problem that requires either self-rescue or waiting for others to report you overdue. The cost of a PLB has fallen to the point where it is affordable for recreational campers, and rental options are widely available.

## Training: The Most Important Element

Equipment without training is of limited value. A Wilderness First Aid (WFA) course — typically 16 to 20 hours over a weekend — covers the assessment and treatment skills needed to manage medical emergencies in backcountry settings for up to 72 hours. The Wilderness Medical Associates, NOLS Wilderness Medicine, and Wilderness Medical Institute all offer these courses at outdoor recreation centres across the United States. The WAFA certification is a higher level covering more complex scenarios.
