Cooking over an open fire is one of the most satisfying parts of camping. There is something fundamentally different about a meal prepared over wood coals — the smokiness, the patience required, the way heat behaves unpredictably compared to a kitchen stove. For beginners, campfire cooking can feel intimidating, but most classic camp recipes are actually designed around simplicity: limited ingredients, forgiving heat tolerances, and minimal equipment. With a few techniques and a short list of core recipes, you can eat extraordinarily well on any campsite.

The key to successful campfire cooking is fire management. Most beginners make the mistake of cooking over flames rather than coals. A roaring fire looks dramatic but is hard to cook on — food chars on the outside while remaining raw inside. The goal is a bed of glowing coals, which provide steady, even radiant heat. Build your fire at least 45 minutes before you plan to cook, let it burn down, and work with the coals rather than the flames. This one shift in technique improves almost every campfire recipe dramatically.

## Foil Packet Potatoes and Vegetables

The foil packet is the most forgiving technique in campfire cooking and the best starting point for any beginner. Cut potatoes into roughly 2 cm cubes, mix with sliced bell pepper, onion, olive oil, salt, pepper, and any herbs you enjoy. Place the mixture on a double layer of heavy-duty aluminium foil, fold the edges tightly to create a sealed packet, and place it directly on the hot coals. Turn after 15 minutes and cook for another 10 to 15 minutes. Open carefully — steam burns are real. The vegetables inside will be tender and fragrant with a slight caramelisation where they touched the foil. Variations are endless: add sliced sausage, substitute sweet potato, or include cherry tomatoes that burst into a sauce.

## Cast Iron Skillet Scrambled Eggs

A cast iron skillet is the single most versatile piece of campfire cookware and worth carrying even when weight is a concern. For scrambled eggs, heat the skillet over moderate coals until a drop of water evaporates immediately on contact. Add butter, let it melt, then pour in beaten eggs mixed with salt and a splash of water. The key is to move the eggs constantly with a wooden spoon, pulling them from the edges toward the centre. Remove the skillet from the heat while the eggs are still slightly underdone — residual heat finishes them. Serve with camp-toast made by holding bread slices over the coals on a long fork or a wire toasting rack.

## One-Pot Pasta with Canned Tomatoes

One-pot pasta cooked in a single pot over a camp stove or tripod grate is a genuinely practical dinner that tastes far better than it sounds. Combine pasta, canned crushed tomatoes, water to cover, garlic, salt, dried oregano, and a drizzle of olive oil in a pot. Bring to a boil and cook, stirring frequently to prevent sticking, until the pasta is tender and has absorbed most of the liquid. The starch released by the pasta thickens the sauce naturally. Add canned tuna or pre-cooked sausage slices for protein. This recipe uses only one pot, produces minimal washing up, and can be completed in under 20 minutes.

## Campfire Chilli

Chilli is an ideal campfire recipe because it improves the longer it cooks and is almost impossible to ruin. Start with ground beef or canned beans (or both), browning the meat in a pot over the fire. Add canned tomatoes, kidney beans, chilli powder, cumin, garlic, and onion. Simmer over low coals for at least 30 minutes, stirring occasionally and adjusting the distance from the heat to maintain a gentle bubble without scorching the bottom. Chilli scales up easily for group camping, keeps well in a sealed container for a second-night meal, and tastes even better reheated. Serve with crackers or cornbread baked in a Dutch oven if you are feeling ambitious.

## Dutch Oven Cornbread

A Dutch oven — a heavy lidded pot that can be placed directly in coals with coals stacked on the lid — functions as a portable oven and opens up baked recipes that seem impossible at a campsite. Cornbread is a forgiving starting point. Prepare a dry mix at home (cornmeal, flour, baking powder, salt, sugar) in a sealed bag. At the campsite, add eggs, oil, and milk from a small carton, stir to a rough batter, pour into a lightly greased Dutch oven, and place on a ring of coals with coals on the lid. Check after 20 minutes: the cornbread is done when a stick or skewer inserted in the centre comes out clean. The result is a warm, golden bread that transforms any camp meal into a feast.

## Banana Boats

Dessert at a campfire does not require bringing specialised equipment or perishable ingredients. Banana boats are prepared by slitting an unpeeled banana lengthways, tucking in chocolate chips and mini marshmallows, wrapping the banana in foil, and placing it on the coals for eight to ten minutes. The banana softens into a warm custard, the chocolate melts, and the marshmallows become gooey. Eat directly from the foil with a spoon. The same technique works with peanut butter instead of chocolate, or with sliced strawberries and a drizzle of honey.

## Camp French Toast

French toast requires only eggs, bread, milk, butter, and a skillet — all ingredients that travel well to a campsite. Beat eggs with a splash of milk and a pinch of cinnamon in a cup or small bowl. Dip thick slices of bread (slightly stale bread works best as it absorbs the egg mixture without falling apart) and fry in butter over moderate heat until golden on both sides. Serve with maple syrup brought in a small bottle, or with jam. French toast cooks in under 15 minutes and provides a filling, high-energy breakfast for a day of hiking or paddling.

## Hobo Stew

Hobo stew is a traditional camp recipe made entirely from pantry staples: canned beef stew, canned corn, and diced potatoes combined in a pot and simmered until the potatoes soften. The original recipe is infinitely adaptable — substitute the beef stew for lentil soup, add canned chickpeas, include whatever vegetables you have available. Season generously with salt, pepper, and hot sauce if you carry it. The appeal is simplicity: no fresh ingredients needed, no preparation skill required, and a deeply satisfying result after a long day outdoors.

## Planning Your Campfire Menu

Before your trip, plan meals around a few key principles. Match recipe complexity to your likely energy level — simple meals for the first night after a long drive, more elaborate cooking when you are settled and rested. Pre-prep as much as possible at home: measure spices into small bags, pre-chop vegetables that store well, mix dry ingredients for baked recipes. Carry a reliable fire starter and know whether your campsite permits fires or requires a camp stove.

You can [browse campgrounds on the map](/map) to find sites with fire rings, established cooking grills, and access to water — all details that affect what cooking approach is most practical for any given location. Good campfire cooking starts before you leave home, with a well-chosen site and a simple, tested menu.

## Equipment That Makes a Difference

For beginners, a short list of equipment covers almost every campfire recipe: a cast iron skillet (10-inch), a heavy-bottomed pot with a lid (3-4 litre), a Dutch oven (optional but transformative), long-handled tongs, a wooden spoon, and heavy-duty aluminium foil. A folding grate that sits over the fire ring at a consistent height makes heat management far easier than balancing pots on rocks. A reliable cutting board and a sharp camp knife round out a functional camp kitchen. With this kit and the recipes above, a camping beginner can cook meals that rival anything from a front-country restaurant.
