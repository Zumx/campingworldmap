Malaysia's forests are among the oldest on Earth — some Borneo rainforest ecosystems have remained continuously forested for 130 million years, surviving ice ages and continental drift that wiped out forests elsewhere. For campers, this biological antiquity translates into a diversity of wildlife encounters almost unmatched in Southeast Asia: proboscis monkeys, pygmy elephants, sun bears, clouded leopards, hornbills, and pitcher plants all inhabit accessible camping terrain. The country divides naturally into Peninsular Malaysia, which stretches south from Thailand to Singapore, and Malaysian Borneo, comprising the states of Sabah and Sarawak on the island of Borneo.

The climate is equatorial — warm and humid year-round with distinct wet seasons that vary by region. The East Coast of Peninsular Malaysia receives heavy monsoon rains between November and March, closing some national park trails. Borneo's Sabah coast has its driest and calmest weather from March to October. Planning around these patterns makes a significant difference to camping comfort and trail accessibility. ## 1. Taman Negara National Park, Pahang

Taman Negara is Peninsular Malaysia's oldest and largest national park, protecting a tract of lowland and highland dipterocarp rainforest estimated to be 130 million years old. The park headquarters at Kuala Tahan are reached by a three-hour boat trip from Jerantut, and camping permits are issued at the park office. Designated camping sites within the park include Bumbun Cegar Anjing, Yong, and the popular Rentis campsite. The park's famous canopy walkway — 530 metres of suspension bridges through the forest canopy — gives a treetop perspective on the ecosystem. Wildlife is abundant but often secretive: night walks reveal flying squirrels, slow lorises, and a remarkable diversity of insects and frogs.

## 2. Gunung Kinabalu, Sabah, Borneo

Mount Kinabalu at 4,095 metres is Southeast Asia's highest peak and the centerpiece of Kinabalu National Park — a UNESCO World Heritage Site renowned for extraordinary plant diversity, including carnivorous pitcher plants and over 1,000 orchid species. The summit climb involves one overnight at Laban Rata or Pendant Hut at 3,270 metres, and camping spots adjacent to the dormitories are bookable. Permits for the climb are limited and must be reserved well in advance through Sabah Parks. The granite summit plateau above the hut — reached in the pre-dawn darkness for sunrise — is a lunar landscape of bare rock and twisted bonsai-like shrubs surviving at the edge of the treeline.

## 3. Bako National Park, Sarawak, Borneo

Bako is Sarawak's oldest national park and one of the best places on Earth to see proboscis monkeys — the large, bulbous-nosed monkeys endemic to Borneo. The park sits on a rocky coastal headland near Kuching, accessible by a 30-minute boat ride. Camping at the park headquarters complex is available alongside simple chalets and dormitories. The trail network covers mangroves, sandstone sea cliffs, pitcher plant-rich kerangas (heath forest), and open plateau, creating exceptional biodiversity in a compact area. Silverleaf monkeys, bearded pigs, and numerous hornbill species are regular campsite visitors.

## 4. Danum Valley Conservation Area, Sabah, Borneo

Danum Valley protects one of the largest remaining blocks of primary lowland Bornean rainforest — habitat for pygmy elephants, Bornean orangutans, clouded leopards, and Banteng wild cattle. The Danum Valley Field Centre offers research-linked accommodation and guided camping experiences, and the Borneo Rainforest Lodge on the Segama River provides upmarket tented options. Night drives on the forest roads are essential — civets, flying squirrels, sambar deer, and occasionally clouded leopards are seen regularly after dark. Access is by permit, and visitor numbers are deliberately kept low to minimize impact on the wildlife.

## 5. Gunung Mulu National Park, Sarawak, Borneo

Mulu is a UNESCO World Heritage Site protecting the world's most studied tropical karst landscape — a complex of limestone mountains riddled with enormous cave systems. The Sarawak Chamber in Deer Cave is reputedly the world's largest cave chamber by volume. Camping in the park is available at the park headquarters and at remote jungle camps accessible via multi-day Headhunters Trail trek. The trail connects Mulu with the Limbang River along an historic Kenyah headhunting route, crossing longhouses and jungle river crossings. The nightly exodus of millions of wrinkle-lipped bats from Deer Cave at dusk is one of Southeast Asia's most spectacular wildlife events.

## 6. Perhentian Islands, Terengganu

The Perhentian Islands off the northeast coast of Peninsular Malaysia are one of Malaysia's finest dive and snorkeling destinations, with coral reefs beginning just metres from several beaches. Camping on the beach at Pasir Panjang on Perhentian Kecil is available through several simple operators, putting campers directly on the sand with the reef accessible by wading from shore. Green and hawksbill turtles nest on several Perhentian beaches and are regularly seen underwater. The island atmosphere is relaxed and low-key compared to more developed Southeast Asian beach destinations, and the November-March off-season closes most accommodation, keeping the pre-season weeks in March beautifully quiet for early campers.

## 7. Royal Belum State Park, Perak

Royal Belum is the wildest of Peninsular Malaysia's protected areas — a jungle covering the northern Perak highlands along the Thai border, accessible only by boat on the Temenggor Lake reservoir system. The park has no road access into its interior; all movements are by boat. Licensed operators run multi-day camping expeditions into the forest, with simple jungle camps on riverbanks. Royal Belum holds the only population of Malayan tigers, Asian elephants, Sumatran rhinoceros, and Malayan tapirs on the peninsula where all four megafauna species still coexist. This is one of the most genuinely remote camping experiences available in peninsular Malaysia.

## 8. Gunung Stong State Park, Kelantan

The Gunung Stong massif in interior Kelantan contains Jeram Stong — one of the most spectacular waterfalls in Malaysia — cascading in multiple tiers down a jungle-covered cliff face. The park has basic camping facilities at several points on the trail network, and the climb to the plateau above the falls through primary montane forest passes through exceptional bird and mammal habitat. Sumatran serow, wild boar, and numerous primate species inhabit the forest. The park is far less visited than the better-known parks of Pahang and Sabah, and the trail infrastructure, while basic, is adequate for experienced jungle campers.

## 9. Cameron Highlands, Pahang

The Cameron Highlands in central Pahang sit at 1,500 metres and have a cool, misty climate entirely different from Malaysia's lowlands. The area is famous for its tea plantations, but the surrounding Brinchang and Gunung Irau ridge area has extensive primary montane forest supporting a rich bird community including Malayan whistling thrush, mountain peacock-pheasant, and numerous endemic highland species. Camping within the forest reserves is permitted in designated areas. The mossy cloud forest on the higher ridges is otherworldly — every surface covered in dripping moss and epiphytic plants — and the fog that rolls through in the afternoons gives the highland camping experience a distinctly atmospheric quality.

## 10. Endau-Rompin National Park, Johor/Pahang

Endau-Rompin straddles the Johor-Pahang border and protects a significant area of lowland and foothill rainforest that serves as one of the last refugia for the Malayan tapir, Asian elephant, and the critically endangered Sumatran rhinoceros on the southern peninsula. The park has a tented camp at Kuala Jasin accessible by 4x4 from Kahang. The Selai River entry on the Pahang side accesses a more remote sector. Waterfalls within the park — particularly the Buaya Sangkut falls — are outstanding, and the network of rivers connecting the camp provide the main navigation routes through the interior. The park requires guided access and advance permits, keeping visitor impact minimal.
