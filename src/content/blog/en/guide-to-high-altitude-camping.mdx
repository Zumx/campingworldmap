Above 2500 metres, the atmosphere begins to work against you in ways that no amount of physical fitness fully compensates for. The air is thinner — not in oxygen percentage, which remains constant at roughly 21 percent regardless of altitude, but in absolute density. Each breath delivers fewer oxygen molecules, and the body's response to this deficit unfolds over hours and days in ways that range from mildly inconvenient to medically serious. High-altitude camping is rewarding precisely because the effort required filters the landscape. Mountains above 3000 metres are often accessible only on foot, camping is frequently the only realistic overnight option, and the combination of altitude, remoteness, and elevated terrain produces some of the most spectacular camping environments on Earth.

## How Altitude Affects the Body

The primary physiological challenge of altitude is hypoxia: reduced oxygen delivery to tissues. At 3000 metres, the partial pressure of oxygen is roughly 30 percent lower than at sea level, meaning the body must work harder to extract the same amount of oxygen from each breath. The immediate responses include increased breathing rate, increased heart rate, and reduced exercise tolerance. These are normal adaptive responses, not signs of illness.

Altitude sickness — acute mountain sickness (AMS) in its mild form — is distinct from normal altitude response. Symptoms include headache, nausea, fatigue disproportionate to exertion, poor sleep, and dizziness. AMS is caused primarily by going too high too quickly, not by low fitness or lack of experience. Highly fit athletes with no altitude experience are just as susceptible as sedentary beginners, and sometimes more so because they ascend faster.

More serious forms of altitude illness — high altitude pulmonary edema (HAPE) and high altitude cerebral edema (HACE) — are medical emergencies. HAPE involves fluid accumulation in the lungs; HACE involves brain swelling. Both require immediate descent of at least 300–600 metres and, where available, supplemental oxygen and pharmaceutical intervention. Any symptom of confusion, loss of coordination, or breathlessness at rest in someone at altitude demands immediate action.

## The Rules of Acclimatization

The guiding principle of altitude acclimatization is climb high, sleep low. The body adapts at the cellular level to reduced oxygen over days and weeks, primarily by increasing red blood cell production and modifying mitochondrial function. This adaptation requires time at altitude; it cannot be rushed. The practical rule most altitude guides apply is a maximum gain of 300–600 metres per day in sleeping altitude above 3000 metres, with a rest day every second or third day of ascent.

Spending a night at an intermediate altitude before going higher accelerates adaptation. A trekker heading to a camp at 4500 metres on the flanks of Aconcagua, Kilimanjaro, or any comparable peak benefits significantly from spending one or two nights at 2500–3000 metres before committing to the higher camps. This is not merely comfort management — it is safety management. The majority of serious altitude illness cases involve either ignoring symptoms and continuing to ascend or ascending too rapidly without intermediate nights.

Hydration supports acclimatization. Altitude suppresses thirst sensation while simultaneously increasing fluid loss through increased respiration rate. Drink consistently and deliberately, targeting pale urine as a hydration indicator. Alcohol dehydrates and suppresses breathing during sleep, worsening overnight oxygen desaturation. Caffeine, by contrast, has a mild stimulant effect on breathing and is generally considered neutral to slightly positive at altitude.

## Sleeping at Altitude

Sleep is typically worse at altitude, particularly in the first several nights. The pattern involves intermittent breathing during sleep — periodic apnoea — caused by the feedback loop between blood carbon dioxide levels and breathing stimulus. This periodic breathing causes repeated half-wakings through the night, leaving campers feeling unrested even after eight hours in a sleeping bag.

Sleeping bag temperature ratings need adjustment for altitude camping. Cold radiated from a tent floor on a rocky ridge at 3500 metres is greater than cold at the same temperature at sea level because wind and radiation cooling interact with altitude conditions. A sleeping bag rated to -10 degrees Celsius is appropriate for many high-altitude summer camps where overnight temperatures might only reach -5 degrees, because the margin provides insurance against unexpected drops and the body's reduced capacity to generate heat at altitude.

Tent platforms at altitude are often rock rather than soil. A self-inflating sleeping mat with a high R-value — R4 or above — is essential for insulation from the ground. Inflatable sleeping pads are harder to inflate by mouth at altitude because of reduced air density; a small pump sack saves effort.

## Food and Water at High Altitude

Water boils at lower temperatures with increasing altitude — approximately 95 degrees Celsius at 2500 metres and 87 degrees Celsius at 5000 metres. This means cook times for foods that require boiling increase substantially. Pasta, grains, and legumes take significantly longer to soften at altitude. Freeze-dried meals designed for backcountry use specify preparation with water at a full boil and are generally more forgiving because they require less actual cooking.

Appetite commonly decreases at altitude. This is normal and is partly a symptom of mild AMS, partly a consequence of the body redirecting resources toward cardiovascular adaptation. Eat deliberately and prefer calorie-dense foods that are easy to consume without strong appetite: nuts, seeds, nut butter, chocolate, and soft cheeses.

Water sourcing in high alpine environments is generally excellent. Snowmelt streams and glacial tarns above 3500 metres in remote ranges are often clean, but filtration or chemical treatment remains advisable because grazing animals, wildlife, and other campers can contaminate even high-elevation water sources.

## Gear Considerations for Altitude

Four-season tents are the appropriate shelter above 3000 metres in any conditions that include the possibility of snow, high wind, or freezing temperatures. Three-season tents can work in calm high-altitude summer conditions but are vulnerable to unexpected snowfall and tent-crushing wind loads that high-altitude environments can produce without warning.

Wind is stronger and more persistent at altitude because terrain channelling is more pronounced and the jet stream interacts with high ridges in unpredictable ways. Stake every guy point on your tent and use snow stakes or rock anchors where soil is absent. A tent that collapses in the night at 4000 metres in a whiteout is a serious emergency, not a camping inconvenience.

## Finding High-Altitude Campsites

The world's great mountain ranges offer camping at altitude across a spectrum of accessibility. The Colorado Rockies, the Swiss Alps, the Andes of Peru and Bolivia, the Himalaya of Nepal, the East African peaks of Kilimanjaro and Mount Kenya, and the ranges of New Zealand's South Island all have established routes and camp sites above 3000 metres.
