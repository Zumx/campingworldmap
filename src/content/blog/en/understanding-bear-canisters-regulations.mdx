Bear canisters — rigid, cylindrical food storage containers that black bears and grizzly bears cannot open — are required in a growing number of wilderness areas across North America and are strongly recommended in many more. The regulatory patchwork is genuinely confusing: a canister that is acceptable in one national park may not be approved in an adjacent wilderness area managed by a different agency, and the requirement may apply year-round in one zone but only during specific seasons in another. Understanding the specific rules for your intended route before you set out prevents fines and protects wildlife.

The fundamental reason for food storage requirements is behavioural conditioning: a bear that successfully obtains human food loses its natural wariness of people, associates campsites with food rewards, and eventually must be killed by wildlife managers to protect human safety. The phrase used by rangers is that a fed bear is a dead bear — food storage regulations exist to protect bears as much as hikers.

## Why Regulations Differ Across Areas

Bear food storage regulations are set by the managing agency for each specific piece of land: the National Park Service, the US Forest Service, the Bureau of Land Management, state wildlife agencies, and tribal authorities all have independent regulatory authority. The NPS has the most uniform and stringent standards; the Forest Service tends toward less prescriptive requirements with more reliance on bear hang alternatives; BLM regulations vary significantly by field office.

The level of bear activity, the bear population density, the documented history of food raids, and available enforcement capacity all influence whether a land manager imposes a hard canister requirement or allows alternatives. In areas like Yosemite's Yosemite Valley and the John Muir Trail corridor, decades of bear incidents culminating in vehicle break-ins and tent raids drove the NPS to impose hard canister requirements earlier than most other areas.

## Yosemite National Park

Yosemite was one of the first national parks to mandate hard-sided bear canisters in backcountry areas, and the requirement covers all overnight backcountry camping in the park. Only canisters approved by the Interagency Grizzly Bear Committee (IGBC) or listed on the park's own approved container list are accepted. The most widely carried approved canisters are the BearVault BV500, the Garcia Machine Bear Box, the Frontiersman IGBC-Certified Container, and the Counter Assault Bear Keg. The Wild Ideas Bearikade is expensive but significantly lighter than most alternatives and is popular with ultralight backpackers. Hanging food from trees is explicitly not permitted in Yosemite as an alternative — the park's well-habituated bear population learned to defeat standard bear hangs decades ago.

## Sequoia and Kings Canyon National Parks

Sequoia and Kings Canyon, which adjoin Yosemite to the south and share access via the John Muir Trail, have similar hard canister requirements for backcountry camping. The parks' bear management programs and the Yosemite program share data and coordinate regulations. The John Muir Trail corridor through these three parks — approximately 170 miles from the Yosemite Valley to Mount Whitney — is subject to canister requirements for its entire length. The High Sierra bear population is largely black bears, but the steep terrain and high-use corridors have produced a sophisticated, food-conditioned population that makes food protection critical.

## Olympic National Park

Olympic requires food storage with an approved hard-sided container or an Ursack AllWhite approved bag for backcountry camping in specific high-use zones, particularly the Quinault and Hoh River valleys and the coastal strip. The Ursack is a soft-sided Spectra bag approved in some but not all California and Northwest bear management areas — its approval status should be verified for each specific zone before reliance on it. Olympic's bear population includes black bears across the peninsula and no grizzlies.

## Glacier National Park and Grizzly Country

Glacier National Park in Montana is grizzly bear habitat — the park supports one of the highest grizzly densities in the lower 48 states — and food storage requirements reflect this. The park accepts IGBC-approved canisters and the use of designated food hanging infrastructure at established campsites (metal poles with hangers at the required 4-metre height). All backcountry camping in Glacier requires reservation through the park's permit system, and rangers at trailheads inspect food storage equipment. The Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem, including Yellowstone National Park and the surrounding national forest wilderness areas, has similar requirements due to the grizzly bear population and is subject to the Interagency Grizzly Bear Committee's guidelines.

## Denali National Park

Denali's backcountry is zoned into units, and food storage requirements apply throughout. Canisters must be IGBC-approved. The park's grizzly bears are the primary concern, and the absence of established trails in most of the park — you camp on open tundra where you choose — means bears can encounter camps anywhere. The park loans canisters to permit holders who cannot obtain one before their trip, at no cost, though supply is limited and advance rental is advisable. The cold temperatures in Denali's backcountry also demand that food storage containers handle extended subfreezing conditions without seal failure.

## Ansel Adams Wilderness and John Muir Wilderness, California

The national forest wilderness areas adjacent to the Sierra Nevada national parks have canister requirements that differ from the parks themselves. The John Muir Wilderness, managed by the Inyo and Sierra National Forests, requires hard-sided food containers in specific over-quota zones including the Mount Whitney Zone, the Humphreys Basin, and the Rush Creek drainage. Rangers conduct permit checks at trailheads during peak season. Outside the over-quota zones, proper food storage is required — meaning hanging with a bear-resistant setup is technically permitted but rangers report that successful bear hangs are rare in the Sierra Nevada's frequently used areas.

## Choosing a Canister

The major decision variables in canister selection are capacity, weight, and price. A solo hiker needs roughly one litre of canister space per day for typical freeze-dried and trail food. The BearVault BV500 at 11.5 litres and 907 grams is the most common choice: adequate for 5 to 7 days of food for one person. The Garcia Backpacker's Cache at 10.25 litres and 952 grams is slightly smaller. For ultralight-focused backpackers, the Wild Ideas Bearikade Weekender at 7.2 litres and 680 grams is the lightest widely available IGBC-approved option, though it costs considerably more than the BearVault or Garcia alternatives.

Canisters certified only by the Sierra Interagency Black Bear Group (SIBBG) are not accepted in grizzly-country areas that require IGBC certification — check the specific certification standard required for your area, as the two are distinct.

## Bear Hang Alternatives

Where canisters are not required, a properly executed bear hang remains a viable option. The PCT hang and the modified PCT hang both use a counterbalance method between two trees, suspending food bags at least 4 metres off the ground and 1.5 metres from the nearest trunk. The Ursack Major and AllWhite bags, when tied to a fixed tree, are approved as an alternative in some but not all regulated areas. Consult the specific area permit conditions.
