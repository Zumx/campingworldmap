Provence is one of Europe's great camping regions — a territory where the landscape shifts in rapid succession from the snowy heights of the Mercantour Alps to the salt flats and flamingo lagoons of the Camargue, from the otherworldly ochre cliffs of the Colorado Provençal to the lavender fields of the Valensole plateau and the spectacular limestone gorges of the Verdon. The Mediterranean climate of hot dry summers and mild wet winters creates ideal camping conditions for much of the year, and the regional food culture — rosé wine, olive oil, tapenade, herbes de Provence — makes eating at a campsite kitchen far more pleasurable than in most of Europe.

The region attracts enormous numbers of French and northern European campers in July and August, and the best campsites require booking well in advance for midsummer. The shoulder months — May, June, September, and October — offer warmer conditions than most of Europe while crowds thin considerably. The Verdon Gorge fills with canoeists and climbers from June through August; September brings calm water and autumn light. ## 1. Gorges du Verdon, Alpes-de-Haute-Provence

The Verdon Gorge — often called the "Grand Canyon of Europe" — is a 25-kilometre limestone canyon carved by the Verdon River, with walls rising over 700 metres from the emerald-green river below. Several campgrounds operate on the plateau rim above the gorge (Moustiers-Sainte-Marie, La Palud-sur-Verdon) with views into the canyon, while others sit at the lakeshore of Lac de Sainte-Croix at the canyon's lower end. The white-water rafting and kayaking section through the canyon's deepest narrows is one of France's finest paddling experiences. The Route des Crêtes along the northern rim provides dramatic viewpoints and access to hiking trails on the canyon edge.

## 2. Camargue Natural Regional Park, Bouches-du-Rhône

The Camargue is the vast rhône river delta — an ever-shifting mosaic of salt marsh, lagoon, rice paddy, and sand dune that is one of Europe's most important wetland ecosystems. The white horses of the Camargue, the black bulls, and the vast flamingo flocks — regularly numbering in the tens of thousands at Étang de Vaccarès — are the iconic images of the region. Campsites at Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer and Les Cabanes de Cacharel provide beach and wetland access. Dawn and dusk in the Camargue, when the lagoons turn pink with flamingos and the light goes golden over the marshes, are genuinely extraordinary natural spectacles.

## 3. Luberon Natural Regional Park, Vaucluse

The Luberon massif divides the Durance valley from the Apt basin in a long limestone ridge covered in garrigue scrubland and crowned with medieval hilltop villages. Camping in the Luberon is typically in small, farm-based sites in the valleys below the hill villages — between Bonnieux and Lacoste, around the ochre village of Roussillon, and in the valley of the Calavon below Apt. The Grand Luberon circuit on foot connects the range's highest points. June brings the lavender bloom at the lower elevations, and the plateau roads through fields of purple lavender in full flower are one of Provence's defining visual experiences.

## 4. Colorado Provençal, Rustrel, Vaucluse

Near the village of Rustrel in the Vaucluse, an eroded landscape of ochre cliffs, red earth ravines, and orange sandstone formations — reminiscent superficially of the American Southwest — has been nicknamed the Colorado Provençal. The former ochre mining site (Roussillon village used ochre pigment from these quarries) is now a classified natural site with walking trails through the extraordinary colorscape. Campgrounds in Rustrel and the surrounding villages provide bases for visiting the formations in morning light. The ochre trail is accessible to all fitness levels and creates some of the most photogenic camping territory in Provence outside the Verdon.

## 5. Montagne Sainte-Victoire, Aix-en-Provence, Bouches-du-Rhône

The limestone crest of Sainte-Victoire — the mountain that Cézanne painted over and over from the surrounding farmland — rises abruptly from the plain east of Aix-en-Provence. The summit ridge has a refuge and a series of climbing routes on the south face that are among the finest single-pitch limestone climbing in Provence. Camping at the Bimont reservoir at the mountain's base and at several farm campsites in the Puyloubier area gives access to the mountain trails. The forested north face contains a large population of wild boar, and deer move through the clearings in early morning. The mountain's cultural weight — as the subject of so many Cézanne canvases — adds a dimension to camping here that is difficult to replicate elsewhere.

## 6. Gorges de la Nesque, Monts du Vaucluse

The Nesque gorge cuts through the Vaucluse plateau in a dramatic limestone canyon less visited than the Verdon and accordingly less crowded. The D942 road along the canyon rim passes viewpoints over the gorge depths and small chapels clinging to the cliff faces. Wild camping possibilities on the plateau above the gorge require attention to fire rules — the garrigue landscape burns readily in summer, and open fires are prohibited. Village campsites at Sault — the lavender capital of the Vaucluse, sitting on a plateau above the gorge — provide the most convenient base. Sault's lavender market in August and the Mont Ventoux summit just to the north make the Nesque area a rich camping destination in its own right.

## 7. Mercantour National Park, Alpes-Maritimes

The Mercantour in the far southeast of France, bordering the Italian Maritime Alps, is Provence's only national park — a wilderness of high alpine meadows, granite peaks, and deep river valleys with a remarkable concentration of prehistoric cave art in the Vallée des Merveilles (thousands of Bronze Age rock engravings accessible by guided hike from the Saint-Dalmas-de-Tende area). The park has an extensive network of mountain refuges and designated camping areas in the high alpine zone. Wolves recolonized the Mercantour from Italy in the 1990s and are now established throughout the park — howling heard from a campsite here carries a different weight than in most of Europe.

## 8. Étang de Berre and Martigues Coast, Bouches-du-Rhône

The Étang de Berre is a large lagoon west of Marseille that despite significant industrial development on its southern shores retains extensive flamingo and wading bird habitat on its northern margins. The Martigues coast around the Ferrières peninsula and the Saint-Blaise archaeological site provide camping with access to the lagoon ecosystems. The lesser-known Plaine de Crau east of the lagoon is the only steppic grassland in France and hosts breeding little bustards and pin-tailed sandgrouse — extraordinary birds in a French context. The area rewards campers interested in the natural history of Provence beyond the standard tourist circuit.

## 9. Les Alpilles Natural Regional Park, Bouches-du-Rhône

The Alpilles are a compact range of limestone ridges between the Durance and the Rhône plain — historically important as a source of bauxite and building stone, culturally central to the Provence of Daudet and van Gogh. Les Baux-de-Provence, the most dramatic of the region's medieval perched villages, crowns one of the Alpilles ridges. Campgrounds at Saint-Rémy-de-Provence and Fontvieille provide bases for exploring the range on foot and by bicycle. The olive groves and garrigue of the lower slopes shelter nightingales, hoopoes, and melodious warblers in summer. The walking trails through the high limestone ridge give views south to the Camargue and north to Mont Ventoux.

## 10. Calanques National Park, Marseille, Bouches-du-Rhône

The Calanques are a series of deep, narrow limestone inlets cut into the coastal cliffs between Marseille and Cassis — some of the most spectacular coastal landscape in France and the centerpiece of the only national park in France that overlaps directly with a major city. Camping within the national park itself is restricted, but authorized bivouac sites exist on several headlands for registered climbers and hikers staying overnight in designated zones. Access by sea kayak and an overnight at one of the remote calanque beaches — when the daytime kayakers have gone and the cliffs rise in silence above the deep turquoise water — is one of the finest coastal camping experiences in the Mediterranean.
