Campfire regulations have become one of the more complex variables in backcountry planning over the past two decades. What were once seasonal restrictions limited to the driest summer weeks in fire-prone areas have expanded in scope, duration, and geographic extent as climate change has lengthened fire seasons, increased the frequency of extreme fire weather events, and pushed fire risk into areas and seasons that were historically lower risk. A camper planning a trip to the western United States, southern Australia, or the Mediterranean region without checking current fire restrictions is taking a planning risk that can result in arriving at a destination where campfires are prohibited or even where public land access has been closed due to active fire or post-fire hazard.

## What Different Restriction Levels Mean

The United States Forest Service and most western state agencies use a tiered restriction system, typically designated Stage 1 and Stage 2 (or Order 1 and Order 2). Stage 1 restrictions generally prohibit campfires except in developed campgrounds with metal fire grates, prohibit the use of charcoal, and may restrict the use of wood-burning stoves in the backcountry. Camp stoves using pressurised gas, liquid fuel, or electricity are typically permitted at Stage 1. Stage 2 restrictions are more stringent and usually ban all open flame including gas camp stoves in the backcountry, permitting only stoves with shut-off mechanisms in developed sites.

The BLM, NPS, and individual national forest districts can issue independent restriction orders, meaning adjacent parcels of land managed by different agencies may be under different restriction levels simultaneously. The InciWeb database, maintained by the US Forest Service, provides real-time information on wildfire incidents and associated area closures. The specific agency websites for the national forest or park you are visiting are the authoritative source for current restriction orders.

Australia's fire danger rating system uses a different framework: No Burn Day, Low, Moderate, High, Very High, Severe, Extreme, and Catastrophic. Total Fire Ban declarations by state fire authorities prohibit the lighting of fires in the open, including camping stoves using solid fuel or open flame, on days when fire weather conditions are assessed at critical levels. The declarations are typically announced the evening before the ban day and are statewide in some states and region-specific in others.

## Where to Find Current Information

The most reliable sources for campfire restriction information are, in order of reliability: the specific land management agency's website (national park, national forest, state park), the agency's ranger station phone line for the district you are visiting, and the InciWeb or national fire agency monitoring platforms. Social media reports from recent visitors are useful supplementary information but should not be relied upon as the authoritative source — conditions change and individuals may have observed incorrect information or visited a different portion of a large area.

Several states and federal agencies have consolidated fire restriction information into public-facing websites. For US national forests, the Fire Restrictions website maintained by the Forest Service provides a map interface showing current restriction levels across the national forest system. For California specifically, the CalFire website and the CalFire mobile app maintain current fire restriction information for state responsibility areas and support links to federal agency restriction pages for federal lands within the state.

In the UK, Scotland's fire guidance under the Scottish Outdoor Access Code asks people not to light fires in periods of dry weather and specifically prohibits fires in several high-risk areas during the spring fire season. Natural England provides fire restriction guidance for National Nature Reserves. The European Mediterranean countries — particularly Spain, Italy, Greece, and Portugal — have strict summer fire prohibition periods in designated forest areas, enforced with significant fines and occasionally criminal prosecution for violations that contribute to wildfire spread.

## How to Cook Safely When Fires Are Banned

The absence of campfire permission does not eliminate camp cooking options for prepared campers. The primary tool is a gas canister stove or alcohol stove — both are permitted in most Stage 1 restriction areas and in any location where restrictions are specifically limited to solid-fuel open fires. An integrated canister stove like the Jetboil Flash, used inside a tent vestibule (with the vestibule partially open for ventilation) or behind a windbreak of your pack and sleeping pad, maintains reasonable cooking performance even when a fire would have been your plan A for warmth and cooking.

The practical adaptation for fire-ban camping is a preparedness shift: bring a stove and fuel even if a fire would have been permitted, check restrictions the day before departure (not just when planning the trip months earlier), and carry a small headlamp and extra insulation layers to compensate for the warmth and light that a campfire would otherwise have provided.

Wood-burning stove users face the most significant restriction impact, as their stoves are functionally prohibited during Stage 1 restrictions and beyond. Carrying a backup alcohol or gas stove as a secondary system is advisable for frequent campers in fire-prone regions.

## Elevation and Fire Restriction Exemptions

Some fire restriction orders include elevation-based exemptions: fires may be prohibited below a certain elevation (commonly 8,000 or 9,000 feet in the US Sierra Nevada context) but permitted at higher elevations where the vegetation is sparser and fire risk is assessed as lower. These exemptions are specific to the restriction order text and should not be assumed — the exact wording of the applicable order governs whether an elevation exemption exists.

Similarly, developed campground exemptions in Stage 1 orders typically specify that fires are only permitted in established metal fire grates provided by the campground, not in portable fire rings or directly on the ground. The distinction between an established fire grate and a portable camping fire ring is material under these orders.

## Leave No Trace and Fire Impact

Even when fires are permitted, Leave No Trace principles recommend fire use only in established fire rings in established campgrounds, using dead and down wood no thicker than a wrist, and burning all wood completely to white ash. Pit fires, cairn fires, and fire mounds on backcountry terrain cause soil sterilisation and lasting visual impact in heavily used areas. The LNT recommendation for most backcountry camping is a camp stove for cooking and no fire, accepting the trade-off in warmth and atmosphere for reduced environmental impact.
