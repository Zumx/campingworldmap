Canoe and kayak camping involves a different gear philosophy from backpacking. The water-based transport allows significantly more gear volume — a standard canoe carries 300 to 400 kg including paddlers, versus a backpack's 15 to 25 kg — but introduces the requirement to keep everything dry and to manage portage carry weight on routes with overland sections. The planning process has several steps that differ from trail camping.

## Route planning for water travel

The primary planning resource for canoe routes in North America is the Adventure Cycling Association's canoe route maps and the individual state DNR or provincial parks paddling guides. For the BWCAW specifically, Quetico Provincial Park, and Algonquin, the permit reservation system (recreation.gov for US parks, Ontario Parks online for Canadian) is the first step — entry points fill during June through August for summer dates, and planning should begin in January.

International canoe routes — the Yukon River, the Murray in Australia, the Okavango in Botswana — require expedition-level planning with additional considerations for border crossings, wildlife hazards, and resupply logistics. The general principle is to plan from water level data first: most rivers have minimum navigable water levels documented by local paddling clubs or gauge stations. Check the gauge reading for your intended travel dates against the historical record for the route.

## Dry bag systems

Everything that cannot be wet must be in a sealed dry bag. The standard system uses a combination of sizes: a 20-litre roll-top dry bag for sleeping gear and clothing; a 10-litre bag for electronics and valuables; smaller 5-litre bags for items requiring frequent access. Colour-coding by function (sleeping = blue, kitchen = red, safety = yellow) speeds access significantly when repacking in rain.

The alternative to dry bags is a canoe barrel — a 60 or 30-litre barrel with a gasket-sealed lid, standard in the Boundary Waters and Quetico paddling culture. The barrel is bear-resistant when locked, floats with contents, and provides a stable sitting surface at camp. It is significantly heavier than an equivalent dry bag set but eliminates the individual bag management step.

## Portage gear management

The portage carry — transporting the canoe and all gear overland between water bodies — is where pack weight matters in canoe camping. The standard approach is the double-carry: all gear in the first carry, canoe in the second, with the yoke pad (a foam pad on the centre thwart) taking the hull weight on the shoulders. On routes with multiple portages in a single day, gear compression becomes a priority.

A portage pack — a high-volume soft-sided pack designed to be carried under the canoe during a double carry, or on the back with the canoe inverted overhead — is the functional solution. The Duluth Pack tradition of the BWCAW uses flat canvas packs that can be stacked under the canoe hull. Modern options include the Cooke Custom Sewing portage pack, which is the performance benchmark.

## Meal planning for water travel

Canoe and kayak camping allows heavier and bulkier food than backpacking, which is an advantage for trip duration and morale. Cast iron is viable; fresh produce in the first two days is practical; glass jars for condiments and pickles are normal practice on canoe trips. The standard planning is:

- Days 1 to 2: fresh proteins (fish fillets, eggs, fresh vegetables)
- Days 3 to 5: cooled proteins if an ice chest is carried, otherwise transition to cured meats, hard cheese, and dried goods
- Days 6 onward: fully dry-goods menu

A 12-quart cooler with a 3 to 4 kg block of ice (not cubed ice, which melts faster) maintains below 4 degrees for two to three days in moderate temperatures. Wrap the cooler in a closed-cell foam pad to extend retention.

## Kayak camping differences from canoe

Sea kayak camping uses a different logistics model — the gear is distributed across the bow and stern hatches and the day hatch, with a combined volume of approximately 100 litres for a standard touring double. Dry bags must fit through the hatch openings (typically 35 to 40 cm diameter). The hull volume limits are more constraining than a canoe; the trade-off is open-water capability and speed.

Sea kayak food planning is more similar to backpacking — weight matters less than volume and hatch-fit. The Tupperware-and-roll-top-bag system for sea kayak hatches is the standard: compress camping gear into bags that fit the hatch aperture and can be pushed back into the hull's tapered section.

## Bear safety on water routes

The bear food storage requirements on canoe routes in bear country (BWCAW, Algonquin, Yukon) require hanging food at each camp. The BWCAW hang is done from a tree branch; Algonquin and the Yukon interior typically require a free-hanging hang from the highest available branch. The canoe barrel with a locked lid is accepted as an alternative to hanging in some BWCAW entry areas — check the specific entry point regulations.

## Safety equipment requirements

Canoe and kayak camping safety requirements: a Transport Canada-approved or US Coast Guard-approved PFD per person (it must be worn, not stored); a bailer or bilge pump per vessel; a 15-metre throw bag; a whistle; and navigation lights for dusk or dawn paddling. A dry bag containing a fire-starting kit, a space blanket, and a day's emergency food should be in every paddler's immediate-access location. A float plan lodged with a non-travelling contact is standard practice; a satellite communicator is strongly recommended on remote routes.
