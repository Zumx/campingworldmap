The choice between water purification tablets and physical filtration is one of the most common gear decisions for campers, and it has no single correct answer. Both methods can produce safe drinking water; both have failure modes the other does not; and the optimal choice shifts depending on the type of trip, the source water quality, the ambient temperature, and the trip duration. This comparison addresses the decision directly rather than rehearsing general principles.

## What tablets do that filters do not

The primary advantage of chemical tablets — specifically chlorine dioxide tablets — over physical filtration is their effectiveness against viruses. The hollow-fibre and ceramic filter membranes used in Sawyer, MSR, and Platypus filters physically remove protozoa (Giardia, Cryptosporidium) and bacteria by size exclusion: the membrane pores are smaller than these organisms. Viruses, at 0.02-0.2 microns, pass through standard filter membranes.

In practice, viral contamination of backcountry water sources in developed countries (US, Canada, UK, Australia, New Zealand, Western Europe) is uncommon in remote, low-use wilderness areas. Most viral contamination comes from human waste, and remote backcountry with low use has minimal human fecal input to water sources. However, the risk is not zero, and in areas with heavy use, near campsites and trails, or downstream from agricultural operations, viral contamination is a realistic concern.

For international travel — particularly developing countries, areas with dense agriculture, or anywhere with a significant human population upstream — viral risk is elevated and a filter-only approach is insufficient without added chemical or UV treatment.

## What filters do that tablets do not

Filters provide immediate access to treated water. Chlorine dioxide tablets require 30 minutes of contact time for bacteria and protozoa in clear water at warm temperatures, and up to 4 hours for Cryptosporidium in cold or turbid water. Iodine tablets are faster (10-30 minutes) but less effective against Cryptosporidium and have other limitations.

When water needs are immediate — mid-hike hydration stops, camp setup in fading light, winter camping where standing around waiting is cold — the 30-minute wait for tablets is a practical disadvantage. Filters deliver treated water in the time required to fill a bottle.

Filters also produce better-tasting water. Chlorine dioxide at treatment concentrations is detectable as a slight chemical taste; iodine taste is more marked. Filters produce water that tastes like the source with any turbidity and particulate removed.

## Weight and volume comparison

Chlorine dioxide tablets (Aquatabs, Potable Aqua chlorine dioxide): typically 30 tablets treating 30 litres, weighing 20-30 grams. The entire treatment system weighs under 30 grams and fits in a pocket.

Sawyer Squeeze filter: 85 grams, requiring one or two soft bottle squeezes and the filter unit. SteriPen Adventurer UV: 100 grams including batteries.

For ultralight travel on short trips in low-risk environments, tablets are the lightest viable option. For anything beyond a weekend trip, the cost per litre of tablets adds up — filters are cost-effective over their rated lifespan.

## Cold weather performance

Both tablets and filters degrade in cold weather, but in different ways.

Tablets slow down: the disinfection chemistry is temperature-dependent. At 5°C water temperature, chlorine dioxide requires longer contact time (check the product instructions for specific temperature corrections). Iodine efficacy also decreases markedly below 15°C. In cold-weather camping, tablets are reliable if you extend contact time appropriately.

Hollow-fibre filters physically break: the water in the filter membrane pores freezes and expands, rupturing the fibres. A frozen Sawyer Squeeze no longer filters — the damage is invisible and the filter will appear to function while providing no pathogen removal. This is a genuine safety risk. Hollow-fibre filters must be kept above freezing — stored in a sleeping bag at night, kept in an inside pocket during cold days. Ceramic filters (MSR MiniWorks) tolerate freezing better but still require care.

For winter camping, chemical treatment — specifically chlorine dioxide — is often more reliable than filtration because it has no mechanical failure mode from freezing.

## Turbid water handling

Both methods are degraded by turbid (cloudy) water, but differently.

Physical filters clog faster in turbid water. A Sawyer Squeeze that flows freely in clear mountain water will slow significantly when processing glacial meltwater or cattle-pond water. Pre-filtering through a bandana or coffee filter removes the coarsest particulate and extends filter life significantly.

Chemical treatment is rendered less effective by turbidity: suspended particles physically shield pathogens from the disinfectant. UV treatment is more severely affected — turbid water makes UV pens unreliable. The solution for both is pre-filtration to clear the water before chemical or UV treatment.

## The practical decision matrix

Use tablets as primary treatment when: the trip is 1-3 nights with minimal water needs; weight is the overriding priority; winter conditions make filter freezing a concern; viral risk is elevated (international travel, high-use areas).

Use a filter as primary treatment when: the trip is multi-day with high daily water volumes; taste quality matters; immediate water availability is important; the source water is clear.

Use both in combination when: viral risk is elevated and volume needs are high — filter for bacteria and protozoa, add chemical or UV treatment for viruses.

## Failure planning

Carry tablets as a backup regardless of your primary treatment system. A broken filter, depleted UV pen battery, or contaminated filter membrane are all realistic mid-trip failures. A 30-gram packet of chlorine dioxide tablets weighs nothing against the security it provides as a backup system.
