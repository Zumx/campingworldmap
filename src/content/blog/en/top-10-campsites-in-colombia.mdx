Colombia is one of the most biodiverse countries on Earth, compressing within its borders the ecosystems of two oceans, three Andean mountain ranges, the Amazon basin, and the Caribbean coast. For campers, that translates into an extraordinary range of settings: you can sleep beneath the stars in a high-altitude paramo grassland where strange frailejón plants dot the fog-shrouded slopes, or pitch a tent on a beach on the Caribbean side of the Sierra Nevada, or overnight in the Amazon rainforest south of Leticia. The country's improved security situation over the past decade has opened many of these regions to visitors who previously could not have traveled safely.

Camping infrastructure varies widely — some protected areas have well-run concession operators with equipment and guides, while others require fully self-sufficient expeditions. Understanding which category your destination falls into is essential planning. Browse the [campsite map](/map) to orient yourself before reading the site-by-site highlights below.

## 1. Tayrona National Natural Park, Magdalena

Tayrona sits on the Caribbean coast at the foot of the Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta — the world's highest coastal mountain range — and protects a stretch of coast where the jungle descends directly to coral reef-fringed bays. The park has licensed camping concessions at Cabo San Juan del Guía and Arrecifes, where hammock-under-palapa rentals are available alongside tent platforms. The hike in from Calabazo through dense humid forest takes around three hours. The combination of white sand, turquoise water, and jungle backdrop makes this one of the most scenic campsite locations in South America.

## 2. Lost City (Ciudad Perdida) Trek, Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta

The five-to-six-day trek to the pre-Columbian Tairona city of Ciudad Perdida is one of Colombia's great wilderness experiences. Licensed operators run the route with all camping equipment, meals, and guides provided — independent access is not permitted. Campsites along the route are basic but well-run, positioned in river valleys and on ridges through the Sierra Nevada's tropical dry and humid forest zones. The city itself is reached by ascending some 1,200 stone steps carved by the Tairona people, and camping near the ruins on the final nights is a genuinely extraordinary experience.

## 3. El Cocuy National Park, Boyacá

The Sierra Nevada del Cocuy in northeastern Boyacá is a compact cluster of glaciated peaks rising above 5,000 metres — among the most dramatic high-altitude terrain in the northern Andes. Registered camping within the park requires prior booking and a registered guide from one of the local communities. The classic circuit covers a series of high lakes and glacial cirques below the permanent snowline, passing through paramo dominated by frailejónes, the giant rosette plants endemic to Andean high grasslands. The area is cold and frequently wet, requiring serious cold-weather equipment and acclimatization from lower altitudes first.

## 4. Caño Cristales, Meta

Caño Cristales in the Serranía de la Macarena national park is known as "the river of five colours" — an apt description for a waterway whose riverbed is carpeted in the endemic aquatic plant Macarenia clavigera, which turns vivid red, pink, yellow, and green in the optimal light conditions between July and November. The town of La Macarena is the access point, and tour operators based there run camping packages into the park. Independent access is not permitted. The broader Serranía de la Macarena protects a remarkable convergence of Andean, Orinoco, and Amazon ecosystems in a single relatively compact area.

## 5. Amacayacu National Park, Amazonas

Amacayacu is one of Colombia's most remote national parks, sitting in the Amazon lowlands near the town of Puerto Nariño on the Amazon River. Access is by fast boat from Leticia, itself reached only by air. The park has a small ranger station and established camping area near the river. Wildlife in the surrounding varzea and terra firme forest includes pink and grey river dolphins, giant otters, tapirs, and an extraordinary diversity of birds. Overnight boat trips from camp allow wildlife spotting after dark. The immersion in Amazonian ecology is as complete as it is possible to achieve while staying within Colombia.

## 6. Puracé National Natural Park, Cauca

Puracé is an active stratovolcano in the Colombian Massif, surrounded by a national park protecting paramo, cloud forest, and thermal springs. The summit crater can be reached by a guided day hike from the park entrance. Camping is permitted at the ranger station area, and the surrounding landscape includes the headwaters of four major Colombian rivers — the Magdalena, Cauca, Caquetá, and Patía. Hot springs near Coconuco provide a welcome warm soak after cold nights at altitude. The indigenous Puracé community manages parts of the park in partnership with the national parks authority.

## 7. Tatacoa Desert, Huila

The Tatacoa is not a true desert but a dry tropical scrubland covering around 330 square kilometres in the upper Magdalena River valley. The red and ochre badland formations, jagged canyons, and cactus-dotted terrain feel startlingly otherworldly in a country more associated with jungle. The area has an astronomical observatory and is one of the best stargazing sites in Colombia, with very low light pollution and more than 300 clear nights per year. Several guesthouses and camping areas operate near the village of Villavieja, and the fossil beds of the area — rich in Miocene megafauna remains — are accessible on guided walks.

## 8. Los Flamencos Sanctuary, Guajira

On the arid Caribbean coast of La Guajira department, the Los Flamencos Wildlife Sanctuary protects a series of coastal lagoons, mangroves, and barrier beaches that host large populations of flamingos — particularly American flamingos — alongside diverse shorebird and wading bird communities. The Wayuu indigenous people are the dominant cultural presence across La Guajira, and community-based tourism operators offer camping with Wayuu families in traditional encampments. The combination of an extreme desert coast, flamingo lagoons, and indigenous culture is unlike anything else in Colombia.

## 9. Farallones de Cali National Park, Valle del Cauca

The Farallones national park protects the western Andes above Cali, one of the most biodiverse cloud forest systems in the world. The park is home to spectacled bears, Andean condors, and hundreds of bird species. Camping within the park requires permits and is managed by the national parks authority. The rugged terrain and frequent cloud cover make navigation challenging, but guided camping expeditions with local naturalist guides offer genuine wildlife encounters and the possibility of condor sightings over the Dagua valley. The proximity to Cali — one of Colombia's largest cities — makes this an accessible escape for serious naturalists.

## 10. Serranía de Chiribiquete National Park, Caquetá and Guainía

Chiribiquete is Colombia's largest national park and a UNESCO World Heritage Site protecting an extraordinary complex of ancient tepui (table-top mountain) formations rising from the Amazon rainforest. The park contains tens of thousands of pre-Columbian rock paintings — the largest such collection in the Americas — alongside pristine lowland and highland Amazonian ecosystems. Access is tightly restricted to protect both the paintings and the uncontacted indigenous groups believed to inhabit the interior. Permitted visits are expedition-style, operated by a handful of licensed tour companies, and involve multi-day river and foot travel. This is one of the most remote and significant natural areas in the entire Amazon basin.
