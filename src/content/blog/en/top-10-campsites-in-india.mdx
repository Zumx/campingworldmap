India's camping landscape is as varied as the subcontinent itself — the Himalayan valleys of Himachal Pradesh and Uttarakhand provide high-altitude alpine camping; the Western Ghats in Karnataka and Kerala hold forested wildlife camps; the Thar Desert in Rajasthan offers dune camping that is a distinct experience; and the northeastern states conceal some of the country's least-visited trekking terrain. Indian camping ranges from government forest rest houses and basic dhabas to well-organised trekking camps operated by established operators, but independent backcountry camping requires navigation of the forest department permit system and an understanding of tiger reserve access restrictions.

## 1. Galibore Fishing Camp, Cauvery Wildlife Sanctuary, Karnataka

On the banks of the Cauvery river at the edge of the Cauvery Wildlife Sanctuary, Galibore is one of the most acclaimed eco-camps in southern India. The site is operated by the Karnataka Forest Department in partnership with Jungle Lodges & Resorts and is accessible only by coracle or jeep. The Cauvery here supports mahseer — a large sporting fish — and is home to smooth-coated otter and mugger crocodile. The riverside camp setting, with the jungle canopy directly overhead, is distinctive.

## 2. Bison Camp, Kabini, Karnataka

Near the Nagarhole National Park boundary in the Kabini backwaters area, this forest camp is positioned for wildlife observation on the Kabini reservoir and the Nagarhole floodplains. The dry season (March through May) concentrates elephants, gaur, leopards, and tigers at the water's edge, producing some of the most accessible wildlife photography in India. Camp conditions are basic; organised operator camps provide more complete facilities.

## 3. Naina Peak Camp, Nainital, Uttarakhand

Above the town of Nainital in the Kumaon Himalayan foothills, the camping areas on Naina Peak give access to the Kumaon oak and rhododendron forests and views across the lower Himalayan ridges toward the high peaks. The Nainital lake town provides logistics support; the camps on the forested ridges above are quieter and cooler than the town itself.

## 4. Bandaje Campsite, Western Ghats, Karnataka

Deep in the Western Ghats near Subramanya, the Bandaje waterfall area has two established camping areas in the Shola forest zone. The approach trek passes through cardamom and coffee plantations before entering the Ghats proper. Lion-tailed macaques, Malabar giant squirrels, and the distinctive Malabar whistling thrush are regular sightings. The monsoon (June through September) makes this area inaccessible; the dry season camp is typically November through May.

## 5. Tada Camp, Andhra Pradesh

In the Eastern Ghats near the Tada waterfalls and the forest reserve north of Chennai, this camping area is one of the most accessible wild camping destinations for visitors based in Chennai. The area's combination of granite boulders, semi-deciduous forest, and the Tada river pools makes it a popular weekend destination. Leopards are resident in the forest reserve; wildlife observation is best at dawn.

## 6. Sapney Organic Farm, Bir, Himachal Pradesh

At Bir — India's premier paragliding destination at 1,400 metres altitude in the Kangra valley of Himachal Pradesh — Sapney Organic Farm provides camping in a working apple and apricot orchard. The landing field for paragliders is adjacent. The Tibetan refugee settlement at Chokling Monastery and the monastery gompa are walkable from the farm. The surrounding Dhauladhar range rises steeply from the valley floor.

## 7. Aina Camp, Spiti Valley, Himachal Pradesh

In the high-altitude cold desert of the Spiti Valley at approximately 3,800 metres, Aina is representative of the camping available in one of India's most remote inhabited valleys. Spiti is a Tibetan Buddhist cultural zone in the rain shadow of the Himalaya — dry, high, and treeless. The Key Monastery, Dhankar, and Pin Valley are the primary cultural and trekking destinations from this region.

## 8. Vuhangama Holiday Retreat, Pahalgam, Kashmir

In the Pahalgam valley of Kashmir — a mountain resort valley at 2,130 metres altitude — this camping area provides access to the Lidder river valley and the higher meadows (margs) above. Pahalgam is the staging point for the Amarnath Yatra pilgrimage trek; the valley's security situation has been variable and current advisories should be checked before planning. In stable periods, the Himalayan meadow camping here is outstanding.

## 9. Camping near Valley of Flowers, Uttarakhand

The Valley of Flowers National Park in Garhwal Uttarakhand — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — is accessible from Ghangaria village, which itself requires a 13 km trek from Govindghat. The valley is in bloom from late July through September. Camping is not permitted inside the park itself; the camping areas at Ghangaria are the base for day access. The adjacent Hemkund Sahib Sikh pilgrimage site adds cultural context.

## 10. Nature Park Camping, Western Ghats

Various designated camping areas within the Kerala Forest Department's eco-tourism network in Wayanad and Idukki districts. The Western Ghats south of Karnataka into Kerala hold one of the highest concentrations of endemic species in Asia — the Nilgiri biosphere reserve's mixed-species forests. Asian elephant herds move through these forests seasonally; camping requires coordination with the forest department and a guide.

## Permits and seasonal logistics

Most camping in India's protected areas requires a forest department permit and an approved guide. The permit system varies by state. High-altitude Himalayan camping is best from June through October north of the main Himalayan chain; November through May south of it. Tiger reserve camping must be organised through approved operators; independent camping inside core zones is prohibited.
