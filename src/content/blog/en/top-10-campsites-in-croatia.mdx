Croatia has one of the highest proportions of camping tourists relative to total tourist arrivals in Europe. The combination of the Adriatic coast — clear water, limestone karst, pine-backed beaches, and 1,200 islands — with well-maintained facilities and a long season (May through October) has made Croatia one of Europe's primary camping destinations. The Dalmatian coast concentrates the most popular sites; Istria in the north holds large, professionally run sites with complete facilities; and the inland national parks offer nature-oriented camping. July and August are extremely busy; June and September are significantly better for both weather and crowd levels.

## 1. Camping Soline, Krk Island, Kvarner

On the sheltered bay of Soline on Krk island — the first island reachable by bridge from the mainland — this site is set in a Mediterranean maquis landscape with direct sea access. Krk's combination of accessibility and good Adriatic swimming water makes it the busiest island camping destination in Croatia. Soline has electric pitches, sanitary facilities, and a beach bar. The island is walkable across its width in one day.

## 2. Camping Stella Maris, Umag, Istria

Near the town of Umag on the northern Istrian Adriatic coast, Stella Maris is a large, professionally run site — typical of the Istrian camping model — with multiple pools, electric pitches throughout, a restaurant, sports facilities, and direct sea access. Istria's camping sites are among the most facilities-complete in Croatia. The truffle-producing interior of Istria is accessible as a day trip from coastal sites.

## 3. Camp Kovačine, Cres Island, Kvarner

On Cres — a long, narrow island with a dramatically different character from the busier Dalmatian islands — Camp Kovačine is positioned on the bay opposite Cres town. Cres is known for the griffon vulture colony in its northern section and for the freshwater Vrana Lake, unique among Adriatic islands. The campsite has sea access, electric pitches, and a calmer atmosphere than comparable Dalmatian sites.

## 4. Camping Korana, Plitvice Lakes, Lika

Adjacent to the entrance of Plitvice Lakes National Park — a UNESCO World Heritage site of cascading lakes and travertine waterfalls — Camping Korana provides the park's primary camping option. The park itself is a day-trip destination for most visitors; camping here allows early access before tour groups arrive and late afternoon light. The campsite has full facilities and motorhome service points.

## 5. Camping Šimuni, Pag Island

On Pag island — famous for its stark, limestone-white landscape, Pag cheese, and the summer party scene at Novalja — Šimuni provides a quieter alternative to the northern end of the island. The campsite is in a sheltered bay on the island's western coast with electric pitches and sea access. Pag's landscape is unlike anywhere else in Croatia: a treeless white karst plateau with blue sea on both sides.

## 6. Autocamp Trstenica, Orebić, Pelješac Peninsula

On the Pelješac Peninsula across from Korčula island, Trstenica has a long beach and excellent views to the Korčula channel. The Pelješac Peninsula is the heart of Croatian wine production — Dingač and Postup red wines are produced from posip and plavac mali grapes on the peninsula's steep south-facing slopes. Day trips to Korčula town by ferry are straightforward.

## 7. Camping Stobreč, Split

Within the greater Split metropolitan area — Croatia's second city and gateway to the Dalmatian islands — Stobreč camping provides direct sea access and good facilities within 6 km of Diocletian's Palace. The site is busy and urban in character but logistically useful for visitors using Split as a ferry hub for island camping.

## 8. Camp Solaris, Šibenik

Near Šibenik in northern Dalmatia, Solaris is a large resort-style campsite with complete facilities — pools, restaurants, a beach, and access to the nearby Krka National Park waterfalls. The Krka waterfalls — comparable to Plitvice but with swimming permitted at Skradinski Buk — are the primary day-trip attraction from this location.

## 9. Camping Adriatic, Nin, Zadar County

Near the ancient walled town of Nin in the Zadar hinterland, this site has access to the therapeutic mud flats of the Nin Lagoon — used medicinally since Roman times — and the shallow lagoon beach that makes Nin a popular family destination. The nearby Zadar old town and its Sea Organ are a half-day excursion.

## 10. Camp Vira, Hvar Island

On the island of Hvar — Croatia's sunniest island, averaging 2,724 hours of sunshine annually — Camp Vira is in a sheltered bay on the island's north coast, away from the nightlife of Hvar town. The lavender fields and dry-stone terrace vineyards of the island interior are walkable from this location. Ferry access to Split from Stari Grad, 12 km east.

## Planning notes

Book July and August sites by April at the latest — this is non-negotiable for the Dalmatian and Istrian coast. The ACSI CampingCard provides significant discounts in May, June, and September at many Croatian sites. Wild camping outside designated areas is technically prohibited, though enforcement varies by region.
