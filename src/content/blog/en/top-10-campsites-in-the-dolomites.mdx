The Dolomites are a mountain range unlike any other in Europe. Their rock — dolomite, a calcium-magnesium carbonate — takes on colours at dusk that have no equivalent elsewhere: pale amber shading to deep red, then mauve, then grey, a phenomenon the Ladin-speaking inhabitants call enrosadira. The UNESCO World Heritage listing of 2009 recognised nine separate Dolomite systems encompassing the highest concentrations of vertical rock faces in the Alps. Camping here puts you directly beneath those faces, in valleys where the Alta Via long-distance routes descend from the high passes, and along river systems cold enough in June to numb your feet in seconds. The camping season runs broadly from late May through September, with July and August requiring advance booking at the most popular sites.

## 1. Camping Sass Dlacia, Alta Badia, South Tyrol

Alta Badia is one of the most celebrated skiing and hiking valleys in the Dolomites, surrounded by the Sella Group, Sassongher, and the Fanes-Sennes plateau. Camping Sass Dlacia is a well-equipped site in the valley above Corvara, with modern facilities and direct access to the Dolomites' signed hiking network. The village of Corvara has excellent restaurants serving both Tyrolean and Ladin specialties, and the cable cars from nearby Colfosco and San Cassiano connect to the high plateau trails. The Alta Via 1, one of the classic Dolomite traverse routes, passes through the area. The campsite is small enough to retain a personal atmosphere while being well organised.

## 2. Camping Catinaccio, Vigo di Fassa, Trentino

The Val di Fassa runs east to west through the heart of the Dolomites, with the Catinaccio (Rosengarten) massif dominating its southern skyline. Camping Catinaccio at Vigo di Fassa is positioned in the valley with views toward the towers of the Catinaccio group, which the German-speaking world knows as Rosengarten — the rose garden — a name that refers to their sunset colouration. Vigo has a cable car ascending to the Ciampedie plateau, from which the Rifugio Vajolet and the Torri del Vajolet peaks are accessible in a half day. The campsite has good facilities and operates from late May. The Fassa valley road connects south to Trento in under two hours.

## 3. Camping Miravalle, Moena, Trentino

Moena, the gateway village of the Fassa valley, calls itself the fairy of the Dolomites, a designation that refers to its forested setting in a broader valley section before the Dolomites' main towers close in. Camping Miravalle sits on the valley floor with mountain views and access to the Alpe Lusia ski and hiking area above. Moena's own old town has an unusually active local culture compared to the more tourism-dominated villages higher in the valley. The Passo San Pellegrino to the south is a classic cycling climb featured in the Giro d'Italia itineraries. The campsite is particularly popular with cyclists using it as a base for multi-day pass collections.

## 4. Camping Olympia, Cortina d'Ampezzo, Veneto

Cortina d'Ampezzo is the Dolomites' most glamorous town, host of the 1956 Winter Olympics and the 2026 Winter Games, and surrounded by some of the most impressive rock architecture in the range — the Tofane, the Cristallo, the Cinque Torri. Camping Olympia lies in the Boite valley below the town, offering an affordable base in one of Italy's more expensive resort destinations. The Cinque Torri area above Cortina, accessible by chairlift, preserves original First World War trenches and artillery positions carved into the rock at high altitude. The Tre Cime di Lavaredo, among the most iconic formations in the Alps, are 25 kilometres northeast via the Misurina lake road.

## 5. Camping Palafavera, Val Zoldana, Veneto

The Val Zoldana is less visited than the major Dolomite valleys, offering a quieter character and lower prices without significant sacrifice of scenery. Camping Palafavera is positioned at the head of the valley beneath the Civetta Group, whose northwest face — the Wall of the Walls — is considered one of the great Alpine north faces and was first climbed in 1925. The Rifugio Coldai above the camp is reachable in about two hours and overlooks the Lago Coldai, a high alpine lake with Civetta reflected in its surface on calm mornings. The camp is modest and family-run, serving as a basecamp for multi-day hiking circuits of the Civetta massif.

## 6. Camping Sosi, Falcade, Veneto

Falcade is a village in the Val Biois that sits at the foot of the Passo Falzarego road and the Marmolada approach. The Marmolada at 3,343 metres is the highest peak in the Dolomites and the only one with a permanent glacier, now much reduced from its early 20th-century extent. Camping Sosi provides a lower-cost base for the Marmolada cable car ascent and the Gran Vernel circuit, one of the longer day hikes in the eastern Dolomites. The valley is a popular cycling route connecting to the Passo Fedaia and Passo Pordoi to the north. The campsite has basic but clean facilities and extensive mountain views from the tent pitches.

## 7. Camping Seiser Alm, Castelrotto, South Tyrol

The Seiser Alm (Alpe di Siusi in Italian) is the largest high-altitude alpine meadow in Europe, a plateau above the Isarco valley carpeted in wildflowers through June and July. Camping Seiser Alm near Castelrotto (Kastelruth) at the plateau's edge is the preferred basecamp for exploring this extraordinary landscape on foot or mountain bike. The Sciliar (Schlern) massif rises above the plateau as a pair of dramatic dolomite towers visible from much of South Tyrol. The Castelrotto village itself is one of the most traditional Tyrolean villages in the province, with a particularly large onion-domed church bell tower that rings on a famously complex schedule. The plateau is accessible by cable car from Ortisei and Siusi.

## 8. Camping Al Plan, San Vigilio di Marebbe, South Tyrol

San Vigilio di Marebbe (St. Vigil in Enneberg) is the main village of the Enneberg valley, which gives access to the Fanes-Sennes-Braies Nature Park, one of the wildest sections of the Dolomites. The Ladin culture, an ancient Romance language group predating both Italian and German settlement of the Alps, is most intact in this valley and in the adjacent Alta Badia. Camping Al Plan is a straightforward, family-friendly site used primarily as a basecamp for the Fanes park trails. The three-day Fanes hiking circuit, accessing the Lavarella and Conturines peaks and the high Fanes plateau, begins from trails near the campground. The Pragser Wildsee (Lago di Braies), a turquoise lake at the park's northern edge, is 20 kilometres east.

## 9. Camping Vidor, Fiera di Primiero, Trentino

Fiera di Primiero is a market town in the Primiero valley, historically important as a silver mining centre and now the service hub for the Pale di San Martino group, the largest and most massive of the Dolomite systems. Camping Vidor is a sizeable, well-equipped site on the Cismon River with a swimming pool. The Passo Cereda to the south opens views toward the Pale's southern escarpment, and the San Martino di Castrozza resort above the valley has cable car access to the Altopiano delle Pale, a vast high plateau of limestone pavement and snow patches that persists through summer. The park's Via Ferrata della Bali is a popular aided climbing route accessible from the plateau.

## 10. Camping Toblacher See, Dobbiaco, South Tyrol

Dobbiaco (Toblach) lies in the Pusteria valley at the junction of three major Dolomite valleys, making it one of the better-positioned bases in the region for exploring multiple massifs in a single stay. Camping Toblacher See sits beside a lake formed in a glacially widened valley section, with reed beds and a resident population of waterfowl unusual for a Dolomite campsite. The Three Peaks (Tre Cime di Lavaredo) are 20 kilometres south via the Misurina road. The composer Gustav Mahler spent his final productive summer at Dobbiaco in 1909 and 1910, writing the Ninth Symphony in a small hut that is preserved nearby. The campsite is popular with cyclists using the long-distance Dolomiti route.
