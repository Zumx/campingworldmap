Lightning is the camping hazard that most reliably generates fear disproportionate to the actual probability of being struck, yet it is also one that most campers manage poorly when conditions actually develop. The fear is understandable: lightning is dramatic, unpredictable in fine detail, and capable of killing instantly. The poor management is also understandable: accurate information about lightning risk and appropriate response is spread unevenly, and many common assumptions — that a tent is protection, that rubber-soled shoes insulate, that crouching under a tall tree provides shelter — are wrong. This guide covers what the evidence actually shows.

## Understanding Lightning Risk

Lightning strikes in the United States kill an average of 20 to 30 people annually, with several hundred non-fatal strikes per year. The geography of lightning is uneven: Florida and the central mountain states of Colorado, Wyoming, and New Mexico have the highest strike densities. In Colorado, afternoon thunderstorms develop almost daily in summer over high terrain as convective heating builds to produce towering cumulonimbus clouds. Many of the lightning fatalities in that state involve hikers and campers caught above treeline on exposed terrain in the afternoon.

The pattern is consistent: morning weather is often clear in mountain terrain, storms build through midday and peak in the early to mid afternoon. Experienced mountain campers in storm-prone regions plan high-elevation activities for the morning and descend or seek lower shelter before noon. This single behavioral change — timing adjustment — reduces lightning exposure more effectively than any response action once a storm is upon you.

## Monitoring Conditions

Weather forecasts for backcountry areas are available from the National Weather Service in the US (weather.gov), the Mountain Weather Information Service in the UK, and equivalent national services in other countries. Check forecasts the morning of any high-exposure day and know the forecast pattern for your region in the season you are camping.

Watch the sky actively during any day in potentially convective conditions. Cumulus cloud development — flat-bottomed, vertically building clouds that begin white and puffy — indicates convective instability. When these begin growing rapidly and developing a cauliflower-textured top, storm development is active. If the top begins to spread into an anvil shape (the characteristic shape of a cumulonimbus cloud), the storm is mature and producing lightning.

The 30-30 rule is a simple field standard: if the time between seeing lightning and hearing thunder is 30 seconds or less (approximately 10 kilometres), seek shelter. Wait 30 minutes after the last lightning strike before leaving shelter. This is conservative by design and errs on the side of caution.

## Position in an Established Campground

A hard-sided vehicle — a car, truck, or RV — provides excellent protection from lightning during a storm. The metal body acts as a Faraday cage, conducting lightning around the occupants and into the ground through the tires. This is not because rubber tires provide insulation — a vehicle's protection is entirely structural, not electrical insulation. Do not touch metal components inside the vehicle during a strike. Turn off the engine and avoid using electronics connected to the vehicle's electrical system.

Substantial buildings at established campgrounds — ranger stations, restroom buildings with concrete construction — provide good protection. Stay away from windows and avoid plumbing and electrical fixtures. Open picnic shelters with metal roofs provide no meaningful protection and should be avoided.

A tent provides essentially no lightning protection. The poles may slightly increase the local strike probability relative to the surrounding open ground, though the risk is small. If a storm develops while you are in a tent in an exposed campsite, your best option is typically to remain in the tent unless you have a safer location accessible without crossing open high ground.

## Position in Backcountry and Open Terrain

Lightning follows the path of least electrical resistance. Tall isolated objects — a single tree in an open field, a person standing on a high summit, a metal tent pole — create this path. The safe principle is to minimize your profile relative to the surrounding terrain.

Avoid all of the following during an active lightning storm: open summits, exposed ridgelines, tall isolated trees, cave mouths and overhangs (which can transmit ground current), open water, and open flat terrain where you are the tallest object.

Seek: dense low forest where no tree is much taller than the others (this disperses strike probability widely and provides some ground cover for shelter from rain), low terrain in a valley or depression below surrounding ridgelines, and depressions in terrain that do not fill with water.

If caught in the open with no meaningful shelter available, use the lightning crouch: feet together, crouching low, minimizing contact with the ground, not lying flat. The crouch reduces your height and minimizes the ground area conducting electricity through your body if a strike occurs nearby. Do not crouch under an isolated tree. Spread group members apart by at least 15 metres so that a single strike does not incapacitate the entire group.

## What Does Not Work

Several common beliefs about lightning protection are false and worth directly correcting. Rubber-soled shoes do not protect from lightning — the voltage in a lightning strike is so high that the insulation value of rubber shoe soles is entirely negligible. Lightning rods on tents are not a standard or recommended product; a tent is not a structure worth protecting with a lightning rod. A tent's metal poles do not meaningfully attract lightning relative to the surrounding terrain under most conditions.

The idea that lying flat on the ground is safer than crouching is also false. Ground current — electricity spreading outward through soil from the strike point — can pass through a prone body more effectively than a crouching one with minimal ground contact.

## Recovery from a Lightning Strike Victim

A person struck by lightning does not carry residual charge and is safe to touch. Provide CPR if the person is not breathing. Cardiac arrest is the most common cause of death from lightning strikes, and prompt CPR significantly improves outcomes. Treat for shock and evacuate as quickly as possible.

Know the emergency contact protocol for your specific camping area before you need it.
