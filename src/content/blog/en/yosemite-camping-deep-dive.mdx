Yosemite National Park receives approximately 4 million visitors per year. The majority arrive in the Yosemite Valley — a 12-km granite canyon that contains El Capitan, Half Dome, Yosemite Falls, and the Merced River — and the competition for campsite reservations in the valley is among the most intense in the US national park system. Understanding the reservation architecture, the high-country alternatives, and the backcountry permit systems turns Yosemite from an apparently inaccessible destination into a manageable one.

## The valley campgrounds

Three main campgrounds operate in Yosemite Valley: Upper Pines (238 sites), North Pines (81 sites), and Lower Pines (60 sites). A fourth, Camp 4, has a separate permit system and is the historic home of Yosemite Valley climbing culture.

**Upper Pines** is the largest valley campground and the most frequently available — its size means cancellations appear more frequently. Sites are among the pines at the valley's east end, within walking distance of the Curry Village services and the shuttle bus network. Electric hookups are available at some sites; flush toilets and potable water throughout.

**North Pines** is located between the Merced River and the valley wall, with some sites directly on the river. The views from the eastern sites toward Half Dome are among the most scenic of any frontcountry campground in the US. 81 sites means high demand relative to Upper Pines.

**Lower Pines** is the smallest and most centrally located of the valley campgrounds, adjacent to the mirror image reflection pool and the main valley bicycle path. Sites here are flat and well-shaded; the proximity to Curry Village is convenient.

**Camp 4** is the walk-in only campground associated with the climbing community since the 1950s. The Bear Board, the Yosemite Decimal System of rock difficulty ratings, and the culture of dirtbag climbing that produced many of America's greatest alpinists developed here. A separate reservation system at recreation.gov applies; six people per site; maximum two-night stay. Camp 4 is now a National Historic Landmark.

## The reservation system mechanics

Valley campground reservations at recreation.gov open on a rolling 5-month window, with new dates becoming available at 7 AM Pacific time on the 15th of each month, 5 months in advance. Example: April 15 dates become available on November 15. The system processes approximately 2,000 reservations in the first seconds of each opening window. Automated tools and preparation are required for competitive dates.

The cancellation strategy: cancellations appear at irregular times throughout the reservation period. Checking recreation.gov twice daily, building a monitoring alert, or using a third-party notification service produces access to cancelled sites across the season. Weekend and holiday sites in July and August are the hardest; weekday and shoulder-season sites are significantly more available.

## Wawona and Bridalveil Creek

**Wawona Campground** (93 sites) in the park's southern section, 45 km from the valley, provides the nearest alternative to valley camping. Wawona's setting — a Victorian-era hotel and golf course in a meadow surrounded by ponderosa pines — is quite different from the valley. The Mariposa Grove of giant sequoias (including the 3,000-year-old Grizzly Giant) is 8 km south. Wawona is significantly less crowded than the valley campgrounds.

**Bridalveil Creek** (110 sites) is on Glacier Point Road at 2,200 metres elevation — the road that gives access to Glacier Point, with the valley views from directly above. Sites are in open pine forest; the road typically opens in June. The Four-Mile Trail descent to the valley from Glacier Point makes Bridalveil a useful high-country base with valley access.

## Tuolumne Meadows and the high country

At 2,620 metres elevation on the Tioga Road, **Tuolumne Meadows Campground** (304 sites, reservation required) is the base for the Yosemite high country — the Cathedral Lakes, the Grand Canyon of the Tuolumne, and the approach trails to Half Dome from the east. The high-country season is late June through late September; the first snow can close Tioga Road in October. Summer temperatures at Tuolumne are 8-10°C cooler than the valley.

White Wolf Campground (74 sites) and Yosemite Creek Campground (75 sites, walk-in) are the two additional Tioga Road campgrounds. Yosemite Creek is first-come-first-served and one of the few remaining first-come options in the park.

## Half Dome permits

The Half Dome day-hike permit system controls access to the upper cables route — the only non-technical route to the summit. Permits are required for the cable section from late May through mid-October.

Lottery system: 225 permits per day are distributed through a pre-season lottery (approximately mid-March for the following season) and 50 per day are released 2 days before the hike date as a daily lottery. Entry fee for each lottery attempt is $10. Winners are notified by email; the permit allows four people per permit.

The hike itself is 25-26 km round trip with 1,500 metres of elevation gain from the Happy Isles trailhead. The cable section is a steel cable ladder on a near-vertical granite surface. It is strenuous, requires good fitness, and is dangerous in wet conditions — the park closes the cables section when rain or lightning is forecast.

## Backcountry wilderness permits

The Yosemite wilderness permit system allows backpacking into the park's 95% backcountry. 60% of permits are reservable at recreation.gov (opens March, for following season); 40% are available as day-of walk-up permits at trailhead-specific ranger stations.

The main backpacking circuits include the John Muir Trail through the park (9-day traverse from the valley to Tuolumne to the Ansel Adams Wilderness), the Cloud's Rest circuit (a less-visited but arguably superior summit to Half Dome), and the Hetch Hetchy reservoir circuit in the park's less-visited northwestern section.

Bear canisters are required throughout the Yosemite wilderness — a legally mandated, not merely recommended, requirement. The Garcia PCT canister and BearVault BV500 are the two most common; the rental canisters available at trailhead stores are an alternative for first-time visitors.
