Cast iron cookware is the traditional medium for campfire cooking because it handles direct flame, coal heat, and camp stove use interchangeably, distributes heat evenly once fully heated, and improves with use rather than degrading. The practical limitations — significant weight and the requirement for correct maintenance — are manageable at car camping and group camping scales where pack weight is not the primary constraint.

## The case for cast iron at camp

A well-seasoned cast iron skillet or Dutch oven produces cooking results that aluminium or stainless camp cookware cannot match: genuine searing heat for protein; even heat distribution for bread and cornbread baking; the ability to go directly from campfire coals to the table as a serving vessel. The weight — a 10-inch Lodge skillet weighs approximately 2.3 kg, a 6-quart Dutch oven approximately 5.2 kg — is why cast iron is impractical for backpacking but entirely appropriate for car camping or base camp use.

## Seasoning your cast iron before the trip

Seasoning is a layer of polymerised oil baked onto the iron surface that provides non-stick properties and prevents rust. New cast iron from Lodge comes pre-seasoned from the factory. To build or maintain seasoning: wash the piece with hot water (no soap on new seasoning), dry completely over low heat, apply a thin coat of flaxseed oil or Crisco (not olive oil — too low a smoke point), wipe off all excess so only a molecular film remains, then bake upside-down in an oven at 230 degrees C for one hour. Repeat twice before a camping trip for best results.

## Fire management for cast iron

The fundamental error with campfire cast iron cooking is using open flame rather than coals. Open flame is uncontrolled and creates hot spots; coals provide steady, controllable radiant heat. To generate usable coals: start a fire with hardwood (not softwood, which burns too fast) one hour before cooking. Push the burning wood to one side; rake the coals into the cooking area. The coals are ready when they glow orange with a light grey ash forming on the surface — this is the equivalent of a medium-high gas burner.

## Dutch oven temperatures with briquettes

Camp cooking with charcoal briquettes (rather than wood coals) provides predictable temperature control. The standard rule: for a 10-inch Dutch oven, 10 briquettes on the bottom and 20 on the lid produces approximately 175 degrees C (350 F) — the standard roasting temperature. For higher heat, add 2 briquettes to both top and bottom for every 25 degrees of additional temperature required. The lid charcoals provide top heat for baking; the bottom charcoals provide bottom heat for cooking. This system works consistently with standard Weber-size briquettes.

## The camp bread recipe

A standard camp Dutch oven bread: 450g bread flour, 7g instant yeast, 8g salt, 310ml warm water, 15ml olive oil. Mix and knead until smooth (10 minutes by hand). Allow to rise in the Dutch oven lined with parchment paper for 45 minutes. Bake with 8 briquettes below and 16 above for 35 to 40 minutes until the internal temperature reaches 93 degrees C. The parchment liner means the Dutch oven needs only a wipe-out after use. This works equally well on a camp stove using a heat diffuser plate under the Dutch oven.

## Cleaning at camp without facilities

The Leave No Trace protocol for cast iron cleaning: scrape food residue out with a spatula or metal scraper while the skillet is still warm. Add a small amount of water and bring to a simmer to loosen any stuck material. Pour out and wipe dry. Do not use soap on a seasoned surface — it strips the seasoning layer. For stubborn residue, a handful of coarse salt used as an abrasive scrub followed by rinsing is effective. After cleaning at camp, dry the piece fully over the fire, apply a thin oil wipe, and store. Rust appears immediately on an unprotected wet cast iron surface.

## The best cast iron vessels for camping

The 10-inch skillet is the most versatile single piece — it handles breakfast eggs, searing meat, making pan sauces, and even shallow baking. The 12-inch is better for groups of four or more. The 4-quart Dutch oven is the minimal size for camp bread and bean dishes for two people; the 6-quart handles groups of four. The cast iron griddle (flat, ribbed, or reversible) is the best single-item for pancake and toast use at a car camping setup with a two-burner camp stove.

## Weight reduction for semi-remote camps

If weight is a concern but you still want cast iron performance, the enamelled cast iron Lodge pieces are slightly lighter than bare cast iron and require no seasoning maintenance. Carbon steel — used in woks and Paella pans — is the closest performance equivalent to cast iron at roughly 40 percent of the weight. A 1.8 kg carbon steel skillet handles all the same tasks as a 2.3 kg cast iron equivalent; it requires the same seasoning attention and is more vulnerable to warping over extreme direct heat.

## Camp cooking dishes suited to cast iron

Beyond bread, the cast iron Dutch oven handles the full range of slow-cooked camp dishes: venison or chicken stew (two to three hours over low coals), rice dishes and paella (30 to 40 minutes with lid), bean dishes (four to six hours with periodic coal replenishment), and campfire cobbler (45 minutes from scratch). The cumulative advantage of cast iron over a multi-day base camp is the building flavour of the seasoning itself — a skillet used daily for three camp days develops a surface coating that improves every subsequent cook.
