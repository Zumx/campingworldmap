Tasmania is the wild heart of Australia — an island state approximately the size of Ireland where nearly half the land area is protected in national parks and reserves, and roughly 20 percent is listed as World Heritage wilderness. The landscapes are old and extraordinary: Gondwanan forests of Huon pine (individuals over 10,000 years old), alpine environments preserved in relict form since the last ice age, and a geological complexity that ranges from dolerite sea cliffs to quartzite mountain ranges to ancient glacial lake systems. Camping in Tasmania is camping in deep time — the ecosystems predate human arrival on the island by tens of millions of years.

The Tasmanian wilderness demands respect. The southwest wilderness area is one of the most remote and weather-affected environments in the Southern Hemisphere — rapidly changing conditions, significant rainfall even in summer, and cold temperatures year-round. The Overland Track is Australia's most famous long-distance walk and requires advance booking, proper equipment, and genuine preparation. More accessible camping at Freycinet and the Bay of Fires rewards less experienced campers with extraordinary scenery at lower risk. ## 1. Overland Track, Cradle Mountain-Lake St Clair National Park

The Overland Track is Australia's premier multi-day walking experience — a 65-kilometre route from Cradle Mountain in the north to Lake St Clair (Australia's deepest lake) in the south, typically covered in five to seven days. The track passes through subalpine moorland, ancient myrtle beech forest, and the dramatic alpine terrain around Barn Bluff and Mount Ossa — Tasmania's highest point at 1,617 metres. Advance booking is required for the summit season (October through May), and only a fixed number of walkers may start each day. The huts along the route provide emergency shelter, with camping on established tent platforms adjacent to each hut.

## 2. Freycinet National Park, East Coast

Freycinet Peninsula on Tasmania's east coast protects the pink granite mountains of the Hazards, the brilliant white sand of Wineglass Bay (consistently listed among the world's finest beaches), and a series of smaller bays and coves along the peninsula's outer edge. The Freycinet Camp at the peninsula's end is accessible by a 9-kilometre walk from the car park and offers designated sites in a coastal wilderness with extraordinary pink granite and turquoise water scenery. The Freycinet Peninsula Circuit is a 30-kilometre multi-day walk connecting all the major features with overnight camps at Bryans Beach and Cooks Beach.

## 3. Southwest National Park, Tasmania

The Tasmanian Southwest is one of the world's last great temperate wilderness areas — largely inaccessible by road, maintained at a pristine level by the absence of tracks across most of its territory, and home to endemic species found nowhere else on Earth including the rare orange-bellied parrot. The South Coast Track from Cockle Creek to Melaleuca requires extensive river crossings, helicopter resupply, and significant navigation skills — it is one of Australia's most demanding multi-day walks. Port Davey at the southwest coast and Melaleuca in the interior have basic facilities accessible by light aircraft. This is genuine expedition camping for properly equipped and experienced wilderness campers.

## 4. Bay of Fires, Northeast Tasmania

The Bay of Fires on Tasmania's northeast coast is named for the orange lichen-covered granite boulders that line the shore — a visual signature as distinctive as any coastline in Australia. The Tasman Coastal Trail connecting Eddystone Point south to the Bay of Fires Lodge is a guided luxury walk, but the national park campgrounds at Swimcart Beach and The Gardens at the northern end of the reserve provide access to the same landscape for self-sufficient campers at no tour cost. The water at Bay of Fires is cold but brilliantly clear, and the combination of orange rocks, white sand, and blue ocean in morning light is exceptional.

## 5. Walls of Jerusalem National Park, Central Highlands

The Walls of Jerusalem is a World Heritage wilderness area in the central Tasmanian highlands, accessible by a steep 90-minute approach walk from the road end at Lake Rowallan. Inside the park, a series of natural amphitheatres — the Damascus Gate, Temple Bar, the Pool of Bethesda — are formed by dolerite cliffs and ancient glacial terrain. Wild camping throughout the park is permitted, with no designated sites or facilities. The alpine environment with its stands of pencil pine — one of the world's most ancient conifers — and pandani (the world's tallest heath plant) is otherworldly. The park is snow-covered for much of winter and requires crampons for some approaches.

## 6. Maria Island National Park, Southeast Tasmania

Maria Island is a car-free national park reached by ferry from Triabunna — a wildlife sanctuary where wombats, Cape Barren geese, and introduced Tasmanian devils roam freely and have become remarkably approachable. The island has been largely rewilded, and the devils — transferred from the mainland population to establish an insurance colony — are regularly seen foraging near the campsite at Darlington. The former penal colony buildings at Darlington are among the most atmospheric convict-era structures in Tasmania, complementing the wildlife camping experience with colonial history of unusual intensity.

## 7. Mount Field National Park, Southern Tasmania

Mount Field is Tasmania's oldest national park, established in 1916, and encompasses both the famous Russell Falls in its low-altitude temperate rainforest zone and the high alpine environment of the Lake Dobson Road plateau. The family-oriented camping at Lake Dobson is surrounded by snow gum woodland at 1,000 metres, with ski facilities used in winter months. The alpine plateau above, accessible by walking track, holds endemic alpine flora and endangered swift parrot habitat. The combination of accessible waterfalls, ski country, and genuine alpine camping within 80 kilometres of Hobart makes Mount Field one of Tasmania's most visited parks.

## 8. Cape Pillar and Tasman National Park, Tasman Peninsula

The Tasman Peninsula's Tasman Coastal Trail — a three-to-four-day walk connecting Fortescue Bay, Cape Hauy, and Cape Pillar — passes some of the most dramatic sea cliff scenery in the Southern Hemisphere. The dolerite sea cliffs at Cape Pillar drop 300 metres directly to the ocean, and the Tasman Arch and Devil's Kitchen geological formations are accessible on day walks. Camping at Fortescue Bay is possible at the national park campsite at the beginning of the trail. The bay is one of the finest kayaking launches in Tasmania, with cave and cliff coast accessible by sea kayak on calm days.

## 9. Arthur-Pieman Conservation Area, Northwest Tasmania

The Arthur-Pieman Conservation Area on Tasmania's remote northwest coast protects an extraordinary wild coastline of white sand beaches, ancient tea-tree forest, and wind-sculpted coastal heathland stretching from the Arthur River to Sandy Cape. The area is accessible only by 4x4 on beach tracks subject to tidal conditions, and camping on the deserted beaches is entirely self-sufficient. The Arthur River estuary — "the edge of the world" according to local designation — is one of the most remote and pristine river-coastal environments accessible by vehicle in Australia. The silence here is profound — the nearest town is many kilometres away and the prevailing westerly brings unfiltered Southern Ocean air directly from Antarctica.

## 10. Hartz Mountains National Park, Southern Tasmania

The Hartz Mountains above the Huon Valley protect a compact but spectacular World Heritage alpine plateau accessible from Hobart via the d'Entrecasteaux Channel road — one of the shortest approaches to genuine alpine terrain in Tasmania. The summit plateau has glacial lakes, endemic alpine plants, and views south toward the Southwest wilderness. Camping is permitted at Lake Osborne at the plateau edge, reached by a 90-minute walk from the carpark. Snow can fall at any time of year on the Hartz Peaks, and the weather is classically Tasmanian — changeable and sometimes dramatic. The combination of accessibility and genuine wilderness character makes Hartz an excellent first taste of Tasmanian alpine camping.
