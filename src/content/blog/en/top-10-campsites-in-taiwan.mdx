Taiwan is a remarkable camping destination that remains relatively unknown to international campers. The island is dominated by the Central Mountain Range — a spine of peaks running the length of the island with 286 summits exceeding 3,000 metres, a density of high terrain that exceeds even Switzerland. The highest, Yushan (Jade Mountain), rises to 3,952 metres and is the highest peak in Northeast Asia outside of the Tibetan Plateau. Camping culture is deeply embedded in Taiwanese outdoor recreation, with a well-developed permit and mountain hut system in the national parks and extensive campground infrastructure at lower elevations.

Taiwan's location in the Pacific typhoon belt means that weather awareness is essential for camping planning. Typhoon season runs from June through October, and high-mountain trails can be closed by typhoon damage or be genuinely dangerous during severe weather events. The optimal camping seasons are October through December and March through June — cooler, drier conditions that make high-altitude camping manageable and the lower elevations pleasantly warm. The diverse endemic wildlife, dramatic mountain terrain, and the island's extraordinary indigenous Austronesian cultures make every camping trip multidimensional. ## 1. Yushan National Park, Central Taiwan

Yushan National Park protects the highest terrain in Taiwan — a wilderness of alpine meadow, conifer forest, and rocky ridgelines centered on the Yushan massif. The main Yushan peak ascent requires an advance permit (allocated by lottery for the prime season) with overnight stay at the Paiyun Lodge at 3,402 metres. The lodge has both dormitory accommodation and a small camping area. The summit is reached by trail from the lodge in a pre-dawn push for sunrise, with views on clear days extending across the Central Range and in exceptional conditions to both coasts of the island. The park's secondary peaks and the Nanzishi River valley offer less congested but equally beautiful trekking and camping.

## 2. Taroko National Park, Hualien County

Taroko is Taiwan's most dramatic gorge landscape — the Liwu River has carved through 4,000-metre marble mountains in a series of narrows where the sheer white-and-grey marble walls rise hundreds of metres above the rushing water. The park campgrounds at Tianxiang and the Lushui area provide camping in the gorge environment. The Dali-Tali Hiking Trail and the Zhuilu Old Trail on the cliff face give access to the gorge walls. The upper sections of Taroko above the gorge — the Hehuanshan (Mount Hehuan) area at 3,275 metres — are a different environment of open alpine meadow popular for high-altitude camping and star gazing, with one of Taiwan's highest roads cresting the divide.

## 3. Hehuanshan, Nantou County

The Hehuanshan area where Provincial Highway 14A crosses the Central Mountain Range at 3,275 metres is Taiwan's most accessible high-altitude alpine environment — reachable by car from Puli or Hualien without technical hiking. The surrounding peaks (Hehuanshan proper at 3,422 m and the adjacent summits) are covered in Yushan cane grassland above the treeline, with spectacular views across the Central Range. Primitive camping areas around the visitor centre and along the summit trail are available. The area is a premier stargazing location — the Astrophysics Observatory of the National Central University at Lulin, just below the ridge, is testament to the quality of the dark sky here.

## 4. Shei-Pa National Park, Hsinchu and Miaoli Counties

Shei-Pa National Park protects the northern section of Taiwan's high mountain zone, centered on the twin peaks of Dabajianshan (3,492 m) and Xueshan (Snow Mountain, 3,886 m) — Taiwan's second highest peak. The Xueshan trail from the Wuling Recreation Area passes through ancient stands of Taiwan hemlock and red pine before emerging onto the high-altitude alpine zone. The Black Forest and 369 Mountain Hut at 3,100 metres provide the mandatory overnight stay before the summit push. The trail corridor has one of Taiwan's best concentrations of Taiwan macaques, Formosan black bears (occasionally), and Mikado pheasants — Taiwan's national bird.

## 5. Alishan National Scenic Area, Chiayi County

The Alishan mountain area in Chiayi County is famous for its sunrise sea-of-cloud views, Japanese-era mountain railways, and the groves of ancient red cypress trees that survived the colonial logging era. Several campgrounds in the Alishan Forest Recreation Area allow camping beneath the cypress and Japanese cedar canopy. The Alishan Forest Railway — a narrow-gauge mountain railway built by the Japanese colonial government — still operates on sections of its original route, providing a unique way to arrive at elevation for camping. The Duei-Gao-Yue (Jade Mountain and Alishan Sunrise) phenomenon, where twin mountain silhouettes rise from the morning cloud, is visible from the camping areas on clear autumn mornings.

## 6. Wuling Farm, Taichung City

Wuling Farm, the starting point for the Xueshan trail in Shei-Pa National Park, sits at 1,740 metres in a river valley planted with cold-climate fruit orchards and flower gardens. The campground adjacent to the farm facilities provides a cool mountain camping base surrounded by cherry trees that bloom spectacularly in late February — one of Taiwan's most sought-after spring camping weekends. The valley streams hold Formosan landlocked salmon — one of Taiwan's most endangered endemic species — and fish conservation programs allow observation of the salmon in protected stream segments. Deer and Formosan serow (mountain goat) inhabit the forest above the farm.

## 7. Kenting National Park, Pingtung County

Kenting on Taiwan's southern tip is the only national park with a true tropical character — coral reef, monsoon forest, and savannah grassland meeting the warm Bashi Channel. The park has several campgrounds with beach access, and the surrounding water is one of Taiwan's most accessible and rewarding snorkeling and diving environments. Whale sharks are occasionally encountered in the channel. The Eluanbi lighthouse at the island's southern tip, the Chuanfan Rock coral formations, and the uplifted coral fossil terraces of the cape provide geological context for the camping environment. The campgrounds on the east coast of the cape face the Pacific and catch consistent trade winds.

## 8. Neidong National Forest Recreation Area, New Taipei City

Neidong Forest Recreation Area in the mountains behind the town of Wulai, a short drive from Taipei, protects a gorge of impressive waterfalls — the Neidong Waterfall drops 80 metres — surrounded by giant Chamaecyparis formosensis (Taiwan red cypress) trees. The campground within the recreation area provides a forest camping experience within day-trip distance of Taipei, making it an excellent introduction to Taiwan mountain camping for visitors based in the capital. The indigenous Atayal community at Wulai maintains traditional weaving and hot spring culture in the valley below, adding cultural dimension to the natural setting.

## 9. Siougulan Campground, Hualien County

The Siougulan (Xiuguluan) River in Hualien County is one of Taiwan's most popular white-water rafting rivers — a 24-kilometre run through the eastern coastal range with Class II-III rapids and dramatic canyon scenery. The camping area at the river terminus at Rareseed Beach combines river camping with the Pacific coast, and the combination of the river canyon, the rift valley views to the west, and the Pacific Ocean at camp creates a geographic drama unique in Taiwan. Camping followed by the river run — or vice versa — gives a full-day outdoor experience combining water sport and camp leisure in a single itinerary.

## 10. Guanwu Forest Recreation Area, Miaoli County

Guanwu in the northern Central Range is one of Taiwan's finest birding and forest camping destinations, protecting old-growth Chamaecyparis and Tsuga forest at 2,000 metres with a well-established campground and trail network. The area is the primary location for Taiwan's endemic Mikado pheasant — a magnificent bird with vivid blue and chestnut plumage — and reliable sighting locations on the forest trails are known to regular visitors. The forest at Guanwu is among the most pristine remaining in Taiwan's northern mountains, with enormous ancient cypresses surviving the colonial logging era. The campground's quiet forest setting and the dawn chorus of Taiwan endemics make early mornings here exceptional.
