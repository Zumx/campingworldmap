Utah contains five national parks within a relatively compact area of the Colorado Plateau — Zion, Bryce Canyon, Capitol Reef, Canyonlands, and Arches — together with vast tracts of Bureau of Land Management desert where dispersed camping is free and unrestricted. The Colorado Plateau is an elevated block of layered sedimentary rock that the Colorado River and its tributaries have been systematically eroding for millions of years, creating a landscape of mesas, canyons, arches, hoodoos, and slot canyons in shades of red, orange, cream, and purple. Summer in the canyon country is extreme — temperatures routinely exceed 40 degrees Celsius in July and August at canyon-floor elevations, and afternoon thunderstorm activity can trigger flash floods in slot canyons without warning. Spring and autumn are the preferred camping seasons.

## 1. Watchman Campground, Zion National Park

Zion is the most visited national park in Utah and one of the busiest in the United States. The Watchman campground at the park's south entrance in Springdale is the only campground in the canyon floor accessible to RVs with electrical hookups, which makes it perpetually in demand. Reservations open months in advance and fill immediately for spring and autumn peak season dates. The campground sits beneath the Watchman formation — a broad sandstone plateau named for its sentinel-like profile above the Virgin River. The park shuttle system, mandatory for private vehicles from March through November, stops at the campground entrance. The Riverside Walk and the Narrows wading trail are accessible by shuttle from the campground in under 20 minutes.

## 2. Devils Garden Campground, Arches National Park

Arches National Park contains the highest density of natural sandstone arches in the world — over 2,000 documented features within its boundaries, including Delicate Arch, which appears on Utah license plates. Devils Garden campground is the only developed campground in the park, near the end of the main park road, 18 miles from the entrance. The Devils Garden trail from the campground accesses Landscape Arch, at 88 metres the longest natural arch in the United States. The campground requires reservations during the March through October busy season — the reservation window opens a full six months in advance and fills quickly. Night skies in Arches are exceptionally dark, and the park holds an International Dark Sky Park designation.

## 3. Bryce Canyon Campground, Bryce Canyon National Park

Bryce Canyon is technically not a canyon but an eroded amphitheatre — a series of bowl-shaped natural theatres carved from the Paunsaugunt Plateau's edge. The hoodoos, the slender spires of eroded limestone in colours ranging from cream to deep orange, are most dramatically illuminated at sunrise and sunset. Bryce Canyon campground sits on the plateau rim at 2,400 metres, making it one of the higher-elevation campgrounds in Utah and genuinely cool even in summer. The Rim Trail, a paved path along the plateau edge, passes directly through the campground. The overnight temperature in June can drop below freezing at this elevation. Bryce is consistently rated among the best dark-sky sites in North America.

## 4. Fruita Campground, Capitol Reef National Park

Capitol Reef is the least-visited of Utah's five national parks and arguably the most underrated. The park is centred on the Waterpocket Fold, a 160-kilometre long wrinkle in the Earth's crust where rock layers buckled upward in a dramatic monocline visible from the air as a long, tilted ridge. Fruita campground sits in a historic pioneer fruit orchard established by settlers in the 1880s and maintained by the National Park Service. Visitors can pick fruit from the trees in season — cherries in June, peaches in August, apples and pears in September — paying a nominal fee per pound. The orchard also attracts over 230 bird species. The campground is small and popular; summer reservations essential.

## 5. Squaw Flat Campground, Canyonlands National Park, Needles District

Canyonlands is divided into four non-connected districts by the confluence of the Colorado and Green Rivers. The Needles district in the southeast is the most accessible to foot travellers, named for the red-and-white banded sandstone spires that characterise its geology. Squaw Flat campground is positioned within the Needles maze of canyons, with trails radiating to the Chesler Park meadow, the Joint Trail (a narrow slot canyon passable only sideways in places), and the remote Confluence overlook above the meeting of the two rivers. The campground has no showers, water is limited, and temperatures are extreme in summer — but the landscape justification is significant.

## 6. Island in the Sky Campground, Canyonlands National Park

The Island in the Sky mesa in Canyonlands' northern district is a flat-topped peninsula ringed by 300-metre vertical sandstone walls, elevated above two canyon floors created by the Colorado and Green Rivers. The campground on the mesa top is small and basic, with the trade-off being unrestricted access to some of the most panoramic overlooks in the Southwest: the Grand View Point looks south to the Needles district across a depth of canyon terrain. The 100-mile White Rim Trail below the mesa is a famous mountain bike and 4x4 route that takes three to four days to complete. Advance permit required for overnight trips on the White Rim.

## 7. Sand Hollow State Park Campground, Hurricane, Utah

Sand Hollow is a reservoir in an extraordinary landscape of red Navajo sandstone slickrock immediately adjacent to the campground, 30 minutes north of Zion National Park. The campground has full hookup sites and tent-only primitive areas. The reservoir provides swimming and motorised water recreation in a desert setting; the slickrock around the water's edge is accessible for free-form scrambling. Hurricane is the closest town to the campground with full grocery access, and the drive to Zion's south entrance takes under 30 minutes. The Sand Mountain area west of the reservoir is a large OHV recreation area. The campground serves as an alternative base for Zion visitors when the park's own sites are full.

## 8. Goblin Valley State Park Campground, Utah

Goblin Valley is one of Utah's more unusual state parks, protecting a basin filled with thousands of eroded sandstone hoodoos in rounded, mushroom-shaped forms — the goblins of the name — clustered so densely that walking among them feels genuinely surreal. The campsite has good facilities for a state park and a reservation system that is less competitive than the national park sites. The park is 50 kilometres from Capitol Reef and serves as a convenient camping stop on routes between the national parks. The Gilson Buttes overlook trail and the cave at Goblin Valley's far end are the primary hiking objectives. Little traffic passes through the area at night, making star-gazing excellent.

## 9. Dead Horse Point State Park Campground, Moab, Utah

Dead Horse Point is a mesa above the Colorado River gorge with a 270-degree view across the canyon country that many photographers consider the finest accessible overlook in Utah — the river curves through a gooseneck directly below the point 600 metres below. The state park campsite is small, requires reservations in advance, and has a strict cap on tent sites to prevent overuse of the mesa edge. The location 30 kilometres from Moab means access to the mountain biking infrastructure, gear shops, and restaurants of Utah's outdoor recreation capital. Canyonlands Island in the Sky is 10 kilometres south of the park entrance.

## 10. Coral Pink Sand Dunes State Park Campground, Kanab, Utah

The Coral Pink Sand Dunes are a field of pink sand — its colour derived from iron-oxidised Navajo sandstone — maintained by wind funnelling through the gap between the Moquith Mountains, continuously replenishing the dune field as fast as wind erosion moves it. The campsite in the pines at the dune edge has reliable facilities and is not heavily booked outside summer holidays, making it one of the more accessible options in Utah's south. Kanab, 20 kilometres east, is the gateway to both Zion and Bryce Canyon and is home to several outfitters serving the wave permit lottery applicants for the Wave formation in Coyote Buttes North, one of the most spectacular and access-restricted sandstone landscapes in the United States.
