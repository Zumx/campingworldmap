Colorado is arguably the premier camping state in the contiguous United States. With more than 300 sunny days per year, 58 mountains exceeding 14,000 feet elevation, two national parks, and millions of acres of national forest and BLM land open to dispersed camping, the state offers an almost bewildering array of outdoor options. Tent campers, backpackers, RV travelers, and overland enthusiasts all find territory suited to their style, often within a short drive of each other on the same mountain road.

The main challenge in Colorado is competition for permits and sites. The state's camping resources are in high demand from June through August, and many popular sites require reservation months in advance or lottery-style permit applications. Planning ahead — and considering shoulder seasons in May, September, and October when crowds thin and conditions can still be excellent — is the single most effective strategy for a quality Colorado camping experience. ## 1. Maroon Bells, White River National Forest

The twin peaks of Maroon Bell and North Maroon Peak, reflected in Maroon Lake at the end of the Maroon Creek valley west of Aspen, are the most photographed mountain scene in Colorado and among the most recognizable in North America. Silver Bar, Silver Bell, and Silver Queen campgrounds sit in the valley below the bells, and the surrounding Maroon Bells-Snowmass Wilderness offers extensive backpacking. The road into the valley is closed to private vehicles during peak season — shuttle buses from Aspen run frequently. Sites book up months in advance for summer weekends.

## 2. Rocky Mountain National Park, Grand Lake Side

Rocky Mountain National Park's western side, accessible from the town of Grand Lake, sees far fewer visitors than the popular eastern Estes Park entrance. The Timber Creek Campground is the park's only drive-in campsite on the west side and serves as a base for hiking into the remote backcountry of the Never Summer Mountains. Backcountry sites in the Grand Lake district are lightly used relative to the east side wilderness. The west side trails offer exceptional wildlife viewing — moose are regularly seen in the willow wetlands around Colorado River headwaters — and the aspen groves turn spectacular gold in late September.

## 3. Black Canyon of the Gunnison National Park, South Rim

The South Rim Campground at Black Canyon of the Gunnison sits at the lip of one of the most dramatic and vertiginous canyons in the American West. The canyon walls descend nearly 2,700 feet to the Gunnison River below, with some sections so narrow that sunlight reaches the bottom for only minutes per day. The campground has both reservable and first-come sites, and the rim walks at dawn and dusk are extraordinary — the light changes constantly in a canyon of this depth. The North Rim campground is more primitive and accessible via a rougher road, offering even more solitude.

## 4. Great Sand Dunes National Park, Alamosa County

The tallest sand dunes in North America rise abruptly from the floor of the San Luis Valley against the backdrop of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains — a genuinely surreal landscape unlike anything else in the Rockies. Pinon Flats Campground at the park boundary offers sites with views of the dune field. Backcountry tent camping is permitted anywhere in the dune field itself with a free permit, creating the possibility of sleeping beneath stars among the dunes — one of the most unusual camping experiences available in any US national park. The nearby Medano Creek flows seasonally across the dune base, creating a shallow wading creek in spring.

## 5. Weminuche Wilderness, San Juan National Forest

The Weminuche is Colorado's largest wilderness area, covering more than half a million acres of the San Juan Mountains in the state's southwest corner. The area is accessible via the Durango and Silverton Narrow Gauge Railroad, which drops hikers at backcountry trailheads — a uniquely scenic way to reach a wilderness. Popular entry points include Silverton, Purgatory, and the Pine River trailhead. The wilderness contains hundreds of miles of trail, dozens of high alpine lakes, and the famous Chicago Basin cluster of 14,000-foot peaks. Camping anywhere away from trails and water is permitted under Leave No Trace wilderness regulations.

## 6. Indian Peaks Wilderness, Roosevelt National Forest

The Indian Peaks Wilderness immediately south of Rocky Mountain National Park offers comparable alpine scenery with a permit system that — though competitive — provides more reliable access than RMNP backcountry. The wilderness is threaded with well-marked trails connecting high cirque lakes: Brainard Lake, Long Lake, and the series of lakes below the Continental Divide at the Pawnee Pass area. Permits are required for overnight stays from mid-June through mid-September and are issued through a reservation system. The eastern approach from the Brainard Lake Recreation Area is the most accessible, sitting at 10,500 feet with trails gaining elevation into the alpine zone quickly.

## 7. Eleven Mile State Park, Park County

Eleven Mile Reservoir in South Park — the high, broad intermountain basin surrounded by mountains — sits at 8,600 feet and offers one of Colorado's most productive fishing and camping combinations. The state park has more than 350 campsites ranging from full hookup to primitive tent areas, and the reservoir holds trophy rainbow and brown trout, carp, pike, and kokanee salmon. Coyotes, mule deer, and pronghorn are regular visitors to the campground margins. The view of the Tarryall and Puma Hills across the reservoir, with the Pikes Peak massif rising to the southeast, makes this an underappreciated scenic camping destination.

## 8. Rifle Falls State Park, Garfield County

Rifle Falls protects a triple-cascade waterfall on East Rifle Creek — an unusual geological feature where travertine deposits have built up a broad tufa mound over which the water falls through limestone cave openings and moss-covered ledges. The campground is small, shaded by cottonwood and box elder, and sits close enough to the falls to hear them from your tent. The surrounding canyon holds significant cliff swallow colonies. The park is just north of the town of Rifle on State Highway 325, making it convenient for travelers moving between the Western Slope and the Front Range on Interstate 70.

## 9. Mueller State Park, Teller County

Mueller State Park in the Pikes Peak region protects one of the most accessible pieces of old-growth ponderosa pine and mixed conifer forest on the Front Range. The campground's 132 sites are distributed through the forest with good spacing between sites, and the park has over 90 miles of trail for hiking, mountain biking, and horseback riding. The park provides excellent wildlife viewing opportunities — black bears, mule deer, wild turkeys, and occasional mountain lions inhabit the terrain. Pikes Peak is visible from several high points within the park. The elevation of approximately 9,500 feet keeps temperatures moderate even during midsummer.

## 10. Dolores River Canyon, BLM Land, Montezuma County

The lower Dolores River canyon below McPhee Reservoir carves through red sandstone and enters a remote desert canyon system unlike the alpine terrain most visitors associate with Colorado. BLM-administered land along the river allows dispersed camping throughout the canyon, with river access points via dirt roads off State Highway 141. The canyon is one of Colorado's finest whitewater stretches in high water years; in lower water it becomes an excellent paddling and camping destination accessible to intermediate canoeists and kayakers. Petroglyphs and archaeological sites associated with ancestral Puebloan cultures dot the canyon walls, adding historical depth to the landscape.
