The Pacific Northwest encompasses some of the most varied camping terrain in North America. Within a few hours' drive in Washington and Oregon, you move between temperate rainforest receiving over four metres of annual rainfall, high volcanic peaks capped by glaciers visible on clear days from Portland and Seattle, the arid high desert east of the Cascade crest, and a coastline of sea stacks, tide pools, and fog-laden headlands. The camping infrastructure is correspondingly diverse: from fully serviced state park sites with firewood bundles sold by the cord, to national forest dispersed camping where you choose your own spot in designated free-camping zones, to backcountry sites requiring a Northwest Forest Pass and a permit booked months in advance. This is a region where knowing the reservation system matters as much as knowing where you want to go.

## 1. Kalaloch Campground, Olympic National Park, Washington

Kalaloch sits on a bluff above the Pacific Ocean on Olympic National Park's coastal strip, a 60-mile section of wild coast separated from the main mountainous park. The campground is large and open, with some sites directly above the beach — among the most sought-after car camping positions on the Washington coast. The beach below is wide, sandy, and backed by driftwood piles large enough to shelter behind. Kalaloch Lodge operates adjacent to the campground. Ruby Beach, two miles north, is famous for sea stacks and tidepooling. Reservations are essential from June through September and competition for peak-season sites is significant.

## 2. Sol Duc Hot Springs Resort Campground, Olympic National Park, Washington

The Hoh and Sol Duc valleys on the western side of Olympic National Park contain the finest temperate rainforest in North America: Sitka spruce and western red cedar draped in club moss and sword fern, with rainfall measured in feet rather than inches. The Sol Duc campground sits in old-growth forest near the Sol Duc Hot Springs resort, where natural hot spring pools (non-sulfurous and genuinely pleasant) are accessible to campground guests for a fee. The Sol Duc Falls trail is one of the most beautiful short hikes in the Pacific Northwest — a two-mile round trip through cathedral forest to a multi-channel waterfall dropping through a narrow basalt canyon.

## 3. Hoh Rain Forest Campground, Olympic National Park, Washington

The Hoh River valley receives up to 4.5 metres of annual precipitation and is the most visited of Olympic's rainforest sections. The campground at the Hoh Rain Forest visitor centre sits beneath old-growth Sitka spruce, some of the largest trees in the park. The Hall of Mosses trail — a short loop through particularly dramatic moss-draped maples — begins at the visitor centre. The Hoh River Trail continues 17 miles to a backcountry campsite at Glacier Meadows at the foot of Mount Olympus. The ambient noise in the Hoh valley at night — rain on the canopy, the river, wind through the moss — is unlike any other camping soundscape in the lower 48 states.

## 4. Campsite at Rialto Beach, Olympic National Park, Washington

Rialto Beach is the northern anchor of Olympic's coastal section near La Push, the small Quileute Nation community on the Washington coast. A walk-in campsite on the beach is accessible about three miles north of the parking area along a coastal route that involves crossing Hole-in-the-Wall arch at low tide — a natural sea arch wide enough to walk through. The beach is rocky, driftwood-strewn, and sea-stacked, and camping on it in fog feels genuinely remote. The Quileute people have occupied this coast for thousands of years and the La Push area has a small marina and a visitor facility operated by the tribe.

## 5. Timberline Lodge Campground, Mount Hood, Oregon

Mount Hood is Oregon's highest peak at 3,429 metres and the dominant landmark of the northern Willamette Valley. Timberline Lodge, a 1937 Works Progress Administration structure on the mountain's south shoulder, is one of the finest examples of American Craftsman architecture applied to a public building and is listed on the National Register of Historic Places. The campground below the lodge accesses the Timberline Trail, a 40-mile circuit of the entire mountain that crosses multiple glacial moraines and river crossings that can be dangerous in early summer snowmelt. The Palmer Glacier snowfield above the lodge supports year-round skiing and is used by the US Olympic ski team for summer training.

## 6. Crater Lake National Park Campgrounds, Oregon

Crater Lake occupies the caldera of Mount Mazama, a volcano that collapsed in a catastrophic eruption approximately 7,700 years ago. The resulting lake, fed entirely by precipitation and snowmelt with no surface inlet or outlet, is the deepest lake in the United States at 594 metres and among the bluest in the world — its exceptional clarity produces the intense cobalt colour that makes photographs appear digitally enhanced. Mazama Village campground is the primary facility, open from late June when the park road clears snow. The Rim Drive around the caldera is one of the great scenic drives in American national parks, with viewpoints above the lake at intervals throughout its 33-mile length.

## 7. Cape Lookout State Park Campground, Oregon

Oregon's coast is publicly owned from the high tide line inland — a policy established in 1967 that makes it uniquely accessible compared to most American coastlines. Cape Lookout State Park occupies a forested headland south of Tillamook, with a campground in the forest and beach camping on the sand spit south of the cape. The Cape Lookout trail from the campground traverses the headland through Sitka spruce forest to a point above the open ocean where gray whales are commonly spotted during their spring and fall migrations. The Tillamook Creamery nearby is a regional landmark known for its cheese and ice cream production, open to visitors year-round.

## 8. Leavenworth-area Campgrounds, Wenatchee National Forest, Washington

Leavenworth is a Bavarian-themed town in the Cascades foothills east of Stevens Pass that, despite its unlikely appearance, is surrounded by genuine wilderness: the Enchantments alpine zone, Icicle Creek canyon with its dozens of climbing crags, and the Stuart Range. National Forest campgrounds along Icicle Road south of town are the base for the Enchantments permit zone, where a lottery system allocates overnight backcountry permits for one of the most spectacular alpine landscapes in Washington — a chain of lakes set among granite peaks and home to a resident mountain goat population. The permit is notoriously difficult to secure; day hiking requires only a Northwest Forest Pass.

## 9. Newhalem Creek Campground, North Cascades National Park, Washington

The North Cascades have been called the American Alps — a characterisation that undersells them in some respects. The park contains more glaciers than any other area in the contiguous United States and a landscape of steep, jagged peaks that sees far fewer visitors than the Cascades' more accessible southern sections. Newhalem Creek campground in the Skagit River valley is the most accessible facility in the park, near the town of Newhalem and the Seattle City Light hydroelectric complex whose tours explain how three dams on the Skagit supply power to Seattle. The campground serves as a base for the Ladder Creek Falls nature trail and drives toward Diablo Lake, whose glacial flour gives it an extraordinary turquoise colour.

## 10. Champoeg State Heritage Area Campground, Oregon

Not all great Pacific Northwest camping is wilderness. Champoeg State Heritage Area on the Willamette River south of Portland occupies the site of the 1843 meeting at which Oregon Territory settlers voted to establish a provisional government independent of British Hudson's Bay Company control — a foundational moment in Oregon history. The campground is in open parkland along the riverbank with oak savanna and good birdwatching. The heritage area museum interprets the 1843 vote and the Kalapuya people's much older occupation of the valley. For campers based in Portland, Champoeg offers a genuine greenway escape within an hour's drive of the city.
