Ecuador is one of the most geographically concentrated countries for camping diversity anywhere in the world. Within a territory roughly the size of Colorado, you can camp at 4,800 metres on the flanks of an active volcano, sleep in the Amazon basin with the sounds of howler monkeys overhead, pitch a tent in cloud forest draped in orchids and bromeliads, or — with the appropriate permits — camp on Galapagos islands among marine iguanas and blue-footed boobies. The country's compact size means it is possible to move between radically different ecosystems within a single camping trip.

Ecuador's national park system is well-developed by regional standards, and most protected areas have established camping infrastructure or permit systems for backcountry use. The altitude of the Andean Sierra — Quito itself sits at 2,850 metres — means acclimatization is essential for volcano trekking and high-altitude camping. Spend a few days in Quito or another highland town before attempting the higher campsites. ## 1. Cotopaxi National Park, Cotopaxi Province

Cotopaxi is one of the world's highest active volcanoes at 5,897 metres, and its perfectly symmetrical cone rising above the high-Andean plateau is one of Ecuador's most iconic images. The José Rivas Refuge at 4,800 metres is the primary high-altitude camping base for summit attempts; groups camp in the refuge dormitory or in tents on the surrounding platform. The approach road through the paramo grassland passes herds of wild horses and Andean deer. Summit attempts require crampons, ice axes, and guides — the climbing route crosses crevassed glaciers. The national park's lower zones offer vehicle-accessible camping with extraordinary views of the volcano.

## 2. Quilotoa Loop, Cotopaxi Province

The Quilotoa Loop is a multi-day trekking circuit through Andean indigenous villages and agricultural terrain in the western cordillera, ending at the turquoise crater lake of Quilotoa — a caldera lake formed in a volcanic explosion around 800 years ago. Camping is possible at several points on the loop, and guesthouses in the indigenous communities of Chugchilán, Isinliví, and Sigchos provide meals and simple accommodation for those who prefer a mix of camping and village stays. The scenery throughout the circuit is spectacular, with deep quebradas (ravines) and patchwork highland agriculture framing views of distant volcanoes.

## 3. Chimborazo Faunal Production Reserve

Chimborazo, at 6,268 metres, is the highest mountain in Ecuador and — measured from the Earth's centre — the point on the planet's surface furthest from the core. The mountain's lower slopes within the reserve are home to wild vicunas, reintroduced successfully over the past three decades after near-extinction. The Estrella del Chimborazo community camping area and the higher Whymper and Carrel refuges provide overnight options for trekkers and climbers. The paramo surrounding the mountain is vast and empty, one of the most elemental high-altitude landscapes in the Andes. The nearest town is Riobamba, with good transport connections.

## 4. Cajas National Park, Azuay Province

High above the city of Cuenca in southern Ecuador, the Cajas National Park protects an extraordinary landscape of more than 230 glacial lakes set in a moorland of polylepis woodland, paramo grass, and ancient rock outcrops. The altitude ranges from 3,100 to over 4,400 metres. Day hiking and multi-day trekking routes connect the lakes, and the park entrance at Laguna Toreadora has camping facilities. Cajas is known for its dense bird diversity — the rare bearded mountaineer hummingbird, many species of antpitta, and numerous hummingbird species inhabit the polylepis groves. Thick fog rolls through the lake basins frequently, creating atmospheric morning conditions.

## 5. Podocarpus National Park, Loja Province

Podocarpus in the far south of Ecuador is one of the most biodiverse cloud forest protected areas in the world, spanning from 900 metres in the Amazon foothills to over 3,700 metres in the upper paramo. The park is named for the podocarpus — South America's only native conifer — which forms dense stands in the upper cloud forest. The park has camping areas at both the Cajanuma entrance on the highland side and the Bombuscaro entrance in the lowland forest near Zamora. Bird diversity is extraordinary: the park sits in the region with the highest known bird species richness anywhere on Earth, and dedicated birding campers regularly add hundreds of species to their lists.

## 6. Sangay National Park, Chimborazo/Morona Santiago

Sangay is one of Ecuador's most active volcanoes and the centerpiece of a national park that spans from high Andean paramo at 5,230 metres down through cloud forest to Amazon lowland at 900 metres. UNESCO lists the park as a World Heritage Site for its exceptional ecological transition. The volcano erupts frequently, and camping near the summit is expedition-level. The lower slopes in the Mocha canyon area offer established camping, and multi-day treks through the cloud forest transition zones are possible with registered guides. The combination of volcanic activity, pristine forest, and megafauna — mountain tapirs, spectacled bears, and giant otters — makes this one of the most compelling wild camping destinations in the country.

## 7. Yasuni National Park, Orellana Province

Yasuni is Ecuador's largest national park and one of the most biodiverse areas on the planet, located in the Amazon basin east of the Andes. A one-hectare plot within Yasuni contains more tree species than exist in the entire continental United States. Access is by boat from the town of Coca (Puerto Francisco de Orellana) on the Napo River. Permitted camping within the national park requires licensed guides and is organized through a small number of authorized ecotourism operators. Community-based tourism operators in the Kichwa communities on the park boundary offer cultural camping experiences. This is one of the last remaining strongholds of uncontacted indigenous groups in South America.

## 8. Reserva Ecologica Cotacachi-Cayapas, Imbabura/Esmeraldas

This large reserve bridges the western Andes and the Pacific lowlands in northern Ecuador, protecting high Andean paramo around the volcanic crater lake of Cuicocha and descending through cloud forest to tropical dry forest and coastal ecosystems. The Cuicocha lake circuit is a popular day hike from Cotacachi, with camping available in the crater rim area. The western lowland sector of the reserve near Borbon on the Esmeraldas coast protects rare Pacific Chocó biodiversity — one of the most threatened forest types in Ecuador — and has community-based camping programs with Afro-Ecuadorian communities.

## 9. El Cajas Community Camping, Imbabura Province

The area around Lago Cuicocha and the Cotacachi volcano in Imbabura Province — Ecuador's "lake district" — has developed a network of community tourism camping sites in Kichwa indigenous communities. These offer a very different camping experience from wilderness tent pitching: structured stays in community facilities, with local guides, traditional meals, and insight into the agricultural and cultural practices of the highland Kichwa people. The backdrop of volcanic lakes — Cuicocha, Mojanda, and the nearby Laguna de San Pablo — is beautiful, and the markets of Otavalo provide a complementary cultural experience just a short bus ride away.

## 10. Machalilla National Park, Manabi Province

On Ecuador's Pacific coast, Machalilla National Park protects coastal dry forest, marine terraces, archaeological sites from the Manteña civilization, and offshore islands including the Isla de la Plata — sometimes called the "poor man's Galapagos" for its breeding colonies of blue-footed and masked boobies, frigatebirds, and the possibility of humpback whale sightings from June to September. The town of Puerto Lopez serves as the park gateway. Camping at the Playa de los Frailes — a pristine beach within the park accessible only on foot — is permitted and represents one of the most scenic coastal camping spots on Ecuador's mainland coast.
