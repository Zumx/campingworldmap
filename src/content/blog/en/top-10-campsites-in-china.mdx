China's vast territory encompasses some of the most dramatic and varied landscapes on Earth — Himalayan peaks, subtropical rainforests, Gobi Desert dunes, alpine meadows, and pristine river valleys. Camping culture has expanded rapidly in recent years as urban Chinese residents seek outdoor escapes, and the country now supports a growing network of developed campsites alongside vast wild-camping terrain in protected areas. Whether you arrive with a backpack and a bivy or a fully equipped overland vehicle, the camping possibilities are extraordinary and largely uncrowded by international visitors.

Navigating the system requires some preparation. Many national parks and scenic areas require advance booking through official platforms, and some wilderness zones require permits or registered guides. That said, the infrastructure at established sites has improved considerably, and the landscapes reward the effort many times over. Use the [campsite map](/map) to plan your route before diving into the individual highlights below.

## 1. Siguniang Mountain, Sichuan

Known as the "Alps of China," the Siguniang Mountain Scenic Area in western Sichuan protects a cluster of high peaks rising above 6,000 metres, surrounded by valleys strewn with wildflowers and yak pastures. The Changping Valley and Shuangqiao Valley offer established camping areas at altitude, with permits required for higher routes. Trekking camps among the spruce and fir forests below the snowline are exceptional in late spring and early autumn when the rhododendrons bloom and the skies clear. The area is accessible from Chengdu by road and serves as a base for both day hiking and multi-day circuits.

## 2. Zhangjiajie National Forest Park, Hunan

The towering sandstone pillars of Zhangjiajie — the inspiration behind the floating mountains in a famous science fiction film — are one of China's most iconic landscapes. Camping within the park boundaries is restricted, but several licensed campsite operators work in the buffer zone and on adjacent hills, offering views across the pillar formations at dawn and dusk when tourist crowds thin. The surrounding Wulingyuan Scenic Area has extensive trail networks through subtropical forest. Book through official channels well in advance, particularly for peak season between May and October.

## 3. Kanas Lake, Xinjiang

Remote Kanas Lake in the Altai Mountains of far northern Xinjiang sits close to the point where China, Russia, Mongolia, and Kazakhstan converge. The lake is extraordinarily beautiful — a deep blue-green ribbon flanked by birch and larch forest that turns gold in September. The Tuva people, a small ethnic group related to those of southern Siberia, live in log-cabin villages near the lakeshore. Designated camping areas sit within the national nature reserve, and the surrounding alpine zone offers serious trekking. Access involves long distances from Urumqi, making it a destination for committed travelers.

## 4. Tiger Leaping Gorge, Yunnan

The Tiger Leaping Gorge on the Jinsha River, flanked by the Jade Dragon Snow Mountain and Haba Snow Mountain, is one of the deepest river gorges on Earth. The classic two-day high trail passes through Naxi minority villages and terraced fields before descending to riverside guesthouses. Wild camping on the upper trail is permitted and practiced by many trekkers — flat spots above the gorge offer extraordinary views of the snow peaks. The trail is genuine mountain terrain with significant elevation changes, and afternoon thunderstorms are common in summer. Best conditions are found in spring and autumn.

## 5. Sanjiangyuan National Park, Qinghai

The source region of the Yangtze, Yellow, and Mekong rivers on the Tibetan Plateau protects an enormous area of grassland, wetland, and alpine terrain at elevations above 4,000 metres. Camping here is wild and expedition-style, with permits required. The landscape is sparsely inhabited by Tibetan herder communities and supports snow leopards, Tibetan antelope, and black-necked cranes. The altitude demands acclimatization and proper cold-weather equipment even in summer. What you gain is a sense of absolute remoteness and an ecosystem that remains largely undisturbed — one of the largest intact high-altitude grassland systems remaining anywhere.

## 6. Huangshan (Yellow Mountain), Anhui

Huangshan's granite peaks, gnarled pine trees, and sea-of-cloud formations are among the most painted and photographed landscapes in Chinese art history. The mountain has a sophisticated cable car and hotel infrastructure at altitude, but tent camping is also permitted in designated areas on the summit plateau. Sleeping at altitude allows you to witness the cloud inversions at dawn that make the mountain famous — a spectacle almost impossible to see if you ascend by day trip. The lower slopes are covered in classic Chinese mountain forest with well-maintained stone staircases threading between peaks and temples.

## 7. Daocheng Yading Nature Reserve, Sichuan

The Yading reserve in the remote Garzê Tibetan Autonomous Prefecture protects three sacred snow peaks — Chenrezig, Jambeyang, and Chanadorje — surrounded by turquoise glacial lakes and high alpine meadows. The circuit trek around the inner sanctuary is one of the finest high-altitude walks in Asia. Camping is permitted at designated sites within the reserve; at elevations above 4,000 metres, nights are cold even in August. The approach from Chengdu involves a two-day overland journey through Tibetan farming valleys, and the remoteness keeps visitor numbers lower than comparable scenery would attract elsewhere.

## 8. Changbai Mountain, Jilin

Changbai Mountain (Baekdu Mountain in Korean) on the border with North Korea is an ancient stratovolcano whose summit caldera holds Tianchi — Heaven Lake — a strikingly deep crater lake. The surrounding temperate forest is one of the most intact deciduous ecosystems in northeast Asia, supporting Siberian tigers, Amur leopards, and brown bears. Camping is permitted in designated areas in the lower nature reserve zone. The mountain is accessible from the Chinese side via Antu County, and the combination of volcanic landscape, old-growth forest, and biodiversity makes it one of the most ecologically significant camping destinations in the country.

## 9. Qilian Mountains, Gansu and Qinghai

The Qilian Mountain range forms the northeastern edge of the Tibetan Plateau and the southern wall of the Hexi Corridor — the ancient Silk Road route. The range is a mosaic of glaciated peaks, subalpine meadows grazed by yaks, and lower valleys planted with Qilian juniper, a species found almost nowhere else. Several river valleys have established camping areas accessible from the Zhangye side. The danxia landforms of Zhangye — striated coloured hills of red, orange, and yellow sandstone — are within day-trip distance, making the area a multi-landscape camping destination.

## 10. Potatso National Park, Yunnan

Potatso in the Diqing Tibetan Autonomous Prefecture near Shangri-La was China's first national park established under a new protected-area framework. It protects pristine forest, alpine lakes, and subalpine meadows at elevations between 3,400 and 4,000 metres. The Bita Lake and Shudu Lake loops are walk-accessible and offer designated camping areas with basic facilities. The park is home to black-necked cranes, Yunnan snub-nosed monkeys, and an extraordinary diversity of rhododendron species. The town of Shangri-La serves as the gateway, with reliable transport connections to Kunming and Lijiang.
