Kenya's camping ranges from the public campsites within the Maasai Mara — basic, unfenced, and positioned inside one of the world's great wildlife concentrations — to the high-altitude technical camps on Mount Kenya's upper peaks. The Kenya Wildlife Service (KWS) manages most public campsites, while private and community conservancies operate their own campsite networks. The Maasai Mara's public sites are among the most wildlife-proximate camping in Africa; Mount Kenya provides technical mountaineering camping from 4,000 metres to the 5,199-metre summit.

## 1. Makalia Falls Public Campsite, Lake Nakuru National Park

In Lake Nakuru National Park — famous for its flamingo congregations and now one of Kenya's four primary black and white rhinoceros sanctuaries — the Makalia Falls site is positioned at a waterfall and year-round stream. The rhino population at Nakuru has grown significantly under KWS protection; both black and white rhino are reliably observed. The campsite is unfenced; elephants, buffalo, and leopard move through the site. KWS fee applies.

## 2. Olololo Gate Public Camping, Maasai Mara National Reserve

On the western boundary of the Mara near the Tanzanian border, the Olololo gate camping area is within the reserve and exposed to the full spectrum of Mara wildlife. The Mara river crossing — the wildebeest migration's most dramatic event — occurs 30-40 km east, but the western Mara has high lion and leopard density year-round. Public campsites in the Mara are genuinely basic: a fire grate, a long-drop toilet, and nothing else. All camp kit must be carried in.

## 3. Endachata Special Campsite, Maasai Mara

A private (special) campsite — allocated exclusively to one party — in the Maasai Mara. Special campsites require booking through the Mara conservancy and are priced higher than public sites, but the exclusive allocation means no sharing with other groups. The Endachata site is in open grassland with good visibility. Night wildlife sounds are immediate; camp protocol requires restricting movement after dark.

## 4. Oloongonot Public Campsite, Maasai Mara

Another public campsite in the reserve, Oloongonot is positioned on the Mara plains. The migration timing for the Mara crossing is typically July through September; outside migration, the resident wildlife — lion, cheetah, elephant, giraffe, hippo — maintains year-round density. Public sites require all camping supplies self-carried; no camp support services.

## 5. Lake Ellis Campsite, Mount Kenya

On the south side of Mount Kenya at approximately 4,200 metres altitude, Lake Ellis is on the Chogoria route — considered the most scenic of the three main approaches to the summit. The Chogoria route ascends through bamboo forest, giant heather moorland, and then the open alpine zone. Lake Ellis sits in a glacially carved valley at the route's highest campsite before the final summit push. Technical climbing gear required for Point Lenana (4,985 m) and higher summits.

## 6. Two Tarns Camp, Mount Kenya

At approximately 4,500 metres on Mount Kenya's circuit around the main peaks, Two Tarns is a high camp used for the summit approach to Point Lenana — the highest accessible non-technical summit, reached by most trekking clients. The tarn setting with the glaciated peaks above is the defining image of Kenya's high alpine zone. Altitude acclimatisation protocol requires at least two nights above 3,500 metres before the summit attempt.

## 7. KWS Chogoria Forest Station Campsite, Mount Kenya

At the base of the Chogoria route in the Mount Kenya forest buffer, this KWS campsite provides the first overnight on the standard Chogoria itinerary. The forest zone here — podocarpus and cedar — holds buffalo, elephant, and the rarely seen bongo antelope. The transition from equatorial forest to bamboo to heath is one of the world's most dramatic altitudinal zonation sequences over 3,000 metres of vertical rise.

## 8. Crayfish Camp, Naivasha, Rift Valley

On the shore of Lake Naivasha in the Rift Valley — a freshwater lake renowned for its yellow fever tree forests, hippo populations, and resident fish eagle — Crayfish Camp is one of the most established camping destinations in Kenya's lake zone. Hippos graze on the campsite lawn at night — a genuine hazard that requires understanding of hippo behaviour protocols. Boat hire for lake exploration and the Crescent Island wildlife sanctuary are available locally.

## 9. Twiga Lodge, Naivasha

On the south shore of Lake Naivasha, Twiga Lodge has a campsite alongside its lodge accommodation and is a long-established fixture in the Naivasha circuit. The site has more facilities than the public campsites in the parks — ablutions, a kitchen area, and a bar. Hippos are as present here as at Crayfish Camp; the same night-movement protocols apply.

## 10. Amboseli Public Campsite, Amboseli National Park

In Amboseli — the park that provides Kenya's classic Kilimanjaro view — the public campsite is positioned in the park interior. On clear mornings, the Kilimanjaro summit (in Tanzania) rises directly above the park's flat, dusty, acacia-dotted landscape. Amboseli has some of the most well-studied wild elephant families in the world; the Amboseli Elephant Research Project has continuously monitored the population since 1972. Late afternoon light on the mountain is exceptional for photography.

## Practical notes

KWS park fees must be paid at entry gates. Public campsite fees are additional. The Maasai Mara has different fee structures for the national reserve and the surrounding private conservancies. All camping in KWS parks and the Mara requires self-sufficiency in food, water, and shelter — no services are provided at public sites.
