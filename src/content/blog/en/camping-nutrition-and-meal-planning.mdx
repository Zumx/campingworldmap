Nutrition and meal planning for extended camping trips is more consequential than it appears. A well-nourished camper has the energy to complete long hiking days, maintain mental clarity for navigation decisions, recover properly overnight, and resist the increased susceptibility to illness that comes with fatigue and inadequate nutrition. Poor planning leads to caloric deficits that compound over days, an uncomfortable reliance on whatever processed snacks are left at the bottom of the food bag, and the creeping demoralisation that sets in when food is both scarce and unappealing.

The challenge of camp nutrition is balancing competing constraints: weight and volume (particularly important for backpacking where every gram counts), shelf stability without refrigeration, cookability on limited fuel with limited equipment, and nutritional adequacy across the macronutrient categories — carbohydrates, protein, and fat — as well as the micronutrients that support sustained physical activity. Understanding these constraints and planning proactively around them produces camp meals that are both practical and genuinely satisfying.

## Calculating Caloric Needs

The starting point for meal planning is understanding how many calories you will burn each day. A typical adult burns roughly 2,000 to 2,500 calories at rest (depending on body size and metabolic rate); a day of moderate backpacking with elevation gain adds 1,000 to 2,000 additional calories on top of that baseline. Sustained multi-day trekking, particularly in cold weather or at altitude (where the body burns more to maintain core temperature), can push daily requirements to 4,000 to 5,000 calories.

The practical implication is that under-eating is easy on an active camping trip. Camp food often weighs less and is less calorie-dense than normal food, and the effort of cooking can reduce appetite appeal. Plan deliberately calorie-rich meals and carry high-calorie snacks — nuts, nut butter packets, dried fruit and chocolate, energy bars — to fill gaps between meals.

## Macronutrient Balance

Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for aerobic exercise and should comprise the majority of caloric intake on active camping days. Complex carbohydrates — oats, lentils, pasta, rice, quinoa, whole grain crackers — provide sustained energy release, while simple carbohydrates (sugar, dried fruit, gels) provide rapid energy for immediate demand. Breakfast and lunch should be primarily carbohydrate-driven; dinner can include more protein and fat.

Protein is essential for muscle repair after heavy physical days and becomes more important on trips longer than three days when cumulative physical demand begins to stress and rebuild muscle tissue. Protein sources that travel well without refrigeration include: canned tuna (heavy but highly nutritious), dried legumes (lentils cook faster than most beans), dehydrated meat or jerky, hard cheeses (last several days unrefrigerated in cool weather), shelf-stable tofu, and whole nuts. Aim for at least 1.6 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight on active days.

Fat is the most calorie-dense macronutrient (9 calories per gram versus 4 for carbohydrates and protein) and is essential for sustained warmth in cold weather camping, where the body burns fat as a primary thermogenic fuel. Carry full-fat foods: olive oil (a small bottle adds an enormous number of calories to any meal with minimal weight), nuts and nut butters, cheese, and dark chocolate. Fat also improves the satiety of camp meals significantly — a meal with adequate fat content keeps hunger away for longer.

## Meal Planning Framework

A practical approach to camp meal planning is to work through each day of the trip and assign a breakfast, lunch, snack allocation, and dinner. For backpacking trips, target approximately 450 to 550 grams of food per person per day as a planning baseline (this varies with caloric density of chosen foods). Heavier, less calorie-dense foods are acceptable for car camping where weight is not a constraint.

Breakfast should be quick and energy-providing: overnight oats prepared the night before, instant porridge with nuts and dried fruit, or granola with powdered milk are standard options. Lunch for active hiking days should not require cooking — hard crackers with nut butter and jam, salami and hard cheese, energy bars, and dried fruit and nut mix are easily eaten while moving or at a short stop. Dinner is the social and nutritional anchor of the camp day and justifies more elaborate cooking; one-pot pasta, rice and lentil dishes, rehydrated dehydrated meals, and soup with protein additions are all practical.

## Food Preservation Without Refrigeration

Shelf-stable foods that do not require refrigeration span a wide range: all dried and dehydrated products, canned goods, hard cheeses (waxed or vacuum-sealed), shelf-stable nut butters, whole nuts and seeds, dried legumes and grains, chocolate, coffee and tea, and ultra-high-temperature processed products. For car camping with cooler access, the cold chain extends the range considerably — fresh vegetables for the first two days, proteins in sealed bags on ice, and dairy products that would otherwise be impractical.

For backpacking without cooler access in warm weather, the food list is restricted to shelf-stable items. Dehydrated backpacking meals (Mountain House, Backpacker's Pantry, and Outdoor Herbivore are well-regarded brands) provide complete dinners in a sealed pouch that requires only boiling water. They are expensive but extremely convenient, and the newer generation of these meals has improved substantially in taste and nutritional quality over older formulations.

## Home Dehydration for Long Trips

Home dehydration using a food dehydrator allows substantially more interesting and nutritious camp food than commercial options, at lower cost. Dehydrated home-cooked meals — pasta sauces, bean stews, vegetable soups — rehydrate reliably and retain much of the flavour of the original dish. A dehydrator (Excalibur and NESCO make reliable units) runs at low temperature over six to twelve hours to remove moisture from cooked foods, producing shelf-stable products that can be vacuum-sealed for trips of several weeks. Planning a dehydration session several weeks before a long trip allows for a varied and personalised menu.

## Water Needs and Electrolytes

Water is the most critical nutritional input on active camping days, and the need for water increases substantially with temperature, altitude, and exertion level. Carry electrolyte supplements (salt tablets, electrolyte powder packets) for days of heavy sweating; drinking large quantities of plain water without electrolyte replacement can cause hyponatremia, a genuinely dangerous condition. Electrolyte drinks or powder added to water bottles during heavy hiking days prevents this risk.

[Explore the map](/map) to identify your planned campgrounds and assess whether potable water is available on-site or whether water treatment for natural sources will be needed — this directly affects how much water you need to carry and what filtration equipment is essential for your trip. Good nutrition and adequate hydration are the foundation of an enjoyable and safe extended camping experience.
