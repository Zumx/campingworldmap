Scotland has the most extensive wild camping rights in the UK, enshrined in the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003, which grants the public responsible access to most land for recreational purposes including overnight camping without a landowner's permission. This transforms the Highlands into one of Europe's great wild camping destinations — you can pitch a tent beside a lochan on Rannoch Moor, on a ridge in the Cairngorms above the tree line, or in a glen in Torridon, with no charge and no reservation required, provided you follow the Scottish Outdoor Access Code. Commercial campgrounds offer alternatives for those who prefer facilities, but the wild option defines Highland camping culture. Midges — the small biting insects that appear in clouds from late spring through early autumn in still, humid conditions — are the primary planning challenge, and a head net and insect repellent are not optional.

## 1. Sands Caravan and Camping, Gairloch, Wester Ross

Gairloch is a scattered crofting village on the northwestern coast of Scotland where the Torridon mountains meet Loch Gairloch and its islands. Sands campsite sits on a machair grassland above a long sandy beach with views west over the Minch to the Outer Hebrides. The beach is consistently one of the finest swimming beaches in the Scottish Highlands, with clear water and sand white enough to surprise first-time visitors. The Beinn Eighe National Nature Reserve, Britain's first national nature reserve established in 1951, is 15 kilometres southeast and protects remnant Caledonian pinewood and the mountain plateau above. The campsite has good facilities for Highland standards and a restaurant in summer.

## 2. Badrallach Campsite, Little Loch Broom, Wester Ross

Badrallach is reached by nine kilometres of single-track road along the south shore of Little Loch Broom — a deliberate journey that ensures the campsite attracts those who specifically want remoteness. The site is small, bothie-adjacent, and positioned above the loch with views across to the An Teallach ridge, one of the finest ridge walks in Scotland, a technically demanding scramble over a series of Munro summits. The campsite has a small stone bothy for shelter and fires, and the sense of removal from everyday life is stronger here than at almost any commercial campsite in Scotland. Midge nets are essential in still summer conditions.

## 3. Inverey Campsite, Braemar, Cairngorms

Braemar in upper Deeside is the gateway to the Cairngorms' southern approaches and to the Linn of Dee, where the River Dee passes through a narrow gorge before widening into the upper valley. The National Trust for Scotland operates a campsite at Inverey, west of Braemar in the Mar Lodge Estate, which is the largest rewilding project in the British Isles: Caledonian pinewood regeneration across former grouse moor on an estate the size of a small county. The Lairig Ghru, the high pass bisecting the Cairngorm massif from south to north, begins from near the campsite. The Cairngorms plateau above holds the largest sub-Arctic landscape in the British Isles and is home to breeding ptarmigan, dotterel, and snow buntings.

## 4. Milarrochy Bay Campsite, Loch Lomond, Stirlingshire

Loch Lomond and The Trossachs is Scotland's most visited national park, and its proximity to Glasgow — the park boundary is within 20 kilometres of the city edge — makes it the accessible face of Highland camping for Scotland's central belt population. Milarrochy Bay on the eastern shore of Loch Lomond is a Forestry and Land Scotland site with lochside pitches, good facilities, and the West Highland Way passing directly through. The Way continues north to Glen Nevis at Fort William, but the Loch Lomond section through the park is among the most popular day and overnight hiking routes in Scotland. The loch is the largest by surface area in Great Britain.

## 5. Glencoe Mountain Resort Campsite, Glencoe, Argyll

Glencoe is perhaps the most dramatically situated glen in Scotland — its walls close in on a narrow road hemmed by the Aonach Eagach ridge on the north and the Three Sisters buttresses on the south, a landscape shaped by volcanic eruption, glacial carving, and the 1692 massacre of the MacDonald clan by government troops. The campsite at the base of Glencoe Mountain Resort has direct access to ski lifts (in winter) and to the chairlift that in summer runs to a viewpoint above the glen. The Lost Valley (Coire Gabhail) hidden in the hillside above the Three Sisters is one of the most interesting short hikes in the glen, a glacially carved corrie where the MacDonalds reputedly hid their cattle.

## 6. Torridon Youth Hostel and Campsite, Torridon, Wester Ross

Torridon's mountains are ancient by Alpine standards — the Torridon sandstone is around 750 million years old, among the oldest rock in Britain, and the mountains it forms have a distinctive layered architecture of red and brown tiers topped by white quartzite. The Beinn Alligin and Liathach traverses from the Torridon campsite are both full-day mountain routes requiring navigational competence in poor visibility and with some scrambling on the upper ridges. The National Trust for Scotland visitor centre at Torridon has exhibits on the ecology and geology. Red deer are common on the lower slopes around the campsite, especially in the morning. The village is a 20-minute drive from Shieldaig.

## 7. Kinlochewe Caravan Club Site, Kinlochewe, Wester Ross

Kinlochewe sits at the junction of three glens where the River Kinlochewe flows out of Loch Maree, the most beautiful large loch in Scotland according to many Highland travellers. The Caravan Club site is well managed with solid facilities and positioned for climbing Slioch, the quartzite pyramid rising above the eastern end of Loch Maree, and for exploring the Beinn Eighe nature reserve pinewood. Loch Maree is a designated Special Area of Conservation for its population of freshwater pearl mussels, a protected species whose shells were historically harvested for pearl production. The Victorian hotel at Loch Maree was visited by Queen Victoria in 1877.

## 8. Shiel Bridge Campsite, Glen Shiel, Highland

Glen Shiel carries the main road from Inverness to Kyle of Lochalsh and the Skye Bridge, and the Kintail mountains above the glen include the Five Sisters of Kintail and the South Cluanie Ridge — both long ridge traverses that require significant logistics. The Shiel Bridge campsite operated by the National Trust for Scotland is at the foot of the Five Sisters and adjacent to the Cluanie Inn, which serves food to walkers and has a drying room. The Falls of Glomach, reached by a 10-kilometre walk from Dorusduain, are among the highest waterfalls in Britain at 113 metres and are almost never visited by the coach-tour crowd due to the walk-in requirement.

## 9. Red Squirrel Campsite, Glencoe, Argyll

Red Squirrel is a privately operated campsite in the lower glen below the main Glencoe road junction, shaded by Scots pine and birch on the south side of the River Coe. It has a reputation among outdoor climbers and mountaineers as an informal gathering place with a relaxed atmosphere — a contrast to the more regimented national trust facilities further into the glen. The campsite is close to the Lost Valley approach and to the Buachaille Etive Mor, the pyramidal peak at the glen's eastern entrance that is one of the most photographed mountains in Scotland. The annual Glencoe climbing festival is based here.

## 10. Loch Morlich Campsite, Cairngorms National Park, Highland

Loch Morlich is a glacially carved lake in the Glenmore Forest Park within the Cairngorms, surrounded by Scots pine forest that is remnant Caledonian woodland — the ancient pine forest that once covered much of the Scottish Highlands. The campsite is large and well-equipped, operated by Forestry and Land Scotland, with a sandy beach on the loch shore. The Cairngorm Mountain funicular provides winter skiing access and summer access to the Northern Corries. The reindeer herd maintained in the Cairngorms since 1952 — the only free-ranging reindeer in Britain — grazes on the hillside above the forest. The Loch Morlich to Loch An Eilein forest walk is a straightforward four-hour circuit through mature pinewood.
