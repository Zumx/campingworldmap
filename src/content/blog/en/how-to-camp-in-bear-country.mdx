Bears and people share camping landscapes across large areas of North America, parts of Europe, and Asia. The encounters that end badly for either party are almost always preventable, and the overwhelming majority of them involve food. Bears are highly intelligent opportunists: once an individual bear learns that human campsites contain accessible food, it will return repeatedly and become increasingly bold. A food-conditioned bear typically ends up being killed by wildlife managers, not because of anything the bear did wrong but because of failures in human food storage practices. Understanding how to camp in bear country is therefore both a personal safety matter and a conservation responsibility.

## Understanding Bear Behaviour

North America is home to two bear species that camping regulations address: black bears (Ursus americanus) and grizzly bears (Ursus arctos horribilis). Their temperaments differ meaningfully. Black bears are generally more risk-averse and less likely to press a confrontation. Grizzlies, particularly mothers with cubs, are more likely to stand their ground or charge. Knowing which species inhabits your camping area informs your response protocol in an encounter.

Bears are primarily motivated by calories. Their pre-hibernation hyperphagia phase in late summer and autumn drives them to consume extraordinary quantities of food, and this is when bear-human food conflicts peak at campgrounds. Bears have an acute sense of smell and can detect food odours over remarkable distances. Any item with a scent — food, cooking equipment, sunscreen, toothpaste, lip balm, fuel canisters — is a potential attractant and must be treated accordingly.

Bears associate specific visual and behavioural cues with food rewards. Coolers, backpacks, and vehicles with food inside are all targets. A bear that successfully opens a cooler at one campsite will try every cooler it encounters afterward. This is learned behaviour, and it is humans who teach it.

## Food Storage: The Foundation of Bear Safety

Proper food storage is the single most impactful measure in bear country camping. The options range in security and practicality.

Bear canisters are hard-sided containers made from polycarbonate or similar materials that bears cannot open regardless of effort. They are required in many areas — including Yosemite National Park's backcountry, Rocky Mountain National Park's designated zones, and sections of the Appalachian Trail — and recommended universally in bear country. Canisters should be placed on flat ground at least 60 metres from your tent, away from the edge of slopes or streams where a displaced canister could roll into water.

Bear boxes — fixed metal storage lockers — are provided at established campgrounds in many national and state parks. Use them for all food, scented items, and cooking equipment every night. Do not leave items in a vehicle; bears have broken into locked cars for food, causing expensive damage. The side windows and rear doors are particularly vulnerable to bear entry.

Hanging food from a tree branch — the classic PCT hang or PCT bear hang — is a traditional method that is now considered unreliable in areas with bear populations habituated to humans. Experienced bears in well-trafficked wilderness areas have learned to defeat standard food hangs. In regions where this is the only option, the Ursack soft-sided bear bag with proper hanging technique provides an intermediate level of protection.

## Camp Kitchen Practices

Where you cook and eat significantly affects bear activity around your camp. In backcountry camping, establish a triangle of separation between your sleep area, cooking area, and food storage area, with each point at least 60 metres from the others. Cook and eat downwind from your sleep area so that food smells are carried away from your tent.

Burn all food residue completely if you have a fire, and pack out ash if directed. Do not bury food scraps — bears dig. Pack out all food waste including fruit peels, shells, and scraps. Strain dishwater and pack out the solids; disperse water far from your sleep area.

Do not cook or eat in your tent. Do not wear the same clothing to sleep that you cooked in. Change into clean clothes for sleeping and store cooking clothes in your bear canister or food storage area. This practice reduces the amount of food odour present in your sleeping area.

## Encountering a Bear at Camp

Most bear encounters at campgrounds involve a bear that has detected food and is investigating. In this situation, your goal is to remove the reward without creating a dangerous confrontation. Make yourself visible and make noise — bears that know humans are present typically withdraw. Do not run. Running triggers pursuit instinct in bears and is almost always slower than a bear's top speed.

If a black bear is in your campsite and has obtained food, make noise, wave your arms, and advance toward the bear while making yourself appear large. Black bears rarely press a confrontation. If a grizzly is in your campsite, particularly a mother with cubs, increase your visual size, speak calmly, and back away without turning. Never position yourself between a mother grizzly and her cubs.

Carry bear spray in grizzly country and know how to deploy it. Bear spray is a capsaicin-based aerosol designed to be sprayed in a wide cloud when a bear is charging. It is effective at deterring charges in the vast majority of documented cases and is considered more reliably effective than firearms in stopping a grizzly charge. Keep it accessible — in a hip holster, not buried in a pack — while hiking in grizzly habitat.

## Sleeping in Bear Country

Your tent provides no physical barrier against a bear. If a bear investigates your tent while you are sleeping, it is almost certainly because it has detected food or scented items inside. Keeping your sleep area free of all food and scented items eliminates the primary reason a bear approaches a tent at night.

Established campgrounds with bear boxes and active wildlife management have significantly lower rates of tent-entry incidents than backcountry areas. For backpacking in high-density grizzly areas — parts of Glacier National Park, the Yellowstone backcountry, the Denali backcountry in Alaska — electric fences around the sleeping area are used by some expeditions as an additional deterrent. Portable electric bear fences are commercially available and lightweight enough for backcountry use.

## Finding Bear Country Campgrounds

Some of the most spectacular camping in North America is in areas with healthy bear populations. Glacier National Park in Montana, Denali National Park in Alaska, Great Smoky Mountains National Park in the southern Appalachians, Yosemite in California, and Banff and Jasper National Parks in Alberta all have active bear populations and well-developed systems for managing bear-human interaction.
