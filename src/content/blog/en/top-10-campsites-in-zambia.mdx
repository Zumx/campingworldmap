Zambia is one of Africa's great safari destinations — less marketed than Kenya or Tanzania but with wildlife experiences that many experienced safari-goers rank higher for their authenticity and exclusivity. The country pioneered walking safaris through its South Luangwa National Park in the 1950s, a practice now widely adopted but nowhere more refined than in Zambia's Luangwa Valley. The country's park network includes Kafue — the second-largest national park in Africa — and Lower Zambezi, positioned across the river from Zimbabwe's Mana Pools. Camping here ranges from well-equipped private bush camps to the Zambia Wildlife Authority's basic public sites, where self-sufficient campers with tents and provisions can access remote wilderness at minimal cost. The dry season from June through October concentrates wildlife around diminishing water sources and provides the best game viewing.

## 1. Flatdogs Camp, South Luangwa National Park, Eastern Province

South Luangwa is the home of the walking safari and contains one of the most intact large mammal ecosystems in Africa. The Luangwa River and its oxbow lagoons are the focal point, drawing hippos, crocodiles, elephants, and the park's famously dense leopard population. Flatdogs Camp takes its name from the Luangwa's abundant Nile crocodiles and is positioned on the park boundary with the river in front. It has camping facilities, chalets, and organises walking safaris and game drives. Hippos are audible from tents at night and elephants occasionally walk through the camp area. The camp's communal fire area is a social gathering point for overlanders and budget travellers. Mfuwe Lodge and the Norman Carr Safaris camps upstream offer higher-end alternatives.

## 2. Croc Valley Camp, South Luangwa National Park

Croc Valley is on the eastern bank of the Luangwa at the Mfuwe Bridge entry area, positioned below the main tourist infrastructure. The camp has shaded tent pitches, a communal kitchen area, and direct views across the river to the park's interior. The camp has a pool and serves meals, making it accessible for self-drive visitors who prefer not to cook in camp. The Mfuwe area is famous for the elephants that walk through the Mfuwe Lodge reception and management office to reach a wild mango tree that fruits within the building — one of the more reliably documented unusual wildlife behaviour events in Africa. Walking safaris with licensed guides can be booked from Croc Valley.

## 3. KaingU Safari Lodge Campsite, Kafue National Park, Western Province

Kafue National Park is one of the largest parks in the world, covering 22,400 square kilometres — larger than Wales. The Busanga Plains in Kafue's north are arguably the finest wildlife area in the park, flooded in the rains and draining to grassland in the dry season, attracting large herds of red lechwe, puku, and the sitatunga antelope. KaingU Safari Lodge on the Kafue River in the northern park has a camping section accessible to self-drive visitors. The Kafue River through the park holds hippos and crocodiles, and fish eagles are omnipresent on the riverbanks. The dry season drives on the Busanga Plains from June onward encounter lion prides and cheetah in open conditions that allow long-range observation.

## 4. Mukambi Safari Lodge Camping, Kafue National Park

Mukambi is on the Kafue River in the central park, positioned in woodland that transitions to seasonal flood plain east of the river. The lodge's camping section caters to overlanders on the Lusaka-Livingstone and Lusaka-Mongu routes that pass through Kafue. Wild dog are seen in the central Kafue with regularity — the Kafue ecosystem holds one of the larger African wild dog populations in southern Africa. The Itezhi-Tezhi reservoir south of Mukambi was created by a dam on the Kafue River and provides a large water body that concentrates wildlife on its shores. Boat-based game viewing on the reservoir is a distinctive Kafue experience not available in Luangwa.

## 5. Zambezi Waterfront Campsite, Livingstone, Southern Province

Victoria Falls on the Zambezi River between Zambia and Zimbabwe is one of the seven natural wonders of the world — the widest curtain of falling water on Earth at 1,708 metres across. The Zambian side of the falls shows the full width of the Falls during the April through July high-water period, when spray rises 400 metres and can be seen 50 kilometres away. Zambezi Waterfront in Livingstone, 10 kilometres from the falls, is the main budget camping facility for the Victoria Falls area with a riverside location and access to the activities industry — bungee jumping, white-water rafting below the falls, and sunset cruises. The town of Livingstone has a comprehensive tourist infrastructure.

## 6. Mvuu Camp, Lower Zambezi National Park, Lusaka Province

The Lower Zambezi Valley below Lake Kariba is a long, narrow park squeezed between the escarpment and the Zimbabwean border on the river's south bank. Elephants are common in the river's edge jesse bush and canoe safaris allow close approaches impossible from vehicles. Mvuu Camp has a camping section for budget visitors and organises boat-based safaris and walks. The Chifungulu Channel, a braided section of the Zambezi through the park, is particularly productive for hippo and crocodile observation. The view across the river to Zimbabwe's Mana Pools, a separate UNESCO World Heritage Site, adds a cross-border dimension unusual in southern African camping.

## 7. Lake Kariba Campsite, Siavonga, Southern Province

Lake Kariba was created by the Kariba Dam, completed in 1959, and is one of the world's largest man-made lakes by volume. Operation Noah, conducted during the lake's filling, rescued tens of thousands of animals from drowning islands in a landmark conservation operation. The lake's Zambian shore near Siavonga has several camping facilities with waterfront pitches and fishing access — tigerfish and bream are the primary species. Kariba's sudden storms — the lake generates its own weather systems — are a navigational hazard for small boats, and afternoon departures from exposed anchorages are inadvisable. The dead trees standing in the lake's shallows, killed by the rising waters in 1959, are a haunting landmark of the reservoir's history.

## 8. Shiwa Ng'andu Estate Campsite, Muchinga Province, Northern Zambia

Shiwa Ng'andu is a remarkable anomaly: an English manor house and estate built in the Zambian bush in the 1920s by Colonel Stewart Gore-Browne, a decorated First World War officer who spent his life in Zambia and was buried on his estate. The estate, still run by Gore-Browne's descendants, covers a large area of northern Zambia around Shiwa Lake, and offers camping on the estate grounds. The house itself is open to guests, with a library, formal garden, and hot spring pool. The surrounding miombo woodland is rich in birds, and the estate's history — including Gore-Browne's close friendship with Zambian independence leaders — is one of colonial Africa's more nuanced stories.

## 9. Mutinondo Wilderness Campsite, Mpika District, Muchinga Province

Mutinondo is a private wilderness area of miombo woodland, granite domes, and rivers in northern Zambia, managed as a conservation and ecotourism estate by a family who have owned it for decades. The camp has simple but well-maintained facilities and walking trails through the estate that access waterfalls, swimming pools in river channels, and hilltops with views across the woodland canopy. The bird list is exceptional, with over 400 species recorded including the shoebill, Bohm's bee-eater, and the black-and-rufous flycatcher. Mutinondo represents the miombo biome — the dominant vegetation of central-southern Africa — at its most intact, a landscape less celebrated in safari marketing than the big-five parks but ecologically fundamental.

## 10. Chimfunshi Wildlife Orphanage Camp, Chingola, Copperbelt Province

Chimfunshi Wildlife Orphanage near Chingola is the world's largest chimpanzee sanctuary, established in 1983 by David and Sheila Siddle who began rehabilitating orphaned chimpanzees on their farm. The sanctuary now holds over a hundred chimpanzees in large bush enclosures where they live in social groups with minimal human contact. Basic camping is available at the sanctuary for visitors who come to learn about the rehabilitation work. The chimpanzees at Chimfunshi were sourced primarily from the DRC bushmeat trade and illegal pet market, and the sanctuary's work addressing orphan chimpanzee welfare has influenced primate conservation policy across Central Africa.
