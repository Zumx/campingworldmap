The Sahara is the world's largest hot desert, covering approximately 9 million square kilometres across northern Africa from the Atlantic coast of Mauritania and Morocco to the Red Sea coast of Egypt and Sudan. Camping in the Sahara is one of the most extreme and most memorable overnight experiences available to a traveller — the scale of the landscape, the silence, the quality of starlight in air with zero moisture and zero light pollution, and the cold of desert nights (temperatures drop dramatically when there is no cloud cover to retain daytime heat) combine to create something genuinely unlike any other camping environment.

The Sahara is not a uniform landscape. The popular image of endless sand dunes covers only about 25% of its area; the majority is rocky plateau (hamada), gravel plains (reg), dried salt lake beds (chott), and mountain ranges like the Hoggar in Algeria and the Tibesti in Chad. Each terrain type has different access requirements, different camping experiences, and different logistical demands. For most travellers, the accessible entry points to Saharan camping are southern Morocco (the dunes of Erg Chebbi and Erg Chigaga), Tunisia (the Chott el Djerid salt lake and the dunes around Douz), and parts of the Egyptian Western Desert and Sinai — all areas where local guide and camel operator infrastructure exists to support visitor camping.

## Southern Morocco: Erg Chebbi and Erg Chigaga

The most accessible Saharan camping for most European travellers is in southern Morocco. The Erg Chebbi near Merzouga is the classic Moroccan Sahara experience — a 22-kilometre-long erg of star-shaped dunes reaching 160 metres in height, rising abruptly from the flat reg plain. Camel trek and overnight camp options run by local operators from Merzouga are well established and range from basic Berber tent camps on the dunes to luxury glamping setups with proper beds and lantern-lit dining. A full experience requires camping beyond the dune field edge visible from the town, which requires a guided camel or 4WD approach.

The Erg Chigaga, accessible from M'Hamid at the end of the Draa Valley, is larger and more remote — a 40-kilometre erg with no road access. Reaching Chigaga requires either a multi-day camel trek (three to four days) or a 4WD vehicle with a guide. The remoteness translates directly into a purer desert camping experience with fewer visitors, and the sense of space and silence is more complete than at the more developed Merzouga area.

## Tunisia: The Chott el Djerid and the Douz Dunes

Tunisia's Sahara, while smaller in scale than Morocco's, provides excellent desert camping access in the south of the country around Douz and the enormous Chott el Djerid salt lake. The Chott el Djerid is a 5,000-square-kilometre shallow seasonal saltwater lake that dries in summer to a white salt flat of extraordinary flatness, creating mirages and optical distortions that give the landscape a surreal quality. Camping on the salt flat or on the dune fields south of Douz requires a local guide and a 4WD vehicle.

The Star Wars filming locations in the Tunisian Sahara — Mos Espa near Nefta and the Matmata troglodyte dwellings — add a cultural tourism dimension to the desert camping experience in this region. Tunisia's general political stability and established tourist infrastructure make it one of the more straightforward African countries for independent travel.

## Egypt: The White Desert and Siwa Oasis

Egypt's Western Desert contains several remarkable desert camping destinations. The White Desert (Sahara el Beyda) National Park near Farafra in the New Valley Governorate protects a landscape of white chalk formations sculpted by wind erosion into shapes resembling mushrooms, animals, and abstract sculptures. Camping among the chalk formations is possible with a local guide and a 4WD vehicle; tours departing from Cairo or from the oasis town of Bahariya are the standard access method.

The Siwa Oasis in the Great Sand Sea of the Egyptian Western Desert is an isolated Berber community 560 kilometres west of Cairo, famous for its spring-fed lakes, palm groves, and ancient oracle temple visited by Alexander the Great. Desert camping in the dune fields surrounding Siwa, organised through local guides, includes sandboarding on the dunes and a traditional Siwi meal prepared at the camp.

## Safety and Essential Preparations

Desert camping safety fundamentally centres on water management. The human body requires at least three litres of water per day in desert conditions; during physical exertion or in peak summer heat, this can rise to six litres or more. Always carry substantially more water than calculated minimum requirements — the consequences of running short in a desert environment are severe and can be fatal. Carry water in multiple containers in case of leakage.

Navigation in featureless desert terrain requires GPS capability and a downloaded offline map at minimum; a compass and the ability to take a bearing from visible landmarks provides a backup. Sand driving requires specific technique — reduced tyre pressure, momentum management, and recovery equipment including sand ladders and a high-lift jack if driving a 4WD. Travelling solo in remote desert is inadvisable; a minimum of two vehicles provides recovery capacity if one becomes stuck.

Timing is critical: avoid Saharan camping in June through August when temperatures in the hamada regularly exceed 50 degrees Celsius at ground level. October through April is the preferred season across most of the Sahara, with temperatures ranging from warm and pleasant at midday to cold at night — often below 5 degrees Celsius between December and February.

## The Desert Night Sky

One of the greatest rewards of Saharan camping is the night sky. The Sahara has among the lowest levels of light pollution of any inhabited region on earth, and the absolute clarity and dryness of the desert air — zero humidity eliminates the atmospheric scattering that reduces stargazing quality — produces skies of extraordinary clarity. The Milky Way is visible to the naked eye as a distinct structural feature rather than a vague smear, and planets, meteor showers, and deep-sky objects visible to the unaided eye are remarkable.
