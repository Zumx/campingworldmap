The Himalaya is the highest mountain range on earth, a 2,400-kilometre arc of peaks spanning northern Pakistan, India, Nepal, Bhutan, and the Tibetan Plateau of China. For trekkers and mountaineers, camping in the Himalaya is the fundamental mode of access to routes that are too long, too remote, or too elevated for teahouse-based travel. The experience of sleeping in a tent at 4,000, 5,000, or above 6,000 metres — in the shadow of the highest peaks on the planet, with the silence of high altitude broken only by wind and occasional rockfall — is unlike anything available at lower elevations.

Camping in the Himalaya requires preparation across three overlapping categories: physical conditioning and altitude management, administrative preparation (permits, visas, and regulations that vary substantially by country and region), and equipment selection for extreme conditions. The Himalaya is not a region where arriving underprepared has acceptable consequences. Altitude illness — acute mountain sickness, high-altitude pulmonary oedema, and high-altitude cerebral oedema — is a real physiological risk at elevations above 3,500 metres, and the weather can deteriorate from calm to life-threatening in hours. With proper preparation, however, the trekking and camping opportunities in the Himalaya represent some of the most profound mountain experiences available to humans.

## Altitude and Acclimatisation

The single most important factor in safe Himalayan camping is acclimatisation — the physiological process by which the body adapts to reduced oxygen availability at altitude. The standard guideline is to ascend no more than 300 to 500 metres per day once above 3,000 metres, with a rest day every third day or whenever symptoms of altitude sickness appear. Symptoms of mild AMS include headache, nausea, fatigue, and disturbed sleep; more serious symptoms including persistent dry cough, difficulty breathing at rest, loss of coordination, or confusion require immediate descent.

The most widely followed acclimatisation rule is: climb high, sleep low. Day hikes to higher elevations followed by returning to sleep at a lower camp allow the body to build acclimatisation without spending nights at altitude before the body has adjusted. Most well-designed commercial trek itineraries build in this principle; independent trekkers should research the elevation profile of their route carefully and build similar rest days into their schedule.

## Nepal: The Everest and Annapurna Regions

Nepal provides the most accessible and developed infrastructure for Himalayan camping trekking. Two major permit systems cover most popular routes: the Trekker's Information Management System (TIMS) card (required for most trail zones in Nepal) and specific national park or conservation area entry permits. The Sagarmatha National Park permit covers the Everest region; the Annapurna Conservation Area permit covers the Annapurna Circuit and Sanctuary.

Camping on the Everest trek is concentrated at established camping sites and teahouse camping yards along the route from Lukla to Everest Base Camp (5,364 m). Independent camping (wild camping off established sites) is not standard practice in the national park. The Everest Base Camp trek is typically done teahouse-to-teahouse for most of the route, with camping becoming the standard accommodation above Gorak Shep for those continuing to higher elevations.

The Annapurna Circuit — a classic route circumnavigating the Annapurna Massif over the Thorong La Pass at 5,416 metres — is campable throughout with organised camping itineraries available through Kathmandu-based trek operators. The route now has road access for much of its lower sections, but the high-altitude sections above Manang remain trail-only and retain their wilderness character.

## India: Ladakh, Spiti, and Uttarakhand

Himalayan camping in India requires navigation of a more complex permit landscape than Nepal. The Ladakh region (Jammu and Kashmir Union Territory) and the Spiti Valley in Himachal Pradesh require Inner Line Permits for foreign nationals on roads approaching sensitive border zones. The Nanda Devi and Valley of Flowers National Parks in Uttarakhand have their own entry and camping permit systems managed by the state forest department.

Ladakh's Markha Valley trek is one of the finest camping treks in the Indian Himalaya — a multi-day route through ancient village communities and high passes in the rain shadow of the Zanskar Range. The Great Himalayan Trail route through Spiti passes through Buddhist monastery towns including Kaza and Kibber, with camping in high meadows above 4,000 metres. The Roopkund trek in Uttarakhand leads to a glacial lake at 5,029 metres famous for its archaeological importance (a cache of human skeletal remains from approximately the 9th century was discovered there).

## Tibet (China): Everest North Base Camp and Mount Kailash

The Tibetan Plateau side of the Himalaya, administered by China as the Tibet Autonomous Region, requires a Tibet Travel Permit in addition to a Chinese visa, and independent travel is restricted — all travel must be through a licensed Chinese travel agency with a designated guide. Camping at Everest North Base Camp (5,200 m) on the Tibetan plateau is permitted through organised tour groups. The Mount Kailash Kora — a circumambulation of the sacred mountain in western Tibet, a pilgrimage route for Hindus, Buddhists, Jains, and Bön practitioners — involves three days of camping at high altitude around the 6,638-metre peak.

## Bhutan: Regulated High-Altitude Trekking

Bhutan caps tourist numbers through a high daily tourism royalty fee, which includes accommodation, guides, and a contribution to conservation. The Snowman Trek — considered one of the most difficult and remote treks in the world — crosses high passes above 5,000 metres in a 25-30 day route through Bhutan's northwestern wilderness. Camping throughout this route is fully organised by the licensed Bhutanese operator who is required to accompany all foreign trekkers.

## Equipment for Himalayan Camping

Sleeping bags rated to -20 degrees Celsius or below are appropriate for nights above 5,000 metres in clear conditions; four-season mountaineering tents with proven wind resistance are essential above base camp elevations. Trekking poles significantly reduce knee stress on descents and provide balance on moraine and glacier traverses. A good-quality down jacket, waterproof hard shell, insulated gloves, and a balaclava are non-negotiable items for any trek reaching high elevations.
