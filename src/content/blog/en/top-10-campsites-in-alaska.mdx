Alaska is the largest state in the United States by a significant margin, covering over 1.7 million square kilometres of mostly undeveloped terrain. Its camping ranges from drive-in state park campgrounds along the road-accessible corridor between Anchorage and Fairbanks to true wilderness camping reachable only by floatplane, requiring entirely self-sufficient expedition preparation. The wildlife density — grizzly and black bears, wolves, moose, Dall sheep, caribou, and marine mammals along the coast — makes Alaska camping unlike anywhere in the lower 48. These ten sites represent the spectrum from accessible to genuinely remote. Browse the [map](/map) to locate each site within Alaska's vast geography.

## 1. Wonder Lake Campground, Denali National Park

Wonder Lake sits at the end of the 148-kilometre park road in Denali National Park, accessible only by the park's transit bus system — private vehicles are restricted beyond the first 22 kilometres. The campground sits at the shore of Wonder Lake with a direct view of Denali, the highest peak in North America at 6190 metres. On clear days, the reflection of the mountain in the lake is one of the most photographed scenes in Alaska. The camping itself is basic — sites are designated, bears are active in the area, and bear-resistant containers are required — but the location is without parallel. The bus ride from the visitor center takes approximately six hours each way, making this a commitment that rewards patience.

## 2. Savage River Campground, Denali National Park

The Savage River Campground at mile 13 on the park road is accessible by private vehicle and serves as the last car-accessible camp in Denali. It sits at the transition between boreal spruce forest and open tundra, with wide views toward alpine terrain. The Savage River Alpine Trail begins nearby and provides day hiking into true alpine zone without requiring a bus or permit. This campground is an excellent choice for campers who want genuine wilderness proximity with vehicle access, and it serves as a staging camp for those planning deeper park exploration by bus.

## 3. Chugach State Park, Eagle River Campground

Chugach State Park is one of the largest state parks in the United States and borders Anchorage directly, making it an accessible wildland by global urban park standards. The Eagle River Campground within the park provides tent sites among spruce and birch along the Eagle River drainage. Day hiking access from the campground reaches terrain of genuine alpine quality including Hanging Valley and surrounding ridgelines above 1200 metres. Moose are commonly seen from camp. The location within 30 minutes of Anchorage makes it a realistic option for travellers with limited Alaska time.

## 4. Glacier Bay National Park, Backcountry

Glacier Bay National Park has no designated backcountry campgrounds. Visitors who camp in the backcountry choose their own sites on any of the park's unoccupied beaches, glacial moraines, and tidal flats, with no infrastructure. Access is by kayak or water taxi from Bartlett Cove. The terrain is post-glacial — the land here was under ice as recently as the early 20th century and vegetation is in rapid succession — and the scale of tidewater glaciers, including Johns Hopkins, Grand Pacific, and Margerie Glaciers, is extraordinary. Camping in Glacier Bay backcountry is one of the most striking wilderness experiences available anywhere in Alaska.

## 5. Katmai National Park, Brooks Camp

Brooks Camp in Katmai National Park is world-famous for brown bear viewing at Brooks Falls, where bears stand in the falling water and catch sockeye salmon during the July and September runs. The established campground at Brooks Camp provides tent camping with electric bear fencing around the perimeter. The number of campers permitted is restricted and reservations are extremely competitive. Campers at Brooks Camp share space with the highest concentration of brown bears in any accessible location in Alaska — the experience of moving between your tent and the bear-viewing platforms while following park protocol is genuinely unlike anything else.

## 6. Wrangell-St. Elias National Park, Backcountry

Wrangell-St. Elias is the largest national park in the United States and one of the least visited per unit of area. The road system within the park is minimal — a gravel road to McCarthy and another to Nabesna — and the vast majority of the park's six million hectares is accessible only by floatplane or multi-day foot travel. Backcountry camping here involves genuine expedition planning: routes are largely unmarked, river crossings are unavoidable, and medical evacuation takes serious time and planning. The rewards are commensurately vast: the Bagley Icefield is one of the largest non-polar icefields in the world, and the Kennicott area provides a rare combination of glacial landscape and well-preserved mining history.

## 7. Kenai Peninsula, Resurrection Bay Area

The Resurrection Bay coastline near Seward on the Kenai Peninsula offers dramatic camping above a glacially cut fjord. The Caines Head State Recreation Area, accessible by boat or by a tide-dependent coastal trail, has basic camping with ocean views and wildlife watching including sea otters, Steller sea lions, and humpback whales in the bay during summer. The trail covers approximately 13 kilometres from the Lowell Point trailhead and is partially submerged at high tide, requiring tide chart planning. The campground at the North Beach of Caines Head occupies a site with views across Resurrection Bay to the Kenai Mountains.

## 8. Chena River State Recreation Area

The Chena River State Recreation Area northeast of Fairbanks provides accessible interior Alaska camping in a boreal landscape of spruce, birch, and open tundra. The Angel Rocks Trail from the Tors Trail Campground reaches distinctive granite tor formations with views across the Chena River drainage. In mid-summer, near the solstice, Fairbanks experiences nearly 24 hours of daylight, and camping under the midnight sun — dinner at 11 p.m. in full light — is a distinctly Alaskan experience.

## 9. Kenai National Wildlife Refuge, Hidden Lake Campground

Hidden Lake Campground on the Kenai Peninsula sits on Kenai Lake within the Kenai National Wildlife Refuge. The lake is accessible by road and provides one of the most photogenic car camping settings in accessible Alaska: the turquoise glacially-fed lake against the backdrop of the Kenai Mountains is striking in any weather. The campground has individual sites with table and fire ring infrastructure, and the lake's fishing for sockeye and rainbow trout is productive. The surrounding refuge is one of the best places in Alaska to see moose, including cows with calves in early summer.

## 10. Chilkoot Trail, Canyon City Camp

The Chilkoot Trail is a historic 53-kilometre route from Dyea near Skagway, Alaska, to Bennett Lake in British Columbia, following the path used by tens of thousands of gold rush prospectors in 1897 and 1898. Canyon City Camp is approximately 12 kilometres from the Dyea trailhead, set in boreal forest along the Taiya River. The full trail requires both US and Canadian backcountry permits and is typically completed in three to four days. Camping along the route traverses landscapes from coastal temperate rainforest through subalpine terrain to the alpine pass at 1060 metres. The route is managed jointly by the US National Park Service and Parks Canada, and reservations are required during the summer season.
