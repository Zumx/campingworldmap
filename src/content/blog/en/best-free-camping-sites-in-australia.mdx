Australia's scale and the proportion of its land area under public management make it one of the world's great destinations for free camping. State forests, national parks (many of which allow basic camping in designated areas without a fee), Crown land, and the network of rest areas along the highway system together provide an extraordinary range of no-cost overnight options for self-sufficient travellers. For the growing community of van lifers, grey nomads in caravans, and tent campers working their way around the country, knowing where free camping is legal and where it is outstanding is invaluable knowledge.

Free camping in Australia is managed at the state and territory level, with policies varying significantly. In Queensland and New South Wales, many national park campgrounds charge modest fees but state forest camping is often free. In Western Australia and South Australia, vast areas of Crown land and pastoral leasehold are accessible for camping with minimal restriction. The Northern Territory has exceptional free camping possibilities in areas that see very little traffic. Across all states, the Wikicamps app and the Campermate app are the most widely used resources for locating free and low-cost camping; both rely on community-reported sites and are frequently updated.

## Gibb River Road, Western Australia

The Gibb River Road is a 660-kilometre unsealed road through the Kimberley region of northwestern Western Australia, connecting Derby to the Great Northern Highway near Kununurra. It passes through some of the most dramatic landscape in Australia — red gorges, boab trees, cattle stations, and ephemeral rivers — and provides access to numerous free and low-cost camping sites along its length. Bell Gorge and Manning Gorge have camping areas on pastoral stations that charge small fees, but much of the land along the Gibb is accessible Crown land where free camping is customary. The road is accessible by 4WD only and is typically passable between May and October; the wet season closes it completely.

## Innamincka Regional Reserve, South Australia

At the junction of Cooper Creek and the Strzelecki Track, Innamincka sits in the remote Channel Country of outback South Australia. The Cooper Creek corridor around Innamincka has free camping areas managed by the South Australian Department for Environment and Water. The landscape is extraordinary: vast red sand dunes, braided creek channels lined with river red gum, and the silence of genuine outback wilderness. This is the country where Burke and Wills perished in 1861, and the historical markers add a sobering dimension to what is otherwise an uplifting camping destination.

## Carnarvon National Park, Queensland

Carnarvon National Park in central Queensland protects a system of sandstone gorges with walls up to 200 metres high, draped in ferns, mosses, and palms fed by permanent springs — a dramatic contrast with the surrounding dry sclerophyll forest. The national park charges a camping fee, but several state forest areas surrounding the park offer free camping options. Carnarvon Gorge walking tracks lead past Aboriginal rock art sites, the Moss Garden, and the Cathedral Cave formation. Book ahead for park campgrounds; the gorge is popular and walk-in sites fill quickly during school holidays.

## Cape York Peninsula, Queensland

Cape York Peninsula in the far north of Queensland is one of the last great wilderness areas in Australia, a mosaic of tropical savanna, rainforest corridors, and ancient river systems. The Peninsula Developmental Road provides access to numerous informal camping spots, and the Old Telegraph Track — a challenging 4WD route — passes through spectacular country with free camping along creek crossings. The peninsula is accessible only in the dry season (roughly May to October) and requires a capable 4WD vehicle. National parks and Aboriginal land trusts in the area require permits, but large sections of state land permit free camping without formalised sites.

## Mount Surprise Area, North Queensland

The Undara Volcanic National Park and surrounding state forest south of Cairns contain extensive free camping areas in the Gulf Savannah region. The volcanic lava tubes at Undara are a significant natural feature, but the state forest camping to the east toward Greenvale and Mount Garnet offers complete solitude in open woodland. This part of North Queensland is warm and dry between April and September and oppressively wet in the monsoon season. The Lynd Junction Rest Area on the Kennedy Highway is a popular free overnight stop.

## Flinders Ranges, South Australia

The Flinders Ranges in South Australia's outback extend north from Port Augusta in a series of progressively more remote ranges. Ikara-Flinders Ranges National Park charges a camping fee, but the surrounding Flinders Ranges pastoral country and state forest contain numerous free camping options. The scenic Brachina Gorge drive passes through geology spanning hundreds of millions of years, and several pullouts along it permit informal overnight camping. The Wilpena Pound resort is the main service hub; free camping spots nearby allow you to explore the same landscape at no accommodation cost.

## Murray River National Park, South Australia

The Murray River forms the border between New South Wales and Victoria before flowing west into South Australia. The Murray River National Park on the South Australian reaches contains free camping areas on the river floodplain accessible by boat or 4WD. The Katarapko section near Loxton has free designated camping spots in river red gum floodplain forest. Pelicans, waterbirds, and Murray cod make the river corridor worth staying in for multiple nights.

## Tasmania's Southwest Wilderness

Tasmania's Southwest Wilderness is one of the most remote and spectacular landscapes in Australia — an unroaded wilderness of buttongrass moorland, quartzite peaks, and ancient Huon pine forests. The Southwest National Park has free camping at designated sites along walking routes including the South Coast Track and the Port Davey Track. These are serious multi-day wilderness walks requiring high-level navigation skills and complete self-sufficiency; there are no roads into the southwest, and weather conditions are extreme and unpredictable. The reward is access to a landscape of almost mythical wildness.

## Using the Map to Plan Free Camping in Australia

Australia's free camping landscape is complex — the rules differ by state, by land tenure type, and sometimes by specific local government area near towns that restrict camping on their land. [Browse the map](/map) to explore publicly managed land across Australia, identify national park and state forest boundaries, and cross-reference with campground databases before committing to a remote destination. Many of Australia's best free camping spots are on unsealed roads requiring a capable vehicle; research road conditions through the relevant state roads authority before departure.

Carry enough water for your entire planned stay plus a safety margin — many free camping areas in arid Australia have no water supply at all. Self-contained camping, with a proper grey water system rather than dumping waste on the ground, is both legally required in many areas and simply the right approach to preserving the landscape that makes free camping in Australia so exceptional.
