Fly fishing and camping share an almost inseparable relationship. The best wild trout rivers are rarely beside a town or within easy day-trip distance of a city; they run through high mountain valleys, remote wilderness drainages, and protected national park corridors where the only way to spend meaningful time on the water is to sleep beside it. For a fly angler, camping is not just a cost-saving measure — it is the strategy that unlocks the water. Being on the river at first light, before other anglers arrive and before the hatch begins, is only possible when you wake up fifty metres from the bank.

The world's premier fly fishing waters span every continent and a remarkable range of terrain. Cold, oxygen-rich rivers with clean gravel bottoms support wild brown and rainbow trout, grayling, and in the northern reaches, Arctic char. These conditions — intact riparian vegetation, stable cold-water flows, abundant invertebrate populations — also tend to occur in landscapes that are beautiful and undisturbed enough to make camping a genuine pleasure rather than a pragmatic compromise. What follows is a selection of destinations where the camping and the fishing are both exceptional.

## 1. Gallatin River, Montana, USA

The Gallatin runs through Gallatin Canyon from Yellowstone National Park to its confluence with the Missouri near Three Forks, and the upper canyon section offers both outstanding fly fishing and campsite access within Gallatin National Forest. Wild rainbow and brown trout hold in the pocket water and riffles throughout the canyon. Several established Forest Service campgrounds sit directly above the river, and dispersed camping is permitted in many sections under standard national forest rules. The canyon road follows the river closely, making access easy, but walk-in effort rewards anglers who move away from road-accessible pools. The Gallatin is a freestone river that fishes well with attractor dry flies in summer and nymphing rigs in late season.

## 2. South Fork of the Snake River, Idaho, USA

The South Fork Snake is one of the most productive wild trout rivers in the American West, supporting a dense population of large native cutthroat and brown trout in a corridor of cottonwood-lined banks and braided side channels. Float camping — drifting the river in a raft or drift boat over multiple days and camping on the gravel bars — is the classic way to experience it. Several outfitters operate guided float camps, but self-guided float camping is permitted with the appropriate Idaho float camp permit. The river holds significant hatches of pale morning dun, green drake, and trico mayflies through summer and autumn.

## 3. Tongariro River, New Zealand

The Tongariro, flowing north from the volcanic plateau of the Central North Island into Lake Taupo, is among the most celebrated trout rivers in the Southern Hemisphere. It holds trophy-sized rainbow trout that run from the lake to spawn, and the Tongariro National Park surrounds the river with protected wilderness camping access. Several Department of Conservation campgrounds and hut-based camping options sit within walking distance of productive pools. New Zealand has strict biosecurity rules around wading equipment — all boots and waders must be dried and cleaned thoroughly before entering the country to prevent the spread of didymo algae.

## 4. Rio Limay, Patagonia, Argentina

Patagonia holds some of the finest wild brown and rainbow trout rivers on earth, and the Rio Limay below Nahuel Huapi Lake in Neuquen Province is among the best. The river is large, clear, and intensely cold, running through a landscape of steppe grassland and low shrub under the vast Patagonian sky. Wild camping along the Limay is possible in undeveloped sections, and the region has established estancias and basic campgrounds that provide riverside access. Fly fishing in Argentine Patagonia requires a provincial fishing licence, which is obtainable locally. The season runs from November to April, corresponding to the Southern Hemisphere summer.

## 5. Upper Kennet River, England

For European fly anglers, the chalk streams of southern England represent a singular fishing environment. The upper Kennet in Wiltshire is one of the finest chalkstream trout rivers in the country, with clear, spring-fed water flowing through managed meadows and ancient watercress beds. Camping near the Kennet requires booking private riverside campgrounds or farms, as wild camping is not permitted in England. The fishing is predominantly dry fly on the surface to rising fish — a tradition codified in Victorian angling writing and still observed on many Kennet beats. The season runs from April to September.

## 6. Orkla River, Norway

The Orkla in central Norway is one of the country's top Atlantic salmon and sea trout rivers, running from the highlands of Trollheimen to the Trondheim fjord. Norway's allemannsretten (right to roam) law permits wild camping in uncultivated land, including riverbanks, making tent camping along the Orkla straightforward and legal. Salmon fishing requires a Norwegian fishing licence and a beat licence (sold by local landowners and fishing associations). The salmon season runs from June through August, when long Arctic summer days allow fishing well into the evening. The river also holds resident brown trout fishable on a day licence throughout the season.

## 7. Rio Baker, Chilean Patagonia

The Baker is the largest river in Chile by volume, draining the Northern Patagonian Ice Field through a narrow valley of blue-green glacial water. Its wild rainbow and brown trout are remarkably large — fish exceeding 5 kg are not unusual. The Rio Baker corridor has extremely limited road access, and reaching the best camping and fishing requires multi-day trekking or boat access. The Carretera Austral passes through the region and provides access to the Cochrane area, where several basic campgrounds exist near the river mouth and lower sections.

## 8. Beaverhead River, Montana, USA

The Beaverhead in southwestern Montana is a tailwater fishery below Clark Canyon Dam, maintaining cold, clear flows through summer when many other rivers run low and warm. The fish population is dense — scientific surveys have documented exceptionally high trout biomass per kilometre. BLM land along the river corridor permits camping in several areas, and the river is floatable by drift boat or raft. The Beaverhead fishes best with small nymph patterns imitating scuds, sow bugs, and midge larvae, all of which thrive in the rich tailwater environment.

## Planning Your Fly Fishing Camping Trip

Successful fly fishing camping trips require planning across two overlapping domains: understanding the fishing conditions and regulations for your chosen water, and securing appropriate camping access. Many of the best trout rivers in the US are within national forests or on BLM land, where dispersed camping is available within guidelines — [check the map](/map) to identify campgrounds and public land access points near major fly fishing rivers. Regulations vary dramatically: New Zealand requires decontamination of wading gear, European chalk streams require beat licences, and many Western US rivers have special regulations restricting bait and fly patterns in certain sections. Check with the managing agency for each specific water before you arrive.

A camp near a prime fishing river transforms the experience. You fish the dawn hatch before the hatch-and-run day anglers arrive, you can rest during the midday slow period and return to the water in the evening when large fish become active in the shallows. The unhurried rhythm of a riverside camp — coffee before first light, time to study the water between sessions, the luxury of drying out gear and retying leaders at the tailgate — is as much a part of the fly fishing camping experience as the fishing itself.
