Lapland — the vast subarctic region stretching across the northernmost parts of Finland, Sweden, Norway, and the Kola Peninsula of Russia — is one of the world's premier wilderness camping destinations. In summer, the midnight sun illuminates a landscape of birch-scrub tundra, glacier-carved lake systems, and remote fell tops for twenty-four hours a day. In winter, the same landscape sleeps under snow while the aurora borealis flickers and coils overhead. The region is vast and the infrastructure, though good along marked routes, thins rapidly beyond established trails.

A key legal advantage for campers in Scandinavia is the right of public access — allemansrätten in Sweden and jokamiehenoikeus in Finland — which permits camping on uncultivated land for up to two nights in most places without landowner permission. This transforms the Lapland camping experience fundamentally: you are not restricted to designated sites but can pitch a tent on a riverbank, a fell top, or beside any of the thousands of lakes that dot the landscape. The Sami indigenous people are the traditional reindeer herders of the region, and respecting their land and livestock is part of responsible Lapland camping. ## 1. Kungsleden (King's Trail), Swedish Lapland

The Kungsleden is Sweden's most famous long-distance trail, running approximately 440 kilometres from Abisko in the far north to Hemavan in the south. The northern section between Abisko and Kebnekaise is the most dramatic — a high alpine corridor of glaciated massifs, deep valleys, and pristine lakes. The Swedish Tourist Association (STF) maintains a chain of mountain huts spaced a day's walk apart, with camping areas adjacent to each hut. Hikers can thus choose between hut nights and tent nights, or mix them freely. The Kebnekaise summit, Sweden's highest peak, is a day hike from the Kebnekaise fjällstation hut and one of the finest high points in Scandinavia.

## 2. Abisko National Park, Norrbotten, Sweden

Abisko is the driest national park in Sweden by virtue of a rain shadow created by the Norwegian mountains to the west — a meteorological quirk that gives it more reliable clear skies than almost anywhere else in Lapland. The Aurora Sky Station cable car, operational in winter, has made Abisko internationally famous for northern lights viewing, but the summer camping experience is equally rewarding. The Abisko Nature Trail circles the national park through birch forest and along the shore of Lake Torneträsk. The Aurora Sky Station sits at 900 metres and provides orientation for the surrounding fell terrain.

## 3. Sarek National Park, Norrbotten, Sweden

Sarek is one of Europe's last true wildernesses — a national park of some 2,000 square kilometres with no maintained trails, no huts, no mobile phone coverage, and no visitor infrastructure of any kind. Accessing Sarek means crossing unbridged rivers (often requiring wading in glacial meltwater), navigating by map and compass across trackless fell terrain, and carrying everything you need for the duration of your stay. The reward is a landscape of extraordinary glaciated beauty — the park contains approximately 200 glaciers and dozens of high peaks — experienced in complete solitude. Only experienced and well-equipped wilderness campers should attempt Sarek.

## 4. Urho Kekkonen National Park, Finnish Lapland

Finland's largest national park covers a vast area of fell tundra, spruce and pine forest, and braided river systems in the municipality of Saariselkä in Finnish Lapland. The park has an extensive hut network maintained by Metsähallitus (Finland's national parks authority), ranging from day huts with basic facilities to larger overnight huts. Camping beside the huts or in the open wilderness is permitted throughout. The park is a stronghold for wolverine, brown bear, grey wolf, and Arctic fox. In late March, the park is popular for ski touring as the polar night lifts and daylight returns.

## 5. Kilpisjärvi Wilderness, Finnish Lapland

The Kilpisjärvi area in the extreme northwest of Finland, where Finland, Sweden, and Norway meet at the tripoint marker of Treriksröset, is one of the most remote and pristine wilderness zones in Scandinavia. The fell terrain around Lake Kilpisjärvi is above the treeline, and the open views to the Norwegian mountains across the Swedish border are immense. The Kilpisjärvi Biological Station provides weather information and basic services. The route to the Treriksröset tripoint marker is a long day walk over open fell — camping at the lakeside along the route is a classic Lapland experience. Reindeer are ubiquitous throughout the area.

## 6. Jotunheimen National Park, Norway

Jotunheimen — "home of the giants" — in central Norway contains Norway's highest mountains, including Galdhøpiggen (2,469 m) and Glittertind. While technically south of the Arctic Circle and thus on the edge of Lapland territory, Jotunheimen is integral to any serious Scandinavian camping itinerary and its feel is distinctly high-alpine subarctic. The Norwegian Trekking Association (DNT) hut network provides a backbone for multi-day camping and hut-to-hut routes. The Besseggen ridge walk between Gjende and Bessvatnet lakes is one of Norway's most famous day hikes, and the surrounding terrain offers weeks of camping exploration.

## 7. Øvre Dividal National Park, Troms, Norway

Dividal is a Norwegian national park on the Finnish border north of the Arctic Circle, protecting a pristine combination of spruce forest, birch scrub, fell terrain, and river valleys associated with the Divielva watershed. The park is one of Scandinavia's last intact wolf territories and a stronghold for wolverine and Arctic fox. A network of DNT cabins and open shelters supports multi-day winter ski tours and summer trekking. The park sees very few international visitors, and the trail quality is accordingly maintained by predominantly Norwegian users who value the solitude. The town of Øverbygd near Bardufoss provides the most convenient access.

## 8. Lemmenjokiluonnonpuisto (Lemmenjoki National Park), Finnish Lapland

Lemmenjoki is Finland's largest national park and one of the most remote, covering a vast area of pine and spruce taiga and open fell along the Lemmenjoki River valley in the Inari region. The park has been the site of gold prospecting since the nineteenth century, and the Kultaharju gold panning trail through the park passes active gold claims alongside wilderness river paddling routes. The remote hut network requires significant walking time between points. The gold prospectors' culture, Sami reindeer grazing rights, and pristine boreal wilderness combine to create one of the most characterful camping landscapes in all of Lapland.

## 9. Nordkapp Area, Finnmark, Norway

The North Cape — Nordkapp — is the northernmost point in Europe accessible by road, sitting at 71 degrees north latitude on the island of Magerøya. The plateau above the cliffs is one of the most striking camping locations in the Arctic, with the midnight sun circling the sky from mid-May to late July without setting. Wild camping on the plateau (outside the North Cape park itself) is legal and practiced by cycle tourers completing the long ride from southern Europe. The landscape is treeless Arctic tundra, frequently swept by wind and fog, and the experience of camping at the edge of the continent looking north across open ocean is compelling for that reason.

## 10. Pallas-Yllästunturi National Park, Finnish Lapland

This long, narrow national park in western Finnish Lapland connects the Pallas and Ylläs fell areas with a hiking trail corridor through birch forest and open fell. The Pallas-Hetta fell route is one of Finland's most popular multi-day treks, with a series of Metsähallitus wilderness huts at regular intervals. The route is accessible to fit hikers without technical experience and is suitable as a first introduction to Lapland camping. The Pallastunturi fell massif itself has a distinctive atmosphere — the scattered summits are viewpoints across a landscape of lakes and forest extending to the horizon in every direction. Arctic hares, ptarmigan, and occasional golden eagles are regular companions on the higher sections.
