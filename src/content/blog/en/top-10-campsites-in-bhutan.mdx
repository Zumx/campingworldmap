Bhutan manages its tourism under a high-value, low-impact policy — all visitors must book through a licensed Bhutanese tour operator and pay a daily Sustainable Development Fee. This framework shapes camping fundamentally: independent camping without a licensed operator is not permitted, and all camping is conducted through organised trekking packages that include camping gear, cook staff, and horsemen to carry equipment. The result is a highly managed but extraordinarily pristine wilderness camping experience, with trails and campsites receiving a fraction of the traffic that equivalent Himalayan routes in Nepal experience.

## 1. Jomolhari Base Camp, Thimphu District

The first major objective on the Jomolhari Trek — arguably Bhutan's most famous multi-day route — the base camp sits at 4,080 metres with a direct view of Jomolhari (7,326 m) rising above the valley head. The camp is an established trekking site with flat meadow pitches used by the organised groups that form the standard trekking model in Bhutan. The approach from Paro takes two days; the base camp itself is used for one or two acclimatisation nights before continuing over the Nyile La pass.

## 2. Somji Camp, Jomolhari Trek

At 4,090 metres on the Jomolhari Trek's second leg, Somji provides a distinctive Himalayan meadow camping setting. The surrounding terrain — high pastures used by yak herders in summer — is characteristic of the upper valleys of western Bhutan. Views back toward Jichu Drake and across the plateau to the north.

## 3. Tshokam Base Camp, Gasa District

On the approach to the Snowman Trek's northern section, Tshokam camp sits close to 4,900 metres in the Lunana region. The Snowman Trek is considered one of the most demanding multi-week treks in the world — 25 days, crossing eleven passes above 5,000 metres. Tshokam is representative of the high-Snowman camps: sparse grass, cold nights, and views that reward the approach difficulty.

## 4. Sharna Zampa Camp, Jomolhari Trek

In the lower Jomolhari valley on the descent leg, Sharna Zampa is a lower-altitude camp (approximately 3,600 metres) set beside a river in mixed rhododendron and conifer forest. The contrast with the high-altitude camps above is marked; bird diversity is high here, and the temperature several degrees warmer.

## 5. Chamgang Campsite, Thimphu Valley

A relatively accessible campsite in the hills above Thimphu, used for short overnight trips that do not require the full trekking permit infrastructure. Chamgang is the most commonly used site for visitors with limited time who want to experience Bhutanese camping without committing to a multi-day trek. Forest setting, good views over the valley, and day-accessible from the capital.

## 6. Camp at Rodung La Approach, Bumthang

The approach to the Bumthang Cultural Trek crosses terrain between the Chokor Valley and the Tang Valley at altitudes around 3,900 metres. The camps on this route sit in high pastures and spruce forest, with the Bumthang region's famous cheese dairies reachable by day trip from lower camps.

## 7. Laya Camp, Gasa District

The village of Laya at 3,800 metres is the largest Himalayan community accessible on the standard Jomolhari circuit. The Laya villagers maintain a distinctive culture including traditional women's headwear unique to the region. The camp adjacent to Laya is set on open ground with views toward the Gasa Dzong. This is as far as most trekkers going the Jomolhari route progress before descending via Gasa.

## 8. Camp 4 Lingschi, Gasa District

On the Snowman Trek's approach to the Lunana plateau, Lingschi camp is positioned at approximately 4,200 metres in the transition zone between the temperate valley forests and the high alpine. The village of Lingschi — a small community of herders — is nearby, and the dzong ruins above the valley are visible from camp.

## 9. Ka-tay Thang Camp, Lunana

Deep in the Lunana region on the Snowman Trek, Ka-tay Thang is one of the high-altitude stages at approximately 4,600 metres. The Lunana plateau is one of the most remote inhabited regions in Asia; the villages here — Thanza, Woche, Lhedi — are accessible only on foot or by helicopter. Camping here requires full commitment to the Snowman logistics.

## 10. Camp at Bumdeling Wildlife Sanctuary, Trashiyangtse

In eastern Bhutan, the Bumdeling sanctuary holds black-necked crane wintering grounds. The sanctuary camps in the Kuri Chhu valley serve as bases for wildlife observation. The eastern region receives fewer visitors than the Paro-Thimphu-Punakha corridor and offers a different vegetation profile — subtropical forests at lower elevations, montane oak above.

## Logistics for camping in Bhutan

All trekking must be booked through a licensed Bhutanese tour operator. The Sustainable Development Fee (SDF) covers accommodation, guide, and most meals — trekking packages incorporate camping equipment provided by the operator. Independent overnight camping is not permitted. The main trekking season runs March through May and September through November; monsoon season (June through August) makes high passes difficult or impassable.
