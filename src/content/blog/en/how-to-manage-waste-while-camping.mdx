Waste management is one of the least glamorous topics in camping and one of the most consequential for the long-term health of camping landscapes. The damage caused by poor waste management is cumulative and slow to reverse: contaminated water sources, wildlife attracted to human food waste, degraded soils, and a steady erosion of the quality that makes natural areas worth visiting. The rules governing waste in backcountry environments are not bureaucratic box-checking — they reflect what happens to landscapes that receive thousands of human visitors without adequate waste discipline.

## The Leave No Trace Framework

Leave No Trace (LNT) is a set of principles developed through collaboration between the US Forest Service, National Park Service, and Bureau of Land Management and subsequently adopted by outdoor education organizations globally. The seven principles provide a practical framework, with waste management addressed primarily under Principle 3 (Dispose of Waste Properly). The goal is to leave natural environments in a condition that the next visitor cannot tell you were there, and that wildlife and ecological processes are not disrupted.

The principles are not uniformly applicable across all environments. What is appropriate waste management in a desert environment differs from a temperate forest, which differs from above-treeline alpine terrain, which differs from an established campground with bin infrastructure. Understanding the reasoning behind each practice allows you to adapt intelligently to specific conditions rather than applying rules mechanically.

## Human Waste in the Backcountry

The cat hole method is the standard technique for disposing of human solid waste in backcountry environments. Dig a hole 15 to 20 centimetres deep and at least 60 metres from water sources, trails, and campsites. After use, bury the waste and cover with natural material. The soil microbiome at that depth decomposes waste effectively under normal conditions. Cat holes should be dispersed rather than concentrated — use different spots rather than a designated latrine area, which creates a heavily contaminated zone.

Used toilet paper should be packed out in most land management guidelines. Buried toilet paper decomposes far more slowly than the waste it covers and is frequently dug up by wildlife. A small ziplock bag dedicated to used paper is the simplest solution; odour-proof bags (Loksak, Uline, or similar) contain smell effectively for multi-day trips.

In areas where cat holes are not appropriate — above treeline with shallow soil and slow decomposition, heavily used alpine routes like the Mount Whitney Trail in California or the Muir Trail, popular canyon zones including parts of the Grand Canyon — pack-out waste systems are required. WAG bags (Waste Aggregation and Gelling) provide a sealed, biodegradable container for human waste that is packed out and disposed of in approved locations. WAG bags are available at most outdoor retailers and are required by land management agencies in several specific areas.

Greywater from washing dishes and yourself requires similar dispersal care. Food particles in dishwater attract wildlife; strain all dishwater through a fine mesh before dispersal and pack out the solids. Do not wash directly in streams or lakes — soap, even biodegradable soap, affects aquatic ecosystems. Wash at least 60 metres from water sources and broadcast greywater over a wide area rather than concentrating it.

## Food Waste and Wildlife

Food waste is both a waste management problem and a wildlife interaction problem. Improperly stored or discarded food waste conditions wildlife to associate human presence with food, which leads to the habituation and subsequent destruction of individual animals and significant management problems for land agencies.

Pack out all food waste without exception. This includes fruit cores, shells, and rinds — "natural" food waste still attracts wildlife and takes longer to decompose than most campers assume in the actual conditions of a campsite. An apple core persists for weeks to months depending on climate and soil conditions, and during that time it serves as an attractant.

Cooking fires should not be used to dispose of food waste. Incompletely burned food scraps remain attractive to wildlife, and even well-burned food residue in an ash pile creates scent that draws animal investigation.

## Rubbish and Packaging

The pack-it-in, pack-it-out principle is straightforward: every manufactured item you carry into the backcountry comes back out. This includes all food packaging, fuel canisters, used batteries, first aid packaging, and any item you brought in that is no longer useful. Leaving behind "minimal" litter with the assumption that other hikers will clean it up is a collective action failure that degrades commonly enjoyed spaces.

Pre-trip repackaging reduces in-camp rubbish volume: remove unnecessary packaging at home, transfer food into reusable bags or containers, and calculate quantities so you are not carrying large amounts of packaging waste for food you do not eat. Lighter rubbish is also easier to carry out.

Established campgrounds with bin systems make the logistics simple. Use the provided bins, separate recyclables where infrastructure exists, and do not assume overflow bins will be emptied before wildlife gets into them. Secure your rubbish in your vehicle or in campground animal-proof infrastructure overnight rather than leaving bags accessible.

## Greywater in Established Campgrounds

RV and car camping produces significantly more greywater — from camp showers, dishwashing, and food preparation — than tent camping. Established campgrounds with dump stations provide appropriate disposal for black water (from chemical toilets) and greywater systems from RV tanks. Use these facilities rather than dispersing greywater into camp drainage.

Temporary camp shower arrangements and washing stations in campgrounds without formal greywater disposal require the same 60-metre dispersal approach as backcountry washing: away from water sources and broadcast broadly. Use the minimum amount of soap necessary for the task.

## Waste Considerations in International Camping

Waste infrastructure and regulation varies enormously between countries. In some national parks in developing regions, bin systems are absent or unreliable. Carrying adequate waste bags for the entire duration of any international backcountry trip is safer than assuming infrastructure will be available. In agricultural areas, burning food waste may be locally normal but is still not recommended near campsites because of wildlife attraction.

Research the waste management practices of specific countries and parks before departure. What is legally and ecologically appropriate in one jurisdiction may be harmful or illegal in another.
