Camping in Switzerland is expensive by European standards — Swiss campground prices reflect the country's general cost structure — but the value proposition is strong: you sleep within view of the Alps' highest and most dramatic peaks, with access to a trail network that is arguably the best maintained in the world. Swiss campgrounds are also uniformly well-organised, clean, and positioned to serve as serious hiking and mountain biking basecamps. The Bernese Oberland between Interlaken and Grindelwald, the Valais around Zermatt, and the Graubünden (Grisons) around the Engadine and Davos offer fundamentally different Alpine characters — the former with its north-facing glacier views, the latter with a higher, drier, and sunnier climate more typical of the inner Alpine zone.

## 1. TCS Camping Jungfrau, Lauterbrunnen, Bernese Oberland

Lauterbrunnen is one of the great Alpine valley settings — a U-shaped glacially carved trough with 72 waterfalls cascading from the overhanging valley walls, including the Staubbachfall, which falls 297 metres in a free-hanging sheet and was celebrated by Goethe and Schiller in the 18th century. TCS Camping Jungfrau is in the valley floor with views up to the Jungfrau massif and the Eiger north face above the village of Kleine Scheidegg. Trains from Lauterbrunnen connect to Wengen and Mürren (both car-free) and to the Jungfraujoch station at 3,454 metres, the highest railway station in Europe. The campsite is large and well-equipped and among the best-positioned in the Alps by any measure.

## 2. Camping Manor Farm, Interlaken, Bernese Oberland

Interlaken sits between Lake Thun to the west and Lake Brienz to the east, with the Jungfrau above and serves as the tourism hub of the Bernese Oberland. Camping Manor Farm on the eastern shore of Lake Thun is a large, high-quality site with lake access for swimming and kayaking, and views across the water to the Niesen pyramid. The campsite is a 10-minute cycle from Interlaken's train station and the region's extensive rack railway network to mountain villages. Paragliding from Beatenberg above the lake to a landing field near the campsite is one of the most popular tandem paragliding routes in Switzerland.

## 3. Camping Alphubel, Saas-Fee, Valais

Saas-Fee is a car-free village in a high valley above Visp, surrounded by 13 four-thousanders — peaks exceeding 4,000 metres — more high summits than any other Swiss village can claim. The campsite is below the village boundary, accessed by the electric shuttle that serves the car-park at the valley entrance. Saas-Fee's own glacier, the Feegletscher, is retreating significantly but still dominates the view from the village level. The Saas valley's Hannig and Spielboden cable cars access terrain at 3,000 metres where marmots are common enough to approach closely. The campsite serves hikers on the Haute Route Pédestre connecting Saas-Fee to Zermatt over the Augstbordpass.

## 4. Camping Attermenzen, Randa, Valais

Randa is the valley village below Zermatt on the Mattertal road, at the point where the valley dramatically narrows. Camping Attermenzen allows campers to base themselves in the Zermatt valley without Zermatt prices — the village is connected to Zermatt by the Matterhorn Gotthard Bahn narrow-gauge railway in about 15 minutes. The Randa rockslide debris above the campsite is a landmark — in April 1991 a landslide of approximately 30 million cubic metres blocked the Matter Vispa river and created a temporary lake. The Europa-hütte trail above Randa provides some of the finest Matterhorn views available on foot, at eye level with the peak rather than looking up from the village.

## 5. Camping Lac des Dix, Hérémence, Valais

The Grande Dixence dam, completed in 1965, created the Lac des Dix reservoir in the Val des Dix, the highest point reached by any Swiss road. The dam wall is 285 metres high — the tallest gravity dam in the world at the time of construction. Camping near the reservoir at an elevation above 2,000 metres is genuinely high alpine, with cool nights even in July and views of the glaciers feeding the reservoir from the Pigne d'Arolla and surrounding peaks. The Chemin de la Dixence trail follows the pipeline from the reservoir down to Sion through extraordinary terrain. This is an unusual campsite choice that rewards those interested in the engineering history of the Alps.

## 6. Camping Äschried, Grindelwald, Bernese Oberland

Grindelwald is the most accessible Bernese Oberland resort, road-connected (unlike Wengen and Mürren) and positioned below the north faces of the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau. Camping Äschried sits on the valley slope with elevated views of the Eiger north face from the pitches — a theatrical backdrop that loses none of its impact regardless of how many times you have seen it in photographs. The Eiger Trail from Alpiglen to Grindelwald passes below the north face close enough to hear rockfall and ice calving. The First and Männlichen cable cars above the village access the high pasture trails of the Bachalpsee and the Kleine Scheidegg panorama walk.

## 7. Camping Morteratsch, Pontresina, Engadine, Graubünden

The Morteratsch glacier in the Bernina group east of St. Moritz is one of the most accessible glaciers in Switzerland, with a marked path leading from the Morteratsch railway station along the glacier's former extent — a series of dated posts showing the glacier's retreat over 150 years. The campsite near the train halt is directly below the glacier's lower moraine, with the Piz Palü and Piz Bernina summits visible above. Pontresina, the larger village, is two kilometres down the valley with shops and restaurants. The Diavolezza cable car ascent above the glacier is one of the finest high-mountain panoramas in the eastern Alps. The campsite is one of the best in the Engadine for backcountry mountaineering access.

## 8. Camping Zu den Drei Seen, Sufers, Graubünden

Sufers is a small village in the Rheinwald valley of southern Graubünden, in the headwaters of the Rhine above the Splügen pass. The campsite takes its name from the three reservoirs visible from the valley — the Sufers, Rossa, and Zapporthorn lakes — created by hydroelectric development in the early 20th century. The San Bernardino pass to the west and the Splügen pass to the south were both historically important Alpine crossings used by Roman legions and medieval traders, and the valley preserves several historic bridge and road sections. The Zapport Glacier at the valley head supplies water to the reservoir system and is accessible on foot in a full day.

## 9. Camping Aaregg, Brienz, Bernese Oberland

Brienz is the village at the eastern end of Lake Brienz, less visited than Interlaken but with its own substantial attractions: the Rothorn rack railway — one of the last steam-operated rack railways in Switzerland — climbs to 2,350 metres above the village with views over the lake and the Jungfrau group. The Ballenberg Open-Air Museum of Swiss rural culture is two kilometres from the village and contains over 100 authentic historic buildings relocated from across Switzerland and maintained with traditional craft demonstrations. The campsite on the lakefront has direct lake swimming access and the lake's deep, cold water is strikingly blue-green.

## 10. Camping Lac de Salanfe, Saint-Maurice, Valais

Camping Lac de Salanfe sits at 1,925 metres on a plateau above the Rhône valley near the town of Saint-Maurice, accessible via a jeep track from Van in the Salanfe valley. The plateau's reservoir and the surrounding limestone mountains form a high alpine environment without the infrastructure of the more famous Valais resorts. The Dents du Midi massif above the plateau has a classic seven-summit ridge traverse that is a serious mountaineering objective. The campsite is basic and rarely crowded, attracting primarily Swiss and French alpine walkers. Sunrise on the Dents du Midi from the plateau is one of the finer unheralded mountain spectacles in the western Alps.
