Dispersed camping — camping outside of designated campgrounds, directly on public land — is one of the most underused privileges available to Americans and visitors to the United States. On most national forest land and vast stretches of Bureau of Land Management territory, you can legally drive or hike to a spot that appeals to you, set up a tent or park a vehicle, and camp for free, typically for up to 14 consecutive days in any one location. The result, when done responsibly, is a level of solitude, scenery, and personal freedom that no developed campground can match.

The rules governing dispersed camping vary by land management unit. Some national forests require you to be at least 150 feet from any water source, trail, or road. Others have specific areas designated for dispersed camping with defined setback requirements. A minority of high-use areas require a fire permit or prohibit campfires altogether during fire season. Before heading out, it is always worth downloading the relevant forest map, checking the specific ranger district's dispersed camping rules, and confirming whether a free campfire permit is required in that jurisdiction. With that groundwork done, the options across the American West, Southeast, and beyond are extraordinary.

## Coconino National Forest, Arizona

Surrounding Flagstaff in northern Arizona, Coconino National Forest encompasses ponderosa pine forest at elevation, red rock canyon country around Sedona, and the dramatic volcanic landscapes of the San Francisco Peaks. Dispersed camping is available throughout most of the forest, with the popular areas around Flagstaff drawing weekend visitors and the more remote sections offering genuine solitude. FR 418 south of Flagstaff and the forest roads around Mormon Lake are particularly popular for car camping dispersed sites. Elevation keeps summer temperatures manageable — Flagstaff sits at 2,100 metres — making this a viable desert-adjacent camping destination even in July and August.

## Kaibab National Forest, Arizona

The Kaibab surrounds Grand Canyon National Park on the North and South Rims, and dispersed camping within the forest is a practical way to base yourself for Grand Canyon day hikes without competing for the limited inside-park campground reservations. The forest road network on the North Rim provides access to dispersed sites with enormous views across the Kaibab Plateau. Elk are common and pronghorn antelope graze the open meadows. The North Rim section of the Kaibab is only accessible from mid-May to mid-October due to snow closure.

## Gunnison National Forest, Colorado

Colorado's Gunnison National Forest encompasses the valleys around Gunnison and Crested Butte, including some of the highest terrain in the state. The Taylor River Road and the forest roads radiating from Almont into the Gunnison River drainage provide access to dispersed camping in exceptional alpine scenery. The Taylor River itself is a blue-ribbon trout fishery. At higher elevations, dispersed camping above treeline (following the relevant rules outlined separately in this site's alpine camping guide) is possible along the jeep roads accessing the high basins around Crested Butte.

## Dixie National Forest, Utah

Utah's largest national forest stretches across the plateau country south of Bryce Canyon and Capitol Reef, providing a dispersed camping base for exploring southern Utah's national park corridor without paying for or reserving developed campgrounds. The forest roads around the Escalante River drainage and along the Aquarius Plateau access remote canyon country with minimal visitor pressure. Red Canyon, a section of the Dixie NF just west of Bryce Canyon, has well-used dispersed sites accessible via highway pullouts and short forest roads.

## Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest, Washington

In the North Cascades region of Washington State, the Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest provides dispersed camping access in dramatic alpine terrain. The Methow Valley side of the forest, accessible from Winthrop, has a network of forest roads leading to dispersed sites in open pine forest with views of the North Cascades peaks. The Entiat River drainage and the Lake Chelan-Sawtooth Wilderness area offer more remote dispersed camping options for backpackers.

## Humboldt-Toiyabe National Forest, Nevada

The largest national forest in the contiguous United States, the Humboldt-Toiyabe spans central Nevada and eastern California and is almost entirely open for dispersed camping. The Spring Mountains near Las Vegas (Mount Charleston area) provide accessible cool-climate dispersed camping for southern Nevada. The Ruby Mountains in northeastern Nevada are geologically unusual — a range of glacially carved peaks rising abruptly from the Great Basin desert — and offer extraordinary dispersed camping amid alpine lakes and wilderness quality terrain with relatively few visitors.

## BLM Land in the Moab Area, Utah

The BLM-managed land surrounding Moab is arguably the most iconic dispersed camping landscape in the United States. The canyon country around Canyonlands National Park and Arches National Park, managed by BLM, contains designated dispersed camping areas along roads like the Lockhart Basin Road, the Gemini Bridges Road, and the long-haul Canyonlands by Night overlook access roads. Dispersed camping here means sleeping beneath canyon walls and sandstone formations of extraordinary beauty. Note that some specific areas have been redesignated as permit-only or fee areas in recent years; check with the Moab BLM Field Office for current rules.

## Ouachita National Forest, Arkansas

Dispersed camping in the Ouachita National Forest of western Arkansas and eastern Oklahoma is less well-known nationally but provides excellent access to Ouachita Trail hiking and outstanding spring and autumn scenery in the deciduous hardwood forests. The forest road network is extensive, and most of it is accessible for dispersed camping outside of developed recreation areas. Spring brings wildflower displays in the lower elevations; autumn colour peaks in October and November.

## Allegheny National Forest, Pennsylvania

In the northeastern United States, the Allegheny National Forest in northwestern Pennsylvania offers dispersed camping across most of its area, with particular access along the Allegheny Reservoir shoreline and the forest roads of the Marienville and Bradford Ranger Districts. The forest is largely second-growth hardwood with a long timber history, but the river and stream corridors are productive for trout fishing and the trail network supports multi-day backpacking.

## Finding Dispersed Camping Sites

The practical challenge with dispersed camping is not legality — most public land allows it — but finding specific locations where road access, terrain, and site quality combine to make a comfortable camp. [Open the map](/map) to explore national forests and BLM land across the United States, identify the road networks that provide access, and plan your route before heading into areas without reliable cell coverage. Download offline maps for your destination; dispersed camping areas by definition lack reliable internet, and a downloaded forest map on your phone or a physical MVUM (Motor Vehicle Use Map, available free from ranger districts) is essential for navigating forest road networks confidently.

The 14-day rule applies broadly across both national forests and BLM land: you may not camp in the same location for more than 14 consecutive days. After 14 days, you must move at least 40 kilometres from your previous campsite before you can camp again. This rule is designed to prevent resource damage from long-term occupation and to ensure that public land remains accessible to everyone.
