The Appalachian Trail runs 2,190 miles from Springer Mountain in Georgia to Mount Katahdin in Maine, and approximately every eight to twelve miles along its length — sometimes more frequently, sometimes further apart — there is a three-sided wooden lean-to or enclosed shelter where hikers can sleep off the ground, out of the rain, and in varying degrees of communal company. The shelter system is one of the defining structural features of the AT experience, different from Western trails like the Pacific Crest Trail and Continental Divide Trail where designated shelters are rare and tenting is the default. Understanding how the system works, where the shelters are, and when to rely on them versus your own tent shapes how you plan and experience a thru-hike or section hike.

## Who Maintains the Shelters

The AT is managed by the Appalachian Trail Conservancy (ATC), which coordinates 30 local trail-maintaining clubs along the trail's length. Each club is responsible for maintaining shelters, privies, water sources, and trail tread in its section. This distributed maintenance model means shelter quality varies significantly — a recently renovated shelter in a club with strong membership and active volunteers may have a new roof, fresh paint, a swept floor, and a detailed trail register; a shelter in a neglected section may have a leaking roof, missing mouse guards, and a partially collapsed privy.

The ATC publishes the Official A.T. Guide series, updated annually, which lists every shelter with its mileage, water source, capacity, and privy status. The AWOL Guide and the ATC's Companion are the two most widely used physical guides among thru-hikers; the Guthook/FarOut app provides the same information in smartphone format and allows real-time comments from recent hikers about water source status and shelter conditions.

## Shelter Design Variations

The majority of AT shelters are open-fronted three-sided structures — Adirondack-style lean-tos — with a sleeping platform raised off the ground, a sloped roof, and an open front facing the camping area. A typical shelter sleeps 8 to 12 hikers on the platform. Some older shelters have a second-floor sleeping loft accessed by a ladder. The open-front design provides ventilation and the ability to watch weather approach, at the cost of any protection from wind-driven rain entering from the front.

A smaller number of enclosed shelters exist, particularly in Maine and the White Mountains of New Hampshire, where weather conditions are more extreme and enclosed structures offer meaningful additional protection. The AMC (Appalachian Mountain Club) huts in the White Mountains are a distinct system — staffed, full-service mountain huts that serve cooked meals and sleep hikers in bunk rooms. They charge significantly for the service and represent a different end of the spectrum from trail shelters.

## The Mouse Problem

The most consistent complaint about AT shelters from hikers who have spent time on the trail is mice. Shelter structures — particularly older ones with poor sealing — harbour populations of white-footed mice that emerge at night and are experienced as relentlessly bold. Mice chew through pack straps, stuff sacks, and tent fabric to reach food. The solution is to hang food from the mouse guards provided at most shelters: metal baffles or cable systems that suspend a rope from the ceiling, on which food bags can be tied in a position the mice cannot reach. Some hikers use rodent-resistant bags.

Beyond food, mice on sleeping platforms can run across sleeping hikers during the night with some frequency at high-use shelters. Some hikers prefer to tent rather than shelter stay in high-rodent locations — a decision that is always legitimate since shelters are first-come, first-served with no reservation system (except in a few specific zones) and tent camping beside the shelter is always an option.

## Shelter Etiquette and Social Dynamics

AT shelters concentrate hikers in a way that frontcountry campgrounds do not. On a busy weekend in May or October — the peak southbound and northbound bubble seasons — a shelter may fill its platform capacity by early evening. The social norms that have evolved around this concentration are part of trail culture: you do not take up more than your fair share of platform space; if the shelter is full, you tent rather than claim a platform spot; experienced hikers leave space for a newcomer arriving in the dark; headlamps pointed directly at sleeping hikers are considered poor form.

Shelter logbooks are a trail institution. Maintained in most shelters as a spiral notebook or composition book, they record hikers' names, trail names, dates, and whatever observations they choose to leave. For thru-hikers, reading the logs is a way of tracking the position of hikers met weeks earlier and of gauging trail conditions ahead. Some shelter logs have been kept continuously for decades and serve as a historical record of who passed through.

## When Tenting Makes More Sense

Carrying a tent or shelter even if you intend to use AT shelters routinely is essential for most serious hikers. Shelters fill, particularly on weekends and in the mid-Atlantic states (Virginia, Maryland, Pennsylvania) which see high weekend use from Washington, Baltimore, and Philadelphia day hikers and section hikers. Weather-related and availability-related contingencies make a tent an insurance policy that is worth the weight.

Tenting is also preferable in many conditions where shelters are technically available: if the shelter is occupied by a group snoring loudly, if the weather is clear and cool and the outdoors more appealing, or if a designated tenting site near the shelter has better tree coverage than the shelter itself. The AT's shelter system works best as a default plan with tenting as the flexible backup.

## Reservations: Where and When Required

Most AT shelters operate on a first-come, first-served basis with no advance reservation possible. Exceptions exist at several high-use sections. The Great Smoky Mountains National Park requires all overnight backcountry campers — including AT thru-hikers — to make advance reservations through the park's online system and pay a nightly fee. Permits are limited by campsite capacity. Baxter State Park in Maine, the location of Katahdin, restricts camping in the park and requires thru-hikers to make reservations for their Katahdin summit and final campsite day.

## Water Sources at Shelters

Every AT shelter is listed with its associated water source, which may be a spring, stream, or seep anywhere from immediately adjacent to a kilometre away. Water quality on the AT requires filtration or treatment regardless of source — giardia and cryptosporidium are present in most backcountry water sources in the eastern United States. The standard tool is either a Sawyer Squeeze squeeze filter or a SteriPen ultraviolet purifier. Tablets (iodine or sodium dichloroisocyanurate) work but leave a taste that most hikers find unpleasant. Spring sources at shelters are generally more reliable in dry conditions than stream sources but should never be assumed safe without treatment.
