Yellowstone National Park sits atop one of the world's largest active supervolcanoes — a magma system 72 km long and 55 km wide whose heat drives the geysers, hot springs, mud pots, and fumaroles that define the park's character. The park's 8,991 square kilometres straddle the Continental Divide at average elevations above 2,000 metres, and the wildlife it contains — the largest free-roaming bison herd in North America, significant grizzly bear and wolf populations, and the full suite of Yellowstone megafauna — makes it the closest thing the continental United States has to an African game reserve. Camping here requires planning, flexibility, and wildlife-safety knowledge.

## The campground network

Yellowstone has twelve designated campgrounds ranging from the large, fully-serviced sites at Madison and Bridge Bay to the primitive first-come-first-served sites at Indian Creek and Pebble Creek. Five campgrounds accept reservations through recreation.gov (Bridge Bay, Canyon, Fishing Bridge RV Park, Grant Village, Madison); seven operate first-come-first-served throughout the season.

**Madison Campground** is the most strategically located — central to both the geyser basins and the northern range, with the Madison River running adjacent and excellent bison and trumpeter swan viewing from the site. 278 sites; reservations essential in July and August. Electric hookups at some sites; no showers on site (shower facilities at Old Faithful and Fishing Bridge).

**Canyon Campground** is the park's largest at 272 sites and sits adjacent to the Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone — the 300-metre deep yellow rhyolite canyon with the Upper and Lower Falls. Morning light on the canyon from Artist Point is among the most photographed scenes in the American West. The Canyon Visitor Center and services are adjacent.

**Bridge Bay** is the lakeside campground on Yellowstone Lake — the largest high-altitude lake in North America at 2,357 metres elevation — with boat launch access. The lake's extraordinary fishing, particularly for cutthroat trout, and the thermal features visible across the water from the shore make this a distinctive camping position.

**Slough Creek** is one of the premier wolf-watching campgrounds in North America. In the Lamar Valley — the "Serengeti of North America" — Slough Creek's position gives access to the valleys where the Wapiti, Junction Butte, and other wolf packs have territories. Dawn and dusk are the productive wolf observation windows. The campsite is first-come-first-served; arrive before noon in peak season. The creek has excellent cutthroat trout fishing.

**Indian Creek** and **Pebble Creek** are the two northern range first-come-first-served sites that provide the quietest camping in the park. Pebble Creek in the extreme northeast corner has excellent wildlife access (bison, bear, moose) and is 50 km from the main visitor concentration.

## Geothermal features and camping proximity

The geyser basins — Upper, Midway, Lower, Norris, and West Thumb — are the primary draws for most Yellowstone visitors. The campground proximity hierarchy:

Old Faithful area: no campground is directly at Old Faithful — the nearest is Madison (23 km west). The advantage of Madison as a base for geyser basin access is the ability to visit morning and evening when the tour-bus crowds have departed.

Norris Geyser Basin: the Norris Campground (30 sites, first-come-first-served) provides the only camping within walking distance of a major geyser basin. Norris has the highest measured water temperature (93°C at the surface) and the most active steam venting in the park.

Grand Prismatic Spring: the Midway Geyser Basin containing Grand Prismatic — the 91-metre wide hot spring whose pigmented microbial mats produce the rainbow colours visible from the overlook — is closest to Madison Campground.

## Wildlife and safety protocols

Yellowstone's wildlife-safety rules are non-negotiable. Bears: bear-resistant containers or hard-sided vehicle storage for all food, toiletries, and scented items; 100-yard minimum distance from bears. Bison: 25-yard minimum distance, no exceptions — bison injure more visitors than bears annually. Wolf watching: stay on the road or designated pull-outs; binoculars (10x minimum) and spotting scopes make the Lamar Valley sightings viable from the safe viewing distance.

Carry bear spray and know how to deploy it — hold-until-within-10-metres, then discharge. Bear spray statistically outperforms firearms in reducing injury in bear encounters.

## Backcountry camping

Yellowstone's backcountry permit system requires advance reservation ($3 per person per night, plus a $25 non-refundable reservation fee) for any overnight travel off designated campgrounds. Over 300 backcountry campsites are distributed throughout the park. The permit system opens in February for the following season.

Key backcountry areas: the Thorofare region (the most remote backcountry in the lower 48 states, 60+ km from the nearest road), the Bechler meadows in the southwestern park with waterfall access, and the backcountry of the Lamar Valley drainages. All backcountry travel in Yellowstone requires a bear canister.

## Season by season

**May-early June**: the park opens section by section; the north road (Mammoth-Cooke City) is open year-round. Snow is possible at any time; the Beartooth Highway approach from the northeast typically opens in late May. Wildflowers begin in the valleys. Grizzlies emerge from dens in late March and are most active and concentrated at carcasses.

**July-August**: full park open, maximum crowds, maximum road traffic. Campground reservations essential. Afternoon thunderstorms common at altitude. The bison rut in August produces spectacular display behaviour and makes bison unpredictable.

**September**: crowds thin significantly after Labor Day (US). The elk rut begins in mid-September — bulls bugling, gathering harems — and the light quality improves. One of the best months for camping in the park.

**October**: campgrounds begin closing (first-come-first-served sites close earliest). The first snows at altitude come in October; roads may close temporarily. The Mammoth area remains open year-round.
