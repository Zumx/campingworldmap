New England's six states — Maine, New Hampshire, Vermont, Massachusetts, Rhode Island, and Connecticut — pack an extraordinary density of quality camping into a region the size of many individual western states. The landscape ranges from the rugged granite peaks of the White Mountains and Mahoosuc Range to the spruce and fir wilderness of northern Maine, the rolling Green Mountains of Vermont, the rocky Atlantic coastline of Maine and Cape Cod, and the deciduous forest of the Connecticut River valley. Camping culture here is old and deeply embedded: the Appalachian Trail passes through every New England state, and the shelter and campsite infrastructure along it is among the finest in the United States.

The signature New England camping experience is autumn — from mid-September through mid-October, the deciduous forest ignites in red, orange, and gold, and camping in the middle of that color storm is one of the most spectacular seasonal experiences in outdoor recreation anywhere in North America. Summer is the high season for most campgrounds, and weekends at popular sites book far in advance. Winter camping in the White Mountains is a serious undertaking that attracts dedicated mountaineers and alpine skiers. ## 1. Acadia National Park, Mount Desert Island, Maine

Acadia is the most visited national park east of the Mississippi, and its combination of granite sea cliffs, carriage roads through birch forest, and dramatic coastal scenery justifies the attention. Blackwoods and Seawall campgrounds are the primary frontcountry options, while Isle au Haut — reachable by mail boat from Stonington — has a remote campsite limited to just twelve sites per night, providing genuine wilderness solitude within a national park. The summit of Cadillac Mountain, reachable by carriage road or trail, is one of the first points in the continental United States to see sunrise from October through March. Early morning camps on the summit road are unforgettable.

## 2. Pinkham Notch and Tuckerman Ravine, White Mountains, New Hampshire

Pinkham Notch on Route 16 is the eastern base camp for Mount Washington — the highest peak in the Northeast at 1,917 metres — and the gateway to Tuckerman Ravine, the glacial cirque that holds some of North America's finest spring skiing and dramatic summer hiking. The AMC's Pinkham Notch Visitor Center has a full-service lodge and campground, and the backcountry campsite at Hermit Lake within Tuckerman Ravine is reached by a 4-kilometre trail from the notch. The ravine holds snow until May or June in most years. The Crawford Notch and Franconia Notch corridors offer additional campgrounds within the White Mountain National Forest.

## 3. Baxter State Park, Millinocket, Maine

Baxter State Park in northern Maine is the northern terminus of the Appalachian Trail at the summit of Katahdin — one of the great mountain experiences in eastern North America. The park, donated to the state by former Governor Percival Baxter, is managed with a strict wilderness mandate: no RVs, extremely limited vehicle access to the interior, and a reservation system that limits daily visitors. The Chimney Pond campground at the base of the Katahdin Great Basin is one of the finest mountain camping locations in the East, with the dramatic headwall of the Cirque towering overhead. The Knife Edge ridge between Pamola Peak and Baxter Peak is a genuine scrambling challenge on the summit circuit.

## 4. Long Trail, Green Mountains, Vermont

Vermont's Long Trail runs 274 miles from the Massachusetts border to Canada — the oldest long-distance hiking trail in the United States, predating the Appalachian Trail it partly overlaps. The trail is maintained by the Green Mountain Club, which operates a system of backcountry shelters and tent platforms spaced a day's walk apart. The northern section above US Route 2 is the most remote and challenging, climbing over Camel's Hump and Mount Mansfield — Vermont's highest peak — through boreal forest and open ridge terrain. The trail corridor is narrow enough that true wilderness solitude is found only minutes from the shelters on side ridges and viewpoints.

## 5. Grafton Notch State Park, Newry, Maine

Grafton Notch in western Maine's Oxford County protects a glaciated notch of extraordinary beauty — the Screw Auger Falls gorge, the Old Speck summit, and the challenging terrain of the Mahoosuc Range immediately to the east. The Mahoosuc Notch is often called the most difficult mile on the entire Appalachian Trail — a boulder-choked slot canyon requiring hands-and-feet scrambling through car-sized fallen rocks. Camping at the notch shelter provides a genuine wilderness experience. The Mahoosuc Trail through this section offers outstanding multi-day backpacking through some of the most rugged terrain in New England.

## 6. Cape Cod National Seashore, Massachusetts

The National Seashore protects 40 miles of the outer Cape Cod coastline — the glacially deposited sandbar that hooks back on itself at Provincetown in an arc of Atlantic-facing dunes, beach plum, salt marsh, and freshwater kettle ponds. Camping within the national seashore itself is limited to the Nickerson State Park just outside the boundary and several private campgrounds within the seashore zone. The Outer Beach — the Atlantic-facing shore — is remarkable for its length and openness. Spring and fall bring spectacular shorebird concentrations, and right whales feed in the Cape Cod Bay shallows within view of shore.

## 7. Mount Greylock State Reservation, Adams, Massachusetts

Greylock is Massachusetts' highest point at 1,064 metres — modest by western standards but commanding in New England terms, with views on clear days extending to five states and the Hudson River Valley in New York. The summit has a Veterans War Memorial tower and the historic Bascom Lodge, which offers hostel accommodation and meals. Camping on the summit and at several trail-side sites is managed by the state. The Appalachian Trail crosses the summit on its northbound route through Massachusetts. The reservation's 12,000 acres of forest include old-growth hemlock stands in Money Brook Hollow that are among the finest in the state.

## 8. Allagash Wilderness Waterway, Maine

The Allagash Wilderness Waterway is a 92-mile protected river corridor through the most remote forest in the eastern United States — a paddling route from Telos Lake to the town of Allagash on the St. John River through the heart of the Maine North Woods. Camping along the route is at designated sites maintained by the Maine Bureau of Parks and Lands, with a quota system for entry during peak season. The forest is extensive paper company land — not technically wilderness by federal designation, but remote enough that canoeists can paddle for days without road crossings. Moose, black bears, loons, and brook trout are regular companions on the waterway.

## 9. Acadia to Downeast Camping Corridor, Maine Coast

East of Mount Desert Island and Bar Harbor, the coast of Washington County — known as "Downeast" — becomes increasingly remote and beautiful, with fewer visitors than the more accessible southern coast. The Bold Coast area around Cutler has a coastal trail system along sea cliffs with primitive camping at designated sites. Cobscook Bay State Park in Dennysville protects tidal estuary camping with 12-metre tidal ranges — the second largest in the continental United States. Quoddy Head State Park at the country's easternmost point has camping near its lighthouse. The area has a raw, granite-and-spruce quality that feels northern and genuine.

## 10. White Mountain National Forest Dispersed Camping, New Hampshire

Beyond the established campgrounds and AMC hut system, the White Mountain National Forest allows dispersed camping throughout much of its 750,000-acre territory, with simple rules about distance from water, trails, and roads. This opens up a vast amount of high-quality camping terrain above and between the established sites — river valleys, ridge approaches, and forest-edge locations that provide a more self-directed camping experience than the established AMC infrastructure. The Kancamagus Highway corridor particularly rewards explorers who leave the road campgrounds and head into the forest. The Wilderness areas within the WMNF — the Pemigewasset and Presidential — require camping at least 200 feet from water and 0.25 miles from trails.
