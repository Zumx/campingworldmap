Hammock camping has undergone a significant revival over the past decade, shifting from a niche choice for tropical travellers to a mainstream alternative for a wide range of backpackers and car campers. The appeal is intuitive: a hammock gets you off the rocky, root-crossed, or wet ground; it conforms perfectly to your body without any sleeping pad pressure points; and it can be set up in minutes in any forest with adequate trees. Modern camping hammocks with integrated bug nets and rain tarps have solved the two classic complaints — insect exposure and rain vulnerability — and lightweight underquilts address the cold-air-beneath problem that makes traditional hammocking cold in shoulder seasons.

The fundamental requirement for hammock camping is trees — specifically, two live trees at least 20 to 30 centimetres in diameter, spaced roughly 4 to 5 metres apart. This constraint immediately defines which landscapes suit the style. Dense boreal and temperate forests, rainforests, and woodland environments are natural hammock country. Open desert, tundra, and alpine terrain above treeline require creative rigging solutions or a willingness to pitch a freestanding hammock stand. What follows is a selection of destinations where trees and terrain combine to produce exceptional hammock camping.

## Great Smoky Mountains National Park, USA

The Smokies on the Tennessee-North Carolina border are perhaps the finest hammock camping terrain in North America. Old-growth forest covers portions of the park — tulip poplar, yellow birch, basswood, and eastern hemlock in the creek valleys — with trees of exceptional girth and spacing. The park has designated backcountry campsites with reservation and permit requirements, and hammock camping is permitted in most backcountry sites provided you use tree-friendly suspension straps at least 2.5 centimetres wide and avoid any visible damage to bark. The dense canopy provides good rain protection even without a tarp.

## Olympic Peninsula, Washington, USA

The Hoh, Quinault, and Queets rainforests on Washington's Olympic Peninsula are among the wettest places in the contiguous United States, with rainfall often exceeding 3,500 mm per year. The consequence is forest of almost unreal density and moisture — moss draping every surface, ferns growing from fallen logs, and Sitka spruce reaching 70 metres. Hammock camping here means accepting that a rain tarp is not optional but essential; the trees are ideal but the rainfall is relentless outside of summer. Several established backpacking routes penetrate the interior valleys, and dispersed camping is permitted in designated zones.

## Monteverde Cloud Forest, Costa Rica

Costa Rica's Monteverde Reserve and the surrounding private forest reserves protect cloud forest at elevation in the Tilarán Mountains, a landscape of extraordinary botanical richness and permanent mist. Several eco-lodges and private reserves in the Monteverde area permit or provide hammock camping, and the forest itself is among the most species-rich in the Americas. The trees are festooned with epiphytes — bromeliads, orchids, ferns — making a hammock strung between them feel like sleeping inside a botanical garden. Night temperatures are cool for a tropical destination, typically dropping to 12 to 16 degrees Celsius.

## Daintree Rainforest, Queensland, Australia

The Daintree in far north Queensland is the oldest continuously surviving tropical rainforest on earth, with plant lineages traceable to the Gondwana supercontinent. Hammock camping in the Daintree requires either staying at an eco-accommodation that provides hammock space or setting up on the limited accessible private land — the national park itself restricts camping to designated areas. The experience of sleeping in the Daintree is remarkable: the soundscape, driven by insects, frogs, and birds, is extraordinarily loud and complex, and the humidity and warmth are intense.

## Black Hills National Forest, South Dakota, USA

The Black Hills of South Dakota are an anomalous island of pine forest rising from the surrounding Great Plains — ponderosa pine-covered hills with granite outcrops, cold clear streams, and an interesting mix of wildlife including bison (in the park) and elk. The national forest permits dispersed camping with good tree cover for hammocking. The proximity to Mount Rushmore and Wind Cave National Park makes the Black Hills a logical camping base for a wider regional trip, and the forest roads provide access to many informal sites away from the developed recreation areas.

## Appalachian Trail Corridor, Eastern USA

The Appalachian Trail runs 3,500 kilometres from Springer Mountain in Georgia to Katahdin in Maine, passing through fourteen states and a diversity of eastern hardwood and boreal forest types. Hammock camping is permitted along most of the trail corridor, and the forest is ideally suited to it — second-growth hardwood forest in the southern sections, mixed hardwood and conifer in the middle states, and boreal spruce-fir approaching Maine. Many AT shelter sites explicitly accommodate hammock campers between the shelter and the required campsite boundaries.

## Taman Negara National Park, Malaysia

One of the world's oldest rainforests, Taman Negara in the Malay Peninsula interior has been continuously forested for an estimated 130 million years. The park offers jungle camping with hammock options at several interior camps accessible by river boat and on foot. Night in a Taman Negara hammock camp is an overwhelming sensory experience — the forest is never quiet, and the density of animal life around you is palpable even when invisible. The Orang Asli indigenous guides who lead forest treks through the park carry and string hammocks as their standard sleeping setup.

## Scandinavian Forests under Allemansrätten

Sweden, Norway, and Finland operate under freedom-to-roam laws (allemansrätten in Swedish, allemannsretten in Norwegian) that permit camping on any uncultivated land for up to one or two nights, including private forest. This means the vast boreal forests of Scandinavia — birch, pine, and spruce stretching to the horizon — are available for hammock camping without any permit, fee, or pre-planning requirement beyond choosing a tree-dense spot. The light summer nights create an otherworldly atmosphere; the forest is quiet but for wind and birdsong, and the absence of venomous snakes or dangerous spiders makes sleeping close to the ground (or suspended from the trees) more comfortable than in tropical alternatives.

## Practical Hammock Camping Tips

Regardless of destination, a few principles make hammock camping work well. Use wide tree straps (at least 2.5 cm wide, preferably 5 cm) to protect bark — many parks require this and it is simply the right practice. Set your ridgeline so the hammock hangs at roughly a 30-degree angle from horizontal when loaded — this produces the flat lay that makes hammock sleeping comfortable rather than banana-shaped. Carry an underquilt for any temperature below about 16 degrees Celsius; without insulation beneath you, body heat drains rapidly through the hammock fabric. A good rain tarp rigged on the ridgeline above the hammock handles all but the most extreme weather.
