The dependency on charged devices while camping has grown substantially over the past decade. Navigation via smartphone GPS, satellite communicators, headlamps, camera batteries, CPAP machines, and the growing category of electronic camping gadgets all require power. The question of how to supply that power without access to the grid has three practical answers: carry enough battery to last the trip, recharge from a vehicle, or generate power from solar. Each approach has specific merits and limitations, and many campers combine them. This guide focuses primarily on solar options while situating them clearly against alternatives.

## Understanding Your Power Needs

Before selecting any solar setup, quantify your power consumption. Most people significantly overestimate or underestimate their needs. A smartphone requires roughly 15 to 20 watt-hours per full charge from empty. A GPS device draws 1 to 2 watts continuously. A headlamp on its standard setting uses 1 to 3 watts. A CPAP machine on a camping trip requires 40 to 100 watt-hours per night depending on pressure settings and whether humidification is enabled. A camera with a mirrorless body depletes a battery of roughly 15 watt-hours per charge, and a serious photographer may use two to four batteries per day.

Adding up daily watt-hour requirements across all your devices gives a baseline consumption figure. Multiply by the number of days between recharging opportunities to determine storage needs. This arithmetic prevents both the expensive mistake of overbuilding a solar system for light needs and the practical failure of underbuilding for genuine needs.

## Portable Solar Panels

Portable solar panels for camping are available in a wide range from wallet-sized to briefcase-sized. The two primary panel types are monocrystalline and polycrystalline silicon. Monocrystalline panels are more efficient per unit of area, perform better in low-light and indirect conditions, and are the dominant technology in quality portable camping panels. Polycrystalline panels are cheaper and slightly less efficient.

Efficiency matters because it determines how much area is required to generate a given wattage. A 21-percent efficient monocrystalline panel produces more power from the same panel size than an 18-percent polycrystalline equivalent. In backpacking where weight and packability are primary constraints, high-efficiency panels produce more power per gram of panel carried.

For backpacking, flexible folding panels in the 10 to 30 watt range are the practical window. Goal Zero Nomad and Nemo Helio Pressure Solar are examples that offer a balance of efficiency, packability, and durability. These panels charge a power bank during hiking via a panel attached to the back of a pack, a practice that works well in open terrain and poorly in forested areas with overhead canopy.

For car camping, rigid panels in the 60 to 200 watt range, either mounted on a vehicle roof rack or set up on a ground frame, provide substantially more output. At 100 watts in full sun, a panel produces roughly 500 to 600 watt-hours on a good summer day — enough to meet most car campers' daily needs comfortably.

## Power Banks and Solar Power Banks

A power bank is a battery that stores energy for later use. Solar power banks integrate a small solar panel into the power bank body. The small integrated panels on solar power banks are almost always insufficient as a primary charging method — a 2-watt integrated panel takes around 10 hours of direct sun to fully charge a 10,000 mAh (37 watt-hour) bank. The panel is useful as a trickle supplement but not as a standalone charging solution.

Quality standalone power banks from Anker, Goal Zero, and similar brands offer reliable capacity and USB-C Power Delivery output. Capacity is measured in milliamp-hours (mAh) or watt-hours (Wh). The 20,000 mAh (74 Wh) class of power bank is a practical size for a multi-day backpacking trip where you charge one or two small devices daily and do not have access to a panel.

For charging a power bank via solar, use a separate dedicated panel with a proper output matching your bank's input specification. Many quality power banks accept USB-C input at 18 to 65 watts; a 30-watt panel is a practical and compact match.

## Portable Power Stations and Solar Generators

Portable power stations — large lithium battery units with multiple output types including AC mains outlets — have transformed car camping power management. Products like the EcoFlow Delta, Jackery Explorer, and Goal Zero Yeti series provide 500 to 3000 watt-hours of storage with AC, DC, and USB output, enabling operation of devices that cannot run from standard power banks. CPAP machines, electric air mattress pumps, laptop computers, and even small appliances operate from portable power stations.

Pairing a portable power station with a 100 to 200 watt solar panel provides a self-sustaining power system for extended car camping. Under good solar conditions, a 200-watt panel can replenish 500 to 800 watt-hours per day, maintaining a power station that is used for moderate consumption without any mains charging.

Weight and cost are the primary limitations. Quality portable power stations weigh 5 to 15 kilograms depending on capacity, which makes them impractical for anything other than vehicle-based camping. They are also expensive relative to their battery capacity compared to simpler power banks.

## Vehicle-Based Charging

For many vehicle-based campers, the vehicle's alternator is the most practical charging source. A USB power adapter that plugs into the 12V accessory socket provides charging for small devices while driving. A dual-battery system with an auxiliary lithium or AGM battery provides a dedicated power reserve for camping use that is isolated from the starting battery, preventing the critical failure of draining the starting battery overnight.

Roof-mounted solar on a vehicle charges the auxiliary battery during the day, maintaining it at full capacity with no fuel consumption and no noise. This combination of auxiliary battery and roof solar is the standard overlanding power setup and requires minimal daily management.
