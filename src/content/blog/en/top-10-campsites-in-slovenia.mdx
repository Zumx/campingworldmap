Slovenia is the most densely green country in Europe — 60% forest coverage in a country the size of Wales. Its camping landscape is built around three primary environments: the Julian Alps in the northwest (Triglav National Park), the Soča river valley with its extraordinary turquoise water, and the Karst plateau with its cave systems and underground rivers. The country's small size makes a camping tour viable without a vehicle — the distances between sites on the main circuit are manageable by bicycle, and the rail and bus infrastructure is solid. Slovenia's campsite standards are among the highest in the region, and the country has invested significantly in cycling and hiking infrastructure that connects the sites.

## 1. Camping Bled, Lake Bled

At the edge of Lake Bled — the alpine lake with a castle-topped island that has become the most photographed location in Slovenia — this campsite is on the lake shore with direct access to the walking circuit and the rowing boats that visit the island church. The Julian Alps rise directly above the lake on the western side; Triglav (2,864 m) — Slovenia's highest peak and national symbol — is visible on clear days. The site is busy June through August; May and September offer the same scenery with far fewer visitors.

## 2. Camping Korita, Soča Valley, Bovec

On the Soča river at Bovec — the adventure sports capital of Slovenia — Korita provides the best camping access to the Soča's extraordinary turquoise colour, produced by calcium carbonate in the glacially sourced water. Rafting, kayaking, canyoning, and the via ferrata on the surrounding peaks are the primary activities. The Triglav National Park western boundary is adjacent; the walk to the Soča spring takes three hours from Trenta valley.

## 3. Camp Koren, Kobarid

Downstream from Bovec on the Soča, the Kobarid area was the site of the 1917 Battle of Caporetto — one of the most decisive battles of World War One — and the Kobarid Museum provides exceptional documentation of the Isonzo campaign. The campsite is on a bend of the Soča with direct swimming access to the river and excellent facilities. Ernest Hemingway drew on this landscape for A Farewell to Arms.

## 4. Camping Triglav, Ukanc, Bohinj

At the western end of Lake Bohinj — the larger, quieter alpine lake inside Triglav National Park — this campsite is the primary base for the ascent of Triglav from the Bohinj side. The Via Alpina long-distance trail passes through the area. The Savica waterfall and the Vogel cable car (winter skiing, summer hiking) are both close to the site. Bohinj is less crowded than Bled for equivalent alpine scenery.

## 5. Camping Polovnik, Bovec

A smaller, more basic site in the Bovec area on the Koritnica river, Polovnik suits campers seeking the Soča Valley experience without the larger site's summer crowd. The World War One Fortress Kluže — a Habsburg mountain fort in a dramatic gorge position — is walkable from the camp.

## 6. Camping Menina, Kamnik-Savinja Alps, Luče

In the Savinja Valley east of Ljubljana, the Menina campsite provides access to the Kamnik-Savinja Alps — a less visited alpine area than the Julian Alps with equivalent scenery. The Logarska Dolina valley (accessible by day trip) is one of the most pristine alpine valleys in Slovenia, a private estate managed as a nature reserve. The campsite itself is at a lower elevation in the valley, with electric pitches and good facilities.

## 7. Camping Terme Ptuj, Ptuj, Styria

In Slovenia's oldest town — Ptuj, with continuous habitation since Roman times — this thermal spa campsite is part of the Terme Ptuj complex with access to thermal pool facilities. The combination of hot spring access with the mediaeval castle and wine cellars of the Ptuj wine region makes this a distinctive Slovenian camping experience distinct from the alpine mainstream.

## 8. Camping Adria, Ankaran, Slovenian Riviera

On Slovenia's 47 km Adriatic coastline near Koper — the Italian-influenced coastal strip that was contested between Italy and Yugoslavia after World War Two — this coastal campsite provides the Adriatic seaside experience in a Slovenian cultural context. The mediaeval Venetian architecture of Piran, 25 km south, is the most photogenic town on the Slovenian coast.

## 9. Camping Smlednik, Ljubljana area

North of Ljubljana on the Sava river, Smlednik is the most practical camping option for visitors to the Slovenian capital. Ljubljana's old town, its castle-topped hill, and the Triple Bridge are accessible by bus; the surrounding Gorenjska countryside provides cycling terrain. The Škocjan Caves UNESCO World Heritage Site — a system of underground canyons below the Karst plateau — is a day trip south.

## 10. Kamp Lazar, Soča Valley, Tolmin

In the lower Soča valley near Tolmin — the area where the Soča meets the Idrijca and the valley broadens before reaching the Italian border — Kamp Lazar is a small campsite with river access and a quieter character than the Bovec sites. The Tolmin Gorges, accessible on foot from the camp, are a carved limestone canyon with clear river pools.

## Triglav National Park regulations

Camping inside Triglav National Park is restricted to designated sites and bivouac zones. The permit system is managed through the TNP administration. Summit camping on Triglav requires mountaineering competence; the standard ascent from Aljažev dom requires a full day. Fires are prohibited throughout the national park.
