Camping in the Philippines requires a different mindset from camping in temperate Europe or North America. The archipelago of over 7,600 islands has a tropical climate where altitude is the primary variable — camping in the lowlands from May through October means wet season heat and rainfall, while higher elevations above 1,500 metres offer genuinely cool nights year-round. The dry season from November through April is the preferred camping window across most of the country. Infrastructure varies enormously: some sites, particularly on mountains popular with Filipino domestic trekkers, have established camp areas with caretakers and basic facilities; others are simply clearings in forest where guides set up tents on a hired basis. The landscape rewards the effort, ranging from the UNESCO-listed rice terraces of the Cordillera to the limestone karst islands of Palawan.

## 1. Mount Pulag Summit Camp, Benguet, Luzon

Mount Pulag at 2,922 metres is the highest peak on Luzon and the third-highest in the Philippines, located in the Cordillera Central range in Benguet Province. The summit camp, called Camp 2 or Eddet River Camp, sits below the treeline at around 2,500 metres, and the final push to the summit is done in the early morning hours to reach the top at dawn — when the Pulag sea of clouds, a layer of cloud below the summit that makes you feel you are standing above the sky, is most likely to be visible. The ascent via the Ambangeg trail takes two to three hours from the camp. Permits are required from the DENR, and the mountain is closed during habagat (southwest monsoon) season.

## 2. Mount Apo Base Camp, Davao del Sur, Mindanao

Mount Apo at 2,954 metres is the highest mountain in the Philippines and a dormant stratovolcano whose summit has active fumaroles and a crater lake. Several routes ascend from different trailheads on its slopes, with the Kidapawan trail and the Kapatagan-Magpet approach being the most commonly used. Base camps at Mainit Lake and the Lake Venado plateau are the traditional overnight stops before the summit push. The mountain is the habitat of the Philippine eagle, the national bird and one of the world's largest and rarest raptors. Camping on Apo is a multi-day commitment requiring local guides and permits from the Protected Area Management Board.

## 3. Batad Rice Terraces Camp, Ifugao, Luzon

The Batad rice terraces in Ifugao Province are part of the UNESCO-listed Cordillera Cultural Landscape, a living agricultural system maintained by the Ifugao people for over 2,000 years. The amphitheatre-shaped terraces at Batad, accessible by a two-kilometre hike from the road end at the saddle above the village, are among the most visually dramatic of the terraced landscapes. Several family guesthouses in the village offer basic accommodation, and camping on the terrace saddle overlooking the valley is possible with local permission. The Tappiyah Falls, a 30-minute hike from the village through the terraces, is a powerful waterfall with a bathing pool. Morning light on the terraces from the campsite viewpoint is exceptional.

## 4. El Nido Bacuit Archipelago, Palawan

El Nido is the gateway to the Bacuit Archipelago in northern Palawan, a cluster of limestone karst islands with white sand beaches, turquoise lagoons accessible only at low tide through cave entrances, and coral reef snorkelling. Camping is possible on several of the islands with permits from the El Nido-Taytay Managed Resource Protected Area; a number of island camping operators run guided overnight experiences that include tent setup, food, and snorkelling equipment. Pinagbuyutan Island and Matinloc Island are among the camping destinations with the most dramatic settings. The boat journey to the outer islands takes one to three hours from El Nido town, and the departure depends on sea conditions.

## 5. Mount Pinatubo Crater Camp, Zambales, Luzon

The 1991 eruption of Pinatubo was the second-largest volcanic eruption of the 20th century, sending ash to the stratosphere and creating a new caldera lake in the volcano's summit. The eruption devastated the surrounding Aeta indigenous communities and the US Clark Air Base. Today, the caldera lake — a striking blue-green colour from volcanic minerals — is accessible via a 4x4 drive across the lahar fields and a two-hour hike. Camping at the crater rim is organised by guides from Capas, Tarlac, and is a popular overnight experience because the morning calm on the crater lake is very different from the midday tour-group rush. The hike traverses one of the most dramatic post-volcanic landscapes in Asia.

## 6. Coron Banuang Daan Camping, Palawan

Coron Island is held by the Tagbanua indigenous people under a Certificate of Ancestral Domain Title and access is regulated by the community. Kayangan Lake on Coron Island, often cited as the cleanest lake in Asia by marine biologists studying its exceptional water clarity, is the primary day-trip destination. Camping on the island requires specific arrangement with Tagbanua representatives and is not available to independent travellers without guidance. The overnight experience — in a place with no commercial development, where the community controls access completely — offers a different quality of engagement than typical resort-adjacent camping. Nearby wreck diving sites, legacy of the Second World War, are among the best in Asia.

## 7. Masungi Georeserve, Rizal, Luzon

Masungi Georeserve is a private conservation and ecotourism area in the Sierra Madre range east of Manila, managed by the Rock Environment and Land Foundation under a stewardship agreement with the DENR. The reserve protects a limestone karst landscape that was significantly degraded by quarrying and illegal logging before restoration began. Guided trail experiences through the reserve visit rock formations, rope bridges, and a bamboo deck suspended above a karst depression. An overnight glamping programme allows small groups to spend the night in the reserve after the day visitors leave, in a setting with views across the Sierra Madre foothills toward Laguna de Bay. The reserve's proximity to Manila — about 50 kilometres — makes it an unusual wilderness experience within reach of the capital.

## 8. Sagada Kiltepan Viewpoint Area, Mountain Province, Luzon

Sagada is a small mountain town in Mountain Province known for its hanging coffins — coffins suspended on cliff faces by the Kankana-ey people as a funerary practice — its cave systems, and the Kiltepan sunrise viewpoint. Camping near Kiltepan, at an elevation of around 1,500 metres, is organised through the Sagada Environment Guides Association. The sunrise from Kiltepan over a sea of clouds filling the valleys below is one of the most photographed moments in the Philippine highlands. The town itself has a vegetarian restaurant tradition unusual in the Philippines, dating from American Peace Corps volunteer influence in the 1960s. The Lumiang and Sumaguing caves require guides for exploration.

## 9. Mount Hamiguitan Range Wildlife Sanctuary, Davao Oriental, Mindanao

Mount Hamiguitan is a UNESCO World Heritage Site — the first in the Philippines awarded purely for natural values — recognising the mountain's exceptional biodiversity, including the largest known population of the Philippine eagle and a pygmy forest on the summit plateau called the mossy forest, where trees grow to only knee height due to the acidic, waterlogged soil. The summit camp is accessible via a two-day guided climb from Cateel municipality. The mossy forest and its resident carnivorous pitcher plants of the species Nepenthes hamiguitanensis, endemic to this mountain alone, make it one of the most botanically unusual camping destinations in Asia.

## 10. Cagbalete Island, Mauban, Quezon, Luzon

Cagbalete Island in Lamon Bay off the Quezon Province coast is a low-lying island with extensive tidal flats that expose large areas of sand and seagrass at low tide, and a community of about a thousand residents. Camping on the island, organised through local homestay operators, offers a coastal experience very different from Palawan's karst scenery — flat, wide-sky, tidal, and genuinely rural. The island has no car traffic, electricity is limited, and fishing boats are the primary transport. During the day, the tidal flats can be walked for a kilometre in bare feet. It is not dramatic camping by any conventional measure, but its accessibility from Manila and its preservation of an older Philippine coastal way of life make it worth the journey.
