The walk-in campsite — a pitch accessible only on foot, requiring a short to moderate hike from the nearest road — is among the most rewarding camping experiences available in the United Kingdom. The effort of reaching the site, however modest, immediately separates the experience from drive-up camping: you are somewhere that cannot be visited without intention and physical engagement, the surroundings are typically quieter and wilder, and the sense of having earned your view is real. Britain's combination of a dense network of long-distance footpaths, the generous wild camping legislation in Scotland, and a growing number of low-impact camping sites on private land in England and Wales makes walk-in camping a genuinely practical option for travellers across the country.

Walk-in camping in England and Wales operates in a different legal framework than Scotland. Outside Scotland, camping requires either being on designated camping land, having landowner permission, or being on Dartmoor, where a specific bylaw permits wild camping on the open moor. In practice, however, many private landowners in England and Wales now permit walk-in camping on their land for a modest fee through booking platforms, creating a network of small, beautiful camping spots that did not formally exist a decade ago. Scotland remains the gold standard — the Land Reform Act's wild camping provisions mean that any open hillside, lochside, or coastal headland is legally available.

## Loch Coruisk, Isle of Skye, Scotland

Accessible by a 10-kilometre walk from Kilmarie or by boat from Elgol, Loch Coruisk sits in the heart of the Black Cuillin — a dramatic ring of gabbro peaks rising directly from the loch's north shore. The Mountain Bothies Association maintains a small bothy at the loch, and wild camping around the loch shore is permitted under Scottish access law. The combination of the loch's dark water, the towering ridge, and the isolation make this one of the most spectacular wild camp locations in the British Isles. The walk in from Kilmarie involves a rough path and some scrambling; the boat option from Elgol makes the location accessible to those without technical hill experience.

## Knoydart Peninsula, Highland Scotland

The Knoydart Peninsula is the most remote populated area in mainland Britain, accessible only by a 7-hour boat journey from Mallaig or an 18-kilometre walk over mountain passes. The village of Inverie, population around 100, has a pub, a small shop, and a basic hostel. The surrounding hills and glens of Knoydart are open for wild camping under Scottish law, and the quality of light, the silence, and the mountain scenery on this roadless peninsula are extraordinary. The Munros of Ladhar Bheinn and Meall Buidhe rise above deep sea lochs; wild red deer are everywhere in the upper corries.

## Dartmoor National Park, Devon, England

Dartmoor is the only location in England where wild camping is explicitly permitted by bylaw — specifically on the open moor above the enclosed farmland, away from roads and settlements. The southern and northern moor areas have established wild camping zones clearly mapped in the Dartmoor National Park guidelines. Tor camping — pitching a tent just below one of the granite tors that punctuate the moorland — is the classic Dartmoor wild camping experience. Hound Tor, Hay Tor, and Yes Tor are among the most recognisable. The moor is boggy in wet conditions; waterproof footwear is always advisable.

## Pen y Fan, Brecon Beacons, Wales

The Brecon Beacons National Park in south Wales permits wild camping on the open mountain land above the enclosed valley fields, and the Pen y Fan plateau is among the most popular destinations for overnight walking in Wales. A night camp on the ridge connecting Pen y Fan with Corn Du places you above the cloud inversion that fills the Usk valley below while the stars above are unobscured by any light pollution. The ascent from the Pont ar Daf car park takes roughly 45 to 60 minutes; a well-prepared wild camper can carry a lightweight setup and have it pitched before sunset.

## Cape Wrath Trail, Northwest Scotland

The Cape Wrath Trail is a 400-kilometre non-waymarked route from Fort William to Cape Wrath on the northwestern tip of Scotland — the most remote mainland headland in Britain. The trail passes through Knoydart, Torridon, Assynt, and the Reay Forest, crossing some of the most challenging and beautiful terrain in the UK. Wild camping under Scottish law is available throughout the route, and the experience of walking for days between human settlements with a tent and sufficient food defines the trail's character. River crossings require careful assessment in spate conditions; the trail is strictly a navigation exercise, not a waymarked path.

## Pembrokeshire Coast Path, Wales

The Pembrokeshire Coast Path in southwest Wales runs 300 kilometres along one of the most varied and spectacular coastlines in Britain — sea stacks, natural arches, dune systems, and quiet harbour villages alternating along a cliff-edge path. Walk-in camping along the path is available at several small, basic campgrounds established specifically for coast path walkers, typically charging a modest pitch fee with access to a standpipe and basic toilet facilities. Some of the most memorable pitches are on cliff-top fields with open Atlantic views. The path is walkable year-round; autumn is particularly good for light quality and reduced crowds.

## Corrour, Highland Scotland

Corrour is a railway station in Rannoch Moor — one of the most remote stations on the national rail network, reachable only by train or by walking several hours across open moorland. The surrounding Loch Ossian area has a Youth Hostel (bookable in advance) and open wild camping terrain on the moor. Waking at Corrour, surrounded by 360 degrees of moorland with no road in any direction, is a particular experience of British wilderness. The Loch Ossian area provides access to several Munros and Corbetts for day walking from a base camp.

## Torridon Hills, Northwest Highlands, Scotland

The Torridon hills — Beinn Alligin, Liathach, and Beinn Eighe — rise from the shores of Upper Loch Torridon in some of the oldest rock formations in Europe (Torridonian sandstone is over 750 million years old). Wild camping in the corries and on the ridges of these mountains is legally unconstrained under Scottish access law and is arguably the finest wild camping in Britain in terms of geological and visual drama. The National Trust for Scotland maintains Torridon Estate and has basic camping facilities at the Torridon campground for those who want a service base with access to the hill walks.

## Booking and Planning Walk-In Camping in the UK

For walk-in sites on private land in England and Wales, platforms like Pitchup, Coolcamping, and Wild Camping Britain list and accept bookings for small, informal camping spots on farms and estates. Scotland's wild camping is available without booking on open land under the Land Reform Act, though popular locations like Loch Lomond's eastern shore have seasonal camping management zones that require a permit from April to September.
