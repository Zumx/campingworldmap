Namibia is the ideal African country for self-drive camping. The road network is well-maintained on the main circuits, distances are large but manageable with a rooftop tent or fully equipped 4WD, and the NamibRand, Etosha, Damaraland, and Skeleton Coast regions are among the continent's most photogenic landscapes. NWR (Namibia Wildlife Resorts) manages the national park campsites; private camps in the conservancies and on farms provide alternatives. The country's low population density and arid climate produce sparse vegetation, extraordinary night skies, and wildlife in concentrations that rival the Kruger and the Masai Mara.

## 1. Okaukuejo Camp, Etosha National Park

The western anchor of Etosha — the great shallow salt pan of northern Namibia — Okaukuejo is centred on a floodlit waterhole that operates year-round. Black rhinoceros come to drink at the hole most nights; lion, leopard, elephant, and the full suite of Etosha ungulates (oryx, springbok, giraffe, zebra) visit throughout the day and night. The campsite is fenced; facilities include electric pitches, ablutions, and a pool. The waterhole viewing requires no vehicle — it is immediately adjacent to the camp.

## 2. Halali Camp, Etosha National Park

Midway between Okaukuejo and Namutoni, Halali has a smaller waterhole than Okaukuejo but is the best Etosha camp for black rhino sightings at close range. The camp is built around a kopje (granite outcrop) and is shadier than the western camps. Black-footed cat and bat-eared fox are camp residents. Game drives from Halali cover the central Etosha plains, which have the highest lion density in the park.

## 3. Bloedkoppie Campsite, Namib-Naukluft Park

In the Namib Desert proper — the world's oldest desert, 55 million years old — Bloedkoppie is a primitive campsite at a red granite inselberg (island hill) rising from the gravel plain. No facilities except a basic toilet structure; bring all water and food. The NamibRand night sky here is exceptional — the area has NamibRand International Dark Sky Reserve designation, one of only a handful of such designations in the world.

## 4. Brandberg White Lady Lodge Campground, Damaraland

Near the Brandberg Massif — Namibia's highest mountain at 2,573 metres and site of the remarkable San rock art including the "White Lady" painting — this campground has basic facilities and direct access to the hiking routes up the mountain. The Ugab River camp upstream is even more remote. Damaraland's desert-adapted elephant herds, desert-adapted lion, and the stark, eroded landscape make this the most dramatic camping in non-Etosha Namibia.

## 5. De Hoop Campsite, Namibia

In the Waterberg Plateau National Park — a large sandstone mesa rising 200 metres from the surrounding savanna — De Hoop is one of Namibia's more distinctive campsite settings. The plateau hosts populations of roan and sable antelope, white rhino, and buffalo that have been rehabilitated here. The plateau top is accessible only by guided walk — the camp is at the base.

## 6. Bagatelle Kalahari Game Ranch Camp, Kalahari

On a private game ranch in the Kalahari thornbush east of Mariental, Bagatelle is one of Namibia's premier cheetah country camps. The Namibian Kalahari has the world's largest free-roaming cheetah population — over 3,000 animals on private farmland. Cheetah tracking, caracal and aardwolf observation, and the distinctive Kalahari red-sand landscape make this one of Africa's most characterful private camping experiences.

## 7. Weaver's Rock Camp, Waterberg

A private camp in the Waterberg region combining farmland camping with access to the game-rich Waterberg Wilderness. The rock outcrops in this area support several endemic succulent plant species and one of Namibia's best collections of weaver bird nest colonies. Basic facilities in a comfortable setting.

## 8. Penduka Camp Site, Windhoek

Near the capital Windhoek, Penduka is the most accessible camping in central Namibia — useful as an arrival-night and last-night camp for fly-drive itineraries. The site is operated by the Penduka social enterprise, which provides income to women in the surrounding township through craft production. Ablutions, electric pitches, and a restaurant adjacent.

## 9. Ugab Rhino Trust Conservation Camp, Damaraland

A community-based camp at the centre of the Save the Rhino Trust conservation area in Damaraland, which protects the world's only free-roaming black rhino population living outside formally protected national park boundaries. Tracking is done on foot with a Save the Rhino Trust guide — the only legal way to approach Damaraland rhino on foot. The camp is basic; the experience of walking with rhino in desert wilderness is irreplaceable.

## 10. Mile 72 Camp, Skeleton Coast

On the Skeleton Coast — the fog-bound Atlantic desert coast where cold Benguela Current upwelling drives extraordinary marine productivity — the camping at Mile 72 (kilometre designation along the coast road) is primitive and for self-sufficient overlanders only. Cape fur seals in colonies of hundreds of thousands inhabit the offshore rocks. The coastal desert's atmospheric fog, bleached whale bones, and rusted shipwreck hulks define the experience.

## Self-drive camping logistics

A 4WD with rooftop tent is the standard Namibian camping setup. Fuel range between petrol stations must be calculated; the Kaokoveld and deep Skeleton Coast require carrying 200+ litre fuel reserves. Water is available at all NWR camps but not at primitive sites. NWR campsites must be reserved through the NWR booking system, which opens approximately one year in advance.
