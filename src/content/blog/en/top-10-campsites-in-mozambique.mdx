Mozambique is one of Africa's most visually stunning countries — a 2,500-kilometre Indian Ocean coastline of white sand beaches, mangrove lagoons, coral reef-fringed islands, and turquoise-green sea. Inland, the country encompasses the Rift Valley highlands of Niassa, the miombo woodland plains of Gorongosa, and the Zambezi River floodplains. Camping here is often a matter of arriving at a beach that exceeds expectations, or encountering wildlife in a national park that two decades ago was nearly empty and is now recovering with remarkable speed.

Mozambique's tourism infrastructure is thinner than neighboring countries like Zimbabwe or South Africa, and that thinness is part of its appeal. Self-sufficient overland campers driving south from Tanzania or north from South Africa find a country where the main coastal road passes beautiful uncrowded beaches regularly enough to camp at will in the right conditions. 4x4 is strongly recommended for the beach tracks and park access roads. The [campsite map](/map) plots the key sites along the coast and in the interior before you dive into the detail below.

## 1. Bazaruto Archipelago National Park, Inhambane Province

The Bazaruto Archipelago off the coast of Vilankulo is one of the last refugia in the western Indian Ocean for dugongs — the gentle sea mammals once mistaken for mermaids. The archipelago's coral reefs, mangrove channels, and sand island beaches are extraordinary, and camping is available on Benguerra Island through licensed concession operators. Snorkeling and diving from island camps accesses some of the richest coral reef biodiversity on Africa's east coast. The islands are reachable by speedboat from Vilankulo, and the combination of barefoot beach camping, pristine reef, and dugong watching makes this one of the finest island camping destinations in southern Africa.

## 2. Gorongosa National Park, Sofala Province

Gorongosa's recovery from near-total wildlife destruction during the civil war is one of conservation's most remarkable recent success stories. The park — occupying the southern end of the African Rift Valley in central Mozambique — now has large elephant herds, buffalo, hippos, lions, leopards, wild dogs, and one of Africa's most bird-rich wetland ecosystems in the seasonal floodplains of the Urema Lake. The Chitengo base camp has campsites alongside lodges, and fly camping in the park interior is available through licensed guides. The conservation center's research program — funded by a private foundation — has brought scientific rigor to the restoration effort in an extraordinary way.

## 3. Quirimbas Archipelago, Cabo Delgado Province

The northern Quirimbas Archipelago forms a national park protecting coral reefs, mangrove systems, and inhabited islands with Swahili-heritage communities along a 300-kilometre stretch of Cabo Delgado coast. Medjumbe Island and Quilalea Island have exclusive private camps; more basic camping options exist through community tourism arrangements on Ibo Island, the historic center of the archipelago. Ibo's Portuguese-era fort and the crumbling colonial town provide a striking backdrop to the Indian Ocean camp experience. The security situation in the northern Cabo Delgado region requires checking current advisories before traveling, as it has been affected by instability in recent years.

## 4. Tofo Beach, Inhambane Province

Tofo is Mozambique's most established backpacker surf and dive destination — a beach community south of Inhambane town built around one of the world's most reliable whale shark snorkeling sites. Whale sharks visit the Tofo area year-round, with peak abundance typically between October and March. Several dive operators run snorkeling trips to encounter the sharks, and manta rays are similarly reliable on the reef. Campgrounds and backpacker lodges are numerous and inexpensive. The Tofo beach camping scene is informal and social, and the combination of great surf, whale shark encounters, and low costs keeps a loyal following of long-term campers returning.

## 5. Banhine National Park, Gaza Province

Banhine in the remote interior of Gaza Province is one of Mozambique's least-visited national parks, protecting a vast flat landscape of seasonal pans, miombo woodland, and grassland. Wildlife is recovering from the civil war period, with elephant, buffalo, zebra, and wild dog present. The park has very basic infrastructure — access requires a 4x4 and full self-sufficiency — and camping is done in the wild with a ranger. The remoteness and the undeveloped character of the camp experience are precisely the appeal for those who find the more organized parks of southern Africa too polished. Banhine is part of the Great Limpopo Transfrontier Park spanning Mozambique, Zimbabwe, and South Africa.

## 6. Pemba Beach, Cabo Delgado Province

Pemba is Cabo Delgado's provincial capital, sitting on a broad natural harbor that is one of the largest in the world. Wimbi Beach outside town has several campgrounds with beach access and reasonable facilities. The harbor interior, protected from Indian Ocean swell, has calm snorkeling in warm water over fringing reef. Pemba functions as a base for exploring the Quirimbas Archipelago and the Mueda Plateau inland. The town has a distinctive Swahili-influenced culture and fresh seafood at genuinely low prices. Access is by air from Maputo or Dar es Salaam, or by an overland drive that passes through some of Mozambique's most interesting traditional architecture in the northern villages.

## 7. Inhambane Town and Surrounds, Inhambane Province

Inhambane is one of the oldest settlements on the East African coast — Arabic traders operated here before Vasco da Gama arrived in 1498. The town's atmospheric Portuguese colonial architecture, the traditional dhow that serves as a passenger ferry across the bay, and the surrounding beaches of Barra, Tofo, and Tofinho create a richly layered destination. The Barra Peninsula campsite at the bay entrance is one of Mozambique's most reliable budget camping options, with easy access to a long beach ideal for swimming and body surfing. Whale sharks and manta rays are found on the reef directly in front of the Barra lighthouse.

## 8. Zinave National Park, Inhambane Province

Zinave on the Save River in central Mozambique is another African Parks-managed reserve undergoing active wildlife restoration. Elephant, hippo, crocodile, and nyala are reliably seen along the Save River, and lion, wild dog, and cheetah have been reintroduced. The Zinave Bush Camp has simple luxury tented accommodation, and fly camping is available for more authentic bush nights. The park's Save River frontage is particularly rewarding for evening and morning game viewing. The concession is deliberately kept small and exclusive, limiting daily visitor numbers in a way that maintains a quality of wildlife encounter unavailable in more commercialized parks.

## 9. Lake Niassa (Lake Malawi), Niassa Province

The Mozambican shore of Lake Niassa — the southern extension of Lake Malawi — is the most undeveloped part of the lake's coastline, accessible from the Niassa Reserve town of Metangula. Wild camping along the lakeshore through traditional fishing villages is possible for independent travelers with 4x4, and the lake's extraordinary endemic cichlid diversity is accessible in the clear shallows from any beach. The Livingstone Mountains on the Tanzanian shore rise across the lake, creating a dramatic backdrop. The absence of tourist infrastructure that characterizes this shore is what makes it extraordinary — the lake life of small-scale fishing communities continues largely unmodified by the presence of travelers.

## 10. Maputo Elephant Reserve, Maputo Province

Just south of Maputo city, the Maputo Elephant Reserve protects a coastal lowland of thicket, woodland, and wetland that has one of the highest densities of elephants per square kilometer of any reserve in southern Africa. The reserve also has hippos, crocodiles, and a rich coastal bird community. The Maputo Special Reserve campsite near the main entrance is accessible by road from the capital and offers an extraordinary bush camping experience within two hours of a major city. Beach camping on the protected coastline within the reserve is also available in the southern sections. The reserve forms part of the Lubombo Transfrontier Conservation Area linking Mozambique, Swaziland, and South Africa.
