Great Smoky Mountains National Park is the most visited national park in the United States — receiving well over 12 million visitors annually, more than twice the nearest competitor. The Smokies earn this popularity genuinely: they are among the most biodiverse temperate forests on Earth, with species richness in wildflowers, salamanders, beetles, and trees that reflects the mountains' role as a refugium through multiple ice ages. The name comes from the naturally occurring hydrocarbon haze produced by the forests themselves, which gives the ridges a perpetual blue-grey atmosphere that deepens through layers of receding ridges. Camping here requires advance reservation from spring through fall at the frontcountry sites, while the backcountry requires a separate permit for shelter stays along the Appalachian Trail and the park's network of backcountry sites.

## 1. Elkmont Campground, Tennessee

Elkmont is the largest campground in Great Smoky Mountains National Park and the most accessible from Gatlinburg and Interstate 40. Positioned on the Little River in a broad valley, it has over 200 sites with a mix of tent-only and RV sites. The river is cold and clear year-round, suitable for wading and trout fishing with a Tennessee or North Carolina license. Elkmont's most distinctive feature is its annual synchronous firefly event: for approximately two weeks in late May or early June, Photinus carolinus fireflies synchronise their bioluminescent flashes in an extraordinary display. Access during peak firefly season requires a shuttle ticket reserved months in advance through Recreation.gov.

## 2. Cades Cove Campground, Tennessee

Cades Cove is an open valley encircled by mountains in the western part of the park, settled by European-American families in the early 19th century and preserved as an open landscape through active management. The Cades Cove loop road passes historic grist mills, churches, and log cabins, and the open meadows are among the best wildlife-viewing areas in the park — white-tailed deer are abundant, black bears are regularly seen, and wild turkeys cross the road routinely. The campground at the cove entrance is large and well-equipped. The loop road closes to vehicles on Wednesday and Saturday mornings through September to allow cyclists and walkers uninterrupted access to the valley.

## 3. Cataloochee Campground, North Carolina

Cataloochee is one of the park's least-visited major valleys despite holding some of its most interesting natural history. The valley was where the National Park Service reintroduced elk to the Southern Appalachians in 2001 — the first elk in the region since the species was extirpated by overhunting in the 19th century. The herd, now numbering over 200 animals, is most visible in Cataloochee meadows at dawn and dusk, particularly in the fall rut from September through October when bull elk bugle. The valley is accessible only via a long gravel road from the Cove Creek area in North Carolina. The campsite is small and quiet, with a historic church and farmstead visible from the camping area.

## 4. Smokemont Campground, North Carolina

Smokemont is the primary campground on the North Carolina side of the park, positioned on the Oconaluftee River near the Oconaluftee Visitor Center and adjacent to the Qualla Boundary, the land of the Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians. The Museum of the Cherokee Indian in Cherokee, just outside the park boundary, is one of the best tribal museums in the eastern United States and provides essential context for understanding the Smoky Mountains as Cherokee homelands from which the tribe was forcibly removed during the 1838 Trail of Tears. The Smokemont Loop trail and the Kephart Prong trail to a historic Civilian Conservation Corps shelter are accessible from the campground.

## 5. Deep Creek Campground, North Carolina

Deep Creek enters the Oconaluftee River near Bryson City on the park's southern edge and its valley is one of the park's most accessible for tubing — floating down the stream on inflatable tubes is a popular summer activity. The campground at the valley's end has riverside pitches and access to a series of waterfalls within easy hiking distance: Juney Whank Falls, Toms Branch Falls, and Indian Creek Falls are all within two miles of the campsite. The Deep Creek valley has unusually dense wildflower displays in spring, with trillium, trout lily, and bloodroot carpeting the forest floor in April. It is less crowded than the Tennessee side campgrounds.

## 6. Cosby Campground, Tennessee

Cosby is the smallest of the four major frontcountry campgrounds and the quietest in the park's northeastern corner, a location that sees far less traffic than the Gatlinburg and Cherokee gateway areas. The campground is in a hollow on Cosby Creek surrounded by old-growth hemlock — though many hemlocks are dead or dying from woolly adelgid infestation, a non-native pest that has devastated hemlock across the Appalachians. The Cosby to Mount Cammerer trail offers one of the best ridge views in the park from the restored 1937 fire tower on the summit, a five-mile climb. The Hen Wallow Falls trail is a shorter option.

## 7. Abrams Creek Campground, Tennessee

Abrams Creek is a remote campground accessed via a gravel road from Chilhowee in the western end of the park. It is rarely full even in peak summer and has pitches along Abrams Creek, the park's most popular stream for backcountry fishing. Brook trout, the only trout species native to the Smokies, survive in the upper reaches of Abrams Creek. The campground serves as the start point for the Rabbit Creek trail and access to the lookout above Hannah Mountain with views across the foothills. Its isolation — 40 minutes from the nearest interstate on gravel roads — ensures it attracts a different camper from the mainstream Cades Cove and Elkmont sites.

## 8. Standing Bear Farm Hostel and Campground, Tennessee

Standing Bear Farm on the Appalachian Trail near the Davenport Gap at the park's northeastern corner is a celebrated long-distance hiker hostel that also has basic camping. It is a resupply point for Appalachian Trail thru-hikers heading north from Springer Mountain, Georgia, or south from Katahdin. The farm maintains a trail culture atmosphere with a small store, laundry access, and a hostel bunkroom. Tent camping in the surrounding field is available at minimal cost. For thru-hikers exiting the national park after the intense 72-mile Smoky Mountains section, Standing Bear provides the first opportunity to resupply and rest in days.

## 9. LeConte Lodge, Mount LeConte, Tennessee

Mount LeConte's summit lodge is not a campground in the conventional sense but a collection of primitive cabins and a lodge dining room accessible only on foot via five different trail routes — the shortest being 5.5 miles. Reservations for the lodge's 50-person capacity are released a year in advance and are typically fully booked within hours of release. For those who secure a spot, the experience of spending the night at 6,593 feet, above the park's cloud layer, with views at dawn from Cliff Top and Myrtle Point, is one of the most distinctive Smoky Mountains experiences available. Llamas carry supplies to the lodge three times weekly during the operating season.

## 10. Appalachian Trail Shelters, Smokies Backcountry

The Appalachian Trail traverses 72 miles through the Smokies along the main ridge, and unlike most of the AT, camping in the Smokies backcountry is restricted to designated shelters — open-fronted Adirondack-style shelters with chain-link bear barriers that are required to be closed at night. Reservations and permits are required from Recreation.gov for all shelter stays. The AT shelters in the Smokies provide a baseline roof that makes wet conditions more manageable than tent camping, though they are crowded on peak weekends. The Tri-Corner Knob shelter on the remote eastern Smokies ridge, accessible only after a long approach hike, is among the park's most isolated shelter stays.
