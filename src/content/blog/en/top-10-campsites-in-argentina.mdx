Argentina's camping landscape spans one of the widest ranges of terrain on earth — Patagonian steppe, Andean peaks, subtropical jungle in the north, Atlantic coastline, and the dense lake district around Bariloche. The national park system (Administración de Parques Nacionales) manages most backcountry camping, with a network of organised sites in the major parks complemented by roadside municipal sites in smaller towns. Patagonia dominates the international imagination, but the northern provinces and Andean northwest contain equally compelling camping with far fewer visitors.

## 1. Poincenot Camp, Los Glaciares National Park, Santa Cruz

The base camp beneath the Fitz Roy massif, accessible via a 9 km hike from El Chaltén. Poincenot sits on the Río Blanco floodplain with the Fitz Roy towers directly overhead — one of the most dramatic campsite settings in South America. The site is managed by the park and limited to two nights per visit. Bring all food and fuel from El Chaltén; no services on site. Wind is the constant challenge here; a four-season tent is recommended even in summer.

## 2. Camping Lago Roca, Los Glaciares National Park, Santa Cruz

On the southern arm of Lago Argentino near the Chilean border, this organised campsite gives access to the glacier trekking on Perito Moreno without the crowds concentrated at the main visitor centre. Electric pitches available, and the park ranger station provides information on ice trekking permits. The lake views and relatively protected position make this one of the more comfortable Patagonian sites.

## 3. Campamento Paine Grande, Torres del Paine (Argentina side approaches)

While Torres del Paine National Park is technically in Chile, the approaches from Argentina via Puerto Natales and the Cerro Castillo border crossing are standard, and the Paine Grande camp is the central hub for the W and O circuits. The CONAF-managed site has refugio accommodation alongside tent pitches. Wind conditions are extreme; the site's position at the junction of the W makes it the most trafficked point on the circuit.

## 4. Camping Municipal Piramides, Chubut

On the Valdés Peninsula — a UNESCO World Heritage site for its marine wildlife — this municipal campground at the village of Puerto Pirámides is the base for whale watching, sea lion colonies, and penguin rookeries. Southern right whales congregate in the Golfo Nuevo from June through December. The campsite is simple but well-located, and the peninsula's wildlife density is exceptional.

## 5. Piedra del Fraile, Lago del Desierto, Santa Cruz

A private camp at the northern end of Lago del Desierto, accessible from El Chaltén by a 37 km road. More remote than the Fitz Roy camps and significantly less trafficked, Piedra del Fraile is a base for climbing the volcanic summits north of the Fitz Roy range and for kayaking on the lake. Hot showers available — a genuine luxury at this latitude and altitude.

## 6. Camping Lago Grey, Torres del Paine Circuit

Adjacent to the Grey Glacier, with direct views onto one of Patagonia's most accessible ice faces. The turquoise lake carries icebergs calved from the glacier. The site is on the western arm of the W circuit and accessible by catamaran from Pudeto as well as on foot. CONAF fee applies; reservations through the official Torres del Paine booking system.

## 7. Camping Isla Pavón, Santa Cruz

On a river island in the Río Santa Cruz, 50 km from the Atlantic coast. A free municipal campsite used primarily by fishing campers — the Santa Cruz holds brown and rainbow trout of significant size. The island setting provides shelter from Patagonian wind. Basic facilities; self-sufficient camping assumed.

## 8. EcoCamping Ñisoleufú, Neuquén

In the Patagonian lake district of Neuquén province, this private eco-site sits at the edge of Lácar Lake with views to the Andes. The area is less internationally known than Bariloche's Nahuel Huapi but offers equivalent lake and mountain scenery. Horse trekking and fishing available locally.

## 9. Campamento Río Frías Superior, Los Alerces National Park, Chubut

Inside Los Alerces National Park — home to the endangered alerce (Fitzroya cypress) tree — this walk-in campsite on the upper Río Frías requires a 15 km hike from the road. The alerce forests here contain trees over 2,000 years old. No services; carry in all water and food. The park has tightened visitor numbers in recent years to protect the habitat.

## 10. Camping Renahue, Los Lagos, Chile-Argentina border region

Near the Carretera Austral approach, Renahue is a private site on the Río Renahue in a section of Valdivian temperate rainforest. The combination of bamboo-like quila thickets, coihue beech, and the fast-running river make this a distinctive camp well removed from the main Patagonian tourist circuit.

## Season and logistics

Argentine Patagonia's camping season runs November through March. April brings increasing storm frequency. The wind — the Patagonian westerlies — peaks in November and December and moderates slightly in February. Reservations for Los Glaciares National Park sites are mandatory in January and February, with the system opening approximately four months in advance.
