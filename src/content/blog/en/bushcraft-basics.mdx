Bushcraft is the practice of using natural materials and traditional skills to meet survival needs in the outdoors. Unlike general outdoor skills, bushcraft emphasises sourcing materials from the environment rather than bringing them from home. The discipline has roots in the skills of indigenous peoples worldwide and in the work of practitioners like Mors Kochanski (Canada), Ray Mears (UK), and Tom Brown Jr (USA). The skills below form the foundation — they are not survival techniques for emergencies but standard competencies for anyone who wants a higher degree of self-reliance in the outdoors.

## Friction fire: the bow drill

The bow drill is the most reliable friction fire method for wet or humid climates. The system requires a fireboard, a spindle, a handhold, and a bow with cordage. The fireboard and spindle must be compatible dry wood species — willow, cottonwood, elder, cedar, and lime are reliable choices; hardwoods like oak and ash generally do not work. The technique produces a coal in the carved notch beneath the spindle socket, which is then transferred to a tinder nest and blown into flame.

The learning curve is steep — correct technique requires downward pressure on the spindle, horizontal bow strokes, and a properly carved notch. The most common failure is wet wood. Carry the fireboard inside your clothing on wet days. A proper bow drill kit from dry materials will produce fire in approximately 30 to 60 seconds; the same kit in wet conditions may not produce fire at all.

## Natural tinder identification

Natural tinder — material that will ignite from a spark or a coal and carry flame to kindling — is the first bottleneck in any friction fire attempt. The best tinders vary by region: dry grass and leaves (universally available), birch bark (northern forests), dry cattail down (any wetland margin), dry fungal bracket material (Inonotus obliquus / chaga is among the best), and dry seed down from fireweed and thistles. All tinder must be genuinely dry — the touch test is insufficient; break material open and test the interior.

## Improvised debris shelter

A debris hut is a simple A-frame structure built from branches and packed with dry leaf litter for insulation. The frame requires a ridgepole long enough to provide head-to-toe length when elevated at the front, with the foot end on the ground. Branches leaned against the ridgepole create the frame; several feet of dry leaves packed over and into the frame provide insulation equivalent to a moderate-weight sleeping bag. The interior should be barely large enough to turn over — volume reduces the shelter's ability to retain body heat.

A debris hut built in dry conditions in a temperate hardwood forest is a genuinely effective emergency overnight shelter. It requires approximately two to three hours of material gathering and construction. The critical material is dry leaf litter in sufficient quantity — a pile two metres deep and two metres in diameter provides enough insulation for a standard-frame adult.

## Water sourcing and assessment

Running water is preferred to still water. Water sourced from above tree level in a forested catchment with no upstream agriculture is the cleanest available from a natural source. All natural water sources in areas with any human or animal activity should be treated before drinking — Giardia and Cryptosporidium are present in many pristine-appearing streams. Boiling for one minute at sea level (three minutes above 3,000 metres) is the most reliable treatment method and the only method that covers all biological contaminants.

The vegetation around a spring or seep provides information about water quality: abundant cattail and watercress indicate a relatively high-nutrient water source, which may suggest agricultural runoff or animal use upstream. High-altitude seeps with sparse vegetation are generally the lowest-risk natural sources.

## Plant identification basics

A working identification of edible and toxic plants in your home region is a foundation skill. The priority identifications are: which plants in your region are fatally toxic (in the UK, hemlock water dropwort and monkshood; in North America, poison hemlock and water hemlock); which produce aggressive dermatitis (giant hogweed, wild parsnip); and which are reliably safe and abundant (nettles, elderflower, wood sorrel, wild garlic). The mistake of eating an unknown plant "because it looks like something edible" is responsible for most plant poisoning cases. If you cannot identify it with certainty, do not eat it.

## Tree identification for material selection

Different tree species have different practical properties: birch bark is waterproof and makes excellent tinder; lime (linden) inner bark is one of the best natural cordage fibres; hazel produces straight wands suitable for basketry and shelter framing; sweet chestnut splits cleanly for friction fire components; willow contains salicin and has been used medicinally for fever treatment across cultures. Starting with birch and lime identification is the most practically rewarding entry point in northern temperate forest regions.

## Natural cordage

Twisted plant fibre cordage is made from inner bark, plant stems, or root fibres. The reverse-twist method — twisting two strands in opposite directions while wrapping them around each other — produces a self-locking structure that holds tension without knots. Lime bark cordage is among the strongest natural fibre cordage available; nettle stem fibre, prepared by retting (soaking) and then peeling, produces softer but equally strong material.

## Navigation by sun and stars

The shadow-tip method: place a stick upright in the ground, mark the tip of its shadow, wait 15 minutes, mark the tip again. The line between the two marks runs east-west; the second mark is always east. This works on any clear day at any latitude. At night, in the northern hemisphere, Polaris (the North Star) sits within 1 degree of true north — find it by drawing a line from the two outer stars of the Big Dipper's bowl, extending five times the distance between them. In the southern hemisphere, the Southern Cross and its two pointer stars provide an approximate south reference.

## The knife as the primary bushcraft tool

A fixed-blade knife with a full or three-quarter tang, a Scandi grind, and a blade length of 10 to 14 cm handles the full range of bushcraft cutting tasks. The Mora Companion and Mora Garberg are the cost-performance reference points. A sharp knife is far safer than a dull one — a dull blade requires excess force and unpredictable cutting behaviour. Carry a diamond hone and maintain the edge. A folding saw weighing 150 to 200 grams extends the capability for timber processing beyond what any knife can handle.
