Indonesia's 17,000-plus islands encompass one of the most varied camping landscapes in Asia. Active volcanoes rise from dense equatorial rainforest, remote beaches on outer islands see almost no visitors, and Komodo dragons patrol shorelines beneath which some of the world's finest coral reefs lie just metres below the surface. The country straddles the equator, meaning the climate is tropical year-round, though the distinction between wet and dry seasons matters considerably for camping — the dry season from May to October is generally preferred for most destinations.

Indonesia's national park system covers many of the country's most significant natural areas, and most parks have established permit and camping systems. Language can be a barrier in remote areas, and a basic grasp of Bahasa Indonesia is useful. That said, Indonesian hospitality is legendary, and campers who venture beyond the main tourist circuits are often met with warmth and curiosity from local communities. The [campsite map](/map) can help navigate the sprawling geography of the archipelago when planning your itinerary.

## 1. Mount Rinjani, Lombok

Rinjani is Indonesia's second-highest volcano at 3,726 metres, and the trek to its summit rim and the crater lake of Segara Anak is the country's most celebrated multi-day camping route. The standard circuit takes three to four days, with established campsites at the crater rim and lakeside. Permits are issued through the Rinjani Trekking Management Board, and registered guides and porters are compulsory — a rule that has created a sustainable income for the surrounding Sasak communities while maintaining trail quality. The view from the rim at dawn, with the smoking summit cone and Segara Anak below and Bali's Agung visible across the strait, is genuinely breathtaking.

## 2. Komodo National Park, East Nusa Tenggara

The Komodo archipelago west of Flores protects the only wild population of Komodo dragons — the world's largest lizard — alongside extraordinary marine biodiversity in the straits between islands. Camping on the islands requires park permits and stays at the official ranger-supervised campsite on Rinca island, as unsupervised overnight stays with Komodo dragons roaming freely are obviously not advisable. Liveaboard boats are the most common way to experience the park at night, allowing diving in the rich straits by day. The landscape — dry savanna, lontar palms, and turquoise sea — is strikingly beautiful and unlike anything else in the Indonesian archipelago.

## 3. Gunung Merbabu National Park, Central Java

Merbabu is a dormant stratovolcano rising to 3,145 metres above the Kedu Plain in Central Java, and its twin-summit ridge offers one of the finest camping trekking experiences on the island. The summit plateau between the two peaks — Syarif and Kenteng Songo — is a broad alpine meadow above the treeline, and camping there provides views across to the active Merapi volcano immediately to the south. The trail through the upper montane forest and edelweiss grasslands is well-maintained. Permits are obtained at the trailhead, and the climb is manageable for fit hikers in good conditions.

## 4. Way Kambas National Park, Lampung, Sumatra

Way Kambas in southern Sumatra is one of the best-protected remaining lowland Sumatran rainforest areas and home to Sumatran elephants, Sumatran rhinos (one of the rarest large mammals on Earth), Sumatran tigers, and tapirs. The park has an elephant conservation centre and several simple accommodation and camping facilities near the Way Kanan area. Night walks with rangers offer wildlife encounter possibilities that daytime visits cannot match. The Way Kanan River runs through the park's interior, and boat trips reveal elephant activity along the riverbanks in early morning and late afternoon. This is one of the few places on Earth where all four of Sumatra's iconic large mammals can theoretically be encountered.

## 5. Gunung Kerinci, Sumatra

Kerinci is Southeast Asia's highest volcano at 3,805 metres, rising dramatically from the Kerinci Seblat National Park in west-central Sumatra — one of the last large blocks of intact Sumatran rainforest. The summit climb from the town of Kersik Tuo involves one overnight camp at a cleared site within the montane forest zone, with a pre-dawn push to the summit for sunrise. The national park below the volcano protects Sumatran tigers, sun bears, and clouded leopards alongside the extraordinary flora of Sumatran cloud and montane forest. The area's remoteness and difficult logistics keep visitor numbers low, preserving a genuine wilderness quality.

## 6. Gunung Bromo, East Java

Bromo is one of Indonesia's most visited volcanic landscapes but remains deeply impressive — an active smoking crater rising from a vast caldera called the Tengger Caldera or Sea of Sand, surrounded by a broad plain of volcanic ash. The Cemoro Lawang village on the caldera rim is the main base, with basic guesthouses and camping near the rim. Camping in the caldera itself requires permits and provides an extraordinary experience of sleeping in a volcanic landscape with the Bromo crater steaming nearby. The surrounding Bromo-Tengger-Semeru National Park also encompasses Semeru, Java's highest peak, which is a more technical multi-day climb.

## 7. Raja Ampat, West Papua

Raja Ampat is one of the most biodiverse marine environments on Earth — the number of coral species recorded in a single dive site here exceeds the total found in the entire Caribbean. Above water, the karst limestone islands draped in jungle and rising from turquoise bays are among the most beautiful island landscapes in the Pacific. Camping on beaches in Raja Ampat is possible through arrangements with local communities, who own and manage most of the land and sea in the region. Several homestay and community camping programs operate in Misool, Waigeo, and the Dampier Strait area. The remoteness and cost of reaching Sorong, the gateway city, ensure that visitor numbers remain moderate.

## 8. Tangkoko Nature Reserve, North Sulawesi

Tangkoko in the far north of Sulawesi is the best place in the world to observe spectral tarsiers — tiny primates with enormous eyes and near-silent movement through the coastal forest. The reserve also has Sulawesi bear cuscus, maleo birds (remarkable hornbill-like birds that bury their eggs in geothermally heated soil), and black macaques. Guided night walks with registered park guides — the only way to access the nocturnal wildlife — depart from the park entrance. Simple camping facilities are available near the park boundary. The landscape of coastal lowland forest between volcanic hills and the sea is distinctive to the Sulawesi biodiversity zone.

## 9. Wakatobi National Park, Southeast Sulawesi

The Wakatobi Marine National Park — named for the four main islands of Wangi-Wangi, Kaledupa, Tomia, and Binongko — protects a coral triangle ecosystem with near-pristine reefs and extraordinary underwater visibility. Camping on the outer islands is possible through community arrangements, though most visitors stay in dive resorts or on liveaboards. The remoteness of the park — reached by flight from Kendari or Bau-Bau — keeps it far quieter than the more accessible dive sites of Lombok or Komodo. Above-water, the traditional Bajau sea nomad communities, whose houseboats and stilt villages line the lagoons, provide a cultural dimension unique to the area.

## 10. Mount Agung, Bali

Agung is Bali's highest volcano at 3,031 metres and the spiritual center of Balinese Hinduism — the island's temples orient themselves toward its summit. The climb to the crater rim is a pre-dawn expedition beginning around midnight from the Pura Pasar Agung temple on the southern slope. Groups camp at the trailhead the previous evening to begin in darkness. The ascent is steep and demanding, requiring good fitness and cold-weather clothing — temperatures near the summit can drop significantly. The summit view at sunrise, with Lombok's Rinjani silhouetted to the east and the Bali landscape dropping 3,000 metres to the sea, is one of the most memorable experiences available to campers in the Indonesian archipelago.
