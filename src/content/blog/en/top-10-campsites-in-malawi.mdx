Malawi is one of sub-Saharan Africa's most accessible and underrated camping destinations. The country is small — roughly the size of Pennsylvania — but its geography is extraordinary: Lake Malawi fills the southern end of the East African Rift Valley with crystal-clear freshwater for 580 kilometres, flanked by escarpment highlands and rolling miombo woodland. Camping costs are low by African standards, the population is famously welcoming to visitors, and the range of experiences — from kayaking among fish eagles on the lake to hiking in genuine mountain wilderness — is remarkable for such a compact country.

Malawi's wildlife was severely depleted by decades of poaching, but active conservation programs in Liwonde, Majete, and Nkhotakota — many led by African Parks, the conservation NGO — have brought back lions, elephants, and black rhinos with increasing success. The bush camping around these reserves is now among the most rewarding in east Africa, combining genuine wildlife encounters with the intimacy of small, well-run camps. ## 1. Cape Maclear, Lake Malawi National Park

Cape Maclear at the southern end of Lake Malawi is perhaps the most famous backpacker camping destination in southern Africa. The UNESCO-listed national park protects the crystal-clear lake and its extraordinary endemic cichlid fish population — over 1,000 species found nowhere else on Earth. Several lakefront campgrounds operate in the village of Chembe, offering tent pitches directly on the beach. Snorkeling from the beach reveals the cichlid diversity in shallow, warm water. The light on the lake at dawn, with the peninsulas and islands reflected in still water, is one of the defining images of Malawi.

## 2. Liwonde National Park, Southern Region

Liwonde on the Shire River in southern Malawi has undergone a dramatic wildlife recovery since African Parks took over management in 2015. Lion, elephant, sable antelope, black rhino, Cookson's wildebeest, and thousands of hippos now inhabit the riverside miombo woodland. The Kuthengo Bush Camp and the Mvuu Wilderness Camp offer luxury tented and standard camping options on the Shire River, with boats providing the primary means of wildlife viewing. The Shire River is one of the finest hippo-viewing rivers in Africa, and mornings on the water deliver extraordinary encounters. Night drives from camp add lions, leopards, and civets to the mammal list.

## 3. Majete Wildlife Reserve, Southern Region

Majete, also managed by African Parks, is Malawi's only Big Five reserve — hosting lion, leopard, elephant, buffalo, and both black and white rhinoceros. The reserve was essentially depopulated of wildlife by 2003, and the comprehensive restocking program since then has been one of Africa's great conservation success stories. Thawale Lodge and Tented Camp offer the primary overnight options, combining conventional lodge rooms with proper tented camping. The landscape of Brachystegia woodland and rocky ridges along the Shire River is aesthetically different from the more open savanna of east Africa, and the density of wildlife relative to visitor numbers remains pleasantly high.

## 4. Nyika National Park, Northern Region

The Nyika Plateau in northern Malawi is one of Africa's most remarkable high-altitude ecosystems — a vast, undulating montane grassland at 2,200 to 2,600 metres elevation, studded with montane forest patches and supporting herds of eland, roan antelope, reedbuck, and zebra. Leopards are present in exceptional density for an African plateau. The Chelinda Camp in the park center has camping facilities alongside chalets and log cabins. Walking in the plateau grasslands offers an experience more like Scottish moorland than conventional African safari, and the combination of wildflowers in the rainy season and the crisp highland air is distinctive. Mountain biking and horse riding are also available from Chelinda.

## 5. Vwaza Marsh Wildlife Reserve, Northern Region

Adjacent to Nyika to the south, Vwaza Marsh protects a very different habitat — the floodplain and marsh grassland around Lake Kazuni in the Luangwa Valley extension. Elephants congregate at the lake in large numbers, and hippos, crocodiles, and a rich waterbird community inhabit the marsh. Bush camping at Kazuni Camp is basic and authentic — one of Malawi's most affordable wildlife camp experiences. The contrast with the nearby Nyika Plateau, reachable in a single day's drive, makes combining the two reserves into a single highland-lowland itinerary particularly rewarding.

## 6. Mulanje Mountain Forest Reserve, Southern Region

Mount Mulanje is a massive granite inselberg rising abruptly from the Shire Highlands to 3,002 metres at Sapitwa — the highest point in southern Africa outside of the Drakensberg. The mountain's plateau contains a series of forestry department huts accessible on foot from several trailheads. Camping within the national forest reserve is permitted at designated areas and beside the huts. The endemic Mulanje cedar — a massive, long-lived conifer found only on this mountain — dominates the montane forest. Multi-day circuits of the plateau are possible, with routes linking the Chambe, Lichenya, and Sapitwa areas through forest and open moorland.

## 7. Dzalanyama Forest Reserve, Central Region

The Dzalanyama Hills southwest of Lilongwe are covered in dry miombo woodland and represent one of the most accessible birdwatching camping destinations in Malawi. The forest reserve has simple forestry huts and camping areas and is managed partly through community conservation programs. The miombo woodland holds a suite of specialist birds — Miombo rock thrush, Chaplin's barbet, and numerous cisticola and sunbird species — alongside sable antelope, kudu, and occasionally leopard. The reserve is within two hours of Lilongwe, making it a practical bush camping option for travelers connecting through the capital.

## 8. Chintheche Strip, Lake Malawi Northern Lakeshore

The Chintheche strip along the northern shore of Lake Malawi is a 30-kilometre stretch of bays, beaches, and baobab trees between the Viphya Plateau escarpment and the lake. Several low-key beach camps operate here, offering simple campsites on sandy bays with good swimming and snorkeling. The area is much less visited than Cape Maclear and retains a genuinely quiet, village-lakeshore atmosphere. Fish eagles call from baobab trees over the water throughout the day. The Chintheche Inn, with its established campground, is the best-known stop on the strip, but smaller community camping areas are scattered along the road.

## 9. Mwabvi Wildlife Reserve, Southern Region

Mwabvi is Malawi's smallest and most remote wildlife reserve, a rugged landscape of rocky ridges, dry woodland, and the Mwabvi River cutting through sandstone gorges near the Mozambique border. The reserve was severely poached in the past but is being rehabilitated, and rough camping in the reserve with ranger guides is possible for adventurous visitors. Sable antelope, kudu, leopard, and diverse reptile and bird communities inhabit the reserve. Access requires a 4x4 vehicle and advance coordination with the reserve staff — the challenge is part of the experience.

## 10. Zomba Plateau, Southern Region

The Zomba Plateau above the old colonial capital of Zomba rises to around 2,000 metres and offers a cool retreat from the lowland heat. Plantation pine and cedar forest coexists with natural woodland and grassy plateau areas crossed by riding trails originally laid out for colonial administrators. The plateau campsite near the dam is surrounded by forest and streams, and the walking trails — clearly marked on locally produced maps — give access to viewpoints over the Shire Valley and Mulanje Mountain in the distance. The Mulunguzi Dam reservoir provides trout fishing for permit holders. The drive up the escarpment road itself is one of the most scenic in Malawi.
