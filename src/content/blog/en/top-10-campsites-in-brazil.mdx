Brazil is the fifth largest country in the world and contains the greatest biodiversity of any nation on Earth. Its camping environments span a range that no other country can match: the largest tropical rainforest, the world's most productive freshwater wetland, high table-land plateaus with unique scrub vegetation, Atlantic coastal rainforest remnants, and one of the most unusual landscape formations anywhere — the white dune and seasonal lagoon system of the Lençóis Maranhenses. Camping in Brazil requires navigation of a complex system of national parks, state parks, and private reserves, some with well-developed campground infrastructure and many with basic or improvised facilities. ## 1. Chapada Diamantina National Park, Bahia

The Chapada Diamantina is an eroded sandstone plateau in the state of Bahia, northeastern Brazil, characterized by tabletop mountains, waterfalls cascading off sandstone edges, river caves, and endemic flora. The town of Lençóis serves as the primary gateway. Camping is possible within the national park at designated areas and through guided multi-day trekking routes that traverse the plateau. The Pai Inácio viewpoint area has camping nearby; the trek to Vale do Pati, considered one of Brazil's finest multi-day hiking experiences, has basic camping at farm buildings along the route. The plateau receives relatively little rainfall in the dry season (May through September), making camping conditions generally reliable.

## 2. Lençóis Maranhenses National Park, Maranhão

The Lençóis Maranhenses is a coastal desert of white silica dunes extending along the Maranhão coast, seasonally punctuated by thousands of freshwater lagoons that form between the dunes after the wet season rains. The combination of desert-scale dunes and clear blue and green lagoons embedded within them is visually extraordinary and unlike any other landscape. Camping within the park is possible at the village of Atins on the eastern edge and through guide-accompanied overnight treks across the dune field. The ideal visiting window is June through September when the lagoons are at maximum extent.

## 3. Jalapão State Park, Tocantins

The Jalapão region in the state of Tocantins is a remote plateau of cerrado scrubland, ferruginous dunes, and clear-water rivers cutting through orange sandstone. The park contains the Fervedouro — natural spring pools where upwelling water pressure prevents visitors from touching the bottom, creating a floating sensation in crystalline water. Camping is basic and requires self-sufficiency; the main access is from the city of Palmas by rough dirt roads requiring four-wheel drive vehicles. The landscape has the character of a Brazilian equivalent to the American canyonlands — arid, colourful, and largely undeveloped.

## 4. Pantanal, Mato Grosso do Sul

The Pantanal is the world's largest tropical wetland, covering an area larger than France across the border region of Brazil, Bolivia, and Paraguay. The Brazilian Pantanal in Mato Grosso do Sul offers the best terrestrial wildlife viewing on the continent during the dry season (May through October), when water recession concentrates wildlife at remaining water points. Camping in the Pantanal is typically at fazendas (ranches) that have converted to wildlife tourism, where tent camping or simple accommodation is offered alongside guided boat and horse tours. Jaguar sightings along the Três Irmãos River confluence near Porto Jofre have become reliably frequent enough to form the basis of dedicated wildlife camping expeditions.

## 5. Serra da Canastra National Park, Minas Gerais

The Serra da Canastra is the source of the São Francisco River, one of Brazil's great waterways, rising from springs on the plateau edge. The national park contains some of the best-preserved cerrado (Brazilian savanna) habitat remaining and is home to maned wolves, giant anteaters, giant otters in the river systems, and marsh deer. The Casca d'Anta waterfall, where the São Francisco begins its descent from the plateau, is one of the largest waterfalls in Brazil. Designated camping is available near the main park entrance, and the plateau trails are suitable for day hikes and overnight treks.

## 6. Ilha do Cardoso State Park, São Paulo

Ilha do Cardoso is a large island at the southern end of the São Paulo state coast, accessible by boat from Cananéia. It protects an outstanding example of Atlantic Forest restinga (sandy coastal forest), mangrove, and beach ecosystem in a region where most Atlantic Forest has been cleared. Camping on the island is permitted at designated areas on beaches within the state park. The trails through the interior forest encounter capuchin monkeys, toucans, and a remarkable plant diversity in a landscape that functions as a living remnant of the coastal forest that once extended thousands of kilometres.

## 7. Chapada dos Veadeiros National Park, Goiás

The Chapada dos Veadeiros in the state of Goiás sits on one of the oldest land surfaces in South America — the pre-Cambrian Central Brazilian Plateau, where ancient quartzite formations create a landscape of waterfalls, crystal-clear rivers, and open cerrado. The town of Alto Paraíso de Goiás is the base for access. The national park has designated camping areas and several multi-day trekking routes. The region is known for its concentration of crystals in the rock formations and has attracted a significant alternative culture community to the surrounding villages.

## 8. Parque Nacional da Serra dos Órgãos, Rio de Janeiro

The Serra dos Órgãos is a dramatically eroded mountain range of the Atlantic Forest backing the city of Petrópolis, north of Rio de Janeiro. The park's famous feature is the Finger of God (Dedo de Deus) — a sheer basalt finger rising above the Atlantic Forest canopy. The park contains campgrounds at the Teresópolis and Petrópolis entrances, and the multi-day Petrópolis-Teresópolis traverse trail is one of the classic multi-day hiking routes in southeastern Brazil. The trail passes through montane Atlantic Forest at elevations up to 2200 metres, with high humidity and cloud forest conditions.

## 9. Anavilhanas Archipelago, Amazonas

The Anavilhanas Archipelago in the Rio Negro west of Manaus is the world's largest river archipelago, containing several hundred fluvial islands within the Anavilhanas National Park. Camping in the Amazon basin is most practically arranged through boat-based expeditions — sleeping in hammocks under tarpaulins on raised river bank platforms is the traditional form. The flooded forest (igapó) of the Rio Negro — its dark water stained by tannins from decaying vegetation — is the most accessible example of Amazonian flooded forest near a major city, and birdlife including parrots, kingfishers, and the red-and-green macaw is exceptional.

## 10. Pico da Bandeira, Caparaó National Park

Pico da Bandeira at 2892 metres is the highest point in the states of Minas Gerais and Espírito Santo and the third highest peak in Brazil. The Caparaó National Park has a well-developed trekking infrastructure including designated camping at Terreirão (1850 metres) and Pedra Menina (2100 metres). The summit ascent from the high camps is typically completed before dawn to arrive for sunrise, one of the classic high-altitude camping experiences in Brazil. The high plateau vegetation — campos de altitude — is unique to Brazilian highland environments and contains dozens of endemic species.
