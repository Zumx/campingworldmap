The Serengeti is one of the oldest and most intact ecosystems on Earth, a savanna extending 30,000 square kilometres across northwestern Tanzania into Kenya's Maasai Mara. The annual wildebeest migration — in which approximately 1.5 million wildebeest, 250,000 zebra, and 500,000 gazelle move in a clockwise circuit following the rains — is the largest overland animal migration on the planet. Camping in the Serengeti means being unenclosed in a landscape where lions, leopards, hyenas, elephants, and buffalo move freely past your tent. This is not camping in the recreational sense but in the literal one: sleeping in the wild, with all that implies. Tanzania National Parks (TANAPA) manages public campsites throughout the park; private concessions operate permanent tented camps at a different price point. Both categories offer proximity to wildlife that most visitors never experience from a lodge.

## 1. Seronera Public Campsite, Central Serengeti

Seronera is the administrative heart of the Serengeti, home to the main visitor centre and ranger headquarters in the park's central zone. The Seronera River valley is one of the best year-round wildlife viewing areas in the park, with a resident population of leopards that use the gallery forest trees as resting platforms in daylight. The public campsite near the Seronera visitor centre is basic — pit latrines, no showers, no electricity — but positioned in the middle of extraordinary wildlife density. Wildebeest and zebra pass through regularly, and hyenas are frequent nighttime visitors. Self-sufficient campers with tents and food supplies can stay here at a significantly lower cost than any lodge option in the park.

## 2. Lobo Wildlife Lodge Campsite, Northern Serengeti

The northern Serengeti near Lobo is the destination of the wildebeest migration from approximately July through October, when the herds have crossed north from the central plains and are in the Lamai Wedge between the Mara River and the Kenyan border. The Lobo area has a permanent campsite with better facilities than Seronera, including limited shower access. River crossings at the Mara River, where crocodiles take wildebeest in dramatic confrontations that have been filmed countless times, occur in this northern zone. The timing of crossings is unpredictable, and spending multiple days in the north during peak migration season maximises the chance of witnessing one.

## 3. Ndabaka Gate Campsite, Western Corridor

The western corridor of the Serengeti extends in a narrow arm toward Lake Victoria, and the Grumeti River crossing in this zone is the earlier of the two major river crossings on the migration route (the Mara crossing comes later). Ndabaka Gate has a public campsite that serves as an entry point for this less-visited part of the park. The Grumeti concession area adjacent holds some of the park's largest Nile crocodiles, which wait in the river pools through the dry season for the migration to arrive. The western corridor sees fewer visitors than the central Serengeti, which appeals to campers who want a less crowded experience of the ecosystem.

## 4. Ndutu Area Campsites, Ngorongoro Conservation Area

Ndutu is not technically inside Serengeti National Park but in the adjacent Ngorongoro Conservation Area at the park's southern boundary. The Ndutu and Masek lakes in the short grass plains attract wildebeest calving from approximately December through March — a period when tens of thousands of calves are born, drawing in cheetahs, lions, and hyenas. Ndutu Safari Lodge campsite and the TANAPA public campsite at Ndutu provide access to this calving spectacle, which many naturalists consider as compelling as the river crossings. The flat, open lake plains allow 360-degree wildlife viewing and are some of the best cheetah-viewing terrain in Africa.

## 5. Soit Le Murteti Campsite, Western Serengeti

This TANAPA-managed site in the less-visited western reaches of the park is positioned away from the main tourist circuits and provides a sense of solitude unusual in a park that attracts over a million visitors annually. Water availability requires self-sufficiency or advance coordination with park rangers. The surrounding bush is typical western Serengeti — acacia woodland and rocky kopjes (isolated granite outcrops) that serve as lion and leopard resting areas through the midday heat. The birdlife in the western woodland is exceptional: various hornbills, rollers, and the spectacular lilac-breasted roller are common throughout the day.

## 6. Klein's Camp Campsite Area, Northern Serengeti

The Klein's private game reserve concession on the Kuka Hills in the northeastern Serengeti has a long history in safari camping, originating with the German hunter who homesteaded the area in the early 20th century. Today it is a private concession that limits vehicle numbers in its area, providing a quality of wildlife experience — particularly for big cat sightings — that differs from the park's public zones. Camping in private concession areas adjacent to the park typically involves higher-end permanent tented camp infrastructure. The Kuka Hills area provides elevated terrain above the Serengeti plains with views across to Kenya and is in the path of migrating herds moving between Tanzania and the Maasai Mara.

## 7. Bologonja River Campsite, Northern Serengeti

The Bologonja River in the extreme north of the Serengeti flows near the Kenyan border at the Bolgonja Gate, one of the remote entry points into the park. A TANAPA public campsite here serves self-drive visitors crossing from the Maasai Mara side, though the border crossing is not open to all vehicles and advance permits are required. The river attracts resident hippo and crocodile, and the riparian forest holds primates including vervet monkeys and baboons. The game density in this remote corner during migration season is comparable to the better-known Lobo area with a fraction of the vehicle traffic.

## 8. Simba Kopjes Campsite, Central Serengeti

The Simba Kopjes south of Seronera are the archetypal Serengeti landscape: rounded granite outcrops rising from the grass plains with lions resting on their sun-warmed surfaces. The TANAPA campsite near the kopjes puts you within the environment that defined the setting for the plains scenes in film and nature documentary representations of the Serengeti. At night, the kopjes are active with hyenas and genets, and the sound of lions calling across the plains is a frequent accompaniment to sleep. The kopje area is best visited during the dry season months when the long grass has died back and wildlife concentrates near the remaining water sources.

## 9. Lake Ndutu Campsite, Ngorongoro Conservation Area

The woodland fringe around Lake Ndutu holds one of the densest concentrations of cheetah in East Africa during the calving season, when prey is abundant and accessible. Several TANAPA-designated camp areas around the lake allow self-sufficient campers to stay multiple nights. The lake itself is a soda lake — slightly alkaline — that attracts large concentrations of flamingos. The woodland-plain transition zone here is exceptional for species that prefer cover: serval cats, bat-eared foxes, and jackals are all commonly observed. The Ndutu area represents the southern migration route and the calving grounds that ensure the migration's next generation.

## 10. Kusini Camp Area, Southern Serengeti

The southern Serengeti plains between Ndutu and the central zone are among the most open landscapes in the ecosystem — flat, short-grass plains extending to the horizon with extraordinary visibility in all directions. During the wet season (December through May) this is where the wildebeest calving occurs, and the plains become dense with animals spread across the grassland in every direction. In the dry season the plains empty as animals move north, but resident plains game — Thomson's gazelle, topi, and hartebeest — remain year-round. A camp in this zone during the height of calving provides an experience of wildlife density that is difficult to encounter anywhere else in Africa.
