Finland's jokamiehenoikeus — the everyman's right — permits wild camping anywhere on public land for up to a few nights. In a country of 187,888 lakes, 75% forest coverage, and 338,000 square kilometres with fewer than 6 million inhabitants, this right produces one of the world's finest wild camping environments. Organised campsites are well-maintained and provide a base for longer stays, but Finnish camping culture genuinely values the ability to pitch anywhere on lakeside rock, forest meadow, or fell ridge without requiring a designated site.

## 1. Virtaniemi, Inari, Lapland

On the shore of Lake Inari — Finland's third-largest lake, 80 km long and studded with over 3,000 islands — Virtaniemi is a remote campsite in the far north accessible by a long gravel road from Inari village. The site provides canoe hire and access to the lake island system. In June and July, the midnight sun allows 24-hour activity; in August, the first hints of autumn colour arrive in the fells. The Sami heritage centre in Inari village is the premier museum for northern Finnish indigenous culture.

## 2. Lomakylä Timitraniemi Camping, Savonlinna, South Savo

In the Finnish Lake District near Savonlinna — famous for its Olavinlinna Castle and annual opera festival — this campsite on the lake shore provides access to the archipelago of islands in the Saimaa lake system. The Saimaa ringed seal, found only in Lake Saimaa, is one of the world's most critically endangered pinnipeds; their presence in the lake system makes this one of the rarer wildlife camping experiences in Europe.

## 3. Teretinniemi, Kuhmo, Kainuu

In the Kuhmo area of eastern Finland on the Russian border — the "land of bears" where Finland's brown bear and wolverine populations are most accessible. The campsite provides a base for bear-watching hides operated by local companies; most watching is from overnight hides at bait sites on the forest fringe. Finland has the densest brown bear population in the EU outside Romania.

## 4. Lapinniemi Telttailualue, Tampere

A designated tenting area (telttailualue) on Lapinniemi point in the Näsijärvi lake system at the edge of Tampere — Finland's third city and a strong candidate for the world's most inland large city. Tampere sits between two large lakes on a ridge, and the lake access from urban campsites here is immediate. The Vapriikki museum complex and the old industrial district are walkable from the water.

## 5. Ahvenisen Uimaranta, South Karelia

A small public camping area at a lake swimming beach in South Karelia near the Russian border region of historical Karelian culture. The South Karelian landscape of birch-fringed lakes, wildflower meadows, and Orthodox churches reflects the area's complex history. Lappeenranta, 40 km west, has the southernmost Saimaa canal access.

## 6. Suomu Telttailualue, Kemijärvi, Lapland

In the Pyhä-Luosto national park area of southern Lapland, this tenting area is set in an old-growth pine and birch fell landscape. The Pyhä fell hiking circuit is accessible from nearby. September sees the ruska — the Lapland autumn colour when birch, aspen, and dwarf shrubs turn orange, yellow, and red across the fells in one of the world's most dramatic seasonal colour events. Wild reindeer are common.

## 7. Wild Camping on Lake Oulujärvi, Kainuu

Lake Oulujärvi — 1,000 square kilometres, Finland's fifth-largest lake — is a major wild camping destination for canoeists paddling the Kainuu route. The lake shore is public land throughout, and the island archipelago in the western section provides secluded tent sites accessible only by water. The surrounding forests hold capercaillie, crane, and osprey in summer.

## 8. Anssilan Maatila, Jyväskylä Region

A farm camping site in central Finland near the city of Jyväskylä — the region with the highest lake density in the country. Farm campsite stays (maatilamajoitus) are a distinct Finnish tradition combining tent or caravan camping with working farm access. This region's lake cycling routes are well-developed and the forest mushroom and berry picking in late summer is exceptional.

## 9. Camping in Urho Kekkonen National Park, Saariselkä, Lapland

Finland's largest national park at 2,530 square kilometres, UKK National Park contains a backcountry hut and lean-to network that allows multi-day fell hiking with sheltered camping options. Tents are permitted throughout; the hut system provides cooking and sleeping shelter for hire by advance booking through Metsähallitus. The Saariselkä fell terrain is above the treeline for most of the park — open, tundra-like landscape with enormous views.

## 10. Maatilamajoitus Hirvola, North Karelia

A farm accommodation site on the edge of the Karelia birch forest zone offering tent pitches alongside traditional Finnish farm experience. North Karelia's Karelian pies (karjalanpiirakka) and sour rye bread are distinct regional foods; the landscape here transitions between the Lake District to the south and the Kainuu taiga to the north.

## Practical notes for Finnish camping

The everyman's right permits camping for one to two nights at any location on public land, further from any house than is respectful. Campfires are permitted where no fire risk exists; fire prohibition periods (metsäpalovaroitus) are broadcast nationally and must be observed. Finland's mosquito season peaks in June and July in the north — a head net is essential in Lapland. Wild berries (cloudberry, bilberry, lingonberry) are free to pick under the everyman's right.
