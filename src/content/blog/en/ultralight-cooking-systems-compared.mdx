Cooking system choice is one of the most discussed decisions in ultralight backpacking, partly because the differences between systems have real consequences for pack weight, fuel logistics, and meal quality, and partly because committed users of each type tend toward strong opinions. The core trade-off is straightforward: alcohol stoves are extremely light and use cheap, widely available fuel but are slow in cold or windy conditions; canister stoves are convenient, wind-resistant, and fast but carry the weight of the canister whether it is full or nearly empty; wood-burning stoves are the only system with no fuel to carry but require dry wood and produce smoke that deposits residue on cookware and is prohibited in many high-use areas. What the right choice is depends on where you are going, in what season, and how much you value simplicity versus performance.

## Alcohol Stoves: The Ultralight Standard

Alcohol stoves are the defining cooking system of the ultralight backpacking movement, popularised in the early 2000s by thru-hikers looking to reduce base weight below the thresholds achievable with canister systems. The stoves themselves — either commercially produced models like the Vargo Triad or Toaks titanium alcohol stove, or homemade designs from soda cans — weigh between 15 and 30 grams, compared to 70 to 100 grams for a typical canister stove head.

Denatured alcohol (ethanol-methanol mix) and methylated spirits are the standard fuels in North America and Europe respectively, available in hardware stores, outdoor shops, and some grocery stores. Isopropyl alcohol at 99 percent concentration works adequately but burns slightly dirtier. Fuel can be carried in lightweight soft-flask bottles rather than the rigid metal canisters required for gas systems.

The practical limitations are real. Alcohol stoves perform poorly below approximately 5 degrees Celsius, where the fuel's vapour pressure drops and boil times extend significantly or the stove fails to achieve a useful burn. In wind, an unshielded alcohol stove loses heat rapidly, and good windscreen design is essential. Simmering and flame control are not practically achievable on most alcohol stove designs — you light the stove, put the pot on, and wait for the fuel to burn through. Cooking anything more complex than boiling water or rehydrating a meal is difficult.

For warm-weather three-season backpacking on trails with reliable resupply points, particularly long-distance trails like the Appalachian Trail where denatured alcohol is available in hardware stores at many trailhead towns, the alcohol stove system is genuinely the best choice by weight and cost.

## Canister Gas Stoves: Convenience and Performance

Canister stoves use pressurised isobutane-propane gas canisters — the Lindal-valve standard used by MSR, Jetboil, Snow Peak, BRS, and most other manufacturers is interchangeable across brands, so canister compatibility is not usually a concern. A small canister weighs approximately 90 to 100 grams full; a canister stove head weighs between 25 grams for a BRS-3000T (one of the lightest available) and 115 grams for an integrated canister system like a Jetboil Flash.

The advantages of canister systems are significant: instant ignition with a piezo lighter, precise simmer control, consistent performance from ignition to fuel exhaustion, and substantially better cold-weather performance than alcohol (though the isobutane-propane mix does lose pressure in cold conditions — below -5 Celsius, performance degrades). The integrated systems like the Jetboil combine stove, heat exchanger, and cup into a single unit with boil times of around two minutes for half a litre, significantly faster than alcohol systems.

The weight disadvantage is not just the stove head but the canister. A 100-gram canister contains enough fuel for approximately 3 to 5 days of basic cooking. For a 10-day trip, two canisters are prudent, adding 180 to 200 grams of canister weight before accounting for the fuel itself. Additionally, empty canisters cannot be recycled in most municipal facilities without specialist equipment, and throwing punctured canisters into general waste is discouraged. For winter camping and four-season use, canister systems with inverted-canister technology or liquid-feed regulators maintain performance at low temperatures.

## Wood-Burning Stoves: The No-Fuel Option

Wood-burning stoves — the best-known designs being the BioLite CampStove (with built-in thermoelectric generator for charging small devices) and the simpler Solo Stove and Firebox range — use sticks, twigs, pine cones, and dry biomass as fuel, eliminating carried fuel weight entirely for trips where wood is available. The Solo Stove Titan weighs 400 grams and the Solo Stove Lite 255 grams — heavier than either alcohol or canister alternatives, but with no fuel weight to carry.

The cooking experience with a wood stove demands more attention than either alternative. You continuously feed small sticks to maintain a consistent burn temperature, which works against efficient cooking and produces visible smoke. The base of your pot accumulates soot that transfers to pack contents unless the pot is stored in a dedicated stuff sack. The system is prohibited in fire-restricted areas and national parks during fire bans — which effectively rules it out for much of the American West during summer.

The BioLite design solves some of these problems by using a thermoelectric module to power a fan that forces combustion air into the firebox, dramatically reducing smoke and improving efficiency. The same module charges a battery pack. This engineering represents a genuinely useful capability for longer off-grid trips where device charging is otherwise impossible. The total BioLite CampStove 2 Plus weight is 935 grams — significant, but serving multiple functions.

## Making the Choice

The decision matrix for most backpackers resolves to the following: canister gas for cold-weather, high-altitude, and winter camping where alcohol performance is unreliable and cooking control matters; alcohol for warm three-season backpacking where weight and fuel cost are the primary variables; wood for specific environments (humid forests with reliable dead wood, no fire restrictions) and for campers who prioritise no carried fuel over convenience.

A fourth option that deserves mention is the cold-soak method — no stove at all, simply rehydrating dried food in water at ambient temperature over an hour or more. This works for most backpacking meals (instant oatmeal, couscous, many freeze-dried meals, ramen) and reduces cook system weight to a small jar and a spoon. For thru-hikers in summer, it is a serious option.

## Summary Weights and Fuel Costs

A useful way to benchmark the systems is their functional total weight for a four-day trip: an alcohol system (stove, 200ml fuel, 900ml titanium pot, windscreen, lighter) comes to approximately 200 to 250 grams. A canister system (stove head, 100g canister, 750ml titanium pot, lighter) comes to approximately 350 to 450 grams depending on stove head choice. A wood stove system (Solo Stove Lite, 750ml pot, lighter, pot cozy) comes to approximately 450 to 550 grams. For trips longer than seven days, the wood and alcohol systems' weight advantage over canisters grows as canister trips require additional fuel.

Fuel cost per meal favours alcohol significantly: a litre of denatured alcohol costs between two and four US dollars and covers approximately 20 to 30 boils, versus eight to twelve US dollars for a 100-gram canister covering roughly similar usage. Over a long trail, this cost difference adds up across dozens of resupply purchases.
