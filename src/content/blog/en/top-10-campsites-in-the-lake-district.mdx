The Lake District is England's largest national park and, since 2017, a UNESCO World Heritage Site recognised for its cultural landscape — the combination of mountains, lakes, and farming practices that shaped the poetry of Wordsworth, the illustration of Beatrix Potter, and the landscape aesthetics that drove 19th-century tourism. It is also England's most popular walking destination. On summer weekends, Windermere's eastern shore and the approaches to the Scafell Pike summit are busy with people, but the western valleys — Eskdale, Dunnerdale, Ennerdale — remain surprisingly quiet. Camping here ranges from fully serviced lakeside sites with electric hookups to wild camping on the high fells, where England's right to wild camp in most of the national park allows you to pitch above the intake walls without restriction.

## 1. Great Langdale Campsite, Great Langdale

The Great Langdale valley is the classic Lake District camping location: dramatic, glacier-carved, and surrounded by the Langdale Pikes, Bowfell, and Crinkle Crags. The National Trust's Great Langdale campsite is large and well-managed, sitting in the valley floor beside Great Langdale Beck. The Dungeon Ghyll Old Hotel is minutes away, serving food and beer to walkers since the 18th century. Trails from the campsite access almost every major summit in the central Lake District within a day walk — Scafell Pike via the Rossett Gill route is a full but achievable day from here. The campsite takes advance bookings and walk-in pitches depending on availability.

## 2. Side Farm Campsite, Patterdale

Patterdale is the quieter southern approach to the Helvellyn range, and Side Farm is a traditional Lake District farm campsite on the eastern shore of Ullswater. The farm has operated camping for decades and maintains the informal character of fell-farm camping: basic facilities, honest prices, sheep in adjacent fields. Ullswater is widely regarded as the most beautiful lake in the district, particularly the southern section around Aira Force waterfall and the wooded shore below Gowbarrow Fell — where Wordsworth and Dorothy saw the daffodils immortalised in verse. The Ullswater Steamers stop at Patterdale pier, enabling car-free exploration of the lake's length.

## 3. Wasdale Head Campsite, Wasdale

Wasdale Head is a remote valley accessible only by a narrow road along the southern shore of Wast Water, England's deepest lake. The campsite at the head of the valley is owned by the National Trust and is the traditional base for Scafell Pike ascents via the Lingmell Col route. The Wasdale Head Inn nearby has served climbers and walkers since the 19th century and has a museum acknowledging its role in the development of British rock climbing — the crags above the valley on Scafell and Pillar were among the first technical climbs in England. The valley's isolation means the night sky is exceptionally dark, with no significant light pollution in any direction.

## 4. Syke Farm Campsite, Buttermere

Buttermere village sits between two lakes — Buttermere and Crummock Water — with the fells of Haystacks, Fleetwith Pike, and the High Stile ridge rising on all sides. Syke Farm is a working farm site in the village with the minimal commercial development that characterises the best Lake District farm camping. The Buttermere circular walk, taking in both lakes and climbing to the ridge above, is one of the most celebrated day hikes in northern England. Haystacks above the campsite was the fell chosen by Alfred Wainwright to have his ashes scattered, as described in his final Pictorial Guide to the Lakeland Fells.

## 5. Low Wray Campsite, Ambleside

Low Wray is a National Trust campsite on the quieter western shore of Windermere, accessed by narrow lanes away from the busier eastern shore road through Bowness. The lakeside pitches at Low Wray look directly across the water toward the Langdale Pikes, and the campsite has its own jetty and rowing boat hire. The Cumbria Way long-distance footpath passes through, and the campsite's location between Ambleside and Hawkshead gives access to the Beatrix Potter properties managed by the National Trust. The site is large and modern with good facilities but retains a more peaceful character than Windermere's eastern shore sites.

## 6. Gillside Farm Campsite, Glenridding

Glenridding is the northern access point for Helvellyn, England's third-highest mountain and one of its most visited. Gillside Farm sits above the village in a gentle bowl with views toward the Helvellyn ridge. The Grisedale trail to Helvellyn via Striding Edge — the knife-blade arête that demands hands-on scrambling in its upper section — begins from Glenridding. The descent via Swirral Edge closes a horseshoe circuit that most experienced hill walkers consider the finest day in the Lake District. The campsite is a popular base before early starts for the Striding Edge route. Ullswater's steamer service is accessible from the village pier a short walk downhill.

## 7. Coniston Hall Campsite, Coniston

Coniston Water is connected in public imagination to Arthur Ransome's Swallows and Amazons novels and to Donald Campbell's fatal water speed record attempt in 1967, for which he is commemorated in the Ruskin Museum in the village. Coniston Hall campsite is a National Trust site on the lake's western shore, set in meadow and woodland with a small shingle beach and lake access for paddlers. The campsite is large and popular but the setting beside the lake with the Old Man of Coniston rising above is genuinely impressive. The Gondola steam yacht operates on the lake from the nearby National Trust pier at Monk Coniston.

## 8. Fisherground Campsite, Eskdale

Eskdale is one of the Lake District's quieter western valleys and Fisherground is a farm campsite of exceptional character — flat, well-drained pitches in a woodland clearing with access to the River Esk for paddling. The Ravenglass and Eskdale narrow-gauge railway, known as the Ratty, runs through the valley and stops at the Fisherground halt effectively next to the campsite, enabling car-free access from the Cumbrian coast. The valley is the approach for Scafell Pike via the Cam Spout route, a longer but quieter approach than the Wasdale or Langdale routes. Boot village at the head of the valley has a pub and a small general store.

## 9. Skelwith Fold Caravan Park, Ambleside

Not exclusively for caravans despite its name, Skelwith Fold is a large, well-run site in ancient woodland above the River Brathay west of Ambleside, one of the better-equipped sites in the district with good shower blocks and level pitches. The woodland setting with mature oaks is particularly attractive in autumn. Ambleside town centre is a 30-minute walk or short drive, with full shopping, the Armitt Museum, and the historic Bridge House — a tiny 18th-century building spanning Stock Ghyll. The Langdale and Grasmere valleys are easily reachable within 20 minutes.

## 10. Quiet Site, Ullswater

Quiet Site near Watermillock on Ullswater's western shore is a privately owned campground that takes its name seriously, banning generator use and enforcing a low-noise policy that makes it particularly attractive to those who find the larger sites noisy in peak season. The pitches are terraced into a hillside with Ullswater views, and the site has an indoor heated area and on-site cafe. Aira Force waterfall, one of the most visited natural features in the Lake District with a 20-metre drop in a wooded gorge, is a short walk along the lakeside path. The Ullswater Way long-distance path circles the full lake and passes the campsite boundary.
