The treeline — the elevation at which trees can no longer grow due to cold temperatures, short growing seasons, and high wind exposure — marks one of the most dramatic transitions in landscape character available to mountain campers. Below it, you have shelter, windbreaks, fuel for fires, anchor points for tarps and hammocks, and the spatial enclosure that forest camping provides. Above it, you step into an open world of rock, sky, and weather where the view in every direction is unobstructed, the wind is rarely absent, and the weather can change from benign to dangerous in a period measured in minutes.

Above-treeline camping is many experienced campers' preferred environment — the solitude, the light quality, the proximity to peaks and glaciers, and the particular clarity that comes from sleeping in a place with no trees between you and the sky are rewards that forest camping does not offer. But it requires equipment, skills, and judgment calibrated to conditions that are more demanding than those faced at lower elevations. Understanding the specific challenges of above-treeline camping and preparing for them is what separates a memorable high-altitude camp from a miserable or dangerous one.

## Site Selection

Finding a suitable campsite above treeline is more complex than finding one in forest. The absence of natural windbreaks means that site orientation relative to prevailing wind is the primary selection criterion. Look for rock outcrops, slight depressions, or ridge shelters that break the wind on the side from which weather typically arrives. In the American Rockies, afternoon thunderstorms typically come from the southwest; in the Alps, weather frequently develops from the southwest and west. Positioning the tent's end (rather than the broad side) into the wind, and bracing this position with available rock features, reduces wind loading on the tent significantly.

Avoid camping on the actual ridgeline, which receives maximum wind and lightning exposure. A site 50 to 100 metres below the ridge on the leeward side typically combines a good view with a meaningfully reduced wind speed and a lower lightning risk. On broad alpine plateaux without pronounced ridge features, choose the most level ground available and note the direction of any available terrain shelter.

Avoid camping on fragile vegetation communities — the cushion plants, sedge lawns, and lichen mats that grow above treeline are extremely slow-growing and highly sensitive to physical disturbance. Rock, gravel, or snow surfaces are the appropriate camping locations when available. Camp on snow if it is consolidated enough to be stable; snow prevents vegetation damage and is a renewable surface in a way that alpine plant communities are not.

## Weather and Lightning

Above-treeline weather is more variable and more extreme than at lower elevations, and the lack of shelter means that caught-out campers have no retreat from weather short of descending below treeline. The fundamental rule is: know the weather forecast for your area before departing, monitor conditions actively throughout the day, and make descent decisions early rather than waiting until a storm has developed overhead.

Lightning is the primary weather danger above treeline. Thunderstorms in alpine areas develop rapidly, frequently arriving by early afternoon in summer when surface heating creates instability in a moist airmass. A well-established rule of thumb is to be below treeline or in a sheltered valley position by noon. If a thunderstorm catches you above treeline: move away from the summit and any high ground, avoid isolated trees on the treeline edge, avoid caves and overhangs (ground current from a nearby strike can flow through these), and assume a lightning crouch position (crouched low, feet together, hands off the ground) if you are in an open area with no immediate shelter available.

## Wind and Cold Management

Wind at altitude accelerates dramatically relative to valley floors — a 20 kph valley wind may be 50 to 70 kph on an exposed ridgeline at the same time of day. Windchill from a strong wind at altitude can create effective temperatures far below the thermometer reading. A summer day that is comfortable in a valley can be genuinely cold on an exposed alpine campsite.

The tent you use above treeline should be rated for four-season or mountain use — double-walled with a full rain fly reaching close to the ground, with vestibules for kit storage and storm entries, and with a pole system designed to flex under high wind rather than snapping. Guy-line all the tent's anchor points including the mid-poles and vestibule tie-outs, and stake with pickets or rocks rather than standard stakes in rocky alpine soil. Practice setting up your tent in wind before the trip; managing a tent in a strong gust during setup is a skill that benefits from prior experience.

## Gear Specific to Above-Treeline Camping

Above treeline, the gear requirements tighten compared to forest camping. A sleeping bag rated at least 5 degrees Celsius below the lowest expected overnight temperature is appropriate — nights above 3,000 metres in the Rockies or Alps can drop below 0 degrees Celsius even in midsummer. A closed-cell foam sleeping pad provides thermal insulation beneath you that an air pad alone does not reliably sustain against cold ground; many alpine campers use both.

Cooking requires a stove that functions reliably in wind and at altitude — isobutane canisters perform poorly in cold temperatures (below about 5 degrees Celsius, canister pressure drops significantly), and liquid-fuel stoves (MSR WhisperLite International or similar) maintain output more reliably in cold conditions. A wind shield around the stove reduces fuel consumption substantially.

## Leave No Trace Above Treeline

Alpine ecosystems are among the most ecologically sensitive camping environments, and the impact of campers is magnified by the slow recovery rates of high-altitude vegetation. Camp on rock and gravel rather than vegetation whenever possible. Carry a catheter-and-bag solid waste system (WAG bags) in sensitive alpine areas where soil is shallow and human waste decomposition is extremely slow. Carry out all waste including food scraps; do not bury food waste in shallow alpine soil where it does not decompose and attracts wildlife.
