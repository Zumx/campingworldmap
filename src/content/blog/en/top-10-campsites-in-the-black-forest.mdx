The Black Forest, or Schwarzwald, stretches roughly 160 kilometres along the western edge of Baden-Württemberg, bordered by the Rhine plain to the west and the Swabian highlands to the east. Despite its popularity among German domestic tourists, it remains a genuinely forested landscape — dense spruce and silver fir on the higher ground, mixed broadleaf on the lower slopes, with valleys carved by small rivers and dotted with traditional farmhouses and spa towns. Camping here means waking to birdsong in conifer woodland, cycling signed trail networks, and hiking routes that range from gentle lake circuits to multi-day traverses of the Westweg. The region's infrastructure is reliable, facilities are high quality, and camping is possible from early April through October.

## 1. Camping Langenwald, Freudenstadt

Freudenstadt is one of the major gateways to the northern Black Forest and possesses Germany's largest market square, a remarkable piece of 17th-century Renaissance town planning. Camping Langenwald sits in forest on the outskirts of town, well managed with modern sanitary facilities and a relaxed atmosphere suited to longer stays. The DKV-Weg and Westweg long-distance trails pass nearby, and the town centre is reachable by bicycle. The altitude of around 700 metres keeps temperatures reasonable even in summer. The campsite operates year-round and is one of the better-known winter camping options in the region.

## 2. Campingplatz Bankenhof, Hinterzarten

Hinterzarten is a small, well-heeled village in the central Black Forest near the Titisee, surrounded by some of the densest trail networks in the region. Campingplatz Bankenhof is a well-established family site with solid facilities and direct access to hiking paths that connect to the Felsberg plateau and the Ravenna Gorge viaduct, one of the most photographed railway structures in Germany. The village has good bakeries and a supermarket. In winter, the area receives reliable snow and cross-country ski trails pass close to the campsite. It is one of the most consistently recommended Black Forest campsites among long-distance hikers completing the Westweg.

## 3. Camping am Titisee, Titisee-Neustadt

Titisee is the most visited lake in the Black Forest, a natural glacial lake about two kilometres long with a regulated water level maintained by a dam at its outlet. Camping am Titisee sits directly on the lakeshore, offering pitches with water views and direct swimming access. The lake is warm enough for comfortable swimming from June through early September. Pedaloes and canoes are available for hire from the lakefront. The village of Titisee-Neustadt beyond the tourist strip retains a working-town character with good transport links to Freiburg im Breisgau, the largest city in the southern Black Forest, about 30 kilometres west.

## 4. Campingplatz Alpirsbach, Alpirsbach

Alpirsbach is a small town built around a remarkably intact Romanesque monastery, founded in 1095 and now one of Baden-Württemberg's most visited medieval sites. The campsite occupies the Kinzig River valley below the town and is compact but well-positioned for exploring the Kinzig valley on foot or by bicycle along the valley cycle path. The local brewery, Alpirsbacher Klosterbräu, has operated since the monastery's founding tradition and produces several beers sold throughout Germany. The town's Saturday market draws local producers from the surrounding villages. The campsite is smaller and quieter than the major lake-side sites, attracting campers who prefer a village base over resort atmosphere.

## 5. Camping Schwarzwaldhorn, Bühlertal

Bühlertal is a narrow valley north of Baden-Baden where the Bühl stream flows through mixed forest below the Hornisgrinde, the highest peak in the northern Black Forest at 1,164 metres. Camping Schwarzwaldhorn is positioned mid-valley with good shade and access to trails leading up to the Hornisgrinde summit and its radio tower, from which views extend on clear days across the Rhine plain to the Vosges mountains in France. Baden-Baden, with its thermal baths, casino, and Belle Époque architecture, is about 20 kilometres north and easily reached by regional bus. The campsite is especially popular with motorcycle tourers who use the Black Forest's winding roads.

## 6. Campingplatz Herrenwies, Forbach

Herrenwies is one of the highest campground locations in the Black Forest, set on an elevated plateau at around 800 metres near a small reservoir. The surrounding terrain is open moorland transitioning to dense forest, which gives it a distinctly different character from the valley sites — cooler, more exposed, with wider views and a greater sense of remoteness. The Ruhstein pass road is nearby, popular with cyclists tackling the steep western ascent from the Rhine plain. The campsite is modest but functional and draws a clientele of hikers and mountain bikers who want proximity to the higher trails without commuting from a valley base.

## 7. Camping Wolfach, Wolfach

Wolfach stands at the confluence of the Wolf and Kinzig rivers, a small baroque town with a 17th-century palace and a glass-blowing tradition that persists at the Dorotheenhütte workshop, one of the few remaining hand-blown glass factories in Germany where visitors can watch production. The campsite lies along the Kinzig and serves as a staging point for the Gutach valley, home to the Vogtsbauernhof open-air museum of Black Forest farmhouses. The valley is also famous for producing Kirschwasser, the cherry schnapps central to Black Forest gateau. Cycling the valley is straightforward on flat riverside paths.

## 8. Campingplatz Müllerwiese, Schiltach

Schiltach is among the most visually striking towns in the Black Forest, a steep-valleyed settlement of half-timbered houses that slopes from the river confluence up to a hilltop church. The campsite sits at the valley floor along the Kinzig, a short walk from the old town market square and the small Schiltach Museum documenting the town's history as a raft-building centre when logs were floated downstream to supply construction timber for the Rhine plain. The Schiltach valley cycle route connects north to Alpirsbach and south toward Rottweil. The campsite has reliable facilities and the proximity to the old town makes it one of the more atmospheric bases in the region.

## 9. Campingplatz Sulzbachtal, Bad Peterstal-Griesbach

Bad Peterstal-Griesbach is a pair of villages in the Rench valley forming one of the quieter spa towns in the northern Black Forest. The campsite in the Sulzbach side valley is shaded by mixed forest and positioned close to the Renchtal hiking routes that climb toward the Kniebis plateau. The area's mineral springs were bottled commercially in the 19th century and several are still accessible as free drinking fountains in the village. Oppenau, five kilometres north, has a weekly market and useful shops. The valley sees less tourist traffic than the more marketed areas around Titisee or Baden-Baden and maintains a local character through the summer season.

## 10. Camping Münstertal, Münstertal

Münstertal is a long, narrow valley south of Freiburg cutting deep into the southern Black Forest toward the Belchen summit, the highest point in the northern range at 1,414 metres. The campsite in the valley floor is close to the village of Untermünstertal and the former Benedictine monastery at St. Trudpert, founded in the 7th century. The ascent to the Belchen summit can be done on foot from the valley in around three hours, or by cable car from the upper valley. On clear days, Alpine views from the summit extend to the Swiss Alps and, with exceptional visibility, to Mont Blanc. The Münstertal is also the start of the E1 European long-distance path heading south to Basel.
