Choosing where to sleep in the wilderness is a skill that experienced campers develop over years of trial and error. A good campsite is the difference between a restorative night and a miserable one: between waking dry and comfortable on a warm dawn or lying sleepless in pooling water, battered by wind that found the one gap in your tent's shelter. The factors that make a campsite good are not mysterious, but they interact in ways that require simultaneous consideration of terrain, weather, ecology, regulations, and simple human comfort.

## Start with the Map Before You Start Hiking

Wild campsite selection begins before you enter the field. Topographic maps, satellite imagery, and recent trip reports give you a framework for where camps are likely to be feasible. Look for terrain features associated with good camping: relatively flat benches above drainage channels, sheltered areas behind ridge features that break prevailing winds, proximity to water sources without being directly adjacent to them.

Identify candidate areas in advance. In mountain terrain, contour lines that are widely spaced indicate gentle slopes suitable for a tent; closely packed contour lines indicate steep ground where flat camping spots are rare. North-facing slopes in the northern hemisphere stay cold and often hold snow later in spring. South-facing slopes receive more sun and dry faster after rain.

Trip reports on platforms such as AllTrails, iOverlander, and specific trail forums often contain specific campsite location information with photographs. Other hikers have already done the reconnaissance for popular routes; use their knowledge before committing to a schedule.

## The 60-Metre Rule and Water Sources

Leave No Trace guidelines recommend camping at least 60 metres from water sources — rivers, lakes, ponds, and seasonal streams. This distance serves two purposes: it prevents contamination of water sources from human waste and cooking activity, and it protects riparian vegetation and wildlife that concentrates near water. Animals that depend on water access should be able to approach at night without a human campsite directly blocking the bank.

On flat terrain, 60 metres is easily paced out. On steep terrain where the only flat ground is adjacent to a stream, the rule requires more creative interpretation — a raised bench ten metres above and back from the stream edge satisfies the spirit of the rule even if not precisely the distance. Use judgment informed by the principle rather than strict measurement in constrained terrain.

The 60-metre rule also applies to trails. Camping near trails creates visual impact for other users and increases the spread of human waste around trail corridors. Choose sites where your tent is not visible from the main trail wherever possible.

## Ground Surface and Drainage

The ideal campsite ground is durable, level within a few degrees of horizontal, and free of fragile vegetation. Rock surfaces, gravel, dry grass, and previously impacted soil are all durable; cryptobiotic soil crusts in desert environments, delicate moss, and wetland vegetation are not. In high-use wilderness areas, using existing established campsites rather than creating new ones prevents the spread of bare, compacted ground across a wider area.

Look at the ground surface around a candidate site for signs of previous water accumulation: grey mineral deposits (indicating evaporated standing water), dark staining on rocks, or the bowl-shaped depression that channels water during rain. Even a slight depression in otherwise level ground can become a puddle under the tent during heavy rain. The best tent site has a subtle slope — perhaps two to three degrees — that allows water to drain away from rather than into your sleeping area.

Remove rocks and sticks from under your tent footprint but avoid large-scale ground modification. Cutting vegetation, digging trenches around tents, and creating artificial wind breaks cause lasting impacts. Work with what the terrain provides.

## Wind Exposure and Natural Shelter

Wind direction is one of the most important comfort factors in campsite selection, yet many campers give it insufficient attention. Prevailing wind direction at your location — determined by checking forecasts, understanding local terrain, or observing vegetation lean — tells you where to position shelter. A natural windbreak can be a ridge, a boulder field, a dense stand of low trees, or a convex terrain feature that deflects airflow overhead.

Camping on ridgelines looks dramatic and offers spectacular views but exposes you to maximum wind. A site 50 to 100 metres below a ridgeline on the lee side, sheltered from the prevailing direction, is typically far more comfortable and protects your shelter from the mechanical stress that sustained high wind creates.

In desert environments, rocks absorb heat during the day and radiate it through the night. A sheltered site among boulders in desert terrain is significantly warmer overnight than open flat ground nearby. This can be either comfortable or oppressively hot depending on the season and your temperature preferences.

## Overhead Hazards: The Widow Maker Assessment

Before finalizing any camp under or near trees, look up. Dead branches overhead — widow makers — are a genuine and underappreciated hazard. In forested environments, wind during the night can bring down branches that are not obviously near failure during calm daytime assessment. Look for large dead branches, trees with recently detached bark, leaning trees, or trees with root systems partially exposed. Maintain a clearance distance of at least the height of any overhead tree.

This applies equally in established campgrounds where tree maintenance may lag behind tree health. Dead wood in campground trees is a known source of campground injuries in jurisdictions that track such data.

Rockfall risk applies in mountain terrain. Camping directly below rock faces, cliff edges, or steep scree slopes is hazardous. Assess whether debris from above has fallen recently by looking at the ground surface around candidate sites — fresh rock scars and disturbed rubble indicate active rockfall.

## Comfort Factors That Matter for Sleep

Beyond safety and ecology, pure comfort determines sleep quality. A slight depression in the ground under your sleeping hip causes cumulative discomfort over an eight-hour night that no sleeping pad fully compensates for. Take two minutes to lie on the ground at a candidate site before pitching a tent; the feeling of the ground surface before setup predicts the feeling after.

Sun exposure at your expected wake time is worth considering for morning camps. A site that receives early morning sun in cool conditions is warmer and more energizing to wake in. In hot climates, morning shade is preferable. In wet conditions, any site that receives sun for part of the day allows gear to dry.

Good preparation reduces the pressure of finding a site in failing daylight and gives you the confidence to push a little further toward the right spot rather than settling for a mediocre one.
