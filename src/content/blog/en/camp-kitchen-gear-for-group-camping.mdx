Cooking for a group of eight, twelve, or twenty people at a campsite is a fundamentally different challenge from individual or couple camping. The equipment that works beautifully for two people — a single-burner backpacking stove, a one-litre pot, a compact cutting board — scales poorly to feeding a crowd. Group camp cooking requires thinking in terms of volume, parallel cooking capacity, food safety over multiple days, and the logistics of washing up for many people simultaneously. Done well, the camp kitchen becomes the social heart of a group camping trip; done poorly, it means cold food, long waits, and a stressed cook.

The good news is that the investment in a solid group camp kitchen setup pays dividends across many trips. A high-quality two-burner propane stove, a large Dutch oven, a proper washing station, and a well-organised food storage system can last for years and serve group camping, backyard gathering, and disaster preparedness uses with equal effectiveness. What follows is a systematic walk through the gear categories that make large group camp cooking practical and enjoyable.

## Stoves for Group Cooking

The most important piece of group camp kitchen equipment is a multi-burner propane stove. Two-burner stoves like the Camp Chef Everest Two-Burner or the Coleman Classic Two-Burner are widely available and provide enough simultaneous cooking capacity for most groups up to twelve people, especially when combined with a Dutch oven or camp oven on the fire. For larger groups, a Camp Chef Expedition three-burner stove or a standalone camp griddle provides more surface area. Propane output (measured in BTUs) matters — a stove delivering 20,000 BTU per burner will boil a large pot of water in roughly half the time of a 10,000 BTU stove, which directly affects meal timing when you are cooking for a crowd.

Always carry more propane than you think you will need. A large group cooking three meals daily for four days can consume a surprising volume of gas, particularly if the stove is also used for heating dishwashing water. The one-pound disposable propane canisters are convenient but expensive per meal; the refillable bulk propane tank (a standard barbecue tank) via a hose adapter is considerably more economical for group use.

## Cookware: Pots, Pans, and Dutch Ovens

For group cooking, a 10- to 12-litre stainless steel pot is the foundation piece — it boils pasta for twelve, makes soup or stew for a crowd, and serves as the vessel for large-batch one-pot recipes. Two of these pots provides complete flexibility. A 12-inch cast iron skillet handles breakfast eggs, sauteed vegetables, and stovetop searing for groups. A 12-quart Dutch oven, suitable for coal cooking or stovetop use, opens up baked recipes — cornbread, cobblers, and slow-cooked stews — at a scale that works for a group.

The Camp Chef Dutch oven, Lodge cast iron Dutch ovens, and equivalent brands from GSI Outdoors all have excellent reputations. Cast iron is heavy but indestructible and heats with exceptional evenness. For group camping where weight is not a constraint (vehicle access, established campsite), cast iron is the right choice.

## Food Storage and Cooler Systems

Large-group camping food safety depends on a well-organised cold chain. Rotomolded hard coolers — brands including Yeti, RTIC, and Pelican — hold ice significantly longer than styrofoam or thin plastic coolers. A 150-litre cooler can hold food for ten people for three to four days with proper ice management: pre-chilling the cooler with ice the night before loading, organising food so frequently accessed items are near the top, and minimising the frequency of opening.

Organise the cooler into clear zones: one section for proteins (separately bagged and kept at the bottom where temperatures are coldest), one for dairy and eggs, one for pre-prepped meal components. Label everything with the meal it belongs to, reducing digging through a cold cooler when dinner prep begins. A second, smaller cooler dedicated to drinks means the food cooler is opened infrequently and maintains temperature better throughout the trip.

## Camp Tables and Work Surface

Group camp cooking requires actual work surface. A folding camp table — at minimum 180 x 60 centimetres — provides the cutting, prep, and plating space that makes cooking practical. The Coleman folding table and equivalent aluminium or plastic fold-flat tables are widely available and pack flat for vehicle transport. A second smaller table serves as the stove stand and keeps the cooking surface at a comfortable height. Organising the camp kitchen as a dedicated station — prep table, stove table, washing station — rather than improvising surfaces on cooler lids and tailgates improves the cooking process dramatically.

## Washing Station

The washing station is the most consistently under-resourced component of group camp kitchens. A three-basin washing setup — soak/scrub basin, rinse basin, and a draining rack — provides proper dishwashing hygiene for group camping. Camp suds-style biodegradable soap is required for washing near any water source; pack-out grey water is required in many national parks and wilderness areas.

A collapsible three-basin camp sink set (Texsport and similar brands make adequate options) stores compactly and sets up in minutes. Heat washing water on the stove and pour it into the basins — hot water cuts grease from cast iron and cookware far more effectively than cold water from the spigot. A folding drying rack keeps washed dishes off the ground and expedites drying.

## Utensils, Serving Equipment, and Spice Management

Group cooking requires serving-scale utensils: long-handled spoons, a large ladle, a sturdy spatula, and serving tongs in addition to cooking tongs. Carry a sharp 20-centimetre chef's knife and a heavy cutting board — group meals involve more prep chopping than individual meals, and the right knife makes the difference between enjoyable prep and a laborious chore. A mandoline slicer for large quantities of vegetables is a genuine efficiency tool for group camp cooking.

Pre-assemble a camp spice kit in a dedicated container — a small plastic organiser or a purpose-made camp spice rack — with the spices your menu requires. This prevents the universal camp cooking problem of realising that a needed spice is buried at the bottom of a bag under six other bags. A small bottle of quality cooking oil, salt in a wide-mouthed container, and pre-mixed spice blends in labelled bags cover most cooking needs.

## Planning and Prep Before Departure

The single biggest factor in successful group camp cooking is preparation done at home before departure. Pre-chop vegetables and store in sealed bags. Marinate proteins at home and freeze them — they serve as their own ice pack and thaw during the drive. Measure and bag dry spice blends for each recipe. Crack eggs into a sealed container if you plan an egg-heavy breakfast for the first morning, avoiding the fragility problem of carton eggs in a loaded vehicle.

[Check the map](/map) to confirm the facilities at your target campground — whether fire rings, cooking grills, and potable water are available directly affects which equipment you need to bring and which fire-based cooking methods are viable for your group.

Well-organised group camp cooking creates some of the most memorable shared meals available. The communal effort, the outdoors context, and the satisfaction of feeding people well from a temporary kitchen are rewards that repay the logistics required to make it work.
