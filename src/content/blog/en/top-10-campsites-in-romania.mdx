Romania holds the largest populations of brown bear, wolf, and lynx in the European Union — roughly 6,000 bears, 3,000 wolves, and 1,000 lynx in the Carpathian forests. The Danube Delta is the largest wetland in Europe outside Russia and one of the world's great bird migration corridors. The Transylvanian plateau's traditional meadow agriculture, largely unchanged for centuries, supports a flowering plant diversity that has been lost throughout most of western Europe. This wildlife and landscape richness, combined with a campsite infrastructure that is developing rapidly, makes Romania one of the most rewarding camping destinations in Europe.

## 1. Camping Rânca, Parâng Mountains, Gorj

In the southern Carpathians above the Jiu Valley, Rânca is one of Romania's highest mountain campsites at 1,600 metres, accessible by a winding road from Novaci. The Parâng massif's high plateau walks extend across the ridge system with views to the Retezat range. Brown bear signs are frequent on the high-altitude trails here; the alpine wildflower meadows in June and July are exceptional.

## 2. Camping in Retezat National Park, Hunedoara

Romania's oldest national park — established 1935 — holds glacially carved terrain with over 80 glacial lakes. The principal access for camping is via the Nucșoara or Clopotiva trailheads. The Bucura lake — the largest glacial lake in Romania at 8.8 hectares — sits in a cirque at 2,040 metres. Designated camping is near the lake; the ranger-managed system restricts camping to specific zones. Wolf and bear activity in the park is high; food storage protocols are strictly required.

## 3. Camping Cheile Bicazului, Neamț

In the Bicaz Gorge — a dramatic 8 km canyon in the Eastern Carpathians with 300-metre limestone walls — the camping area near the gorge entrance is the base for both gorge walking and access to the Red Lake (Lacul Roșu) formed by a landslide in 1837 that drowned a spruce forest, whose grey trunks still protrude from the water. The gorge drive is one of Romania's most spectacular.

## 4. Danube Delta Camping, Tulcea

The Danube Delta is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and Biosphere Reserve. Camping within the delta requires coordination with tour operators from the town of Tulcea — motorboat or wooden boat access to the delta interior. The Letea Forest, the Sulina arm, and the Sfântu Gheorghe beach are the primary destinations. Pelican, spoonbill, glossy ibis, pygmy cormorant, and white-tailed eagle are among over 300 bird species recorded. The delta camping is basic — operators provide tents or simple reed huts.

## 5. Camping Bran, Brașov County

Near the Bran Castle (the castle associated, loosely, with the Dracula legend) in the Transylvanian Alps, the camping area provides access to the castle approach and the Bucegi Mountains above Bran. The Piatra Craiului massif — a narrow 25 km limestone ridge with sheer east face — begins 10 km west. The Bran village landscape of Saxon farmsteads and Transylvanian meadows is the visual archetype of medieval central Europe.

## 6. Camping Cabana Piatra Arsă, Bucegi Mountains

On the Bucegi Plateau above Sinaia at approximately 1,950 metres, this cabana-adjacent campsite is at the base of the Omu peak summit route. The Bucegi plateau is famous for its sphinx-shaped erosion features — the Sphinx and Babele rock formations — and for the dense network of marked trails across the plateau. The plateau is accessible by cable car from Sinaia; the campsite serves those who want to remain on the plateau overnight.

## 7. Agritourism Camping in Maramureș, Maramureș County

In the Iza Valley of Maramureș — the northern Carpathian region where traditional Romanian village life survives most completely — farm camping (agropensiune) provides a distinctive blend of agricultural tourism and mountain access. The painted wooden churches of Maramureș are UNESCO listed; the hay meadows in June hold one of Europe's best lowland wildflower assemblages.

## 8. Camping Cozia, Vâlcea

In Cozia National Park on the Olt river gorge in the Southern Carpathians, this campsite is beside the river with the monastery of Cozia (fourteenth century, one of Romania's oldest) visible from the site. The gorge was carved through the Carpathian mountain mass by the Olt river; the train and road thread through together in a dramatic passage. Brown bear observation hides are operated by local agencies near here.

## 9. Camping Lacul Roșu, Harghita

At the shore of Red Lake in Harghita County — the Hungarian-speaking Székely Land of eastern Transylvania — this site provides direct access to the lake and the Bicaz Gorge to the east. The surrounding spruce-fir Carpathian forest is dense and extensive; the regional food tradition (goulash, stuffed cabbage, kürtőskalács pastry) is distinctly Hungarian-influenced.

## 10. Camping Apuseni, Bihor

In the Apuseni Mountains — the western arm of the Carpathians with extensive cave systems — the camping areas near the Scărișoara Ice Cave and the Bear Cave (Peștera Urșilor) provide access to some of Romania's most visited natural sites. The Apuseni landscape of karst formations, traditional Moți communities, and mixed pine-beech forest is distinct from the higher Eastern and Southern Carpathian ranges.

## Bear safety and wildlife protocols

Romania's brown bear population is dense enough in the Carpathians that bear encounters on trails are common. Bear-safe food storage, noise-making on trails, and awareness of the protocol for encounter — stand ground, do not run, avoid direct eye contact, speak firmly — are required knowledge before camping in the national parks. Bear viewing tours from multiple Carpathian bases have become a significant tourism product.
