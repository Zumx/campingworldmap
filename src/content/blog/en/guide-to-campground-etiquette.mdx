Campground etiquette — the informal code of conduct that governs how campers treat each other and the shared natural environment — is not posted on a sign at the entrance of most campgrounds. It is transmitted through experience, observation, and occasionally through awkward interactions with neighbours who did not receive the same education. Understanding what constitutes considerate campground behaviour is important whether you are a first-time camper who does not want to be the person ruining someone else's experience, or an experienced camper who wants to articulate the principles clearly enough to teach them to others.

The foundation of campground etiquette is simple: you are sharing a natural environment with other people who have made an effort to be there, and many of them are seeking something — quiet, connection with nature, a break from urban noise — that your behaviour can either protect or destroy. The rules that follow from this foundation are practical and largely intuitive once articulated. Most campground conflicts and complaints arise from a small number of repeated behaviours: noise violations, light intrusion, fire mismanagement, pet issues, and failure to respect shared spaces.

## Quiet Hours

Quiet hours — the time period (typically 10 PM to 7 AM) during which noise is expected to be minimised — are the most universally enforced campground rule and the source of the most common conflicts. The standard is not silence during quiet hours, but rather that sound should not carry beyond your immediate site. Conversation at a normal indoor volume, the ambient sounds of a campfire, and quiet music through a phone speaker are generally acceptable. Music through a portable speaker that is audible two sites away, loud conversation, engines left running, and dogs barking unattended are the typical violations.

The enforcement of quiet hours by campground hosts is inconsistent, and many campgrounds rely on social pressure rather than formal enforcement. If your neighbour is violating quiet hours, a polite approach directly to them is usually more effective than waiting for a host who may not be available. Most noise violations are unintentional — the perpetrators simply have not registered that their conversation volume or music volume is carrying as far as it is.

## Campfire Etiquette

Campfires are central to the camping experience and a significant source of neighbour friction when mismanaged. The core rules are: use only the fire ring provided; burn only wood that is appropriate for the fire ring size; do not import firewood from outside the immediate region (invasive species including the emerald ash borer and spotted lanternfly spread through movement of firewood); extinguish the fire completely before sleeping or leaving the site (stir ash, add water, stir again until cool to the touch); and observe any posted fire restrictions without exception.

Wood volume is a commonly misunderstood aspect of campfire etiquette. A fire that produces so much smoke that it drifts persistently into neighbouring campsites is a legitimate nuisance. Hardwood in moderate quantities produces far less smoke than green wood or softwood; a well-managed hardwood fire burns relatively cleanly. Burning trash, foam, plastic, or treated wood is both etiquette-violating and often illegal.

## Site Boundaries and Privacy

The invisible boundary of a campsite — the area you have reserved and paid for — should be respected in both directions. Do not cut through another person's campsite to access a trail, water source, or bathroom. If you need to pass close to a neighbouring site, go around the road-side edge of their site rather than through the middle. Similarly, park vehicles within the lines of your designated site rather than straddling boundaries or parking in the roadway.

Set up your tent and establish the main activity of your camp (chairs, tables, fire ring) oriented toward your own site rather than toward a neighbour's. Where sites are close together, a simple tarp or the placement of your vehicle can reduce visual intrusion. Do not set up camp chairs at the boundary of your site facing your neighbour's tent — this is more directly invasive than campers typically realise.

## Shared Facilities

Bathrooms, dishwashing stations, and potable water spigots are shared resources that require consideration of other users. Limit bathroom use to reasonable duration in high-use campgrounds where morning queues form. Wash dishes at the designated station rather than at your campsite, which can attract wildlife and create grey water disposal issues. Empty the dishwashing basin carefully according to posted instructions — many campgrounds require straining food solids and disposing of grey water in the dump station rather than on the ground.

At water spigots, fill containers efficiently and do not leave water running unattended. At campground dump stations (for RV grey and black water), complete the process efficiently and clean up any spills. The dump station is the facility that most reveals whether campers understand shared resource responsibility.

## Pet Behaviour

Dogs are welcome at many campgrounds with the understanding that they will be kept on a leash at all times, will not be left alone at the site barking, and will not intrude on other campsites. A dog on a 6-foot leash cannot reach a neighbouring site; a dog on a 30-foot long line can reach many sites and create the impression of an uncontrolled animal even if technically leashed. Pick up dog waste immediately and dispose of it in the designated bins. A dog that barks persistently — when its owners are away or when stimulated by other dogs or wildlife — is one of the most disruptive possible campground presences and typically requires either retraining or choosing campsites with more distance between neighbours.

## Light Discipline

Bright lights directed from your site toward neighbouring sites are a significant source of intrusion in campgrounds where sites are close together. Standard LED lanterns directed upward through a tent diffuse well; a lantern pointed at eye level toward a neighbour's tent can be genuinely disruptive to their ability to fall asleep. If your campsite needs significant illumination for safety (a steeply sloping site, equipment spread across a large area), direct lights inward toward your site rather than outward. Headlamps with red-light modes reduce light intrusion during quiet hours and preserve both your own and your neighbours' night vision.

## Leave No Trace at the Site Level

The seven Leave No Trace principles apply to campground use as much as to wilderness travel. At an established campground this means: leaving the site cleaner than you found it (pack out any litter, including previous occupants' waste), keeping all food and scented items in bear-safe containers or your vehicle in bear-active areas, and not cutting live vegetation, carving trees, or doing anything that damages the campground environment.
