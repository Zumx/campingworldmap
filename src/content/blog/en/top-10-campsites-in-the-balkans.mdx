The Balkans offer some of Europe's most varied and least crowded camping terrain. Stretching from Slovenia's Alpine foothills in the northwest to Greece's Aegean shores in the southeast, the region encompasses limestone gorges, old-growth forests, turquoise river canyons, and dramatically long coastlines. Camping infrastructure ranges from polished Adriatic tourist sites with swimming pools and restaurants to wild pitches at the edge of national park wilderness where you may go days without seeing another traveller. For campers who prioritise landscape diversity and value, the Balkans deliver at a fraction of the cost of Western Europe.

## 1. Camp Kamp, Kobarid, Slovenia

Kobarid sits in the Soča Valley, one of the most celebrated river landscapes in the Alps, where the Soča runs an astonishing glacial turquoise through limestone bedrock. Camp Kamp lies on the riverbank within walking distance of town and the Kobariški muzej, which documents the First World War Isonzo Front that Ernest Hemingway fictionalised in A Farewell to Arms. The campsite is well equipped with clean facilities and is a basecamp for kayaking, fly-fishing, and hiking the Napoleon Bridge trail. The valley's microclimate keeps it mild through the shoulder seasons. The nearby Kozjak waterfall hike is a half-day return and suitable for all fitness levels.

## 2. Camping Slapić, Slunj, Croatia

Croatia's Plitvice Lakes National Park draws enormous crowds, but staying inside the park is not permitted for overnight camping. Camp Slapić, roughly 20 kilometres from the park entrance near the village of Slunj, offers a quieter base on the Korana River. The river itself forms its own cascades and tufa formations visible from the pitches. The camp has simple but functional facilities, good shade from mature riverside trees, and the advantage of easy morning access to Plitvice before tour buses arrive. Slunj's own Rastoke village, where the Slunjčica River flows through old mill houses, is a short walk from the site and worth an afternoon.

## 3. Autocamp Maslina, Trogir, Croatia

Trogir is a UNESCO-listed medieval town on a small island connected by bridges to the mainland and the island of Čiovo. Autocamp Maslina occupies an olive grove on Čiovo, giving it the name that translates to olive tree, and pitches are shaded by old trees that make summer heat manageable. The Adriatic water is accessible on foot, and Trogir's old town is a 15-minute walk across the bridge. Split and its Diocletian's Palace are 30 kilometres east. The campsite suits both tent campers and self-contained campervans and draws a loyal returning clientele of Central European visitors who appreciate the combination of historic town access and a working olive farm atmosphere.

## 4. Camp Fiesa, Piran, Slovenia

Camp Fiesa is tucked into a small cove below the hilltop town of Piran, the best-preserved Venetian town on the Slovenian coast. The camp is small — capacity is limited by the steep terrain — which keeps it from feeling overcrowded even in peak season. The sea is immediately accessible from the pitches, the water is clear, and the walk up to Piran through old town walls takes about 20 minutes. Slovenia's coastline is only 47 kilometres long, making Fiesa a rare find: proper coastal camping in a country that barely touches the Adriatic. Nearby Portorož has a larger beach and marina facilities if additional amenities are wanted.

## 5. Camping Kamp Vrelo, Treskavec, North Macedonia

North Macedonia contains some of the least visited wilderness in the Balkans. Camping Kamp Vrelo near Matka Canyon, roughly 15 kilometres west of Skopje, sits above a reservoir formed by a dam on the Treska River. The canyon walls rise steeply on both sides and contain several cave systems open to guided visits, including Vrelo Cave, which holds one of the deepest underwater lakes discovered in Europe. Kayaks are available for hire on the reservoir. The site has basic facilities and is operated with minimal commercial development, preserving the canyon's wild character. Skopje's eclectic architecture and the old bazaar quarter are easily reachable in under 30 minutes.

## 6. Camping Jezero, Lake Ohrid, North Macedonia

Lake Ohrid is one of the world's oldest lakes, estimated at between 1.5 and 3 million years old, and supports dozens of endemic species found nowhere else. Camping Jezero occupies the lakefront south of Ohrid city, offering direct access to the clearest freshwater in the Balkans. The town of Ohrid itself, a UNESCO dual natural and cultural heritage site, has Byzantine churches, a Roman amphitheatre, and an old bazaar district. Summer temperatures are warm but the lake moderates humidity. The site suits long stays and serves as a hub for exploring the Albanian side of the lake at Pogradec. Sunset views over the water to the mountains are among the most striking in the Balkans.

## 7. Camping Crvena Luka, Biograd na Moru, Croatia

Crvena Luka is a large, professionally managed campsite on the Dalmatian coast between Zadar and Šibenik, positioned on a promontory with sea on three sides. Pitches on the outer edges have water views, and the site has its own pebbly beach, diving centre, and restaurant. Kornati National Park, an archipelago of 89 uninhabited islands, is accessible by excursion boat from the marina adjacent to the campsite. Biograd itself is a modest coastal town with a lively harbour promenade and good supermarket access. The campsite operates from April to October and has well-maintained sanitary blocks, a playfield, and Wi-Fi coverage across most of the grounds.

## 8. Autocamp Bojana, Ada Bojana, Montenegro

Ada Bojana is a river island at the mouth of the Bojana River where it meets the Adriatic on Montenegro's southern coast near Ulcinj. The triangular island is formed by two river channels, one of which is natural, and the other is a canal dug in the 19th century. Autocamp Bojana covers much of the island's surface and is one of the most famous naturist camping destinations in the Balkans, though clothed sections exist. The beaches on both the river and sea sides are sandy — genuinely unusual on a largely rocky Montenegrin coast — and the water is shallow and calm on the river side. The surrounding wetlands hold significant bird populations including white pelicans.

## 9. Camp Danica Bohinjska Bistrica, Slovenia

Bohinj is the quieter cousin to Bled among Slovenian Alpine lakes, lacking an island castle and Instagram saturation but compensating with better trail access and a more authentic rural atmosphere. Camp Danica sits in Bohinjska Bistrica village at the lake's eastern end, close to the train station on the scenic Bohinj Railway line. From the camp, the lake is a short cycle or walk, and the trailhead for the climb to Triglav, Slovenia's highest peak, begins nearby at Stara Fužina. The Savica waterfall and Mostnica Gorge are within day-hike distance. The Bohinj Tunnel connects the valley to the coast, making the camp a realistic base for a multi-region Slovenian trip.

## 10. Camping Studenac, Novi Pazar, Serbia

Serbia's camping infrastructure is less developed than its coastal neighbours, but Camping Studenac near Novi Pazar offers a base for exploring the medieval Serbian monasteries of the Raška region, including Sopoćani, Đurđevi Stupovi, and the complex at Studenica — all within a reasonable drive. The site sits in the Ibar River valley, wooded and cooler than the lowland cities. Novi Pazar has a substantial Ottoman old town reflecting the region's historical position on the boundary between Byzantine and Ottoman cultural spheres. This is an under-visited corner of the Balkans where campers encounter almost no other foreign tourists and prices for food and fuel remain low by any European standard.
