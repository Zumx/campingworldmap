Queensland is the largest camping state in Australia — a territory of astonishing geographical diversity stretching from the subtropical rainforests and coral reef islands of the north to the red-dirt gibber plains of the far outback southwest. The state has more than 350 national parks managed by Queensland Parks and Wildlife Service, with a booking system that allows advance campsite reservation across most of the system. Island camping on Great Barrier Reef coral cays and island national parks is among the most unusual camping experiences in the world, accessible by ferry or light aircraft from mainland coastal towns.

The camping season varies significantly by region. Tropical North Queensland above Townsville has a distinct wet season from November through April when some campgrounds close and others become accessible only by 4x4. The Scenic Rim and southern Queensland national parks are excellent year-round, with spring and autumn being particularly pleasant. The outback Outback Queensland is best from April through September; summer temperatures in the Channel Country can exceed 45 degrees Celsius and are genuinely dangerous for exposed camping. ## 1. Whitsunday Islands National Park, Whitsundays

The 74 Whitsunday Islands scattered across the inner Great Barrier Reef provide some of Australia's finest island camping. The national park campsite at Whitehaven Beach on Whitsunday Island — reached by boat from Airlie Beach — is consistently ranked among Australia's most beautiful beaches, a 7-kilometre arc of silica sand so fine it squeaks underfoot. Hook Island's national park campsite at Nari's Beach has fringing reef immediately offshore. Camping permits for all island sites are booked through the QPWS system. Water and gear must be carried in, and the boat charter required to reach the islands adds an adventure element not found in mainland camping.

## 2. Daintree National Park, Tropical North Queensland

The Daintree is the oldest continuously tropical rainforest on Earth — some 135 million years of unbroken forest history. The World Heritage-listed park north of Mossman protects the lowland and montane rainforest of the Wet Tropics bioregion, where the forest meets the Great Barrier Reef at Cape Tribulation. Camping at Cape Tribulation, within the national park boundary at the Kulki campground and adjacent private operators, provides access to the famous beach where jungle and reef meet. Cassowaries — the world's third-largest bird and a keystone species of the rainforest — are regularly seen around campsites in the Cape Tribulation area.

## 3. Girringun National Park, Cardwell, North Queensland

The Girringun National Park and adjacent Wet Tropics rainforest around the town of Cardwell contains the Wallaman Falls — at around 268 metres the tallest single-drop waterfall in Australia. The Queensland Parks campsite at the base of the falls sits beside the pool in pristine rainforest, with the waterfall audible and often visible through the trees. The falls run strongly from February through June following the wet season. The surrounding Girringun rainforest is home to cassowaries, tree kangaroos, and an extraordinary range of rainforest birds including the Victoria's riflebird and numerous bowerbirds.

## 4. Carnarvon Gorge National Park, Central Queensland

Carnarvon Gorge cuts into the sandstone Carnarvon Range in central Queensland in a 30-kilometre canyon of white cliffs, moss gardens, and creek-fed vegetation. The park campground at the gorge entrance is the base for a series of day walks into side gorges containing Aboriginal rock art, cycads, and the extraordinary filter pools — clear pools fed by filtered spring water emerging through the canyon walls. The Aboriginal art sites, particularly the Cathedral Cave, contain stencil and engraving works of great antiquity and significance. The gorge is in the semi-arid zone and has a dry, clear climate — an excellent camping destination from April through October.

## 5. Fraser Island (K'gari), Fraser Coast

K'gari — or Fraser Island — is the world's largest sand island, a 120-kilometre-long island off the Queensland coast composed entirely of sand stabilized by diverse vegetation from rainforest to wallum heathland and perched freshwater lakes. The national park camping areas are scattered across the island, with Lake McKenzie, Eli Creek, and Waddy Point among the most popular. Access is by vehicle barge from Hervey Bay or River Heads, and the island roads require 4x4. Dingoes — pure wild dingoes, among the most genetically unmodified populations in Australia — live throughout the island. Swimming is only recommended in the freshwater lakes and Eli Creek, not the ocean due to sharks and currents.

## 6. Lamington National Park, Scenic Rim

Lamington in the Gold Coast hinterland is one of the most biodiverse temperate-subtropical rainforest parks in Australia, protecting the McPherson Range caldera rim and the largest remaining subtropical rainforest on Australia's mainland. The O'Reilly's Rainforest Retreat at the green mountains section and the Binna Burra mountain lodge both have campgrounds adjacent. Tree kangaroos, Albert's lyrebirds, regent bowerbirds, and crimson rosellas are commonly seen from camp. The Border Track between the two camping areas — 21 kilometres through ancient Antarctic beech forest — is one of Queensland's finest multi-day walks, accessible as a two-day point-to-point camp hike.

## 7. Lakefield National Park, Cape York Peninsula

Lakefield is Queensland's second-largest national park, protecting an enormous area of floodplain wetland, woodland, and coastal mangrove in the lower Cape York Peninsula. It is one of Australia's finest wildlife viewing parks — estuarine crocodiles bask on river banks throughout the park, while brolgas, jabirus (Australian black-necked storks), and whistling kites fill the skies above the seasonal wetlands. The park has basic bush campgrounds on the river systems. Access requires a 4x4 and is restricted to the dry season (May-October) when the roads are passable. The Cape York Peninsula cultural landscape — traditional owner country — adds a significant dimension to the camping experience.

## 8. Undara Volcanic National Park, Gulf Savannah

Undara protects the longest lava tube system in the world — a series of underground tunnels formed by lava flowing through the crust of an ancient volcanic field around 190,000 years ago. The tubes are now inhabited by extraordinary bat colonies (little bentwing bats and white-striped freetail bats in the hundreds of thousands) and the lava tube forest — dense monsoon vine thicket growing in the tubes' collapsed sections — is a micro-habitat found nowhere else in Australia. The safari tents and camping at the Undara Experience are the only overnight accommodation in the park. The experience of standing at the entrance of a lava tube while thousands of bats stream out at dusk is unforgettable.

## 9. Hinchinbrook Island National Park, Cardwell

Hinchinbrook Island is Australia's largest island national park, a rugged wilderness of granite peaks, mangrove estuary, and beach rising abruptly from the Coral Sea channel opposite Cardwell. The Thorsborne Trail — a 32-kilometre multi-day track along the island's east coast — is one of Australia's finest wilderness walks, with strict quota camping at five designated sites. The quota is just forty walkers on the island at any time, ensuring an experience of genuine solitude on a trail that passes remote beaches, creek crossings, and views of the Cardwell Range interior. The track is challenging — creek crossings, rugged terrain, and no resupply — but the wilderness quality rewards the effort.

## 10. Sundown National Park, Southern Queensland

Sundown in the Granite Belt region on the Queensland-New South Wales border is one of Queensland's most underrated national parks, protecting a rugged landscape of granite boulders, gorges, and dry eucalyptus woodland along the Severn River. The Severn River campsite is the most popular, with river swimming, gold panning traditions (the area was a colonial-era goldfield), and excellent night-sky viewing from the open granite outcrops. Platypus are seen regularly in the river at dawn and dusk. The park is remote enough from Brisbane that weekend visitors are few, and the Granite Belt's altitude ensures temperatures cooler than the Queensland coast even in summer.
