Uganda occupies the western branch of the East African Rift Valley — a position that gives it exceptional biodiversity. The country is a meeting point between East African savanna and Central African rainforest, and supports an extraordinary concentration of primates: mountain gorillas in Bwindi, chimpanzees in Kibale, golden monkeys in Mgahinga, and 13 species of primates in total. The equatorial climate means rain can arrive at any month, though December through February and June through August are drier periods. Camping infrastructure ranges from established Uganda Wildlife Authority sites in the national parks to basic community campgrounds adjacent to forest reserves. The most remote experiences — high camps on the Rwenzori Mountains, overnight shelters in the forest above Bwindi — require guides, porters, and advance booking through the Uganda Wildlife Authority.

## 1. Buhoma Community Campsite, Bwindi Impenetrable Forest, Southwestern Uganda

Bwindi Impenetrable Forest National Park holds approximately half of the world's remaining mountain gorilla population — estimated at around 1,000 individuals. The Buhoma sector at the park's northern entrance is the main gorilla trekking departure point for the northern habituated gorilla families. Buhoma Community Campsite is run by the local community adjacent to the park boundary. Gorilla permits must be arranged through the Uganda Wildlife Authority and booked months in advance; the campsite provides an affordable base for permit holders. Evening walks to the Munyanga River viewpoint above the forest and cultural encounters with the Bakiga community are organised through the campsite.

## 2. Red Chilli Rest Camp, Murchison Falls National Park, Northern Uganda

Murchison Falls is Uganda's largest national park, in the Albertine Rift north of Lake Albert, where the Victoria Nile compresses through a 7-metre wide gorge creating the most powerful waterfall per unit of width in the world. Red Chilli Rest Camp is the most established budget camping facility in the park, on the south bank of the Nile where launch trips depart for the falls and for hippo and crocodile-watching on the river. Vehicle game drives on the north bank cross the Albert delta floodplain where lions, elephants, giraffes (the endangered Rothschild's giraffe subspecies), and large buffalo herds are common. The campsite has dormitory accommodation as well as tent pitches.

## 3. UWA Campsite, Kibale Forest, Western Uganda

Kibale Forest National Park near Fort Portal contains the highest density of primates in Africa: 13 species including an estimated 1,500 chimpanzees, the park's most well-known residents. Chimpanzee tracking with habituated communities departs from the Kanyanchu visitor centre. The Uganda Wildlife Authority campsite at Kanyanchu is basic but well-positioned for morning chimpanzee trek departures. The Bigodi Wetland Sanctuary adjacent to the forest is managed by the community and is outstanding for both primates and birds — over 200 species have been recorded in the wetland including the Abyssinian ground hornbill. The campsite serves as a basecamp for combining Kibale chimp tracking with Queen Elizabeth Park game drives to the south.

## 4. Ishasha Wilderness Camp, Queen Elizabeth National Park, Western Uganda

The Ishasha sector of Queen Elizabeth National Park in the park's far southwest is famous for tree-climbing lions — individuals that habitually rest in large fig trees above the floodplain, a behaviour observed in only two places in Africa (the other being Serengeti's Lake Manyara area). Ishasha Wilderness Camp has a community camping area adjacent to its main facilities, positioned in forest at the Ishasha River. Vehicle game drives in the Ishasha plains in the early morning maximise the chance of finding lions in the trees. The park's main Kasenyi plains to the north have higher game density overall, but Ishasha's particular lion behaviour and the quieter, more remote atmosphere give it a different character.

## 5. Rwenzori Mountaineering Services Basecamp, Kasese, Western Uganda

The Rwenzori Mountains — Ptolemy's Mountains of the Moon — rise above the Albertine Rift floor on the border with Democratic Republic of Congo, with a summit zone including Margherita Peak at 5,109 metres capped by shrinking glaciers. The multi-day circuit route through the Rwenzori requires UWA permits, guide, and porter hire through the Rwenzori Mountaineering Services. The trail system passes through a vertical sequence of ecosystems: savanna, bamboo forest, giant heather, and the extraordinary afro-alpine zone of giant lobelias and groundsels at high altitude. Base camps at Nyabitaba and John Matte huts on the approach provide overnight shelter on the ascent. The glaciers on the Rwenzori are projected to disappear within decades.

## 6. Gorilla Valley Lodge Campsite, Mgahinga Gorilla National Park, Southwestern Uganda

Mgahinga Gorilla National Park is Uganda's smallest national park, protecting the slopes of three Virunga volcanoes — Muhabura, Gahinga, and Sabyinyo — that straddle the borders of Uganda, Rwanda, and the DRC. The park has one habituated mountain gorilla family and a population of golden monkeys — a Cercopithecus subspecies endemic to the Virunga volcanoes. The campsite below the park boundary near Ntebeko provides access to both gorilla and golden monkey tracking. The Sabyinyo summit trail is a demanding day hike to the point where three countries meet. The Batwa community, formerly resident inside the forest, have a cultural presentation programme accessible from the campsite.

## 7. Crater Lakes Campsite, Fort Portal, Western Uganda

The Fort Portal area in western Uganda sits above a series of volcanic crater lakes formed by explosive eruptions in a volcanic field related to the Rwenzori block. Over 50 crater lakes dot the landscape around Fort Portal, most within 20 kilometres of the town. Several are accessible on a self-guided crater lake trail, and the community-run campsite near Lake Nkuruba has pitches above the crater rim with views down to the deep, circular lake below. The Kibale forest is 30 minutes south. Black-and-white colobus monkeys, grey-cheeked mangabeys, and L'Hoest's monkeys move through the trees around the campsite.

## 8. Ihimbo Community Campsite, Bwindi Rushaga Sector, Southwestern Uganda

The Rushaga sector of Bwindi in the park's south accesses several gorilla families including the Nshongi family — formerly the world's largest habituated group before it split. Ihimbo Community Campsite is a community-run facility near the Rushaga sector ranger station and trailheads. The southern Bwindi forest has a different character from the northern Buhoma area — deeper, with more diverse understorey on the higher ridges. The Batwa community's forest guide programme at Rushaga offers a perspective on the forest's resources and the Batwa's historical relationship with it that supplements the gorilla tracking experience. Nightly camp fire gatherings include traditional Batwa storytelling.

## 9. Sipi Falls Campsite, Mount Elgon, Eastern Uganda

Mount Elgon is an ancient shield volcano on the Ugandan-Kenyan border whose caldera, 40 kilometres in diameter, is among the largest extinct calderas on Earth. The Sipi Falls, a series of three tiered waterfalls on the slopes above the market town of Sipi, are the most visited natural feature on the mountain's Ugandan side. Several campsites and guesthouses cluster around the falls, some with platform tents above the gorge with views of the falls from your sleeping position. The Sipi coffee cooperative produces high-quality Arabica from the volcanic slopes, and farm tours include the full processing chain from cherry to cup. The trail into the Mount Elgon National Park above Sipi reaches the afro-alpine zone in two to three days of hiking.

## 10. Paraa Safari Lodge Campsite, Murchison Falls National Park

Paraa is the ferry crossing point on the Victoria Nile that divides Murchison Falls National Park's north and south banks, and the Paraa area has the park's highest density of safari tourism infrastructure. The Uganda Wildlife Authority campsite near the ferry landing is on the south bank with launch access for boat trips to the falls. The delta area where the Nile enters Lake Albert — 30 kilometres downstream — holds shoebill storks, a massive prehistoric-looking bird with a broad, hook-tipped bill adapted for hunting lungfish in the papyrus swamps. The shoebill is one of the most sought-after birds in Africa, and the Murchison Falls delta is one of the most reliable viewing locations.
