Morocco sits where the Sahara Desert meets the Atlas Mountains and the Atlantic Ocean, and that convergence of radically different landscapes creates an unusually diverse camping country for its size. In a single trip you can camp beneath Toubkal — the highest peak in north Africa outside Egypt — hike the spectacular gorges of the Dades and Todgha canyons, sleep in a traditional Berber tent under the stars of the Merzouga sand sea, and pitch a tent on an Atlantic headland with breaking waves below. The country has a long tradition of independent travel and a well-developed though informal camping infrastructure.

Morocco is year-round camping territory, but the optimal season varies by region. The High Atlas is best from May through October; winter brings heavy snow to the higher routes. The Sahara pre-Saharan valleys and gorges are excellent in spring and autumn but ferociously hot in July and August. The Atlantic coast is pleasant year-round but wettest from November through February. Camping infrastructure ranges from established paid campsites with basic facilities to completely wild bivouacking in the desert, with every level of comfort between. The [campsite map](/map) provides a starting framework for route-planning across the country's varied regions.

## 1. Toubkal National Park, High Atlas

Jebel Toubkal at 4,167 metres is the highest point in the Atlas Mountains and one of North Africa's finest trekking peaks. The ascent from the village of Imlil — a two-day round trip — passes through the Berber village of Aremd and the Neltner refuge at 3,207 metres, where camping is permitted on the flat ground adjacent to the hut. The Toubkal massif holds a network of high trails connecting multiple 4,000-metre summits, and the Toubkal Circuit is a challenging multi-day route for experienced mountain campers. The Berber communities of the Imlil valley offer mule hire, guide services, and gite accommodation, creating a well-supported trekking infrastructure.

## 2. Erg Chebbi Sand Sea, Merzouga, Draa-Tafilalet

The orange dunes of Erg Chebbi near Merzouga in the pre-Saharan south are Morocco's most photographed desert landscape — dunes rising to 150 metres above the stony reg plateau in photogenic curves and ridges. Dozens of desert camp operators take overnight camel treks to camps in the dune interior, where traditional Berber tents with carpets and tea await. The sky above Erg Chebbi is extraordinary: the area has very low light pollution, and the Milky Way is visible as a dense band on clear nights. Dawn in the dunes — watching the light change across the sand faces — is the classic reason for spending a night here rather than visiting by day.

## 3. Todgha Gorge, Tinerhir, Draa-Tafilalet

The Todgha Gorge cuts through the limestone plateau above Tinerhir in a narrow slot canyon with walls rising over 300 metres from a palm-lined stream bed. Several campsites operate at the gorge entrance and further into the narrow section, offering tent pitches in the dramatic confined space between vertical cliff walls. The gorge is a world-class rock climbing destination with thousands of bolted and traditional routes. Beyond the climbing, the walk further up the gorge into the broader Todgha valley and the Berber villages above is one of the finest gorge walks in North Africa, with opportunities for camping in remote valley encampments.

## 4. Dades Gorge, Boumalne Dades, Draa-Tafilalet

The Dades Gorge runs inland from the town of Boumalne Dades through increasingly dramatic scenery — first the famous "monkey paws" rock formations, then a deep limestone canyon, then high plateau terrain above the gorge accessible by a dramatic switchback road. Multiple small campsites operate within the gorge, and the upper valley above the switchbacks has simple gite accommodation and camping in remote village settings. The Dades Gorge trek connecting to the Mgoun massif is a multi-day high-Atlas route through some of the most spectacular mountain terrain in Morocco, with campsites beside streams at high altitude.

## 5. Souss-Massa National Park, Agadir, Souss-Massa

On the Atlantic coast south of Agadir, Souss-Massa protects a stretch of estuary, dune, and Argan scrubland that is the winter roosting site for one of the last wild populations of the northern bald ibis — a critically endangered bird that breeds only in Morocco and was absent from European skies for centuries. The park has a campsite accessible from the Sidi Rbat beach area. The estuary and beach habitat hosts extraordinary concentrations of migratory shorebirds in autumn, and the Argan woodland — the only place on Earth where Argan trees grow wild — supports a unique agroforestry ecosystem. The adjacent Atlantic beaches are excellent for surfing and beach camping.

## 6. Aït Benhaddou and Draa Valley Trek

The Draa Valley below the Agdz plateau is Morocco's longest river valley — a ribbon of date palm oases, kasbahs, and Berber settlements running south toward the Sahara from the pass of Tizi n'Tinifift. Camping along the valley floor is possible through agricultural terrain with permission from local landowners (rarely refused). The famous fortified ksar of Aït Benhaddou, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is visible from campsites on the hillside opposite, and the valley as a whole — with its kasbah-topped hillocks and contrasting desert terrain — provides one of Morocco's richest cultural-landscape camping contexts.

## 7. Jebel Sarhro, Draa-Tafilalet

The Jebel Sarhro range south of the Draa Valley is a volcanic anti-Atlas massif rising above the pre-Saharan plain in a landscape of black lava formations, pink granite tors, and deep gorges. The massif is home to Barbary sheep (aoudad) and offers some of Morocco's finest multi-day desert mountain trekking with minimal infrastructure. Overnight camps are made in the open desert among the volcanic formations — no formal campsites exist. The Igherm-Nkob circuit is a classic route through the Sarhro. The landscape looks like a moonscape in grey and black rock, completely different from the orange dune world of Merzouga just to the east.

## 8. Parc National de Khénifiss, Laâyoune-Sakia El Hamra

The Khénifiss lagoon system on Morocco's Atlantic coast in the Western Sahara territory south of Tan-Tan protects a vast coastal wetland that hosts one of the most significant flamingo breeding populations in West Africa, alongside numerous raptor and shorebird species. The national park is remote — accessible by piste (unpaved track) off the coastal highway — and camping near the lagoon requires self-sufficiency. The combination of Atlantic coast, vast flamingo flocks, and complete desert solitude makes this one of the most dramatic and least-visited camping destinations in the Moroccan south.

## 9. Cascades d'Ouzoud, Azilal, Béni Mellal-Khénifra

The Ouzoud Falls in the Middle Atlas are Morocco's tallest waterfalls, plunging over 110 metres through a canyon of red rock into a pool inhabited by Barbary macaques. A series of small campgrounds and guesthouses operate in the canyon below and on the plateau rim above, and the sound of falling water at night is a constant companion. The gorge beneath the falls is cool even in summer, providing relief from Atlas heat. Day trippers from Marrakech crowd the viewpoints in peak season, but camping allows early morning access to the falls — before the first buses arrive — when the light on the water is finest.

## 10. Cap Spartel and Rmilat Forest, Tangier, Tanger-Tetouan-Al Hoceima

Cap Spartel is the northwestern tip of Africa — the point where the Atlantic Ocean meets the Mediterranean, and where Europe is visible across the 14-kilometre Strait of Gibraltar on clear days. The Rmilat forest park behind the cape has shaded picnic and camping areas within cork oak and Maritime pine woodland. Camping here combines proximity to the city of Tangier with the dramatic geographical and oceanic significance of the cape itself. The ancient caves of Hercules — a sea cave carved in part by ancient millstone cutters — are within walking distance, and the strait-watching from the cape headland is remarkable during spring and autumn bird migration.
