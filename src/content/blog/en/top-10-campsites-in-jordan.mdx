Jordan is one of the most rewarding countries in the Middle East for camping. Its landscape encompasses some of the most dramatic desert terrain on Earth — the rose-red sandstone towers of Wadi Rum, the deep canyon systems of the Dana Biosphere Reserve, the sculpted cliffs above Petra, and the hyper-saline shores of the Dead Sea. The country is compact and politically stable, its tourism infrastructure is well-developed for the region, and the Jordanian culture of hospitality makes camping a genuine pleasure even in remote areas where you might encounter Bedouin tents and be invited for tea.

Jordan's camping scene ranges from organized desert camps run by Bedouin families in Wadi Rum to fully independent backcountry camping in the Dana wilderness. The Jordan Trail — a 650-kilometre long-distance walking route from Umm Qais in the north to Aqaba on the Red Sea — has created a backbone for multi-week camping itineraries. The trail passes through most of the destinations listed below. Browse the [campsite map](/map) to plan your route and connect the highlights across this small but remarkably diverse country.

## 1. Wadi Rum Protected Area, Aqaba Governorate

Wadi Rum is Jordan's most iconic landscape — a vast desert of pink and red sandstone mountains, domes, and arches rising from a flat gravel plain that glows orange at sunset. UNESCO-listed as a protected area, Wadi Rum allows camping only through licensed Bedouin operators, who provide traditional Bedouin tents, campfire dinners, and guided jeep or camel tours. Several camps are located in secluded valleys between the rock formations, well away from the main tourist circuit, offering genuinely dark skies and silence after the day visitors depart. The Wadi Rum Bedouin have lived in this desert for generations and their knowledge of the terrain adds depth to any stay.

## 2. Dana Biosphere Reserve, Tafilah Governorate

Dana is Jordan's largest nature reserve, covering a dramatic landscape of clifftop sandstone formations descending through several climatic zones to the Wadi Araba below. The Royal Society for the Conservation of Nature operates campsites at Dana village — a traditional stone-built village on the cliff edge — and at Feynan, a remarkable eco-lodge in the valley below accessible only on foot or by 4x4. The Feynan eco-camp is powered entirely by solar energy and candles, and guided night walks with Bedouin rangers are among the most memorable wildlife experiences in Jordan. The reserve holds rare Arabian oryx, sand cats, Syrian serin, and Syrian wolf.

## 3. Petra Backpacker Trail, Ma'an Governorate

While most visitors to Petra see the Siq and Treasury on a day visit, the mountains above the Nabataean city contain a far less visited network of trails connecting carved tomb facades, ancient water channels, and high viewpoints overlooking the Wadi Musa canyon. The Jordan Trail passes through Petra's back country, and overnight camping at approved spots on the ridge above the main city allows sunset and sunrise light on the sandstone formations when the day crowds are absent. Local Bedouin guides from the Bdoul community — who lived inside the Petra monument area until resettled in the 1980s — lead the backcountry routes.

## 4. Wadi Mujib Nature Reserve, Madaba Governorate

Wadi Mujib is a dramatic river canyon descending from the plateau east of the Dead Sea to the sea's eastern shore, with the Mujib River cutting through narrows of sheer limestone walls. The RSCN operates the reserve with guided canyoneering routes through the narrows — some involving wading chest-deep in flowing water. The reserve has a campsite near the Dead Sea entrance, and the juxtaposition of flowing freshwater canyon and hyper-saline sea just kilometres apart is ecologically extraordinary. The reserve is a migration corridor for raptors, and spring and autumn bring impressive raptor-watching opportunities along the cliffs.

## 5. Azraq Wetland Reserve, Zarqa Governorate

The Azraq oasis in Jordan's eastern desert is a remnant of a once-vast wetland complex that historically attracted enormous concentrations of migratory birds crossing the Syrian-African rift valley. Over-extraction of the aquifer in the late twentieth century nearly destroyed the wetland, but restoration efforts since 1994 have brought back open water and reed beds. The RSCN campsite at Azraq is modest but strategically positioned for bird observation during spring and autumn migration. The surrounding basalt desert — known as the badia — has a stark, volcanic beauty, and the ruins of Qasr Azraq, the castle where T.E. Lawrence wintered during the Arab Revolt, are nearby.

## 6. Wadi Rum-Aqaba Road Dispersed Camping, Aqaba Governorate

The desert between Wadi Rum and the Red Sea coast at Aqaba offers extensive dispersed camping on BLM-equivalent public desert land. Overlanders driving the route between Wadi Rum and the coast find flat, open desert with extraordinary views of the Hisma plateau and, on clear days, the mountains of Saudi Arabia across the border. This area is outside the Wadi Rum protected zone and technically accessible for independent free camping, though checking with local authorities is advisable. The Red Sea at Aqaba offers excellent snorkeling and diving immediately adjacent to the town, creating a useful base camp scenario.

## 7. Ajloun Forest Reserve, Ajloun Governorate

In Jordan's northern highlands near the town of Ajloun, this forested reserve managed by the RSCN is one of the few remaining patches of Mediterranean oak and pine forest in the country. The campsite at the reserve sits within the woodland, and the marked trail network includes the Ajloun Forest Trail, which passes Roman and Byzantine agricultural terracing, roe deer habitat, and viewpoints over the Jordan Valley. The climate at this elevation is markedly cooler than the desert south, and the reserve is an excellent camping destination in spring when wildflowers carpet the forest floor.

## 8. Wadi Hasa, Karak Governorate

The Wadi Hasa, where the biblical Zered stream has carved a deep canyon system through the Karak plateau, is one of Jordan's least-visited canyon landscapes. The Jordan Trail descends into the wadi and traverses its length before ascending to the Dana area. Wild camping along the canyon is practiced by Jordan Trail through-hikers, and the scenery — layered limestone and sandstone, spring-fed pools, and stands of oleander in the canyon bottom — is exceptional. The route is remote enough that meeting other campers is rare, and the solitude is a significant part of the appeal.

## 9. Ma'in Hot Springs Area, Madaba Governorate

The therapeutic hot springs of Ma'in cascade down a basalt hillside into the wadi below, with temperatures up to 63 degrees Celsius in the main falls. A resort hotel now controls access to the primary falls, but the wadi system above and below has free camping possibilities for those arriving with their own gear and prepared for a hot, dry environment. The approach from Madaba through the agricultural plateau and then down into the dramatic Dead Sea rift valley is a landscape transition of genuine drama. Combining hot spring bathing with a nearby Dead Sea float and a camping base makes this area a richly varied short trip.

## 10. Aqaba Marine Park, Red Sea

The northernmost point of the Red Sea at Aqaba has some of the most accessible coral reef diving in the world — reefs begin within a short swim of the shore on the protected Aqaba marine reserve coastline south of the city. Several beach camps operate on the coast between Aqaba and the Saudi border crossing, allowing tent camping on the shore with immediate marine reserve access. Freediving and snorkeling in the hyper-clear Red Sea waters reveal a coral diversity that rivals Indo-Pacific reefs. The campsite and beach experience is basic, but the underwater world immediately offshore is extraordinary and the combination of desert mountains, calm sea, and clear night sky is uniquely evocative.
