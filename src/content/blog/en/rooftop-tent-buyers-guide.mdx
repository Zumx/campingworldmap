Rooftop tents have moved from a niche overlanding accessory to a mainstream camping option over the past decade, driven by the growth of vehicle-based adventure travel and an expanding market that now offers products at nearly every price point. The appeal is genuine: your sleeping platform is always ready, always off the ground, and stowed neatly on the roof when you drive. The limitations are also genuine: cost, vehicle compatibility, wind noise, and the fundamental reality that you are sleeping two to three metres off the ground and must descend a ladder to use the toilet at 2 a.m. This guide covers the practical considerations that determine whether a rooftop tent is right for your camping style.

## Hardshell vs. Softshell: The Primary Choice

The market divides cleanly into two categories that represent fundamentally different product designs.

Softshell rooftop tents fold flat when closed, with a fabric body that collapses onto a rigid base. They are typically hinged and open with the fabric walls and rainfly deploying from the folded package. Setup time is around three to five minutes. They are generally lighter than equivalent hardshells, offer more interior space per dollar, and are available in a wider range of configurations including larger three to four person models. Their limitations are longer setup and packdown times compared to hardshells, greater wind noise from fabric walls, and the need to ensure the folded tent is fully dry before storing to prevent mould.

Hardshell rooftop tents have a rigid outer shell — typically made from aluminium composite, ABS plastic, or fibreglass — that opens from a flat clamshell position to the full sleeping configuration in 30 to 60 seconds. Ikar hardshell, ARB Simpson III, Maggiolina AirTop, and the Thule Approach are benchmark products in this category. Hardshells pack to a lower aerodynamic profile, reduce wind noise significantly at highway speeds, and allow camping in rain without the concern about wet fabric storage. They are heavier and more expensive than equivalent softshells, and the fixed shell dimensions limit interior volume compared to a fully deployed softshell of the same footprint.

For campers who move frequently between campsites and prioritize minimum setup time, hardshell is the appropriate choice. For campers who spend multiple nights in the same location and want maximum interior space at lower cost, softshell is more practical.

## Vehicle Compatibility and Weight Limits

Rooftop tents require a rack or crossbar system capable of supporting the tent weight plus the weight of occupants. This is the most critical specification check before any purchase. The roof load rating published in a vehicle's owner manual is the dynamic load rating — the weight the roof can support while driving. The static load rating, relevant for supporting the weight of a sleeping occupant, is typically three to five times higher than the dynamic rating. Many vehicle manufacturers do not publish static ratings explicitly; the widely cited convention is to treat static capacity as approximately four times the dynamic rating, but this varies by vehicle and should be verified through manufacturer documentation or specialist overlanding communities.

The rack or crossbar system has its own weight rating independent of the vehicle roof. A low-quality rack rated to 75 kilograms dynamic may be the limiting factor rather than the roof itself. Quality rack systems from Rhino-Rack, ARB, Yakima, and Thule carry higher ratings appropriate for rooftop tent use.

Most softshell rooftop tents for two people weigh 50 to 80 kilograms. Hardshells in the same capacity range from 60 to 120 kilograms. Adding this to the rack weight — typically 20 to 40 kilograms for a quality system — establishes the total roof load during travel.

## Aerodynamics and Fuel Economy

Any significant structure added to a vehicle roof increases aerodynamic drag. Rooftop tents have a pronounced effect on fuel economy that many buyers underestimate. Testing conducted by automotive publications has measured fuel economy reductions of 10 to 25 percent from rooftop tents at highway speeds, depending on tent size, vehicle aerodynamics, and speed. Hardshell tents, which maintain a lower, more streamlined profile when closed, consistently produce less drag than softshells in driving position.

Wind noise is the other aerodynamic consideration. At highway speeds of 100 to 130 kilometres per hour, even a well-designed softshell rooftop tent produces significant cabin noise. Quality hardshells, particularly those with aerodynamic leading edge profiles, are meaningfully quieter.

## Interior Features and Mattress Quality

The mattress included with a rooftop tent is a significant quality variable. Entry-level tents include foam mattresses that range from adequate to genuinely poor in terms of support and insulation. Premium tents include high-density foam or even pocket-spring mattresses that are comfortable for extended use. Check mattress thickness: a 6-centimetre foam mattress is the practical minimum; 8 to 10 centimetres is a significant improvement for multi-week use.

Condensation management is important in tent design. A tent with inadequate ventilation traps moisture from sleeping occupants, leading to damp sleeping environments and eventual mould on interior fabric. Look for models with multiple ventilation windows that can be opened independently of the rainfly, allowing airflow without precipitation ingress.

Interior lighting, pockets, and shoe storage provisions vary considerably between models. A rooftop tent that will be used frequently benefits from considered interior organization — built-in pockets for phones and glasses, a fabric shoe bag that hangs externally, and attachment points for a reading light or lantern.

## Leading Brands and Price Ranges

The entry-level rooftop tent market is dominated by brands including Roofnest, iKamper Skycamp Mini, and various white-label products distributed through outdoor retailers. Quality control is more variable at this price point; user reviews over time are a reliable indicator.

The mid-range market — where most considered buyers end up — includes the Thule Tepui series (softshell), Yakima SkyRise (softshell), ARB Simpson III (hardshell), and James Baroud Grand Raid (hardshell). These products have established track records for durability in demanding overlanding use.

The premium segment, represented by iKamper X-Cover, Maggiolina AirTop, and roof tents from smaller specialist manufacturers, offers premium materials, sophisticated engineering, and price tags that reflect both of those qualities.
