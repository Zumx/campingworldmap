British Columbia is Canada's westernmost province and one of the most geographically varied regions in North America. Its western edge is defined by the Pacific coast and the fjord-cut Coast Mountains; its eastern boundary is the continental divide of the Rocky Mountains; its northern interior is boreal wilderness with almost no road access. Between these extremes lie the Okanagan valley, the Kootenays, the Thompson Plateau, and Vancouver Island — each with distinct character and distinct camping environments. BC Parks manages one of the largest provincial park systems in Canada, with hundreds of accessible campgrounds and an expansive backcountry. ## 1. Berg Lake Trail, Mount Robson Provincial Park

Mount Robson at 3954 metres is the highest peak in the Canadian Rockies. The Berg Lake Trail is one of the finest backcountry hiking routes in BC, climbing from the Robson River valley to a glacially fed lake at the base of the Berg Glacier. Berg Lake Camp at 1640 metres is reached after approximately 20 kilometres of trail and provides designated tent sites with outhouses and food caches (in an area with heavy black bear activity). The view from camp of Mount Robson's north face, with the Emperor Ridge and the Mist Glacier hanging above, is among the most dramatic backcountry camp views in Canada.

## 2. Joffre Lakes Provincial Park

Joffre Lakes is a chain of three glacially fed lakes in the Coast Mountains northeast of Pemberton, connected by a 10-kilometre round-trip trail. The upper lake campground, at approximately 1500 metres, has been one of the most popular backcountry camps in BC for years and now requires a reservation that is released months in advance. The turquoise-green colour of the lakes, fed by meltwater from the Matier Glacier above, is intense and distinctive. Upper Joffre Lake camp has approximately 40 designated sites; the demand exceeds supply significantly in summer months.

## 3. Garibaldi Lake, Garibaldi Provincial Park

Garibaldi Lake is a large glacially dammed lake in Garibaldi Provincial Park, formed when lava flows from the Black Tusk volcanic formation dammed a valley and created the lake behind it. The camp at Garibaldi Lake is approximately 9 kilometres from the trailhead at Rubble Creek and has designated tent sites with fire rings, picnic tables, and outhouses. The surrounding alpine scenery — Black Tusk rising above the lake's far shore, Sentinel Glacier adjacent to camp — is the visual archetype of British Columbia alpine camping. Reservations through BC Parks are essential; the campground is consistently oversubscribed from June through September.

## 4. Skagit Valley Provincial Park

The Skagit Valley in the southern Interior follows the Skagit River from Manning Provincial Park toward the US border, through a broad valley of Douglas fir and western redcedar. The park contains an extensive trail system and several backcountry campgrounds along the river. The area is less heavily used than the more accessible parks closer to Vancouver and offers a quieter character. Black bears, cougars, and wolves are present; the river supports a significant cutthroat trout population. The valley ecology straddles dry interior and wet coastal forest types, producing a remarkable botanical diversity.

## 5. Strathcona Provincial Park, Vancouver Island

Strathcona is the oldest provincial park in British Columbia, established in 1911, and occupies the mountainous interior of Vancouver Island. The park contains Vancouver Island's highest peak (Golden Hinde, 2195 metres), old-growth forest, and subalpine terrain unusual for a coastal island environment. Buttle Lake Campground is the main developed campground, on a large reservoir within the park. Backcountry camping is extensive, with routes into the Marble Range, Phillips Ridge, and Price Creek areas. The park is reached from Campbell River and is little known outside BC, making its old-growth forest and alpine terrain far less crowded than comparable parks on the mainland.

## 6. Cape Scott Provincial Park

Cape Scott is at the northwestern tip of Vancouver Island, reached by approximately 23 kilometres of trail from the San Josef Bay trailhead or the shorter Nels Bight approach. The park's campground at Nels Bight is on a magnificent crescent beach of hard-packed sand — a full kilometre of camping ground between coastal forest and the Pacific Ocean. The remoteness of the location and the effort required to reach it filters the visitor population significantly. Sea otters were reintroduced to the waters off Cape Scott, and their population has recovered substantially; offshore wildlife watching from the beach camps is exceptional.

## 7. Tweedsmuir Provincial Park South, Bella Coola Valley

Tweedsmuir South is the largest provincial park in British Columbia and is divided by the Coast Mountains into accessible and remote sections. The Bella Coola Valley sector, reached via the famous Chilcotin Highway (a gravel road descending 1500 metres over 26 kilometres — one of the steepest driveable roads in Canada), provides access to the park's river campgrounds. Hunlen Falls within the park drops approximately 260 metres into Turner Lake — one of the highest uninterrupted waterfalls in Canada — accessible by floatplane or multi-day canoe route from the Tweedsmuir Wilderness Lodge area.

## 8. Kluane Wilderness, Tatshenshini-Alsek Provincial Park

The Tatshenshini-Alsek is a UNESCO World Heritage Site shared between BC, Yukon, and Alaska. The Tatshenshini River descent is one of the classic wilderness river camping routes in North America, covering approximately 230 kilometres from Dalton Post in the Yukon to Dry Bay in Alaska. The route traverses the largest non-polar icefields in the world and requires multi-week expedition planning. Bears — both black and grizzly — are common throughout. The Tatshenshini run has been described by outdoor writers as the finest wilderness river journey in North America.

## 9. Kootenay National Park, Marble Canyon Campground

Kootenay National Park in the Canadian Rocky Mountain Parks UNESCO World Heritage Site contains a transition between mountain and interior ecosystems unusual for the Rockies. Marble Canyon Campground is set near the dramatic Marble Canyon — a narrow slot canyon cut by Tokumm Creek through pale grey limestone — and provides a convenient base for exploring both the canyon and the Paint Pots, ochre-stained mineral springs used by Ktunaxa people for ceremonial purposes. The campground is serviced with flush toilets and electrical hookups and is suitable for tent camping alongside RV sites.

## 10. Manning Provincial Park, Lightning Lake Campground

Manning Provincial Park in the Cascade Mountains is the southernmost major provincial park in BC and is accessible from Vancouver in approximately three hours. Lightning Lake Campground is on a chain of connected lakes suitable for canoeing and kayaking, with the peaks of the Cascades visible from the water. The park encompasses a transition between wet coastal and dry interior ecosystems; the Blackwall Peak area above 1800 metres has outstanding wildflower meadow camping conditions in July and August, when the Cascade subalpine zone is at peak bloom and accessible by a drive to the Blackwall parking area.
