Poland's camping scene has developed significantly over the past decade, moving from a network of basic municipal sites to a more varied mix of commercial sites, agritourism farms, and the well-established backcountry options within the national park system. The Tatra Mountains in the south provide the country's most dramatic alpine camping; the Mazurian Lake District in the northeast is the premier kayaking and sailing camping destination; and the Baltic coast from Gdańsk to Szczecin offers sea camping in a northern European coastal tradition. The Polish Tourist and Sightseeing Society (PTTK) maintains a hut and campsite network in the mountains.

## 1. Camping Kalatówki, Tatra National Park, Zakopane

Below the Kalatówki clearing in the Tatra National Park, this site provides the most direct access to the high Tatra peaks from an organised campsite. The Tatras are the only truly alpine mountain range in Poland — the peaks above 2,000 metres include Rysy (2,503 m), the highest point in the country. The PTTK hut at Kalatówki serves as a starting point for the Valley of Five Lakes circuit and the Orla Perć ridge traverse. July and August see high traffic; June and September are optimal.

## 2. Camping Pod Krokwią, Zakopane

At the foot of the Krokiew ski jump in Zakopane — Poland's mountain resort capital — this PTTK site is one of the most established in the Tatras. Electric pitches, ablution blocks, and the full infrastructure of a Polish tourist town surrounding it. Zakopane's position makes it a reliable logistics base; the Tatra cable car to Kasprowy Wierch, one of Poland's most popular mountain access points, departs 2 km from the site.

## 3. Mazury Camping, Giżycko, Mazurian Lakes

On Lake Niegocin in the heart of the Mazurian Lake District — the "Land of a Thousand Lakes" — this site is representative of the large, water-oriented camping in northeast Poland. The Mazurian Lakes are connected by channels and rivers in a system navigable by kayak, canoe, and small sailing boat for hundreds of kilometres. The PTTK kayaking route on the Krutynia River — 100 km through national park landscape — is Poland's premier kayaking trail.

## 4. Bory Tucholskie National Park Camp, Tuchola Forest, Pomerania

In the Tuchola Forest — the largest lowland forest complex in Poland at over 3,000 square kilometres — the national park campsites provide access to the river kayaking routes on the Brda and Wda rivers. The forest's combination of pine, lake, and sandy heath landscape is characteristic of the Baltic lowland terrain. Otter, osprey, and white-tailed eagle are present.

## 5. Camping Trójmiejski, Gdańsk, Pomerania

Within the Tricity agglomeration of Gdańsk-Gdynia-Sopot on the Baltic coast, this campsite provides urban coastal camping with access to the Gdańsk historical city (Hanseatic architecture, the birthplace of Solidarity) and the Baltic beach. The Baltic coast north of Gdańsk toward Hel Peninsula has sandy beaches that are crowded in July but excellent in June and September.

## 6. Bieszczady Mountain Camp, Ustrzyki Górne, Subcarpathia

In Poland's southeastern corner, the Bieszczady Mountains are the least developed mountain range in the country — higher precipitation, denser forest, and far fewer visitors than the Tatras. The campsite at Ustrzyki Górne near the Ukrainian border is the base for the Bieszczady ridge walks including Tarnica (1,346 m) and the Rozsypaniec-Halicz circuit. Wild bison, lynx, and wolf are present in the Bieszczady forest; sightings require patience and luck.

## 7. Camping Wisła Chocho, Wisła, Silesian Beskids

On the Wisła river near its source in the Silesian Beskids, this mountain campsite is at a lower altitude than the Tatras but in a quieter, less crowded landscape. The Silesian Beskids ridge walk connects several summits above 1,200 metres; the traditional wooden architecture of the Beskid villages is a regional distinction.

## 8. Słowiński National Park Camp, Łeba, Pomerania

Near the Słowiński National Park on the Baltic coast — home to Europe's largest moving dunes, which advance up to 10 metres per year — the camping area outside the park provides access to the dune landscape and the long Baltic beach. The Łeba town beach is popular; the national park interior, requiring a permit, is significantly quieter.

## 9. Kampinos Forest Camp, Palmiry, Masovia

In the Kampinos National Park on the western edge of Warsaw, this campsite provides the most accessible forest camping within reach of the Polish capital. Kampinos is a large sand dune and wetland forest 20 km from Warsaw city centre; elk, beaver, and white-tailed eagle are resident. The campsite is basic but functional for both urban day use and overnight stays.

## 10. Pieniny National Park Camp, Szczawnica

In the Pieniny Mountains on the Dunajec river gorge — a smaller but dramatically scenic range south of Kraków — this campsite is the base for the traditional wooden raft descent of the Dunajec gorge. Rafters and kayakers share the gorge with limestone cliff walks above. The Pieniny are smaller in scale than the Tatras but less crowded and equally photogenic.

## PTTK network and facilities standards

Poland's PTTK mountain hut and campsite network provides reliable budget accommodation and camping across the main mountain areas. ACSI CampingCard coverage is expanding in Poland. Wild camping is not permitted in national parks; it is tolerated in state forests outside designated reserves under low-impact conditions.
