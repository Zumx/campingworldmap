The Canadian Rockies form one of the great camping landscapes on Earth. Stretching from the US border north through Banff, Yoho, Kootenay, and Jasper national parks, and into the Willmore Wilderness beyond, they offer glacial lakes of impossible blue-green colour, broad river valleys edged by spruce and lodgepole pine, and mountain terrain that rises to permanent icefields above treeline. Camping here demands some preparation: Parks Canada reservation systems book out months in advance for peak summer dates, bear canisters or bear hang techniques are mandatory in many zones, and weather can shift from warm sunshine to snow at any elevation above 1,500 metres. The rewards — clear dark skies, wildlife encounters with elk, bighorn sheep, grizzly bears, and wolves — are proportional to the effort.

## 1. Tunnel Mountain Village, Banff, Alberta

Tunnel Mountain Village is the flagship campground in Banff National Park, operated by Parks Canada on a plateau above the town of Banff. It is a large facility divided into three distinct areas — two serviced sections and one unserviced tent-only loop — meaning it accommodates both full hookup RVs and tent campers who want a quieter experience. The Hoodoos Trail begins at the campground boundary and descends to the Bow River, and the Banff townsite with its restaurants, gear shops, and the Cave and Basin National Historic Site is a short cycle downhill. The campground tends to book fully weeks in advance for July and August.

## 2. Lake Louise Campground, Banff National Park, Alberta

Lake Louise campground sits below the famous lake in a riverside forest setting on the Bow River, a kilometre from the lakeshore itself. The proximity to one of Canada's most-photographed landscapes — the turquoise lake backed by the Victoria Glacier — makes it extraordinarily popular, and reservation is essential from May through September. The campground has both serviced and tent sections, a comfort station with showers, and a Parks Canada interpretive program running through summer. From here, hikes to the Plain of Six Glaciers teahouse and the Lake Agnes teahouse above Lake Agnes are among the most accessible high-alpine routes in Banff.

## 3. Mosquito Creek Campground, Icefields Parkway, Alberta

The Icefields Parkway between Lake Louise and Jasper is consistently rated among the world's great scenic drives, and Mosquito Creek campground at its southern end offers a genuine backcountry feel at a roadside site. The campground is small, unserviced, and sits in old spruce forest beside the creek. The Mosquito Creek trail from the campground accesses high alpine terrain including North Molar Pass and Fish Lakes in a long day or overnight. Bear activity is high in this corridor and food must be stored in the provided food lockers at all times. The campground has no showers, limited potable water, and is all the better for it.

## 4. Rampart Creek Campground, Icefields Parkway, Alberta

Rampart Creek is one of the quieter Parks Canada campgrounds along the Parkway, set in a forest clearing beside the North Saskatchewan River. The scale of the surrounding peaks — the Weeping Wall cliff face directly across the highway takes on running ice columns in winter — is overwhelming even for experienced mountain campers. The campground is basic but has fire rings and bear-proof food storage. It serves as a rest stop on the long drive between Banff and the Columbia Icefield, and the trailhead for the Hilda Peak scramble is within a few kilometres. Elk and deer move through the campground regularly.

## 5. Wilcox Creek Campground, Columbia Icefield, Alberta

Wilcox Creek campground is positioned at approximately 1,900 metres elevation near the toe of the Athabasca Glacier, the most accessible outlet glacier of the Columbia Icefield. The Wilcox Pass trail from the campground rises through subalpine meadows to a viewpoint overlooking the entire icefield complex — one of the finest easily accessible mountain panoramas in Canada. The Columbia Icefield Discovery Centre is a short drive north. Nights at this elevation in summer can drop well below freezing even in July, and a four-season sleeping bag is advisable. The campground is small and unserviced, maintaining a wilderness feel despite proximity to one of the Parkway's busiest viewpoints.

## 6. Wapiti Campground, Jasper, Alberta

Wapiti is the primary year-round campground near Jasper townsite, on the Athabasca River floodplain south of the town. It is one of the few Parks Canada campgrounds in the mountain parks that remains open through winter, when it serves ski tourers and snowshoers exploring the Marmot Basin area and the frozen Athabasca canyon at Maligne Canyon. The name Wapiti is the Shawnee word for elk, and the animals are a constant presence in the campground through fall. The townsite's gear shops, restaurants, and the Jasper Heritage Railway Station are easily reachable by the paved riverside trail.

## 7. Pocahontas Campground, Jasper National Park, Alberta

Pocahontas campground takes its name from the former mining settlement whose remains lie a few hundred metres from the campground in the Athabasca River valley north of Jasper. The old coal mining townsite, abandoned in the 1920s, is interpreted by a self-guided trail through the foundations and tailings. The campground itself is set in open forest close to the Miette Hot Springs, the hottest natural hot springs in the Canadian Rockies, 17 kilometres up the Fiddle River valley on a paved road. The combination of hot springs access and a relatively uncrowded campground makes Pocahontas one of the more relaxed bases in Jasper for families.

## 8. Kootenay River Campground, Kootenay National Park, British Columbia

Kootenay National Park lies on the western slope of the Continental Divide, accessed from the Alberta parks via Kootenay Parkway through Vermilion Pass. The Kootenay River campgrounds along the parkway are significantly less crowded than their equivalents in Banff and have a more forested, quieter character. The Paint Pots, ochre-stained cold springs used historically by Indigenous peoples to make pigment, are accessible on a short trail from the highway. The Marble Canyon ice walk in winter is one of the more unusual seasonal activities in the parks. The park's western entrance connects to the BC Interior at Radium Hot Springs.

## 9. Takakkaw Falls Campground, Yoho National Park, British Columbia

Yoho National Park is the smallest of the four contiguous Rocky Mountain parks and arguably has the most concentrated scenery. Takakkaw Falls, at 254 metres one of the tallest waterfalls in Canada, plunges from a hanging valley fed directly by the Waputik Icefield, and the campground at its base provides a front-row position. The access road is narrow with a switchback that requires larger rigs to detach trailers, so the campground has an effectively tent-and-small-campervan character by default. The Iceline Trail above the campground traverses glacial moraines with views across the Emerald Glacier and is one of the finest day hikes in the Canadian Rockies.

## 10. Berg Lake Trail Backcountry, Mount Robson Provincial Park, British Columbia

Mount Robson, at 3,954 metres the highest peak in the Canadian Rockies, sits in provincial park territory just west of Jasper National Park. The Berg Lake Trail climbs 22 kilometres from the trailhead to Berg Lake at the mountain's north foot, passing through old-growth forest, the Kinney Lake, Emperor Falls, and a series of designated backcountry campsites. The lake itself is remarkable: icebergs calve from the Mist and Berg glaciers into the lake year-round, audible from the campsite. Permits are required and should be booked months in advance. Few backcountry camping experiences in North America match the combination of scale, isolation, and glacial drama at Berg Lake.
