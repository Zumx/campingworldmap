The Pyrenees form a nearly unbroken mountain wall 450 kilometres long from the Atlantic coast at the Basque Country to the Mediterranean at Cap de Creus. Unlike the Alps, they have no major pass road through their centre — the main vehicle crossings are at either end or through the tunnels at Somport and Bielsa — which preserves a degree of true wilderness in the central massifs that the heavily touristed Alps struggle to match. The Haute Randonnée Pyrénéenne (HRP) and the GR10 on the French side and the GR11 on the Spanish side traverse the range end to end over 40-plus days of sustained hiking through terrain that shifts from Atlantic beech forest to limestone peaks to arid Mediterranean scrub. Camping here is a serious pursuit, with weather systems from the Atlantic capable of delivering heavy snow to the passes even in July.

## 1. Camping Pyrénées Natura, Luz-Saint-Sauveur, France

Luz-Saint-Sauveur is one of the main gateway towns to the Hautes-Pyrénées département and the entrance to the Gavarnie valley, which contains the Cirque de Gavarnie — a 1,500-metre semicircular cliff enclosing the Grande Cascade, the highest waterfall in France at 423 metres. Camping Pyrénées Natura is positioned in the Gave de Pau valley with mountain views and serves as a basecamp for the Gavarnie hike, one of the most celebrated day walks in the French Pyrenees. The Pont Napoléon bridge over the Gave gorge nearby is an early 19th-century engineering landmark. The town hosts a weekly market serving the surrounding valley communities.

## 2. Camping Le Hounta, Gavarnie, France

Gavarnie village itself is at the end of the road, 10 kilometres south of Luz-Saint-Sauveur, and Camping Le Hounta is one of the sites positioned within the village that gives the closest access to the Cirque de Gavarnie. The walk to the cirque viewpoint at the base of the waterfall is two hours from the village on a well-marked path through mountain meadows. The Brèche de Roland, a 40-metre wide breach in the ridge on the Franco-Spanish border that Frankish legend attributed to Roland's sword Durandal, is a full-day hike above the cirque. In summer, Gavarnie can be busy with day visitors, but the campsite empties considerably after 6pm when the coach parties depart.

## 3. Camping Las Nieves, Hecho, Spain

The Hecho valley (Valle de Hecho) in Aragon is one of the quieter Spanish Pyrenean valleys, with a stone village largely unchanged from its medieval layout and a walking area centred on the Selva de Oza forest. Camping Las Nieves serves as a base for the Selva de Oza beech forest trails and the GR11 crossing of the Aguas Tuertas high valley, one of the most beautiful subalpine meadow landscapes in the Spanish Pyrenees. The valley sees far less English-language tourism than the more marketed Ordesa area to the east. The local Cheso dialect, a variety of Aragonese, has a small but active cultural preservation community in the village.

## 4. Camping Ordesa, Broto, Spain

Ordesa y Monte Perdido National Park is the oldest national park in Spain, established in 1918, and contains the Ordesa Canyon — a 1,000-metre deep valley cut through limestone by the Arazas River, walled by vertical strata in a formation that geologists consider among the most spectacular in Europe. No overnight camping is permitted inside the park. Camping Ordesa at Broto, the main valley town below the park entrance, is the standard base for day hikes into the canyon. The Faja de Pelay cliff-edge trail and the Circo de Soaso with its 70-metre Cola de Caballo waterfall are the signature hikes. Advanced reservations are essential in July and August.

## 5. Camping Cauterets, Cauterets, France

Cauterets is a spa town in a deep valley above Lourdes that serves as the access point for the Vallée de Gaube and the Vignemale, at 3,298 metres the highest peak on the Franco-Spanish border. Camping Cauterets is in the town itself, with the thermal baths, shops, and restaurants within walking distance. Above the town, the Pont d'Espagne area has a large car park from which trails climb to Lac de Gaube, a glacially carved lake reflecting the Vignemale north face. The valley above Gaube was explored extensively by the Victorian alpinist Henry Russell, who obtained a 99-year lease on the Vignemale summit from the French state and carved cave dwellings into the glacier.

## 6. Camping d'Arrec, Bagnères-de-Luchon, France

Bagnères-de-Luchon is the most complete of the Pyrenean spa towns, with an intact Belle Époque thermal establishment, a casino, and an allée of plane trees along the main boulevard that makes it feel like a small resort in its own right. Camping d'Arrec occupies a riverside position with the access road to the Hospice de France trailhead — the traditional approach to the Port de Venasque pass into Spain — a short drive south. The Aneto, at 3,404 metres the highest peak in the Pyrenees, is accessible from the Spanish side via the Renclusa refugio above Benasque. Luchon hosts a summer horse race along the main boulevard that is one of the oldest street horse races in France.

## 7. Camping Certascan, Sort, Spain

Sort in the Pallars Sobirà comarca of Catalonia is famous among outdoor sports practitioners primarily for white-water kayaking — the Noguera Pallaresa River through the Sort gorge has rapids rated up to class IV and the town hosts international competitions. The campsite at Certascan higher up the valley accesses the Certascan lake, a high-altitude glacial lake at 2,240 metres that is the largest natural lake in the Catalan Pyrenees. The 4x4 track to the lake parking area is passable to robust vehicles; the hike from the road end to the lake takes about an hour. The Aigüestortes i Estany de Sant Maurici National Park is adjacent to the west.

## 8. Camping Abri de Ruda, Val d'Aran, Spain

The Val d'Aran is the only valley in Spain whose rivers drain to the Atlantic — the Garonne begins here and flows north through the French Pyrenees to the Bay of Biscay. Politically part of Catalonia but with its own Occitan language, Aranese, the valley has a distinct cultural character. Camping Abri de Ruda in Naut Aran is positioned in the upper valley near the Baqueira-Beret ski resort, which operates in winter as one of Spain's premier ski areas and in summer provides cable car access to high-altitude hiking terrain. The Arties and Salardú villages nearby have Romanesque churches with unusual sculptural programs that reflect the valley's medieval prosperity from trade routes through the mountains.

## 9. Camping l'Esplanade, Ax-les-Thermes, France

Ax-les-Thermes is the spa town at the foot of the Ariège Pyrenees, with thermal baths fed by springs at 78 degrees Celsius. The town sits at a road junction serving the Ax-Bonascre ski area above and the GR10 mountain trail. Camping l'Esplanade in the town is a year-round site — one of the few Pyrenean campgrounds that functions in winter, serving skiers and snowshoers. The Plateau de Beille above Ax is a large Nordic skiing area in winter and a mountain biking and hiking plateau in summer. The Ariège département to the north has significant prehistoric art sites including the Grotte de Niaux, with black bison and ibex paintings created around 13,000 years ago, accessible from Tarascon-sur-Ariège 15 kilometres north.

## 10. Camping La Pinède, Amélie-les-Bains, France

The eastern Pyrenees descend rapidly toward the Mediterranean, with the last high summits of the Canigou massif rising above the Roussillon plain's vineyards and apricot orchards. Camping La Pinède at Amélie-les-Bains, a spa town in the Tech valley, is a Mediterranean-flavoured site with a warmer microclimate than the central Pyrenees and access to the southern slopes of the Canigou, the mountain sacred to Catalan culture. The Canigou summit at 2,784 metres is the site of a midsummer bonfire ceremony, the Flama del Canigó, where a flame is carried from the summit to light bonfires across Catalonia and the Pyrenees on the eve of the festival of Sant Joan.
