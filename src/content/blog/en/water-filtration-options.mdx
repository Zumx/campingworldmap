Water treatment is one of the genuinely safety-critical decisions in camping — contaminated water produces giardia, cryptosporidiosis, and bacterial gastroenteritis that ruin trips and can cause serious illness. The range of available treatment options has expanded considerably from the heavy hand-pump filters that defined the category a generation ago. Understanding what each method treats, what it does not treat, and the conditions under which each is appropriate produces correct decisions rather than overconfident ones.

## What's actually in untreated water

Waterborne pathogens fall into three categories relevant to treatment selection:

**Protozoa** (Giardia, Cryptosporidium): large organisms (1-15 microns) removed effectively by filtration at 0.1-0.3 micron pore size. Also killed by chemical treatment and UV.

**Bacteria** (E. coli, Salmonella, Campylobacter, Vibrio): smaller than protozoa (0.5-5 microns) but still removed by 0.1-0.2 micron filtration. Also killed by chemical treatment, UV, and boiling.

**Viruses** (Hepatitis A, Norovirus, Rotavirus): 0.02-0.2 microns — too small to be removed by standard filter membranes. Require chemical treatment, UV, or boiling. Standard backpacking filters (without added chemical treatment) do not remove viruses.

The significance of this depends on your environment. In developed-country backcountry (US, Canada, Western Europe, Australia), viral contamination of surface water is relatively uncommon — most risk is from protozoa and bacteria. In developing countries, areas with significant agricultural runoff, or near human habitation, viral contamination is a realistic concern and a filter alone is insufficient.

## Pump filters

The original technology — a hand pump that draws water through a filter membrane. Examples: MSR MiniWorks, Katadyn Vario.

Advantages: highly reliable, no waiting, works in cold weather, can pump directly into any container. Disadvantages: slow (1-2 litres per minute at best), heavy (280-400g), requires cleaning maintenance in the field, and does not remove viruses without added treatment.

Pump filters are the most reliable for difficult water sources — turbid glacial meltwater, shallow desert pools, low-flow seeps — that gravity and squeeze systems handle poorly.

## Gravity filters

Water is hung in a dirty bag, flows by gravity through the filter into a clean bag below. Examples: Platypus GravityWorks, MSR Trail Base, Sawyer Gravity.

Advantages: hands-free, can process large volumes (4-6 litres), excellent for camp use and groups, no energy input required. Disadvantages: slow (approximately 1.5 litres per minute when clean, slower as the filter loads), requires two bags, freezing temperature degrades ceramic filters irreversibly. Best for camp-based situations where you have time to process water rather than moving quickly.

## Squeeze filters

The Sawyer Squeeze and similar designs push water from a soft bottle through a hollow-fibre membrane. The Sawyer Squeeze processes 0.1-micron filtration, weighs 85 grams, and has an indefinite rated lifespan.

Advantages: light, inexpensive, highly portable, fast when the filter is clean. Disadvantages: flow rate decreases with use and requires backflushing (provided syringe); must not freeze (hollow-fibre membranes rupture when frozen); does not remove viruses.

The Sawyer Squeeze is the default recommendation for three-season backpacking in developed-country backcountry — its weight and performance combination has no equivalent at equivalent price. The freeze limitation requires management in winter conditions.

## Straw-style filters

The LifeStraw-style design lets you drink directly from the source through an integrated filter. Lightweight and extremely simple. Limitations: cannot fill a water bottle (requires a separate squeeze or additional system component), flow rate is low, and awkward for cooking water.

## UV pen treatment

UV pens (SteriPen, Katadyn Steripen) expose water to UV-C light, which damages the DNA of protozoa, bacteria, and viruses, rendering them unable to reproduce. Treatment time is approximately 90 seconds for 1 litre.

Advantages: kills viruses (unlike physical filters), fast, lightweight, no filter to clog. Disadvantages: requires batteries (or USB charging), fails in turbid water (particles shade pathogens from UV), single-use per batch (cannot treat cloudy water effectively), has no mechanical backup if the device fails.

UV treatment is the correct choice for international travel where viral contamination is a concern and turbidity is low. It is commonly used in combination with a filter: filter for clarity and protozoa/bacteria, UV for virus kill.

## Chemical treatment: iodine and chlorine dioxide

Iodine tablets are inexpensive and lightweight but have limitations: long treatment times for Cryptosporidium (4 hours at cold temperatures), unpleasant taste, not suitable for extended use (thyroid effects), and ineffective against Cryptosporidium at standard doses.

Chlorine dioxide (Aquatabs, Aquamira) is a significant improvement: effective against all three pathogen categories including Cryptosporidium (with sufficient contact time — 30 minutes minimum at warm temperatures, longer at cold), minimal taste impact, and no health concerns at camping-dose levels. The limitation is contact time: 30-240 minutes depending on temperature and pathogen.

## Boiling

One minute at a rolling boil (3 minutes above 2,000 metres) kills all pathogens including viruses. Boiling is the zero-failure backup when all equipment fails. Its limitation is time and fuel cost — it is impractical as the primary treatment method when cooking and water heating are not already part of the routine.

## Combining methods for high-risk environments

For international backcountry travel or any situation with potential viral contamination: filter first (removes protozoa and bacteria, and turbidity that would interfere with UV), then UV treat or add chlorine dioxide. This combination covers all three pathogen categories reliably.
