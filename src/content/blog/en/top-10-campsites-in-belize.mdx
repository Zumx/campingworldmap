Belize is the smallest country in Central America by population and one of the most biodiverse by land area. Roughly 40 percent of its territory is protected — national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, marine reserves, and jaguar corridors — making it one of the highest protected-land percentages in the Western Hemisphere. This conservation focus shapes the camping experience: most camping in Belize occurs within or adjacent to protected areas, often at jungle research stations, small eco-lodges with tent platforms, or basic sites at the edges of reserves. The country is small enough that the Caribbean coast, the Maya Mountains interior, and the lowland savanna are all within a few hours of each other. ## 1. Mountain Pine Ridge Forest Reserve, Hidden Valley

The Mountain Pine Ridge Forest Reserve in the Cayo District of western Belize is a highland plateau of pine forest and granite outcrops dramatically different from the lowland jungle most visitors associate with Belize. Hidden Valley Falls, dropping approximately 500 metres, is the tallest waterfall in Central America. The Forest Reserve has basic camping at designated sites, and several small jungle lodges within and adjacent to the reserve offer tent cabins and camping platforms. The area is notable for jaguar tracks, king vultures, and orange-breasted falcons.

## 2. Cockscomb Basin Wildlife Sanctuary

The Cockscomb Basin Wildlife Sanctuary in the Stann Creek District was established in 1986 as the world's first jaguar sanctuary, protecting a limestone basin ringed by the Maya Mountains. The sanctuary has a small visitor centre and designated campground with basic facilities. Trails of varying length traverse the basin, and jaguars, tapirs, peccaries, ocelots, and scarlet macaws are all present. The trail to Victoria Peak, the highest point in Belize, begins in the sanctuary and requires multi-day backcountry camping. The sanctuary is reached by road from Dangriga, with access improving in recent years.

## 3. Caracol Archaeological Reserve

Caracol is the largest known Maya archaeological site in Belize, occupying a high plateau in the Chiquibul National Park near the Guatemalan border. The journey to Caracol requires passing through the Mountain Pine Ridge area on rough road, and the site receives relatively few visitors because of the access difficulty. A small camping area near the on-site research station accommodates visitors who want to experience the ruins at dawn and dusk when tourist traffic is absent. Howler monkeys are vocal at sunrise, ocellated turkeys are visible in the clearings, and the uncrowded condition of the site makes this one of the more atmospheric archaeological camping experiences in Central America.

## 4. Glover's Reef Marine Reserve, Southwest Caye

Glover's Reef is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the best-preserved coral atolls in the Western Hemisphere. The Southwest Caye within the atoll is home to a basic camping and research facility — one of the few in the Caribbean where genuine camping with tent and sleeping bag is available on a caye. Access is by boat from Sittee River Point, a journey of approximately two hours. The reef system surrounding the atoll offers exceptional snorkeling and diving from the beach, with sharks, rays, sea turtles, and dense fish populations directly accessible without motorized access.

## 5. Rio Bravo Conservation Area

The Rio Bravo Conservation Area in northwestern Belize is a private conservation zone of approximately 100,000 hectares managed by Programme for Belize, a conservation nonprofit. It contains one of the largest tracts of lowland tropical forest in Central America and borders similar protected areas in Guatemala and Mexico. Camping at La Milpa Field Station and Hill Bank Field Station is offered to visitors; both are working research stations with basic dormitory and tent accommodation. Wildlife density in Rio Bravo is exceptional: jaguars, pumas, tapirs, all five species of wild cat found in Belize, and over 400 bird species.

## 6. Blue Hole National Park

Blue Hole National Park in the Cayo District contains the famous Blue Hole — a circular pool of cobalt water fed by a subterranean river system — and surrounding cave networks. The park has a small picnic and camping area near the main pool. The adjacent St. Herman's Cave is one of the most accessible Maya ceremonial caves in Belize, and the forest around the park holds keel-billed toucans, motmots, and a variety of jungle species. The park is easily reached from Belmopan, making it one of the more accessible camping experiences in the Cayo interior.

## 7. Chiquibul National Park, Backcountry

Chiquibul National Park is the largest protected area in Belize and is largely roadless. Access beyond Caracol requires four-wheel drive vehicles or foot travel. Backcountry camping in Chiquibul is genuinely remote: the park contains extensive cave systems including Actun Tunichil Muknal (ATM), a cave of extraordinary archaeological significance where Maya skeletal remains and pottery remain in situ. ATM tours begin from San Ignacio with a river crossing and jungle walk; camping near the trailhead allows for a less rushed experience than the common day-trip format.

## 8. Half Moon Caye Natural Monument

Half Moon Caye is a small island at the southeastern edge of Lighthouse Reef Atoll, designated as one of the first protected areas in Belize. It is the nesting site for the red-footed booby — one of the largest nesting colonies in the Caribbean — and also supports frigate birds and the globally threatened loggerhead sea turtle. Day visitors arrive by boat from Belize City; camping overnight on the caye (with permit) allows observation of the booby colony at dawn and dusk, when activity is most intense. The surrounding reef system offers excellent diving.

## 9. Lamanai Archaeological Reserve

Lamanai is among the longest continuously occupied Maya sites in Mesoamerica, with evidence of occupation from approximately 1500 BCE to the 16th century CE. The site is accessible from Orange Walk Town by boat along the New River — a journey through gallery forest where American crocodiles, snail kites, and mangrove swallows are routinely seen. The adjacent Lamanai Outpost Lodge offers tent accommodation at the edge of the reserve, allowing early morning access to the main temple structures before day-trip boats arrive from the coast.

## 10. Sarstoon-Temash National Park

The Sarstoon-Temash National Park in the Toledo District of southern Belize protects a complex of mangrove estuary, lowland rainforest, and river habitat on the border with Guatemala. It is one of the least visited national parks in Belize and one of the most ecologically intact. Camping is basic, access is by boat along the Temash and Sarstoon Rivers, and the experience is genuine frontier wilderness. The Toledo area is also the heartland of Garifuna and Q'eqchi' Maya cultures, and several community-based tourism operations in the district offer culturally embedded camping experiences.
