Rain is one of the most common reasons people cancel camping trips, yet with the right preparation it becomes one of the most memorable conditions to camp in. The smell of a forest floor after a downpour, the sound of drops on a well-pitched tarp, and the satisfaction of staying warm and dry while the weather does its worst are experiences that fair-weather campers never get. This guide walks through the full process of preparing for, setting up in, and enduring rain without misery.

## Understanding Rain and Camping Risk

Not all rain is the same. A light coastal drizzle over two days demands different preparation than a convective thunderstorm in the mountains. Before any trip, check the forecast carefully and understand what type of precipitation is expected. Sustained, moderate rain is generally manageable. Prolonged heavy rain combined with cold temperatures is far more dangerous because wet gear loses insulating value rapidly, and wet skin in wind causes heat loss that can lead to hypothermia even in summer conditions.

Timing matters too. Setting up camp in heavy rain is miserable and error-prone. If the forecast shows a gap in precipitation, plan your camp setup around that window. Arriving earlier than planned and getting your shelter pitched before rain arrives is almost always worth the extra effort.

## Shelter Selection and Pitch Quality

Your shelter is the single most important variable in rain camping. A tent rated to 1500mm hydrostatic head is adequate for light rain but will seep at the seams in sustained downpour. Most quality three-season tents have a 2000–3000mm rating, and dedicated wet-weather tents exceed 5000mm. More important than the rating is seam sealing: factory seam tape deteriorates over time, and older tents should be resealed before wet trips using purpose-made sealant.

Pitch quality matters as much as tent quality. On flat ground, water will pool under your groundsheet if there is any depression in the terrain. Look for ground with a subtle natural slope so water drains away from rather than toward your tent. Avoid camping in stream beds, which can flash-flood even when rain begins far upstream. Avoid camping directly under large trees, where drip-through after rain has passed continues for hours and falling branches pose a risk in high winds.

A footprint or groundsheet under your tent adds a layer of insurance, but it must not protrude beyond the tent edges — exposed groundsheet channels water underneath the tent rather than away from it.

## Rainfly and Vestibule Use

Modern double-wall tents have a mesh inner and a waterproof outer fly. In rain, the fly must be taut and properly staked out. A sagging fly that contacts the mesh inner will transfer water through the fabric. Guy lines are not optional in wind-driven rain: they keep the fly taut and prevent it from being pushed inward. Always stake out all guy point attachments before rain arrives rather than improvising in the dark.

Vestibules are underrated features. A tent with a large vestibule gives you a sheltered space to store wet boots, rain jackets, and cooking gear so they do not dampen your sleeping compartment. When cooking in rain, a properly pitched vestibule with the door partly open gives sufficient ventilation for a canister stove while keeping the cook out of the downpour.

## Tarps as Primary or Supplementary Shelter

A tarp provides significantly more covered area per unit of weight than a tent, which makes it a preferred tool for hammock campers, ultralight backpackers, and experienced campers who want a covered communal space. The classic A-frame tarp pitch is simple and effective but leaves both ends open to driving rain. Adding a diamond configuration at one or both ends, or pitching the tarp lower on the windward side, substantially reduces rain ingress.

For tent campers, a separate kitchen tarp rigged between trees over a cooking area transforms a rainy camp from an exercise in frustration into a functional outdoor room. A 3x3 metre silnylon tarp weighs under 500 grams and provides enough coverage for two or three people to cook, eat, and socialize under comfortably.

## Layering and Clothing Management

Staying dry is partly a clothing management problem. The single most damaging mistake in wet conditions is wearing your insulating layers when moving or working, allowing them to become wet with sweat or rain, and then having nothing dry to sleep in. Rain gear goes on before you get wet, not after. A well-designed hardshell jacket with a sealed hood and cuffs keeps your mid-layers dry during wet camp setup. The moment you are settled and stationary, rain gear comes off so it can dry while you use insulation inside the tent.

Wool and synthetic insulating layers continue to provide warmth when damp; down insulation loses most of its loft when wet. In sustained wet conditions, synthetic sleeping bags and synthetic-fill jackets are significantly more forgiving than down equivalents. Treated down (hydrophobic down fill) offers a middle ground but is not a substitute for good rain management.

## Keeping Gear Dry During Travel

Backpacks are not waterproof. Even packs marketed as water-resistant will saturate in hours of rain. A pack liner — either a purpose-made bag or a robust bin liner inside the pack — is the standard solution. Electronics, sleeping bags, and spare insulating layers go into the liner first. Rain covers over the pack protect the exterior but do not substitute for internal lining because water enters through the shoulder strap attachment points and top opening.

Stuff sacks and dry bags within the pack add a second layer of protection for critical items. Sleeping bags and down jackets in their own waterproof compression sacks remain dry even if the pack liner fails.

## Camp Hygiene and Waste in Wet Conditions

Rain complicates Leave No Trace waste management. The cat-hole method for human waste requires digging in soil that may be heavily saturated; in this case, dig deeper to ensure proper burial and move well away from any drainage channels where waste could wash into water sources. Wet weather accelerates food odour dispersal, making wildlife attraction from food scraps more likely. Keep all food and scented items in bear canisters or hung properly even in conditions where you might be tempted to leave things accessible for convenience.

## Morale and Mental Approach

The psychological dimension of rain camping is real. After two days of persistent rain, even well-prepared campers experience a narrowing of patience. Building morale-sustaining elements into the plan makes a practical difference: a good book, a pack of cards, a thermos of hot drink, and the knowledge that conditions will eventually change. Rain camping is a skill, and like any skill it improves with practice. Explore the [map](/map) to find campgrounds in regions with distinct wet seasons — coastal rainforest parks in the Pacific Northwest, the Scottish Highlands, and New Zealand's Fiordland — where rain camping is not an exception but the expected norm and campsite infrastructure reflects that reality.

## After the Rain

Wet gear needs proper drying before storage. Synthetic sleeping bags and insulating layers can be machine-washed and tumble-dried at low heat. Tents should be pitched or draped indoors until completely dry before being packed away, as mould grows rapidly in stored damp fabric. Footwear dries fastest with newspaper stuffed inside to absorb moisture. Making post-trip gear care routine extends the life of expensive waterproof equipment significantly.
