Thailand's camping landscape is considerably more varied than its reputation as a beach resort destination suggests. The country has more than 130 national parks and wildlife sanctuaries, protecting montane forest in the north, dry dipterocarp woodland in the northeast, wet seasonal forest in the west, and marine parks around the Gulf of Thailand and Andaman coast islands. Wildlife densities in the western forest complex — arguably Southeast Asia's last large intact mainland forest system — rival those of India's best reserves. The northern highlands offer hill tribe village camping and cloud forest trekking that belong to an entirely different world from the beach south.

Camping in Thai national parks generally requires booking through the national park online reservation system operated by the Department of National Parks. Many parks have developed campgrounds with tents available for rental, alongside spaces for those with their own equipment. The dry season from November through April is the most comfortable camping time across most of the country. The western parks experience heavy rainfall from May through October but the forest is at its most lush. The [campsite map](/map) covers Thailand's national parks and main camping areas, helping plan itineraries across the country's different regions.

## 1. Khao Yai National Park, Nakhon Ratchasima Province

Khao Yai is Thailand's most visited national park and a UNESCO World Heritage Site — a 2,168-square-kilometre expanse of seasonal forest and grassland on the Korat Plateau escarpment north of Bangkok. The park holds Asian elephants, gaur, sambar deer, gibbons, Asian black bears, and one of Southeast Asia's densest tiger populations. Night safaris on the park roads regularly encounter the predators and prey of the forest edge. The park campsite at the visitor center area has good facilities, and the Haew Narok waterfall — one of Thailand's largest — is a focal point for day walks. The proximity to Bangkok (roughly two to three hours) makes this the most accessible genuine wildlife camping in the country.

## 2. Doi Inthanon National Park, Chiang Mai Province

Doi Inthanon, at 2,565 metres Thailand's highest peak, is the centerpiece of a national park in the northwestern Chiang Mai highlands protecting montane forest with extraordinary birdlife. The summit holds Royal Twin Pagodas built to honor the King and Queen of Thailand, and the surrounding rhododendron and podocarpus forest hosts hundreds of endemic and regional bird species including some found nowhere else. The park campsite at the summit area sits in cloud forest at high altitude — cold by Thai standards, requiring a sleeping bag appropriate for near-zero temperatures even in winter. The birding from camp at dawn — while the cloud lifts from the valley below — is exceptional.

## 3. Kaeng Krachan National Park, Phetchaburi Province

Kaeng Krachan is Thailand's largest national park, protecting a vast expanse of mixed deciduous and evergreen forest in the Tenasserim Hills on the Myanmar border southwest of Bangkok. The park holds Asian elephants, wild boar, banteng (wild cattle), clouded leopards, and an extraordinary diversity of hornbill species — multiple hornbill species can be seen from the park campsites at dawn. The Ban Krang campsite in the park interior provides a forest camping experience with good mammal and bird watching potential. The Phanoen Thung mountain summit road gives access to misty ridge forest at higher elevation where gibbons call through the morning.

## 4. Doi Suthep-Pui National Park, Chiang Mai Province

Immediately above the city of Chiang Mai, Doi Suthep-Pui protects forested mountain terrain that provides the most accessible forest camping relative to an urban center in Thailand. The park campsite near the Doi Pui peak (1,685 m) is reached by road from Chiang Mai and sits in evergreen montane forest with excellent bird diversity. The Doi Suthep temple — one of Thailand's most revered Buddhist temples — sits on the shoulder below the peak and is reached by a famous 309-step naga stairway. Camping at altitude above the Chiang Mai smog season (January through March) provides clear air and cool temperatures that the city itself lacks.

## 5. Doi Phu Kha National Park, Nan Province

Doi Phu Kha in the far northeast of Chiang Rai province protects the highest terrain in the Nan highlands — remote montane forest on the Laos border with one of Thailand's most diverse endemic flora communities. The park is the only location of the Chiang Dao pitcher plant (Nepenthes mirabilis variety) and has the highest known density of Chamaecyparis trees in Thailand. The campsite near the park headquarters is simple and infrequently used, providing a genuinely uncrowded experience in exceptional highland forest. The local Mabri indigenous community ("yellow leaves" hunter-gatherers) have traditional territory within the park area.

## 6. Thung Salaeng Luang National Park, Phetchabun Province

Thung Salaeng Luang in the Phetchabun-Phitsanulok region protects a remarkable open grassland ecosystem embedded in a broader forest matrix — a savannah landscape more reminiscent of East Africa than typical Thai forest. The grassland (thung means meadow) hosts banteng, gaur, and deer grazing in open areas at dawn and dusk in a way that gives clear wildlife viewing impossible in dense jungle. Camping at the park is excellent, with open grassland campsites and the possibility of wildlife encounters from camp. Elephant herds move through the grassland, and this is considered one of Thailand's best areas for authentic wild elephant observation.

## 7. Huai Kha Khaeng Wildlife Sanctuary, Uthai Thani Province

The Huai Kha Khaeng Wildlife Sanctuary is part of the largest intact seasonal tropical forest in Southeast Asia — the Western Forest Complex — and holds one of the most significant tiger populations remaining in the region. The sanctuary is strictly protected, with visitor access controlled through the wildlife authority. Research camping within the sanctuary is possible for permitted visitors on specific programs. The adjacent Thung Yai Naresuan Wildlife Sanctuary is a UNESCO World Heritage Site together with Huai Kha Khaeng. This is the most biodiverse and strictly protected zone in the Thai national park system, and any permitted camping experience here is among the most significant wildlife encounters available in Southeast Asia.

## 8. Khao Sok National Park, Surat Thani Province

Khao Sok sits at the heart of a rainforest system in southern Thailand that is estimated to be over 160 million years old — pre-dating the Amazon rainforest. The Rajjaprabha Reservoir within the park creates an extraordinary landscape of flooded limestone karst islands surrounded by jungle, accessible by boat. Floating bungalows and camping platforms on the reservoir provide overnight stays in the middle of the jungle lake. Guided night kayaking on the reservoir under the limestone karst towers — listening to gibbons, hornbills, and the sounds of the ancient forest — is one of Thailand's most atmospheric camping experiences.

## 9. Ang Thong Marine National Park, Surat Thani Province

The Ang Thong archipelago — a cluster of 42 uninhabited limestone islands in the Gulf of Thailand north of Koh Samui — protects one of Thailand's most intact marine environments. The national park campsite on Koh Wua Talap is reached by day boat from Koh Samui or Koh Phangan and provides genuine island wilderness camping in a karst island environment. The island's central lake — Ko Mae Ko's emerald lagoon — is connected to the sea through an underground channel and is one of the most photographed natural features in the Gulf of Thailand. Snorkeling around the island fringing reef and kayaking between the karst pillars are the primary activities.

## 10. Phu Kradueng National Park, Loei Province

Phu Kradueng in northeastern Thailand is a table-top mountain with a broad summit plateau at around 1,316 metres — a distinct environment of pine and oak forest, stream valleys, and cliff-edge viewpoints unlike anything in central or southern Thailand. The 9-kilometre ascent trail carries all gear on porters (available for hire at the base) — no camping equipment other than sleeping bags and personal items is required, as tents are rented at the summit campground. The plateau in winter (November through January) has temperatures approaching zero at night and forests draped in mist. The park is closed from June through September during the monsoon season.
