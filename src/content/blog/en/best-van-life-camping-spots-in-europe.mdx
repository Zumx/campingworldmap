Van life in Europe has grown from a niche counterculture into a mainstream travel movement, supported by an extensive network of campsites, motorhome service areas (stellplatze in Germany, areas de services in France), free camping spots on public land, and a growing community of travellers who share location information through apps and social networks. Europe's combination of well-maintained road infrastructure, diverse landscapes across a relatively compact geographic area, and a patchwork of camping regulations — from Norway's permissive allemansrätten to France's stricter rules about roadside overnight stops — makes it both an accessible and a nuanced destination for van-based travel.

The van life community has identified several particularly strong zones across the continent — areas where the scenery is exceptional, wild camping is accessible, services are available at reasonable intervals, and the roads suit the unhurried exploration that van travel enables. What follows covers a representative selection, from the Nordic extremes to the Mediterranean south.

## Lofoten Islands, Norway

The Lofoten archipelago above the Arctic Circle in northwestern Norway is consistently cited among the most dramatic landscapes in Europe — jagged granite peaks rising directly from a cold, luminous sea, fishing villages on stilts in protected bays, beaches of white sand backed by mountains. Norway's allemannsretten law permits camping on uncultivated land for up to 48 hours without permission, and the Lofoten van life community is extensive enough that well-used informal spots are well-documented in apps like Park4Night. The midnight sun in June and July extends usable daylight indefinitely; the northern lights from September onward are a major draw. Roads on Lofoten are narrow and winding, favouring smaller vans.

## Alentejo and Algarve Coast, Portugal

Portugal's southern coast offers the most reliable winter sunshine in continental Western Europe, and the combination of mild temperatures, low cost of living, and a relaxed atmosphere around van camping has made the Algarve and the less-developed Alentejo coastline above it a hub for the European van life community from October through April. Wild camping on the coast is officially restricted in Portugal, but a network of low-cost aires and private quintas accepts vans. The town of Lagos and the coastline toward Sagres at Europe's southwestern tip has a large permanent community of van dwellers. Supermarkets, markets, and services are good throughout the region.

## Scottish Highlands, United Kingdom

Scotland's wild camping rights — codified in the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003 — are among the most generous in Europe, effectively permitting responsible camping anywhere on land that is not excluded (gardens, fields under crop, etc.). For van campers, this means a remarkable degree of freedom to park on forest tracks, beside lochs, and on hillside laybys with views across some of the most dramatic mountain and coastal scenery in Britain. The North Coast 500 route — a 830-kilometre circuit of the northern Highlands — has become a popular van route passing through Torridon, Assynt, and Cape Wrath. Roads on the NC500 are frequently single-track with passing places; large vans and motorhomes require careful driving and patience.

## Dalmatian Coast, Croatia

The Croatian coast from Zadar to Dubrovnik is a series of limestone peninsulas, islands, and national park landscapes bordering the intensely blue Adriatic. For van campers, the shoulder season (May-June and September-October) provides the best combination of accessible campsite space, manageable temperatures, and lower ferry queues for island access. Wild camping is officially prohibited in Croatia, but a network of campgrounds ranging from large holiday complexes to small agritourism farms provides affordable coastal access. Plitvice Lakes National Park and Krka National Park are natural features reachable on a Dalmatian van route.

## The Balkans: Bosnia, Montenegro, Albania

The western Balkans represent some of the most undervisited and scenically extraordinary territory in Europe. Bosnia's river canyons and mountain ranges, Montenegro's Bay of Kotor, and Albania's Riviera — the coast south of Vlore — all offer van camping in landscapes that feel genuinely remote and undiscovered compared to the crowded western European coastlines. Camping regulations are loosely enforced in most of these countries, and informal roadside camping is commonly accepted outside of town perimeters. Road quality varies considerably — Albanian mountain roads and some Bosnian rural routes require patience and are difficult for larger vehicles.

## Dolomites, Italy

The Dolomites in northeastern Italy are a UNESCO World Heritage Site with some of the most extraordinary mountain scenery in Europe — pink-tinged limestone towers rising above high meadows and spruce forest. For van camping, the passes and high valley roads — the Sella Pass, the Passo Giau, the Tre Cime di Lavaredo area — provide access to exceptional terrain. Many Dolomite municipalities now restrict overnight parking on popular roads due to overuse, and designated stellplatze in the valley towns provide official alternatives. The Dolomites are best visited in June-July or September when the spring flowers and post-tourist-season light are at their finest.

## Pyrenees, France and Spain

The Pyrenees mountain range forming the border between France and Spain offers van camping in a less crowded alternative to the Alps. The French side has numerous aires and designated camping areas in the national park zones; the Spanish side is more permissive about roadside camping in the national forest areas. The Spanish Pyrenees town of Ainsa is a popular van hub with good services and access to the Ordesa y Monte Perdido National Park, one of the finest mountain parks in Spain. The Pyrenean roads are less developed than the Alps, meaning longer journey times between passes but considerably fewer vehicles.

## Camargue, France

The Camargue wetlands at the mouth of the Rhone in southern France are a unique landscape of saltmarsh, lagoon, rice paddy, and flamingo-inhabited étangs. The region is famous for its white horses, black bulls, and extraordinary bird diversity. Van camping at campsites in and around the Camargue Natural Regional Park provides access to a landscape quite unlike anywhere else in France or Europe. Spring is optimal — the flamingo colonies are active and the wildflower display in the salt meadows is exceptional.

## Practical Van Life Planning in Europe

European van life requires understanding the patchwork of regulations across different countries and even different regions. Apps like Park4Night, Campercontact, and iOverlander provide community-reported parking and camping spots with current reviews. [Browse the map](/map) to plan routes across Europe, understand the geography between key destinations, and identify campgrounds and aires at logical stopping points along your route. Satellite internet via Starlink has dramatically improved connectivity for van lifers working remotely across Europe, removing one of the traditional constraints on extended van travel.

Border crossings within the Schengen Area are open, but the UK now has different border rules and a 90-in-180-day stay limit applies to non-EU nationals visiting the Schengen zone. Vehicle insurance, breakdown cover, and a valid country-specific first aid kit (required in some countries including France, where a breathalyser is also required) are the essential legal preparations for European van travel.
