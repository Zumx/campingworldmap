Tent setup is the kind of skill that feels simple until conditions are poor. Pitching a tent in a calm car park on a manufacturer's demo video takes two minutes. Pitching the same tent alone in building winds on a ridge campsite in the dark takes a different set of habits. The techniques below address both ends of that spectrum — the site selection decisions that set you up for success and the pitching mechanics that matter when it counts.

## Site selection principles

The single most consequential decision in camping is where you place the tent. Choose wrong, and no tent will keep you comfortable; choose well, and a modest tent performs excellently.

**Drainage**: look at the ground slope and identify where water will flow in heavy rain. A flat, apparently dry surface in a slight depression will pond water under the tent floor in a downpour. Pitch on high ground with surrounding slopes that drain water away from the site, not toward it.

**Wind exposure**: at established campgrounds, the site layout often addresses wind. In the backcountry, look for natural windbreaks — a boulder cluster, a stand of trees, the lee side of a ridge. A tent in full exposure to prevailing wind will be noisier, colder, and under more structural stress than one in a sheltered position. However, do not pitch under large dead trees or branches, especially in wind.

**Flat ground**: a sloping pitch causes both discomfort (you slide toward the low end) and cold (your sleeping pad is not flat against the ground, creating air gaps). If the only available surface has a slope, orient the tent so your head is uphill.

**Distance from water**: most land management authorities and Leave No Trace guidelines specify a minimum 60-metre setback from water sources for camping. Beyond ethics, camping close to water increases dew, condensation, and insect pressure. The ground near water is often soft and poorly draining.

## Pitching freestanding vs non-freestanding tents

A freestanding tent stands without stakes by tension across its pole structure. Stakes are still required for stability in wind — an unstaked freestanding tent can blow away. The advantage is that you can reposition the tent after pitching and shake debris out by lifting it.

A non-freestanding tent — including most ultralight single-wall shelters, tarps, and tunnel tents — requires stakes and guy-lines to stand at all. Stake positions are critical and must be placed before pitching the inner.

For freestanding tents: insert poles before staking. Stake at least the four corners once the structure is up, plus all guyline points if conditions are marginal. For non-freestanding tents: identify the critical stake points from the tent diagram, stake those first, then tension the structure and add remaining stakes.

## Pitching in wind

Wind pitching requires a modified sequence to prevent the tent from becoming a kite before it is staked. The approach:

Unpack the inner and outer (if separate) and identify the orientation. In high wind, lay the tent perpendicular to the wind, with the entrance facing away from the prevailing direction (low wind resistance orientation). Stake the windward side first — the side facing into the wind. With the windward stakes holding the tent down, insert poles and raise the structure. Stake the leeward corners and all guylines before letting go.

Guy-lines in wind: all guy-line attachment points should be staked in conditions above moderate wind. The guylines triangulate the poles against bending and significantly increase the tent's wind resistance. Set guylines taut but not extremely tight — some tension compliance in the lines reduces stress on pole ferrules.

## Pitching in rain

If pitching in active rain, the objective is to minimise how long the inner (mesh or fabric) is exposed to rain before the outer is providing cover. For double-wall tents with a vestibule: stake the footprint, then pitch the outer fly first using the poles inside or outside depending on the design, then attach the inner to the fly while the fly is providing overhead cover.

Some tents — particularly tunnel and geodesic designs — pitch outer-first as standard. Single-wall tents avoid the inner/outer timing problem but are more vulnerable to condensation in mild conditions.

Store anything not immediately needed in a waterproof bag during pitching in rain. Wet tent bags and sleeping systems are the primary failure point of bad-weather camping.

## Footprints and ground sheets

A footprint (custom-cut groundsheet matching the tent floor outline) protects the tent floor from abrasion and puncture. It should not extend beyond the tent floor footprint — if it does, rain will pool on the footprint and wick under the tent floor. A custom footprint is the best option; a DIY alternative is a Tyvek sheet cut to size.

In rocky terrain a footprint is strongly recommended. On soft grass the tent floor alone is usually adequate unless the grass is wet and cold, in which case a footprint adds a thermal buffer.

## Stake selection for different soils

Steel V-stakes (standard peg) work on compacted soil and turf. Titanium shepherd's hook stakes are lighter and effective on moderate soil. Sand or snow pegs — wide-surface stakes or improvised deadman anchors (stuff sacks filled with snow) — are required in loose media where conventional stakes pull out under load. Rock anchors (tying to fixed rocks with cord) where permitted replace stakes on bare rock.

## Condensation management

Condensation forms when humid interior air contacts the cold tent wall. The primary mitigation is ventilation — keep vents open even in cold weather. Position the tent with the primary vent facing the prevailing light breeze. In cold calm conditions, leave one vent cracked — a small airflow exchanges moisture-laden interior air with drier exterior air and significantly reduces condensation accumulation.
