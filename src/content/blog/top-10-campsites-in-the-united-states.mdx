The American campground system is the deepest in the world: a network of
National Park, National Forest, BLM, and state park sites that put you
inside landscapes most countries would put behind a fence. These ten are
the ones every camper should know.

## 1. Upper Pines, Yosemite, California

The classic Yosemite Valley campground, on the floor under Half Dome and
Glacier Point. Books out within minutes when reservations open five months
ahead — set an alarm and try anyway. The valley at dawn is worth it.

## 2. Jumbo Rocks, Joshua Tree, California

Pitch a tent inside a boulder maze in the high Mojave, with some of the
darkest skies in southern California overhead. First-come pitches still
exist; arrive Thursday for a weekend.

## 3. Many Glacier, Glacier National Park, Montana

On the east side of Glacier, beside Swiftcurrent Lake, with grizzly country
beginning at the campground edge. Probably the most concentrated wildlife
viewing of any US frontcountry site.

## 4. Watchman, Zion, Utah

Walking distance to the visitor centre and the shuttle into Zion Canyon,
with the Watchman peak glowing red at sunset directly above your tent.
The most logistically easy stay inside a major desert park.

## 5. Fruita, Capitol Reef, Utah

A green orchard oasis along the Fremont River, surrounded by red sandstone
cliffs and far less crowded than its neighbours Arches and Bryce. Pick fruit
from the historic orchards in season.

## 6. North Rim Campground, Grand Canyon, Arizona

The quieter, cooler, higher rim of the canyon — 2,500 m, ponderosa pine
forest, open only mid-May to mid-October. A different park from the South
Rim, and the better one to camp at.

## 7. Wonder Lake, Denali, Alaska

At the end of the Denali Park Road, with Denali itself filling the horizon
on clear days (which is roughly one in three). Bus access only; tundra,
caribou, and silence.

## 8. Assateague Island, Maryland

Camp on a barrier-island beach where wild horses wander between the tents
and the Atlantic surf is your soundtrack. Bring serious tent stakes — the
sand and wind are real.

## 9. Cape Lookout, Outer Banks, North Carolina

Primitive beach camping on an undeveloped barrier island accessible only by
ferry. No facilities, no shade, no light pollution — and miles of empty sand.

## 10. Devil's Garden, Arches, Utah

The only campground inside Arches, on slickrock among fins and arches, with
the Devil's Garden trail starting from camp. Reserve six months ahead or
forget it.

## How the booking system works

Federal sites (National Parks, National Forests, BLM developed sites) use
**recreation.gov**, with rolling release windows — usually six months ahead
for the most competitive parks, sometimes only two weeks for smaller ones.
State parks use state-specific systems (ReserveCalifornia, ReserveAmerica, etc).
Cancellations land constantly; refresh the page in the weeks before your
date and pitches reappear.

## Season, fire, and bears

Summer is peak demand and peak heat in the desert parks; April–May and
September–October are the camper's sweet spot almost everywhere except
Alaska and the high north (June–August only). Fire bans are common and
strictly enforced — check the park alert page the week of. In bear country
(Glacier, Yellowstone, Sierra, Alaska) use the provided bear boxes or a
certified canister; this is not optional.
